# The Adweek Copywriting Handbook — Full Working Guide

> A complete, faithful distillation of Joseph Sugarman's _The Adweek Copywriting
> Handbook: The Ultimate Guide to Writing Powerful Advertising and Marketing Copy
> from One of America's Top Copywriters._
>
> This guide mirrors the book's structure so we can apply Sugarman's principles to
> copy across The Cadre and other sites. Use it as a reference and a checklist.
> Axioms, the emotion principles, the 31 triggers, the 23 copy elements, and the
> 7 steps are quoted/transcribed directly from the book; chapter summaries are
> faithful paraphrases.

---

## Who he is and why this matters

Joseph Sugarman ran JS&A and built a fortune selling products by **mail order** —
the harshest test of copy there is. In mail order, the words are the entire
salesperson. There's no store, no demo, no handshake. If the copy fails, nothing
sells. He launched **BluBlocker sunglasses** (millions sold) off a single print
ad. So his lessons aren't theory — they're what survived contact with a coupon and
a P&L.

**The single mental model that runs through the whole book — the Slippery Slide:**
every element of an ad exists to get the reader to read the _first sentence_; the
first sentence exists to get them to read the _second_; and so on, until the reader
slides helplessly to the order. Your job is to remove every snag on that slide.

---

## The 15 Axioms (verbatim)

These are the book's spine. Numbers 1–14 belong to Section One; 15 is from
Chapter 20.

1. **Copywriting** is a mental process the successful execution of which reflects
   the sum total of all your experiences, your specific knowledge and your ability
   to mentally process that information and transfer it onto a sheet of paper for
   the purpose of selling a product or service.
2. **All the elements** in an advertisement are primarily designed to do one thing
   and one thing only: get you to read the first sentence of the copy.
3. **The sole purpose of the first sentence** in an advertisement is to get you to
   read the second sentence.
4. **Your ad layout and the first few paragraphs** of your ad must create the
   buying environment most conducive to the sale of your product or service.
5. **Get the reader to say yes** and harmonize with your accurate and truthful
   statements while reading your copy.
6. **Your readers should be so compelled** to read your copy that they cannot stop
   reading until they read all of it as if sliding down a slippery slide.
7. **When trying to solve problems**, don't assume constraints that aren't really
   there.
8. **Keep the copy interesting** and the reader interested through the power of
   curiosity.
9. **Never sell a product or service.** Always sell a concept.
10. **The incubation process** is the power of your subconscious mind to use all
    your knowledge and experiences to solve a specific problem, and its efficiency
    is dictated by time, creative orientation, environment and ego.
11. **Copy should be long enough** to cause the reader to take the action you
    request.
12. **Every communication** should be a personal one, from the writer to the
    recipient, regardless of the medium used.
13. **The ideas presented in your copy** should flow in a logical fashion,
    anticipating your prospect's questions and answering them as if the questions
    were asked face-to-face.
14. **In the editing process**, you refine your copy to express exactly what you
    want to express with the fewest words.
15. **Selling a cure** is a lot easier than selling a preventive, unless the
    preventive is perceived as a cure or the curative aspects of the preventive are
    emphasized.

## The 3 Emotion Principles (verbatim)

From Chapter 11 — memorize these:

1. Every word has an emotion associated with it and tells a story.
2. Every good ad is an emotional outpouring of words, feelings and impressions.
3. **You sell on emotion, but you justify a purchase with logic.**

# Section One — Understanding the Process (Ch. 1–17)

How copy actually gets made and what each piece is doing.

### 1. General Knowledge

A copywriter is a walking accumulation of life. Broad, eclectic experience —
hobbies, travel, reading widely, varied jobs — is the raw material your brain
recombines into ideas and metaphors. The more general knowledge you carry, the more
ways you can connect a product to something that resonates. Cultivate genuine
curiosity about everything.

### 2. Specific Knowledge

Then go deep. Before writing a word, immerse yourself in the product and the
prospect until you know them cold — features, use, the buyer's mindset. Sugarman
handled, tested, and lived with products before writing. Specific expertise is what
lets you spot the one detail that becomes the hook. (General + Specific knowledge =
the "sum total of experience" in Axiom 1.)

### 3. Practice, Practice, Practice

Copywriting is a skill, not a gift — built by reps. Pick up a pen and start. Write
volume over a long enough period and you improve every year.

### 4. The Purpose of All the Graphic Elements (Axiom 2)

Every visual element exists for **one** job: get the reader to read the first
sentence. The headline doesn't sell the product — it sells the first sentence. This
reframes design as a funnel into the copy, not decoration.

**The 10 Graphic Elements** (their only job is to pull the reader into sentence one):

1. Headline
2. Subheadline
3. Photo or Drawing
4. Caption
5. Copy
6. Paragraph Headings
7. Logo
8. Price
9. Response Device (coupon / phone / order mechanism)
10. Overall Layout

### 5. The First Sentence (Axiom 3)

Its only purpose is to get the second sentence read. So it must be **short, easy,
almost effortless** — so simple the reader slips past it almost against their will.
Sugarman's openers are tiny: _"It's easy." / "Losing weight is not easy."_ A long,
hard first sentence kills momentum before it starts.

### 6. Creating the Perfect Buying Environment (Axiom 4)

Layout, typography, imagery, tone, and the opening paragraphs together set a mood
that pre-disposes the reader to buy — the print equivalent of a well-designed store
or a Mercedes showroom. Match the environment to the product (clean and elegant for
premium; energetic for value). The first two paragraphs build environment and
momentum — **don't dump facts and features yet.**

### 7. Resonating with the Reader (Axiom 5)

Build agreement early. Make truthful statements the reader nods along to ("say
yes") so you "harmonize" — proving you understand who they are and what they need.
Each small yes builds trust and momentum toward the big yes.

### 8. The Slippery Slide (Axiom 6) — _the key concept_

Construct copy so frictionless that the reader, once started, cannot stop — they
slide helplessly to the end. The chain: graphic elements → first sentence → second
sentence → … → close. Every sentence's only job is to get the next one read. You
"grease the slide" with short sentences, curiosity seeds, easy rhythm, and open
loops — removing every snag that would let the reader escape.

### 9. Assumed Constraints (Axiom 7)

When solving a creative or marketing problem, people impose limits that aren't
actually there (think the nine-dots puzzle). The constraint you _assume_ is often
the door to the breakthrough. Always ask: "What am I treating as fixed that isn't?"

### 10. Seeds of Curiosity (Axiom 8)

Plant short curiosity-triggers at paragraph ends that compel the subconscious to
keep reading — especially where copy naturally slows. The practical tool for
keeping the slide greased:

- "But there's more."
- "So read on."
- "But I didn't stop there."
- "Let me explain."
- "Now here comes the good part."

The ideal is copy so compelling you don't need them — but they enhance most copy.
**Don't overdo it.**

### 11. Copy as Emotion

"Every word has an emotion and tells a story." Word choice carries feeling — write
to evoke it; a good ad is an emotional outpouring. But close the loop with logic:
**you sell on emotion, then justify with logic.** People buy emotionally, then need
rational reasons to defend the decision to themselves and others.

### 12. Selling the Concept, Not the Product (Axiom 9)

Never sell the thing — sell the larger idea around it. Sugarman didn't sell a
"thermostat," he sold "Magic Stat"; he sold a smoke detector as a decor/fashion
item. Find the concept that makes the product distinct and irresistible.

### 13. The Incubation Process (Axiom 10)

After loading your mind with general + specific knowledge, step away and let the
subconscious work. Solutions arrive when you stop forcing them (shower, walk).
Effectiveness depends on **time, creative orientation, environment, and ego** —
give it time, get into a creative space, cut distractions, and don't let ego block
the idea.

### 14. How Much Copy Should You Write? (Axiom 11)

**The more you tell, the more you sell.** Copy is never too long — only too boring.
Length should be exactly enough to move the reader to act: say everything needed to
make the sale, nothing that derails the slide. Long copy works if every line earns
its place.

### 15. The Art of Personal Communication (Axiom 12)

Write one-to-one, like a personal letter from you to a single reader, regardless of
medium. Conversational, warm, specific — never a faceless corporation broadcasting
to a "market." This personal voice is what makes copy trustworthy.

### 16. The Copy Sequence (Axiom 13)

Order ideas logically, anticipating and answering the prospect's questions in the
same sequence they'd arise face-to-face. Resolve each objection right when the
reader would raise it, so nothing stops the slide.

### 17. The Editing Process (Axiom 14)

Write long and emotional first; then cut hard. Refine to say exactly what you mean
in the fewest words — tighten rhythm, kill clichés, sharpen each sentence so the
slide stays smooth. Editing is where good copy becomes great. The constant
question: **"Is there a simpler way to say this?"**

# Section Two — Understanding What Works (Ch. 18–22)

## Chapter 18 — The 23 Powerful Copy Elements (checklist)

Review every ad against these 23. Part of the lesson is the _relative importance_:
some (resolving objections) are decisive; others (paragraph headings) barely matter.

1. **Typeface** — Personality + legibility, with legibility first. Use **serif** for
   body copy (it beat sans-serif on comprehension ~5:1 in legibility tests).
   Comprehension is job #1; conveying company image is a distant #2.
2. **First Sentence** — Short, easy, compelling enough to force the second sentence.
3. **Second Sentence** — Almost as important as the first. Sustain momentum through
   the first two paragraphs; don't push facts/features yet.
4. **Paragraph Headings** — Mostly there to break up copy so it looks less
   intimidating; they need have nothing to do with the copy beneath. Minor secondary
   job: arouse curiosity.
5. **Product Explanation** — Actually explain what it does. Rule of thumb: **explain
   a complicated product simply, and a simple product in a complex way.**
6. **New Features** — Highlight what's new, unique, or novel — what sets it apart.
7. **Technical Explanation** — Technical detail builds the perception that you're an
   expert, which builds trust — but you must _actually_ become the expert or the
   reader sees through it.
8. **Anticipate Objections** — Sense the objection the reader will raise, and raise
   it yourself. Ignoring an obvious objection is like ignoring the customer.
9. **Resolve Objections** — Then honestly resolve them: an alternative solution, or
   dispel them completely.
10. **Gender** — Know whether your buyer is male, female, or both; avoid offensive
    assumptions; speak in their terms.
11. **Clarity** — Clear, simple, short, to the point. Avoid big words that confuse or
    sound pompous. (Only exception: the technical explanation in #7.)
12. **Clichés** — Avoid them. "If you feel inclined to use a cliché, don't." A cliché
    signals you have nothing significant to say.
13. **Rhythm** — Copy has rhythm like a song. **Vary sentence length** (short, long,
    medium, short…). Use **triads** — things in threes — for pleasing cadence.
14. **Service** — For expensive or hard-to-return products, address service/repair
    and how easy it is. A brand-name manufacturer often does the reassuring for you.
15. **Physical Facts** — Include **all** of them — weight, size, dimensions, speed,
    limits — even ones you think don't matter. "Give readers any excuse not to buy
    and they won't buy."
16. **Trial Period** — For anything they can't touch first, offer a trial — at least
    one month, better two. Longer trials = fewer returns and more confidence.
17. **Price Comparison** — Compare to establish value. If you're priciest, justify
    with more/better; if cheaper, stress value. **Comparisons must be 100% accurate.**
18. **Testimonials** — Credibility from a credible, genuinely-matched source.
    Includes the **"reverse testimonial"** (turning a competitor's paid celebrity to
    your advantage) and man-on-the-street testimonials. Must be authentic.
19. **Price** — Decide its prominence. **Good price → large type. Expensive (price
    isn't the selling point) → underplay, but never hide it.** Put it where the reader
    will logically look (ideally on the coupon).
20. **Offer Summary** — Near the end, restate exactly what they get: "So here's my
    offer…" Many ads skip this.
21. **Avoid Saying Too Much** — The most common mistake. Edit ruthlessly; you can
    often cut 50–80% and say the same thing. (Restraint can itself stimulate the
    sale — see Mental Engagement.)
22. **Ease of Ordering** — Make it effortless: toll-free number, coupon, tear-off
    card. A coupon with dotted lines tests well — it signals "you can order here."
23. **Ask for the Order** — Always explicitly close: "I urge you to order today." Like
    a salesperson who never asks, copy that never closes doesn't sell.

## Chapter 19 — The 31 Psychological Triggers ⭐

The heart of the book. For each, the mechanism plus the example Sugarman uses.

1. **Feeling of Involvement / Ownership** — Use vivid, sensory language so the
   prospect mentally holds and uses the product. _"Hold it in your hand. Feel how the
   keys snap to the touch."_ Reply/involvement devices ("put the yes sticker in the
   yes box") can double or triple response.
2. **Honesty** — The single most important point. People sense a lie. The more
   truthful you are, the more they respond. Signature move: name a flaw up front, then
   explain why it doesn't matter — trust then carries every positive claim.
3. **Integrity** — Every element (copy, layout, graphics, claims) must align and
   reinforce the honesty. One exaggerated or inconsistent element erodes the whole.
4. **Credibility** — Built by anticipating and resolving objections and by explaining
   _why_ (e.g., why the price is low). Honesty + Integrity → Credibility.
5. **Value & Proof of Value** — Educate the reader to the product's real worth;
   doing so is "equivalent to lowering its price." Use comparisons and proof.
6. **Justify the Purchase** — Give logical reasons to rationalize an emotional buy
   (savings, health, "you'll be your kid's hero"). Pricier item → more justification.
7. **Greed** — People want to feel they're getting a steal. Discounts and free trials
   exploit this — but a price that's _too_ low damages credibility.
8. **Establish Authority** — Be the expert: largest, oldest, most experienced,
   third-party recognized — so the prospect trusts your judgment.
9. **Satisfaction Conviction** — Go beyond an ordinary guarantee to a bold,
   risk-reversing one that signals total confidence. (BluBlocker: return them
   "anytime you want," even after use.)
10. **Nature of the Product** — Every product has a personality; match the copy's tone
    to it (playful for a toy, serious for a medical device).
11. **Prospect's Nature** — Match the message to the specific audience's needs, wants,
    and mindset. Know exactly who you're talking to.
12. **Current Fads** — Tie products to trends for relevance and publicity — but be
    ready to move on fast as fads fade.
13. **Timing** — Nobody knows the right timing in advance. Test; the market tells you
    whether you're too early or too late.
14. **Linking** — Connect an unfamiliar product to something the prospect already
    knows and accepts, so the mind grasps it with less effort.
15. **Consistency** — Once someone commits or makes a first purchase, they're inclined
    to keep acting in line with it (fuels upsells and repeat buys).
16. **Harmonize** — Reach product/market fit by first patterning after proven,
    accepted conventions to build trust — then differentiate.
17. **Desire to Belong** — Sell membership in an admired group. People buy a
    **Mercedes** partly to belong to the group of Mercedes owners.
18. **Desire to Collect** — People feel a pull to complete a set. Make products
    collectible / part of a series; offer display devices.
19. **Curiosity** — Plant unanswered questions that pull readers through. The strongest
    motivator in mail order and books, where the buyer can't experience the product
    first. (BluBlocker TV: filming people's amazed first reactions — curiosity _was_
    the selling tool.)
20. **Sense of Urgency** — Deadlines, scarcity, limited editions, coming price
    increases — push "later" to "now," usually near the end.
21. **Fear** — Show the consequences of inaction (security, health) — but use it
    carefully, without overwhelming the reader.
22. **Instant Gratification** — Emphasize immediate benefit and fast delivery.
23. **Exclusivity, Rarity or Uniqueness** — Make the customer feel specially chosen or
    among a limited few.
24. **Simplicity** — Keep language, layout, and choices simple. One clear offer beats
    a confusing menu.
25. **Human Relationships** — Humanize the brand: people, team photos, demonstrations,
    first-person voice, positive human imagery.
26. **Storytelling** — Open with a true, relatable narrative to hold attention and let
    the prospect imagine the benefit.
27. **Mental Engagement** — Make the reader do a little cognitive work to reach the
    conclusion themselves; the small effort makes it more convincing. Guide them
    safely to the right conclusion without being too obvious.
28. **Guilt** — Give value first to trigger reciprocity (charities mailing free
    address labels create a mild obligation to give back).
29. **Specificity** — Concrete numbers beat vague claims and signal you did your
    homework. "New dentists everywhere recommend…" is weak; "**Ninety-two percent** of
    new dentists use and recommend CapSnap" makes them believe a real survey happened.
30. **Familiarity** — Repeated, consistent exposure builds trust. Don't kill a winning
    campaign just because _you're_ bored with it.
31. **Hope** — Motivate through aspiration (a gym sells hope of fitness; new clubs the
    hope of a better game). Imply results via testimonials — but only promise what the
    product can genuinely deliver.

> **Note on a common misattribution:** the "blemished product" / "thalidomide"
> examples are _not_ in this book. The famous flaw-first examples here are the ugly
> **Magic Stat thermostat** (Honesty) and the **BluBlocker** return policy
> (Satisfaction Conviction).

## Chapter 20 — Selling a Cure, Not Prevention (Axiom 15)

**Always sell the cure; avoid selling prevention.** A pill to _prevent_ cancer is a
hard sell at $20; a pill to _cure_ the cancer you already have sells at $1,000.

Two facts of human nature: (1) people assume they'll never get the affliction, so
preventives are a tough sell; (2) once they _have_ the problem, they'll pay far
more, easily. (Athlete's-foot spray ignored until you get athlete's foot; the
burglar alarm bought only after the neighbor is robbed.)

Rescue a preventive only by **positioning it as a cure** or emphasizing its curative
aspects (The Club became a "cure" as car theft rose; a fuel additive sold by what it
_cures_ — knock, ping, failed emissions — not what it prevents). Footnote: truly
breakthrough products are the _hardest_ to sell because people don't believe them —
**belief is one of the strongest motivators.**

## Chapter 21 — Rating Your Writing Level

Self-assessment: honestly gauge where your copy sits and keep raising the bar. Great
copy isn't limited to professionals — applying these principles can improve even an
agency's work dramatically (see the Sony teardown).

## Chapter 22 — Seven Steps to Writing Great Copy

1. **Become an expert on the product or service** you're going to sell. More great
   ideas come from deep product research than from any other source.
2. **Know your prospect** — who they are and what will actually motivate them to buy.
3. **Write your headline and subheadline** to grab the reader and create enough
   curiosity to pull them into the first sentence. Favor **short** headlines; aim
   subheadlines around **16 words**; first sentences as short as possible.
4. **Write the copy** — ignore grammar, structure, and mistakes; dump everything in
   your head onto the page and keep going.
5. **Edit your copy** — fix spelling/grammar/structure; cut unnecessary words;
   tighten.
6. **Incubate** — put it aside, do something else, ideally come back the next day.
   More time = better.
7. **Take a final look** — you'll catch much more and refine further. Repeat steps
   5–6 as many times as needed.

# Section Four — Utilizing Your Skills (Ch. 36 + Appendices)

## Chapter 36 — Writing for Different Media

**Overarching principle:** all the print/mail-order principles transfer to every
medium — but the _relative importance_ of each principle changes by format. Keep it
personal; build the slide; learn the medium's quirks before writing.

**Catalogs** — Cover = the headline (lead with the most dramatic product).
President's letter + photo up front humanizes the store. Each item: short headline +
explanatory subheadline + full copy with **every** salient fact. First-person voice.
Memorable toll-free number (e.g., 1-800-GOFEDEX) on every page. Catalogs may offer
product variations (single-product ads shouldn't).

**Flyers / insert stuffers** — After-the-sale pieces in the package; cheap, high
read rate (going to a customer). Mirror your catalog format; always include ordering
info.

**Direct mail** — The most personal print form. Use **"I"** and your "team," not
"we." The **letter is the most important component** (don't lean on the brochure).
Length can vary. **Never skip the P.S.** — often the most-read line. The mailing
list is critical (your own recent buyers are hottest). **The envelope is the
headline** — get it opened. Test every mailing.

**Newspapers** — Mail-order ads should pay for themselves on the first run. For
retail, **frequency** builds trust. Dailies = fast, short shelf life, mass-market,
low price. _Wall Street Journal_ for higher-ticket/upscale. Consistent format builds
rapport.

**Billboards** — **Brevity and simplicity above all.** One headline + minimal
subhead + a graphic, grasped at 75 mph. Great for buzz or brand reinforcement.

**Press releases** — Write like a news editor. "News Release," date, "For Immediate
Release," city dateline, who/what/when/where/why/how up top. Use **attributed
quotes** to say what you can't say yourself. Triggers that shine here: **linking +
current fads** (tie to a trend to earn coverage). The editor must see news, not a
puff piece.

**Radio** — The most personal mass medium; runs on the listener's imagination. Needs
**frequency**. Convey **one core message**. For direct response, an
easy-to-remember toll-free number is vital. The opening seconds are critical — start
with a curiosity trigger; make it sound like news.

**Television (short spots)** — Six criteria: (1) impulse **price point** (~$29.95 or
less); (2) **value** that looks almost too good to be true; (3) **simplicity** — one
offer, no choices; (4) **urgency** / time limit (they won't "buy later"); (5)
include a **website**; (6) **mass-market** appeal. Test free-gift vs. no-gift — a bad
bonus can kill the main sale.

**Infomercials (long-form)** — Adopt a **familiar format** the audience harmonizes
with (talk show, candid-camera street interviews). Best products: **$39–$250** with
good margin and/or strong back-end/continuity potential. More time = easier to
justify the purchase.

**Back-end / upsells** — On the order call, upsell a $49.95 sale toward $200 (deluxe,
multiples, continuity, coaching). **Match the after-sale channel to how they bought**
(TV buyers → telemarketing; print buyers → direct mail).

**Home shopping (QVC)** — Mostly live; supply a product brief + suggested upsells.
Best _after_ a product has proven itself elsewhere (one QVC "Today's Special Value"
sold 250,000 pairs of BluBlockers in a day).

**The Internet** — Four advantages: a tiny company can look huge; **testing is cheap
and instant**; idea-to-launch speed; many selling formats.

- **Email** — The most personal web form. Personalize, keep it brief, link to the
  longer message. **The subject line is the envelope/headline.** Lead with value and
  relationship-building; sell later.
- **Long internet letter / e-zine** — Same long-copy principles as print, plus
  audio/video testimonials and your photo. Key difference: put **"Buy Now" buttons on
  nearly every screen** — you can't rely on the reader scanning to the end.
- **Internet infomercial** — Any length, easy "Order Now" anywhere; remember web
  attention spans are shorter than TV — keep it fast.
- **Personal blog** — If you write, start one and **write every day**: you improve,
  people read it, and search engines favor fresh content.
- **Viral** — Seed games/animations to drive awareness and traffic.

## Epilogue — Some Final Thoughts

The skills compound. Master the process, internalize the triggers, study what works,
and apply relentlessly across every medium. The fundamentals don't change — only the
format does.

# Quick-Reference: The Cadre Working Checklist

Pull this up before shipping any sales page, email, or class description.

**Before writing**

- [ ] Am I an expert on this product/class? (Step 1)
- [ ] Do I know exactly who the prospect is and what motivates them? (Step 2)
- [ ] What's the **concept** I'm selling, not the product? (Axiom 9)

**Structure (the slide)**

- [ ] Does every graphic element funnel toward the first sentence? (Axiom 2)
- [ ] Is the first sentence **short and effortless**? (Axiom 3)
- [ ] Do the first two paragraphs build environment, not dump features? (Axiom 4)
- [ ] Am I earning small "yeses" early? (Axiom 5)
- [ ] Are there curiosity seeds keeping the slide greased? (Axiom 8)
- [ ] Does the sequence answer objections in the order they'd arise? (Axiom 13)

**Persuasion (triggers)**

- [ ] Honesty — did I name a real flaw and turn it around?
- [ ] Credibility — objections anticipated _and_ resolved?
- [ ] Proof of value + a reason to justify the purchase?
- [ ] Specificity — concrete numbers, not vague claims?
- [ ] A bold satisfaction conviction (risk reversal)?
- [ ] Sense of urgency near the close?
- [ ] Selling a **cure**, not a prevention? (Axiom 15)

**Voice**

- [ ] Reads like a personal letter from one human to one reader? (Axiom 12)
- [ ] Emotional first, justified with logic? (Emotion Principle 3)
- [ ] No clichés, no pompous words, varied rhythm, triads where they fit?

**Close**

- [ ] Did I summarize the offer?
- [ ] Did I make ordering effortless?
- [ ] **Did I actually ask for the order?**

**After**

- [ ] Edited to the fewest words — "is there a simpler way to say this?" (Axiom 14)
- [ ] Let it incubate, then took a final look? (Steps 6–7)
