# How to Create Irresistible Hooks (and blow up your content)

## Core Thesis

Don't memorize hook lists. Master the psychology of the curiosity loop and run a repeatable three-step formula: **Context Lean → Scroll-Stop Interjection → Contrarian Snapback**. The hook's job is to make the viewer feel that the next sentence is the only sentence they could possibly want to hear, and Kane's whole formula is engineered around stacking that compulsion across three or four sentences. For BuildOS, this translates into an agent that drafts hooks not as one-liners but as a four-beat script (with optional visual layer) where each beat has a defined psychological role and can be inspected and rewritten in isolation.

## Operating Lessons

### 1. Hooks are a 3-step formula, not a single line

Kane's whole pitch is that the hook is not "the first sentence" — it's a three-sentence machine: context lean, scroll-stop interjection, contrarian snapback. The first does topic clarity + lean-in, the second is "a stun gun" using a contrasting word like "but" / "however" / "yet", the third snaps the viewer in a direction opposite their initial expectation while staying on topic. He demonstrates the formula on his Vegas Sphere video (8M views) and on his real estate hook example, and proves it works by performing it on the viewer at the start of his own video.

Agent behavior:

- When asked to write a hook, output four labeled fields: `context_lean`, `scroll_stop`, `contrarian_snapback`, and `payoff_promise`.
- Refuse to compress the hook to a single sentence unless the user explicitly asks; instead show the three beats so each can be edited.
- After writing, self-grade each beat: does the lean signal the topic, does the stop use a contrasting connector, does the snapback go in the opposite direction of the lean.

### 2. Topic clarity beats trickery — let the wrong viewers leave

Kane is explicit: "if I'm talking about investing and you don't care about investing I actually don't want to trick you into watching the video because you're not my target viewer." A clear topic in the first 1-2 lines lets non-targets self-select out and lets targets self-select in. Vague hooks pull the wrong audience and tank average watch time.

Agent behavior:

- Ask the founder who the target viewer is before drafting; refuse to write a hook without that input.
- Reject vague universalist openers ("everyone struggles with productivity"). Push for a clear topic noun in the first line.
- For BuildOS founder content, surface the specific archetype (creator, ADHD operator, founder, knowledge worker) inside the lean.

### 3. Four ways to earn a lean — pick deliberately

Kane lists four mechanisms to make a viewer lean in: (a) establish common ground, (b) reference a benefit or pain point, (c) use a metaphor to simplify a complex idea, (d) tell them something so interesting it blows their mind. The Sphere hook used "blow your mind." The magnesium example uses pain-point lean ("if you want better sleep").

Agent behavior:

- For each draft hook, tag which lean mechanism is being used.
- Generate at least two variants using different lean mechanisms so the founder can A/B which lands.
- For BuildOS demo videos, default to pain-point or metaphor leans; for thought-leadership posts, default to "blow your mind" or common-ground leans.

### 4. The scroll-stop interjection is a setup, not a payoff

Kane's framing: "this is more just meant to be a setup line for the haymaker that's coming in step three but in order for them to accept the haymaker you need to stun them first." The stun is achieved with a contrastive connector ("but get this", "but here's the thing", "however", "yet", "although", "on the other hand"). It re-uses an embedded belief or expectation so the snapback feels earned. In his own video, he stuns by saying "I'm NOT going to give you a list of 25 proven viral hooks" — leveraging the viewer's existing fatigue with that genre.

Agent behavior:

- Force a contrastive connector in beat 2 of every hook.
- Build the stun by referencing what the audience expects to hear next (then refusing it), not by adding new info.
- Flag stuns that are merely cliffhangers ("but wait...") with no embedded belief — those are weak.

### 5. The contrarian snapback must redirect, not just escalate

Snapback rule: "a sentence that goes in the opposite direction of the initial lean… still on topic but it snaps the viewer back on a different path. The bigger the shock the bigger the snap the better the effect." Sphere example: lean was about the screen being huge → snapback redirects to the audio being more impressive. Real estate example: lean was high interest rates → snapback redirects to a different waste of money entirely.

Agent behavior:

- Verify the snapback travels in a different direction than the lean (not just "more of the same, bigger").
- Prefer snapbacks that introduce a _different category_ of cause/effect than the lean implied.
- If the snapback is empty (no real payoff exists for the rest of the video), refuse and tell the user — Kane warns that an empty snapback makes you "a fraud" and viewers churn.

### 6. Visual hooks are 100x more powerful than spoken hooks

Kane: "visual hooks are probably 100 times more powerful than just spoken word hooks… because people read faster than they can hear." Two parts: (a) text on screen — 3-5 words, big bold font, distilled — and (b) the visual itself, with "just enough motion" to function like a deer hearing a sound. He cites his life-size floor plans video (~15M views) where he used "future of home design" instead of the literal product name to avoid misunderstanding. Too much motion overwhelms; too little bores. The Colin & Samir Ray-Ban Meta video (60M views) is his example of perfect motion calibration.

Agent behavior:

- For every script hook, also output a `text_overlay` field of 3-5 words that distills the topic.
- Suggest the visual cue type: rapid motion intro, talking-head with subtle physical motion, or product motion.
- Warn when the proposed text overlay uses the literal product/feature name if a more emotionally legible phrase exists (e.g., "future of home design" beats "life-size floor plans").
- For BuildOS demos, recommend overlay text that frames the _category_ or _promise_, not the feature name (e.g., "your second brain catches up" not "BuildOS phase 2").

### 7. Lead with benefit/pain, not with the thing

Kane's magnesium example: a hook starting "you should be taking magnesium because it's one of 21 core building block minerals" requires the viewer to already care about magnesium. A better hook — "if you want better sleep you need to be taking magnesium" — leads with the pain (no sleep) and introduces the thing as the solution. "Even if someone is against supplements or against magnesium they will likely wait and hear you out because their desire to solve the pain of not sleeping well is so strong."

Agent behavior:

- Always check if the draft hook leads with the _thing_ or the _outcome_. Rewrite to outcome-first.
- Generate the pain-point version even if the founder asks for a feature-led hook.
- Apply this to BuildOS positioning: "if you want your messy thinking to turn into structured work" beats "BuildOS uses LLMs to extract tasks."

### 8. Cult hopping — borrow comfort from known references

Kane: people feel dumb when they hear unfamiliar references and bounce. Wrap unknown ideas inside a known cultural anchor — celebrity, brand, movement. Example: complex tax planning → "how Taylor Swift's financial advisor would plan her estate around the Eras Tour earnings she just made." The viewer doesn't need to know tax law; they know Taylor Swift, and that comfort buys you attention while you teach the unfamiliar idea. He frames this as drafting off existing credibility while you're still small.

Agent behavior:

- For technical or niche BuildOS topics (LLMs, agents, ontology), suggest a cult-hop wrapper using a culturally legible person, brand, or movement.
- Maintain a small library of safe cult-hop anchors aligned with the founder's audience (creators, builders, founders).
- Avoid cult-hops that contradict BuildOS's anti-AI positioning (don't anchor to AI hype names; do anchor to creators, authors, athletes, productivity icons).

### 9. Compress speed to value — frontload

Kane's frame: a timer counts down before the viewer ditches. ~4 seconds for short-form, ~1-2 minutes for YouTube. "Anything you make after they click off might as well be a black screen." Inject the first hit of value inside or immediately after the hook. This applies to both educational and entertainment content. The same curiosity-loop logic applies to a value loop: context → value → context → value.

Agent behavior:

- For each hook, also propose the _first value beat_ that lands within 4 seconds for short-form or 30-90 seconds for long-form.
- Reject scripts that delay value past the hook to "build up" — call this out explicitly.
- For BuildOS demo videos, recommend front-loading one tangible payoff (a brain dump getting structured) before any feature explanation.

### 10. Staccato sentences in the hook, vary later

Staccato = short, sharp notes in music. In the hook: shorter sentences force max clarity and increase density of value per word at the moment when time is most expensive. He distinguishes this from his earlier storytelling advice where varied sentence rhythm matters across the whole video. The rule: short up front, expand to medium and long as the video progresses.

Agent behavior:

- Cap sentence length in the hook at ~12 words. Flag and rewrite longer ones.
- After the hook, allow rhythmic variation (short-medium-long) for retention.
- For LinkedIn/Twitter posts, apply staccato to the first 2-3 lines (the visible-before-expand zone).

### 11. The formula generalizes beyond entertainment

Kane stress-tests the formula with a B2B real estate hook ("three massive mistakes people are making with their mortgage… most people think it's because of high interest rates… but it has nothing to do with that because the biggest waste of money is actually coming from…"). He claims the formula works for every category and every space — provided the snapback has a real payoff. This is important for BuildOS: the same formula serves blog post titles, demo video openers, LinkedIn posts, and pitch decks.

Agent behavior:

- Apply the same three-beat structure regardless of channel (blog H1+lede, Twitter thread opener, LinkedIn first 3 lines, demo video first 4 seconds, pitch deck slide 1).
- Maintain channel-specific length budgets but keep beat roles identical.

### 12. Reps over knowledge — make the formula subconscious

Kane closes by emphasizing volume: "I've spent thousands of hours doing this myself what you really need are reps and then guidance from somebody who has more reps than you." Knowing the formula is not the same as wielding it — that requires reps and feedback.

Agent behavior:

- Encourage the founder to draft 3-5 hook variants per piece, not one.
- Maintain a hook log that records which variants shipped and how they performed, so the agent can learn the founder's voice over time.
- After every shipped piece, prompt for performance numbers and update the variant scoring rubric.

## Frameworks Or Templates

### The Three-Step Hook Formula (the megaframework)

1. **Context Lean** — 1-2 sentences. (a) State the topic clearly so wrong viewers exit. (b) Get the right viewer to lean in via one of: common ground, benefit/pain reference, simplifying metaphor, or "blow your mind" insight.
2. **Scroll-Stop Interjection** — 1 sentence. Use a contrasting connector ("but", "however", "yet", "although", "therefore", "on the other hand") to stun the viewer. Reuse an embedded belief they already hold.
3. **Contrarian Snapback** — 1 sentence. Redirect the viewer in the opposite direction of the lean, on the same topic. The bigger the shock, the bigger the snap.

### Walk-through: Sphere video

- Lean: "The tech in the Vegas sphere is insane — biggest screen ever built, 20 times bigger than an IMAX."
- Stop: "But get this — the screen is actually the least impressive part of the whole thing."
- Snapback: "Because the most impressive part is the audio. This is going to blow your mind."

### Walk-through: real estate B2B

- Lean: "There are three massive mistakes people are making with their mortgage. The average person pays an extra $122,000 per year."
- Stop: "Now most people think it's because of high interest rates."
- Snapback: "But it turns out it has nothing to do with that, because the biggest waste of money is actually coming from…"

### Walk-through: this video on itself (meta-demo)

- Lean: "Today we're talking about hooks. If you want your videos to perform better you have to make better hooks."
- Stop: "But here's the thing — I'm not going to give you a list of 25 proven viral hooks because that's not what you need."
- Snapback: "What you need is to understand the psychology behind why those hooks worked, and then the tactics for how to use that psychology in your own stuff."

### Five sauce-on-top tactics

1. **Visual hook = text overlay (3-5 words, bold) + visual with "just enough motion."** Read-speed beats listen-speed.
2. **Lead with benefit/pain, not feature.** Hostile audiences will still listen if the pain is theirs.
3. **Cult hopping** — wrap unfamiliar ideas inside known references (celebrity, brand, movement).
4. **Compress speed to value** — frontload the first hit of value within the hook or immediately after.
5. **Staccato sentences in the hook**, then expand rhythm later.

### The four lean mechanisms

- Common ground.
- Benefit or pain point.
- Simplifying metaphor.
- "Blow your mind" interesting fact.

### Connector words for the scroll-stop

"but", "however", "yet", "although", "therefore", "on the other hand."

## Anti-Patterns

- **Trick hooks that capture wrong audience.** If the lean is too vague, non-target viewers click in and bounce, killing watch time.
- **Memorizing a list of 25 viral hooks instead of learning the psychology.** Kane's whole opening rejects this.
- **Empty snapbacks with no payoff.** "If you just do this game but then you don't actually have anything at the end of the rainbow, people would just churn and you're a fraud."
- **Snapbacks that escalate in the same direction as the lean.** A real snapback redirects, not amplifies.
- **Stun lines that just say "but wait…" without leveraging an embedded belief.** No contrast = no stun.
- **Burying the value past the 4-second mark in short-form.** "Anything you make after they click off might as well be a black screen."
- **Leading with the feature/thing instead of the outcome.** Hostile audiences won't grant attention to a feature; they'll grant it to a pain solve.
- **Visual hooks with too much motion (overwhelm) or too little motion (boredom).** Calibrate like a deer's peripheral vision.
- **Using the literal product name in text overlay when an emotional category phrase exists.** "Life-size floor plans" → "future of home design."
- **Long sentences in the hook.** Density of value per word collapses.
- **Niche references with no cult-hop wrapper.** Viewers feel dumb and bounce.
