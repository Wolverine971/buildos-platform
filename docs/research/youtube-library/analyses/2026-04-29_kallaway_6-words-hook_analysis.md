## Core Thesis

A hook does not require a clever formula or strong writing. It requires **six structural slots**, four mandatory and two optional. If an opening sentence (or sentence pair) fills the four mandatory slots, the scroll stops. The optional two slots intensify curiosity when the topic warrants them.

Kane's frame: stop trying to "write better" and start **stacking power-word chunks** in the right order. Each chunk is a single word or short phrase that does a specific job. The same chunk can serve more than one job (objective and contrast often collapse). Proof and time are skippable for entertainment, near-mandatory for education.

For BuildOS, the translation is: a hook-writing agent should not generate freeform copy. It should treat hook generation as a **slot-filling task** where the agent's job is (a) identify which of the four mandatory slots are missing or weak in a draft, (b) propose candidate fillers, (c) decide whether proof and time slots earn their place. The audit is more valuable than the generation.

## Operating Lessons (the 6 words)

The video is built around six slots Kane calls "power words." Slots 1-4 are mandatory. Slots 5-6 are optional curiosity intensifiers. Each lesson below documents the slot's function, examples from the four hooks Kane breaks down, and the agent behavior implied.

### 1. Subject clarity word (mandatory)

**Function:** Names the actor whose action drives the hook. Subject is grade-school subject-predicate. Without a subject, the viewer has no one to project onto and no one to track.

**Forms it takes:**

- First-person: `I`
- First-person plural: `we`
- Second-person (most common in education): `you`
- Proper noun / entity: `life-size floor plans`, `that AI company over there`

**Examples in the video:**

- "If **I** had to grow from 0 to 100K subs on YouTube" — `I` as subject (founder/creator self-as-subject).
- "**You** can literally walk through your exact home design" — `you` as subject after the entity is named.
- "This is why **you** should always wash your bread" — `you` (man addressed as proxy in some Hormozi hooks; viewer in this case).
- "If **we** were smarter, we would listen to 13-year-olds more" — `we` (royal/collective).

**Agent behavior:**

- Never generate a hook without an explicit subject in the first sentence.
- If draft hook lacks a subject, flag it and propose `I`, `we`, `you`, or the strongest proper-noun candidate.
- Match subject choice to format: education hooks lean `you`; lived-experience hooks lean `I`; cultural-observation hooks lean `we`; entity-reveal hooks lean proper noun.
- In BuildOS-flavored content, the subject is usually `you` (viewer) or `the founder` (proxy persona) — never an unnamed third party.

### 2. Action word (mandatory)

**Function:** The verb (or verb phrase) describing what the subject does to produce the outcome. Without an action, the hook is a static description and there is nothing to replicate.

**Forms it takes:**

- Strong verbs: `grow`, `walk through`, `wash`, `marry`, `listen`.
- Verb + adverb pairs: `should always wash`.
- Compound or split actions: "if you find a girl … just marry her" — `find` and `marry` are two actions bookending the hook.

**Examples in the video:**

- `had to grow` — the action that generates the YouTube outcome.
- `can literally walk through` — viewer-replicable action.
- `should always wash` — instructional / corrective action.
- `find a girl … marry her` — split action.
- `would listen` — counter-intuitive action (placed after the objective, not before).

**Agent behavior:**

- Identify the verb. If it is a weak generic verb (`do`, `have`, `try`), propose a stronger replacement that names the actual mechanic.
- Action word can sit before or after the objective. Order is flexible. Count it present if it exists anywhere in the first sentence.
- Watch for split actions ("if you X … then Y") — they bookend longer hooks and create rhythm.

### 3. Objective / end-state word (mandatory)

**Function:** Names the result the action produces. Kane says the **more shocking the result, the better the hook**. This is the single highest-leverage slot for stopping the scroll.

**Forms it takes:**

- Concrete outcome metric: `0 to 100K subs on YouTube`.
- Sensory experience: `walk through your exact home design before you build it`.
- Implied state-change: `washed bread` (never spoken; implied by "wash your bread").
- Compound list of qualities: "believes in your dreams more than you do, makes you want to be a better man, willing to work alongside you, grateful for whatever you have right now today" — multiple objectives stacked.
- Counter-intuitive collective insight: `we would listen to 13-year-olds more`.

**Examples in the video:**

- `from 0 to 100K subs on YouTube` — quantified, time-bounded.
- `walk through your exact home design before you build it` — sensory, before-vs-after.
- `washed bread` — implied, redundant if explicit.
- `marry her` — outcome embedded in action.
- `listen to 13-year-olds more` — outcome that breaks expectation.

**Agent behavior:**

- Push for shock, specificity, and concreteness. "Get more done" fails. "Ship a 14-day project in 4 days" passes.
- Quantify wherever possible. Numbers do triple duty: they prove, they shock, they create contrast.
- Allow the objective to be implied if explicit phrasing would be redundant (the bread case).
- If objective is vague, the hook is dead — escalate this above all other revisions.

### 4. Contrast word (mandatory)

**Function:** Encodes the **before vs. after** state change. The contrast does not have to be a single word; often the same phrase that carries the objective also carries the contrast (e.g. `0 to 100K`, `before you build it`).

Kane's framing: ask **what is the base state expectation the viewer has today?** Then ask **what new reality does this objective open up?** The contrast is the explicit collision of those two states inside the sentence.

**Forms it takes:**

- Numeric range: `0 to 100K` (zero to large number is contrast and objective in one).
- Temporal pivot: `before you build it` (after-vs-before).
- Negation pivot: `wash your bread` (the assumed default is "don't wash bread"; the new reality is "wash bread").
- Expectation flip: `listen to 13-year-olds more` (assumed default is "listen to elders"; flipped to youth).
- Magnitude flip inside a list: "she believes in your dreams **more than you do**" (contrast embedded inside an objective phrase).

**Examples in the video:**

- `0 to 100K subs` — numeric magnitude contrast.
- `before you build it` — temporal/order contrast (was: after build; now: before build).
- `wash your bread` — assumption-flip contrast.
- `right now today` plus the qualities list — embedded contrast inside objectives.
- `13-year-olds` — age-flip contrast against expected wisdom source.

**Agent behavior:**

- Always require an explicit before/after axis. If a hook describes only the after-state, it has objective without contrast and underperforms.
- Detect collapse: if objective and contrast are carried by the same phrase, mark both slots filled by that phrase. This is normal and tight.
- When a draft hook describes a "win" without an implied "loss-state-it-flips," generate the missing assumption out loud, then rewrite to encode it.

### 5. Proof word (optional)

**Function:** Qualifies why the viewer should trust the speaker on this topic. Often a single word or short phrase. Kane treats proof as **optional but skewed-mandatory for education content** and **rarely needed for entertainment**.

**Forms it takes:**

- Single-word implication: `again` ("if I had to grow from 0 to 100K **again**" — implies it's already been done once).
- Visual proof carried by context: subscriber count visible on profile while the hook plays.
- Deferred to second/third line: "I'm a professional baker that's been doing this for 30 years" — proof can live in line 2 if line 1 is full.
- Implicit credentialing via specificity: "billions of views, 1M+ followers" — Kane's own intro.

**Examples in the video:**

- `again` — the cleanest one-word proof injection in the video.
- Implied proof from on-screen subscriber count (Kane uses this himself).
- The Hormozi marriage hook has **no proof** — entertainment / values content does not need it.

**Agent behavior:**

- For education or instructional content, audit the hook: is there proof in line 1, or queued for line 2?
- If neither, propose a proof injection. Prefer one-word injections (`again`, `still`, `even today`, the credential noun).
- For entertainment, narrative, or values content, skip proof. Forced proof in entertainment makes the hook sound like a sales pitch.
- Distinguish "stated proof" from "implied proof from context" (visible profile, channel name, prior video evidence). Both count.

### 6. Time word (optional)

**Function:** Adds a time constraint or speed parameter that ratchets curiosity by mapping speed to effectiveness. Time words make the desirability of the outcome feel scarce or urgent.

**Forms it takes:**

- Explicit duration: `in 5 months`, `in the next 90 days`.
- Implicit time-pivot inside contrast: `before you build it` (also functions as a time word — Kane explicitly debates this).
- Urgency anchor: `right now today` (Hormozi's marriage hook).
- Speed-to-result framing: any phrase that compresses how fast the objective is reached.

**Examples in the video:**

- `in 5 months` / `in the next 90 days` — proposed alternates for the YouTube hook.
- `before you build it` — debatable time word in the floor-plans hook.
- `right now today` — Hormozi's urgency anchor.
- The bread and 13-year-olds hooks have **no time word** — entertainment/values content does not need it.

**Agent behavior:**

- Audit whether adding a duration would intensify the hook without sounding fake. If the speaker can credibly defend the timeframe, add it.
- Prefer specific durations over vague urgency. `in 90 days` beats `quickly`.
- Time words can be retrofitted into existing hooks at low cost — this is one of the cheapest revisions an agent can propose.
- Do not stack time and proof if the hook already runs long. The four mandatory slots must read clean before optionals are added.

## Frameworks Or Templates

### The 6-Word Slot Stack

The explicit Kane formula. Every hook is built by filling these slots with single words or short phrases:

```
[Subject] [Action] [Objective/End-State] [Contrast] (+ [Proof]) (+ [Time])
```

Mandatory: Subject, Action, Objective, Contrast.
Optional: Proof, Time.

### Audit Pass (the inverse use)

Given any draft hook, walk the slot list and ask:

1. **Subject** — Who is doing this? If absent, insert `I` / `we` / `you` / proper noun.
2. **Action** — What verb? If weak, replace with the actual mechanic.
3. **Objective** — What is the end state? Is it shocking, specific, quantified?
4. **Contrast** — What was the base state? Is it explicit in the sentence?
5. **Proof** (education only) — Why trust the speaker? One word or deferred to line 2.
6. **Time** (when credible) — How fast? Specific duration preferred.

### The Copy-Work Drill

Kane's authoring process — repeatable for an agent:

1. Pick 3-5 top creators in the niche.
2. Pull their top videos by outlier score / view count, filter recent (12 months).
3. Transcribe the first sentence (the hook).
4. Tag each word in the hook with one of the six slots.
5. Delete the topic-specific words. Keep the slot structure.
6. Refill the slots with the user's own subject / action / objective / contrast.
7. Repeat 10-30 times per week.

Pattern recognition lives in the muscle memory of slot-tagging, not in fancy generation.

### Slot Collapse Patterns

These collapses are normal and tight, not errors:

- **Objective + Contrast collapse:** `0 to 100K` is both. Most numerically-anchored hooks do this.
- **Action + Objective collapse:** `marry her` is both — the action is the outcome.
- **Subject doubling:** life-size floor plans introduce the entity, then `you` becomes the operating subject for the action. Two subjects in two clauses is fine.

## Anti-Patterns

What NOT to do, drawn from Kane's framing and from the inverse of his examples:

- **Subjectless openings.** "Here's how to grow on YouTube" — no subject, no projection target.
- **Vague objectives.** "Get better at content" — no shock, no specificity, no number, no contrast.
- **Objective without contrast.** "Walk through your home design in VR" — describes the after-state but doesn't encode the before-state.
- **Forced proof in entertainment.** Tacking "as a 30-year baker" onto a viral entertainment clip kills the texture.
- **Generic time words.** "Quickly," "soon," "fast" without a specific number do less than no time word at all.
- **Stacking all six in one short sentence.** When optionals crowd the mandatory four, the hook reads like a SEO title. Push optionals to line 2.
- **Cleverness over slots.** Trying to write "punchier" without auditing slots produces hooks that feel sharp but don't perform — Kane's whole pitch is that craft is slot-stacking.
- **Hero-founder posturing as objective.** "I figured out the secret to X" — the speaker is the subject AND the objective. The viewer has no end-state to want.
- **Borrowing a hook without re-tagging.** Copying a winning hook and swapping nouns without slot-checking often produces a hook missing contrast or shock.

## Open Questions / Gaps

- **Line-2 / line-3 mechanics.** This video stops at line 1. How does proof or time get reintroduced in line 2 without sounding like a sales pitch? Probably covered in Hooks Impossible To Skip — verify when that transcript is analyzed.
- **Visual hook.** Kane never addresses the on-screen visual hook (b-roll, text overlay, face-cam) that runs parallel to the verbal hook. The 6 slots are verbal-only. The combo needs to layer in the visual axis from a sibling video.
- **Long-form transfer.** Does the 6-slot stack hold for blog leads and email subject lines, or only short-form video? Worth testing on BuildOS blog leads to validate.
- **Slot weights.** Kane says all four mandatory slots must be present, but doesn't rank them. Working hypothesis: Objective and Contrast carry more weight than Subject and Action — i.e. a hook can survive a weak subject if the objective is shocking enough. This needs A/B-style validation against the 100 Viral Hooks dataset.
- **Cultural-flip hooks (the "13-year-olds" pattern).** Kane treats this as just another contrast, but it may be a distinct sub-archetype where the contrast inverts a cultural assumption rather than a numerical or temporal base state. Worth tagging separately when synthesizing across the four hook videos.
- **Anti-formula risk.** A hook that perfectly fills all six slots can read mechanical. The combo skill needs a "polish pass" rule that breaks slot rigidity when the hook starts to sound generated. Likely a Storytelling-combo cross-link.
- **BuildOS voice fit.** BuildOS marketing leans anti-AI, anti-hype. The "0 to 100K" / "shock value" register is on the hype side. The skill draft must include a **voice-translation step** that keeps the slot grammar but shifts register — e.g. for the BuildOS audience, contrast might be "messy thinking → structured work" instead of "0 to 100K subs."
