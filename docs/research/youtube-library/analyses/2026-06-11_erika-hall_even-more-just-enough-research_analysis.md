## Core Thesis

Research is becoming informed enough to make a better decision — nothing more academic than that. The standard for applied research is **"making better products faster because you're making better design decisions,"** not publishing. The single biggest impediment to good design is **people wanting to be right**: certainty feels good, uncertainty is terrifying, so teams defend answers instead of asking questions. The cure is a **research mindset** (questions outlive answers) plus **collaboration** (evidence-based decisions made in a shared reality), and a discipline of starting from goals and risk — never from a method.

Two governing maxims: _"A question is so much more powerful than an answer"_ and _"All business is about making bets on human behavior."_

## Operating Lessons

### The decision-first sequence (forming questions)

Forming the questions is "the most important and the most neglected part." Teams jump to method ("what's the best method? how shiny should the report be?") and skip the questions, so they "can never get a useful answer."

- **No research without a goal.** Hall's repeated failure-diagnosis: teams that "tried research last year" and got nothing had never set goals or priorities in advance — "we just talked to some people." Without a goal you don't know what research you need and can't tell when you've succeeded.
- **Skeptical mindset.** Question the value of any approach: "Is this the right way to answer this question at this time?" Question your own process even after you've started — the sunk-cost / "we can't reopen it" objection is a fear response, and "it's a lot more expensive… to do the wrong thing just because you're afraid of asking the question."
- **The question quality ladder** (verbatim structure): a **good** research question is specific, actionable, practical, within your means (worked example: _"How do recent college graduates decide what to have for dinner?"_ — answerable with confidence in a short time). A **great** question is _"What do we really know, and what are we just assuming?"_ ("people won't like what you find out"). The **best** question is _"the thing you don't know that carries the most risk."_
- **Big risks to scan for** (where to start research): customers don't value what you're making; your best/highest-revenue customers actually need something else; the business model doesn't support it; you can't build it; someone else is doing it better ("better is in the eyes of your customer"); succeeding would cause harm.

### Bet size → how much research (primary confirmation)

> "The amount of research depends on how large the decision is, how much is already known, and how uncertain the team is."

This is stated as a direct rule, not an inference. The corollary maxim from the _Wall Street Journal_ that Hall calls her "very very favorite quote": **"All business is about making bets on human behavior."** The framing deliberately "takes certainty off the table — we just want to place an informed bet." Decision rule she gives for a stakeholder: _"Do you want to set fire to a big pile of money today, or… think about it for 10 minutes and then place a more informed bet?"_ Bigger / harder-to-reverse bets justify more research because the cost of being wrong is higher.

### What-you-need-to-know vs. what-you-ask (interview discipline)

- The research question and the interview prompt are **not the same**. "That might be your research question, but then what you say to the human you're interviewing is **walk me through your last vacation**" — the spending behavior emerges instead of being lied about.
- **Worst market-research question:** _"How likely are you to adopt my product / do this in the next six months?"_ — "Nobody can predict their own behavior." Replace with "tell me what you use" / past behavior.
- **You predict the future by knowing what people do now, what they've done, and why.** "You can't necessarily ask people why," but you need a strong sense of what they do and why — and that only comes qualitatively.
- **The unique value of qualitative:** "The thing you get with qualitative that you cannot get with a survey and you cannot get with analytics is **what you didn't think to ask**." For attitudinal/churn questions ("why are customers dropping off?"), set up ~a dozen conversations with screened representative customers, then "talk to them about their lives" — don't ask the why directly.

### Surveys (the new-edition discipline)

The second edition added surveys specifically because teams are "inundated with the most idiotic surveys." Survey rules:

- **People cannot rank features.** "Surveys seem more objective because you can count… the lies." Prioritizing features / designing a product is the designer's paid job — a survey lets people "off the hook" and produces wasted money.
- **A valid survey question is one the respondent can answer (a) truthfully and (b) willingly.** Good example: _"How many kids under 18 live in your home with you?"_ (factual, knowable). Sensitive questions (income) fail the willingness test.
- **Do not turn feelings into math.** Hall's running villain is the ForeSee-style satisfaction survey: _"rate the number of clicks,"_ "rate how well the site's organized," 1–10 scales — "no human has feelings in 10 degrees of granularity." The product these vendors actually sell is **"the comfort of a right answer… quantitative certitude"** to managers terrified of talking to people. (The worked anti-example — _"rate the number of clicks… the finest number of clicks I've ever seen"_ — is the most quotable line for skewering attitudinal scales.)
- **Large N ≠ confidence.** "Just because you surveyed a thousand people doesn't mean you have statistical confidence… anecdotes you can count is not the same as quantitative data." Real statistical sampling means population → sampling frame → representative sample, plus non-response bias, self-selection of strong opinions, and unrepresented groups. "It doesn't mean you can draw conclusions that are more valid than you could from talking to 12 people."

### Two types of data → method selection

There are **only two** kinds of data, and the data type dictates the method:

- **Quantitative question** (how many / how much) → quantitative method.
- **Qualitative question** (why / thick data / what you didn't think to ask) → qualitative method.
- You often need **both**: "you need to know what's happening and how much." "If this is definitely happening but only three people do it, then it's irrelevant to your business." But **"you cannot math your way to why."**

### Validation is a forbidden word

> "Burn that word. Make that a forbidden word in your organization, because all validation means is **prove me right because we're launching next week.**"

Validation = testing after the decision is already made, structured to confirm. The healthy alternative is being willing to find out you're wrong: "I didn't raise my hand on that one" — i.e., don't go in wanting to be proven right.

### Why bad design ships (the failure model)

Bad design exists "not because we don't know how to do good design but because people want to be right." A good idea "gets processed through everybody's insecurities and fears and constraints" and comes out a Juicero, a video bill, or 3D TVs. Specific failure mechanisms:

- **3D TVs:** launched off Avatar's box office with **no in-home testing** — people will go to a 3D movie but won't sit in their living room wearing glasses. "They didn't do any research until after they'd launched."
- **Groupthink:** "happens when a team optimizes for the appearance of agreement to avoid… a shared fear of confrontation." Conflict is part of good collaboration (engineer argues performance, designer elegance, researcher the user, business profitability — all toward a shared goal).
- **Research in a separate building:** hiring great PhD researchers and isolating them produces "a report for the designers to ignore." Without an established collaborative culture, findings feel like a "cage match" no matter how good they are.

### Collaboration is a prerequisite, not a nicety

- "Data alone doesn't change minds." The canonical worked example: a researcher spent a whole weekend building a presentation proving a product-roadmap assumption wrong; the VP **canceled the Monday meeting** because he didn't want to be told he was wrong. Good data without an existing relationship changes nothing.
- Start from agreement: "The best point of agreement I found is that everybody wants to design better products and services faster. Start there." Don't argue people into research.
- Solving design problems "moves at the speed of decision making." Arguing personal opinions without evidence is "the slowest way of working." Research "lowers risk, saves money, increases value."
- Research scope is three lenses, not just the user: **people in the world** (users/customers), **your own organization** (goals, assumptions, resources), and **the world you don't control** (competition, economy). Ignoring any one creates risk.

### Making research stick (the output rule)

- **"Do not define the output of research as a document."** Plan backward from "what decisions do we want this research to influence" — that's the success criterion.
- Don't put research in a separate channel; use "what's already successful for the retention and transmission of information in your organization."
- New information must **hook into existing beliefs** to be retained — which is why stakeholder/internal research comes first (learn what the decision-maker cares about, then connect findings to it).
- Tell a story; boring reports don't make research more valid. Sparing, deliberate use of memorable/traumatic framing ("dead face on skull" mnemonic for skipping internal-workflow redesign) aids recall, but don't overload it.

## Failure Modes

- **Method-first research:** picking the method / report format before the decision, goals, and unknowns — "you can never get a useful answer."
- **No-goal research:** "we just talked to some people" — produces nothing and discredits research for the org.
- **Validation theater:** running a study to prove a decision already made; structured to confirm.
- **Feature-ranking surveys / dot-voting:** outsourcing the design decision to respondents who can't and shouldn't make it (the plumber dot-voting example; LinkedIn 1–10 scale surveys).
- **Counting anecdotes as quant:** large N mistaken for statistical confidence; ignoring non-response and self-selection bias.
- **Asking intention instead of observing behavior:** "How likely are you to…?" / "Which features do you want?" — unanswerable; replace with "walk me through your last…".
- **Data without relationship:** bringing strong findings to a decision-maker with no collaborative relationship (the canceled-Monday-meeting example).
- **Research in a silo:** isolated researchers generating reports nobody acts on.
- **Document-as-output:** findings that "go and die in Google Drive" because success was defined as a report, not a decision.
- **Paradigm-product hubris:** assuming a "blue sky" innovation escapes the fit-into-the-world test (Segway, autonomous taxis, 3D TVs).
