## Core Thesis

Virality on Shorts is a controllable, measurable craft, not luck. Jenny Hoyos's claim — _"I don't ask if it'll go viral, I can figure out how to make it viral"_ — rests on a reusable structure (hook → foreshadow → pacing-break transition → but/therefore story → abrupt high-payoff close) plus a small set of analytics thresholds she reverse-engineered by scraping and labeling thousands of Shorts (including all of Mr. Beast's and Ryan Trahan's). The governing metric is not raw retention but **retention-driven looping** until the watch-time exceeds 100% — the video gets rewatched because of twists, mechanisms, and Easter eggs. Channel-specific calibration is mandatory: every threshold below is _her_ channel's number and must be re-derived per channel/audience.

## Operating Lessons

### The "make anything viral" claim

> "I don't ask if it'll go viral. I can figure out how to make it viral."

The mechanism behind the claim: _"It's just adding story and a twist."_ Because no click is required on shorts (it's served, not chosen), any topic — "paint drying" — can be made entertaining if there's a story and the viewer is invested. Investment comes from a **personal why / goal**, ideally with **irony**. Worked example: "I cooked for strangers to make money" — baseline has no stakes; the why is "my kitchen is broken, so to fix it I have to cook to raise money." The irony (broken kitchen → must cook) is what makes people invested. _"Jenny's the hero of the story."_

### What makes a "good short" (her definition)

Three load-bearing properties:

1. **Strong hook** — passes the long-form title/thumbnail test: would it still get clicks as a long-form title+thumbnail? The hook must be **visual** (understandable with sound off) and **simple enough to explain to a 5-year-old**.
2. **A story that pushes you through** to the end.
3. **Rewatchability** — you watch _and_ rewatch.

### Retention & rewatchability (the core metric layer)

- **Scroll-through rate** (her term for view-vs-swipe-away %): average ≈ 70%, hers ≈ 85%. Push as high as possible.
- **Retention** ≈ 95% average for her — but that average exceeds 100% mathematically _only because of rewatching_, since only ~85% watch at all. The gap between scroll-through and retention is the looping signal.
- **90% retention = the bare minimum** for blow-up reach, calibrated to her ~34s length and young audience.
- Decision rule for sub-30s: _"If a short is less than 30 seconds, it has to have over 100% retention or else it's not going to take off."_ This is why she runs slightly longer (34s) rather than ultra-short.
- Length↔retention bar is coupled (she credits Paddy Galloway / "Pat Galler's accelerator" research): the shorter the video, the higher the retention bar to hit the same virality.

**Worked example — the 1-second trim:** A short got ~50k views in 5 days (vs. her usual ~1M). The retention graph showed an 83% retention with a final-second cliff: 70% → 45% in one second (a 25-point drop on the last second). She trimmed that one trailing second in YouTube Studio; retention rose **83% → 88%** and **"the video went flying."** Takeaway: the last second is disproportionately load-bearing; never end on a drop. (Note: she says YouTube has since removed the in-Studio trim feature.)

### First-frame hook (visual-first)

- The hook is conceived **visual-first**: she sketches on an iPad "almost as if it's a long-form thumbnail" until something clicks, then writes the VO to accompany the visual.
- **Fast-food series framing (worked example):** show the **location/storefront, not her face** (more people recognize the chain than recognize her); logo centered, food item beside it, text dead-center ("$1 burrito / $1 sandwich / $1 burger"), perfectly aligned. Because it's a consistent, recognizable playlist frame, viewers binge down it and recognize "it's me" each time — aiding rewatch/return viewership.
- Script then goes through a **readability checker** (readabilityformulas.com), targeting **5th grade or under**. Discovery method: she scraped scripts of thousands of top shorts; the best (esp. Mr. Beast) land around **1st grade**; ~5th-and-under is the safe range. Single jargon words ("business," "finance," "profit") can spike readability — she avoids them or replaces the word with its plain-language definition.

### Idea generation & the funnel

- **Volume:** ~1,000 ideas live in a Google doc; she makes ~10.
- **Sources:** watching YouTube and asking "what do I want to recreate/twist?"; AI ("AI has some pretty good ideas"); but the **best ideas come from lived moments** — e.g., eating Ratatouille (favorite food, ~$20 to cook) → "can't I just make a garden for infinite Ratatouille for ~$5?" → the garden video (~30M views, vs. ~20k for typical gardening videos). Or: grandma got free $30 laundry detergent by complaining to the company → "that's a video."
- **Narrowing:** 100 → 25 by asking, in order: (1) **Do I actually want to make it?** ("I don't ask if it'll go viral — I can figure that out") (2) Is it logistically possible? (3) Is the hook good, is the **mechanism** good, will people **rewatch**? Then the editor/strategist cuts 25 → 10, judging if it's _"simple yet complex"_ and shareable.

### The repeatable Shorts structure

Every video, in order:

1. **Hook** (very short).
2. **Foreshadow** — explicitly tell the viewer what payoff is coming at the end ("by the end I'll surprise her with a gift"). Always **spoken**, not only visual. Hook + foreshadow = **two lines, ~3 seconds or less**.
3. **Smooth pacing-break transition** — "a pacing break without the pacing actually breaking." She avoids "let's get started" (kills pace). Worked example: "McDonald's banned this item, so I'm gonna make it at home then convince them to put it back on the menu" (hook+foreshadow) → instead of "let's get started," she says **"so I cooked illegally"** — flows, doesn't break momentum. Rationale: primacy/recency — after two crucial details, more detail makes viewers forget, so you need a breather that still moves.
4. **Story via but/therefore** (or problem/solution).
5. **Abrupt close on the payoff** (see below).

**Writing order:** ideas → pick one → write the **hook** → write the **last line** first (so she knows where she's filming toward) → write foreshadow → fill the middle as a rough script or bullet points → film → revise/finalize script → edit. The last line is left partly blank for the live reaction: "then I surprised my mom and ___."

### Mechanism (end-expectation device)

A mechanism makes the viewer feel they're progressing toward a known end. Mr. Beast's closing circle ("last to leave the circle wins $500k") is the canonical example — without the circle visibly closing, viewers skip to the end. Her simplest reusable mechanism: **"there are 3 steps / 3 things we need to do"** — a visible list gives viewers a good expectation of what's coming, which she names as possibly the single biggest reason her videos go viral: _"people have a good expectation of what they're going to watch and what's actually going to be at the end."_

### But/therefore storytelling

Stories require change. "I went on a walk, then it rained, then I went home" is boring (and-then-and-then). Rewrite with **but / therefore**: "I went on a walk **but** it started raining **therefore** I started running back home... it was raining all over me **but** good thing I had an umbrella **therefore** I got home." Same story, far more intriguing. (She credits the South Park "but/therefore" rule.)

### Expectation, follow-through, and the twist

Follow the expectation you set, then twist at the end. Worked example — **$5 Mother's Day gift:** hook "My mom's never had a Mother's Day gift, so I'm going to change that and buy her the best present with $5" (the **why** is baked into the hook). Cold-open shows her giving the gift, then cut back; expectation = "by the end she'll surprise mom." She follows through (makes/gives the gift) → **twist:** mom drops it, it breaks, mom says "you're the best daughter," she replies "I'm your only one," video ends. Loop closed + twist = rewatch fuel.

### The abrupt close + peak-end

Endings are deliberately **abrupt but satisfying** — "pleasing but abrupt," not confusing. She keeps the payoff to literally the last second(s). Twists are preferred closes because they drive rewatchability. (The peak-end logic is stated more explicitly in the MFM interview; here it shows up as "end right after the payoff.")

### Platform differences (do not cross-post identically)

Same video, wildly different results across platforms — she once got ~1M on TikTok and ~1k on YouTube for the _same_ video; after refocusing on YouTube it inverted (~1M YouTube, ~1k TikTok).

- **YouTube:** slower, more mature audience; wants slower pace and story; her ~34s shorts.
- **TikTok:** dislikes >30s; wants **10–20s**, dense with information, fewer jokes, maximally scrollable.
- **Instagram Reels:** slightly more mature; muted-by-default, so **very visual with subtitles every second**; high sharability (easy to share on IG).

### Audience avatar

"Avatar is everything." She targets her **younger self** and her **7- and 10-year-old nieces who barely speak English** — if she can make non-English-speaking kids understand and care, it's probably very good. It's less about their desires than about _how simply you can speak to them_. Guiding principle: make content intriguing for kids (largest YouTube audience) but mature enough for adults — the Pixar dual-audience target.

### Long-form transition (reality check)

She averages ~10M on shorts vs. ~50k on long-form and is deliberately studying long-form for a year before executing (same method she used for shorts). She calls out creators with ~1M subs who get <1k on long-form and blame YouTube — she argues YouTube is "doing a pretty good job at transitioning them; not everyone's audience will transition." She believes short↔long audiences are genuinely **different audiences** (audience-preference, not platform fault).

## Failure Modes

- **Ending on a retention cliff:** the final second is disproportionate; a 70%→45% drop on the last second tanked an otherwise-fine 83% short until trimmed.
- **Going too short:** sub-30s shorts need >100% retention (i.e., heavy looping) or they die; she runs 34s to relieve that bar.
- **Jargon in the script:** one word ("business/finance/profit") spikes readability past the 5th-grade target.
- **"Let's get started" transitions:** explicit gear-shifts break pace; use an in-narrative line instead.
- **Cross-posting identical cuts:** same video can do 1,000x better on one platform; length and density must be retuned per platform.
- **Blaming the algorithm for failed long-form:** usually a content/skill gap, not platform suppression.
- **Over-stuffing detail before the breather:** primacy/recency — too much info after the hook+foreshadow gets forgotten.

## Contradiction / Caveat to flag for synthesis

Her closing **hot take directly tensions the rest of the interview**: she says _"retention doesn't matter as much as people think it does"_ and that it ultimately reduces to **viewer satisfaction**, citing a short that got ~10M views on only ~70% first-100k retention because it was all returning viewers, while friends' 100%+-retention shorts flopped. Treat the retention thresholds above as strong heuristics she optimizes against, not as a sufficient condition for virality. The deeper variable she names is satisfaction + returning/rewatching viewers.

## BuildOS Application

1. **Hook/foreshadow structure maps to BuildOS short-form scripts:** the hook → foreshadow (≤3s, 2 lines) → pacing-break → but/therefore → abrupt payoff skeleton is directly reusable for the anti-feed TikTok scripts and demo loops; the "write the last line first" rule is a clean scripting constraint.
- 2. **Readability discipline (5th grade or under)** aligns with BuildOS's "lead with relief, not jargon" stance — a concrete, testable bar (readabilityformulas.com) for marketing copy, not just shorts.
3. **Mechanism = visible progress device:** the "there are 3 steps" mechanic is the content analog of a product progress indicator; useful for both scripts and onboarding UX framing.
4. **Channel-specific calibration:** her insistence that 34s/85%/90% are _her_ numbers is the right epistemics for any BuildOS short-form metric target — derive, don't borrow.
