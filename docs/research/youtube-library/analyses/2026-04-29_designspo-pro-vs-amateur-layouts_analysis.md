## Core Thesis

The gap between an amateur marketing site and a pro one is rarely about taste or skill — it is about **layout decisions**: how space is used, how the grid is broken, how proof is composed, and how each section's job is honored visually. Pros design every section as if it were the most important one, use real assets in real layouts (not stock images on rigid grids), and treat composition as the carrier of the message. Amateurs default to safe templates and uniform grids that mute the product's individuality.

> "The difference usually isn't massive. In fact, I'm betting that you have the skills right now to take your designs to the next level with a few simple changes."

## Per-Section Operating Lessons

### 1. Hero Section

**Amateur tells**

- Hero divided into 3-4 stacked cells trying to say everything at once.
- Awkward spacing between cells; nothing dominates.
- Stock photography or generic logos used in place of the actual product/work.
- Basic single-color background that does not match the company's vibe.

**Pro tactics**

- **Two-zone split** — text on one side, hero visual on the other. Clear "what" + "proof".
- Use **real visuals of real work** (events, customers, output), not stock or generic mockups.
- Layout energy should match brand energy — fast-paced businesses get motion and density; calm products get whitespace.
- One message above the fold, not four.

**Decision rule:** Cap the hero at two compositional zones, and fill the visual zone with real work, not stock imagery.

> "The hero is split into four different sections, which is really just too busy to get one message across."

### 2. Portfolio Section

**Amateur tells**

- A rigid 3x3 (or 2x3) grid with identical card sizes.
- Same mockup template re-used for every project.
- Same descriptive copy structure under every project ("Web design for X").
- The grid screams "template," and the work feels interchangeable.

**Pro tactics**

- Arrange projects in **floating space that only loosely conforms to a grid at the edges**.
- Vary mockup style, crop, and color treatment per project to honor each client's individuality.
- Let scale and asymmetry do the work — tall vs. wide, dense vs. airy.
- Treat presentation of the work as part of the work itself.

**Decision rule:** Break grid uniformity for portfolio sections — uniformity reads as templated; intentional asymmetry reads as senior.

> "First impressions matter — they're how you get second impressions."

### 3. Case Study Section

**Amateur tells**

- Hero visual is small and pushed off to one side.
- Long blocks of generic copy about the company/industry but not the project.
- Auto-generated keyword/tag pills at the bottom (a tell for "this is a CMS template").
- Visuals must be clicked to enlarge — pro layouts never hide their best assets behind clicks.

**Pro tactics**

- **Full-bleed, bold hero visuals** at the top of the case study.
- Long-form supporting layout: process explanations, decision rationale, before/after, impact metrics — each gets its own composition.
- Custom-designed for the specific case study, not a reusable template.
- Presentation effort visibly matches the work effort.

**Decision rule:** Each case study deserves a custom layout. If your case studies share one template, they read as low-effort regardless of the work quality.

> "Treat the presentation of your work as if it's as important as the work itself — because for you and for your career, it is."

### 4. Benefit Section

(60-70% of a software/e-commerce homepage — most important section to nail.)

**Amateur tells**

- Sparse vertical stack of feature/visual rows.
- Static thumbnail images that don't clearly correspond to the feature being described.
- Subtitle text repeats the feature name as definition (a copywriting tell that bleeds into layout).
- Same image size and alignment for every benefit — no visual rhythm.

**Pro tactics**

- **Animated or interactive visuals** that show the product in use.
- Visuals carry the explanation when copy is weak; "the visuals are so good I get the benefit even though the copy is feature-heavy."
- **In-line "highlighter" pattern**: buttons embedded inside a paragraph that swap the right-side visual when clicked.
- Vary composition between benefit rows — alternate image sides, vary aspect ratios, mix interactive and static.

**Decision rule:** Benefit sections must visually demonstrate the product. Static screenshots are insufficient when competitors use motion and interaction.

> "You have to be able to stand out and explain your solution from a 1000-foot view."

### 5. FAQ Section

**Amateur tells**

- Bland single-column accordion of plain text — looks like a Wikipedia page.
- Visual style breaks from the rest of the site ("backstage at Disney World").
- Treated as an afterthought, slapped together last.

**Pro tactics**

- **Carry the brand's visual language all the way through FAQ** — illustrations, color blocks, playful interactions.
- Use FAQ to deflect support volume, not to sell. (If you're selling here, your hero copy failed.)
- Layout choices that match the rest of the site preserve the magic.

**Decision rule:** No section of the site is allowed to "look unfinished." Every section gets the same care; FAQ is a famous tell for this rule being broken.

> "If you have an otherwise amazing design and any part of it looks like this, it's kind of like seeing backstage at Disney World — it would just ruin the magic."

### 6. Features Section

(Distinct from benefits — usually its own page or a use-case page; often under-designed because it's not on the homepage.)

**Amateur tells**

- Gray corporate background.
- A small embedded video that's years old.
- Generic icon boxes with no visual weight.
- Plain HTML-style unordered lists for "what we do."
- Page is too short — visitor leaves still confused.

**Pro tactics**

- **Bento-box grid** — varied tile sizes that visually demonstrate the product's range.
- **Double-layered visuals with consistent drop-shadow effect** running through the page.
- A custom cursor or in-page interaction reinforces brand modernity.
- More information, not less. Features pages should be long and dense — sprinkle benefits at the end and close with a clear CTA.

**Decision rule:** A features page is the place to over-explain. Bento boxes are the canonical pro layout for feature density.

> "You can't just assume your visitors know how your product works. Take the time to hold their hands and walk them through it."

### 7. Demo Section

**Amateur tells**

- A static product screenshot labeled "Demo".
- An autoplay or click-to-play video, often with AI/synthetic narration.
- The visitor watches passively; no hands-on interaction with the product.

**Pro tactics**

- **Embed an actual functional slice of the product in the layout.** User can click, type, trigger features.
- Layout supports the demo — clean spacing, illustrations to anchor it, clear visual hierarchy so the demo dominates.
- The signup/conversion ask happens **after** the user has experienced the product (e.g., "publish" button → signup wall).

**Decision rule:** If the product can be demoed inline, embed it. Static demos are strictly inferior on conversion.

> "If you can nail the demo section, you're like 80% of the way to the sale."

### 8. Product Section (E-commerce)

**Amateur tells**

- Stock-photo lifestyle image at the top instead of real product photography.
- Grainy or off-brand product image that doesn't show the brand mark.
- Single short copy paragraph; no specs, sourcing, dosage, ingredients.
- No reviews/testimonials inline.
- Generic two-column layout that gives products no rest space.

**Pro tactics**

- **Full-width real product photography** that looks editorial.
- Two-side layout with generous whitespace — eyes have room to rest.
- Layered scroll: hero → benefit section → magazine-style sub-hero → broken-grid feature section → testimonials.
- Long, transparent, benefit-led page. Show what's inside, what arrives, what changes.

**Decision rule:** Product pages for cold traffic must layer multiple compositions (full-width hero, broken grid, sub-hero, testimonials). One screen is never enough.

> "Products don't sell themselves. As a designer you're really the one to bridge that knowledge gap."

### 9. Comparison Section

**Amateur tells**

- Page reads like an SEO blog post — optimized for crawlers, not humans.
- Generic comparison table with low contrast between rows; ambiguous yes/no colors.
- Competitor's column visually equal to yours — implies they're equally good.
- Wishy-washy copy that fence-sits.

**Pro tactics**

- **Bold problem-led headline** ("Solves 10 big problems with X").
- Each problem gets its own **visual representation** + your fix below it.
- Your column gets distinct color, larger fonts, and a stronger background block.
- Decisive language and a clear CTA at the end — no hedging.

**Decision rule:** On a comparison page, your product gets visual dominance. If the layout is symmetric, you've already lost the comparison.

> "Now is not the time to mince words. You're trying to sell something, so sell it."

### 10. Testimonials Section

**Amateur tells**

- Mixed media (tweets, handwritten notes, videos, plain text) crammed into a small section to one side.
- Side-by-side narrow column with another unrelated section, making everything cramped.
- No visible link/source on testimonials → looks fabricated.
- No structure or segmentation — just a pile.

**Pro tactics**

- **Full-width grid** of clean uniform testimonial cards with quote, attribution, and a clickable link to the original.
- **Segment by customer persona** ("People like you said this") — increases relatability.
- Whitespace and consistent card sizing make the section feel curated.
- The width signals importance: full-bleed = "this matters."

**Decision rule:** Testimonials get full-width and persona segmentation. Cramming social proof into a sidebar is one of the cheapest credibility losses on a marketing site.

> "It's like not only do people like us — but people like you like us."

### 11. Contact Section

**Amateur tells**

- Generic form with double labels (above and inside the field).
- Form design breaks from the rest of the site's visual language.
- One small CTA at the bottom — clearly the lowest-effort section.

**Pro tactics**

- **Conversational sentence form** — labels become part of a sentence ("Hi, my name is **, I work at **, and I'd like help with \_\_").
- Layout matches the brand language used everywhere else on the site.
- Pre-qualify leads with strategic field choices (work email, company name, budget range).

**Decision rule:** The contact section is your final yard. If its layout breaks from the rest of the site, you signal "this is a template." Don't slow down at the finish line.

> "Cutting corners here is a little bit like slowing down right before you reach the finish line."

## Cross-Cutting Layout Principles

These show up across multiple sections:

1. **Break the grid intentionally.** Amateurs use perfectly uniform grids (portfolio, product, testimonials). Pros loosen the grid at the edges, vary card scale, and use bento layouts to convey range.
2. **Full-width signals importance.** Whenever something matters (testimonials, product photography, sub-hero in a product page, comparison), pros take it full-bleed. Sidebars and narrow columns deprioritize content.
3. **Real assets, real composition.** Stock photos and generic mockup templates undermine every section. Pros use real photography of real work/customers/product.
4. **Two-zone splits dominate above the fold.** Hero, demo, contact, and benefit rows all favor a clean text-vs-visual or interactive-vs-explanation split. Three or more zones = busyness.
5. **Layout effort must be uniform across the site.** The fastest pro tell is that _every_ section — including FAQ and contact — gets the same care as the hero. Amateurs invest in the hero and abandon the rest.
6. **Whitespace is not waste.** Pro layouts give the eye places to rest. Amateurs cram, layer, and crowd.
7. **Composition explains the product when copy can't.** When copy is feature-heavy or weak, strong visual layout (animations, in-line highlighter buttons, bento boxes) carries the meaning.
8. **Visual hierarchy is decisive — your column is bigger.** Comparison, testimonial segmentation, and CTA placement all demonstrate that pro layouts pick a winner inside the composition.

## Failure Modes (Never Do These)

- Hero with 3+ cells trying to say everything at once.
- Same mockup template repeated across every portfolio item.
- Auto-generated keyword tags or pills at the bottom of a case study.
- Static thumbnails next to benefit copy when competitors are animating.
- FAQ rendered as a default accordion in a brand-less single column.
- Features page using gray corporate backgrounds and HTML unordered lists.
- A demo that is a screenshot, when an interactive demo is feasible.
- Stock product photography on a product page.
- A comparison table where your column is visually identical to competitors'.
- Testimonials crammed into a half-width sidebar with mixed-media chaos.
- A contact form whose visual style doesn't match the rest of the site.

## BuildOS Application

- **Homepage benefit section is the highest-leverage layout.** It is 60-70% of the page real estate. BuildOS should use animated or in-line interactive visuals (the "highlighter button swaps the right-side visual" pattern is a strong fit for showing brain dump → structured projects).
- **Use a bento-box features page for "everything BuildOS does."** Brain dumps, daily briefs, calendar sync, agentic chat, ontology — each tile a different size in a single bento grid is a more truthful layout for a multi-surface tool than a long vertical stack.
- **Embed an interactive demo, not a video.** Let visitors paste a brain-dump and see a project structure render inline. Gate signup at the "save / publish" step, not before.
- **Testimonials should be full-width and persona-segmented.** "Builders said this / Writers said this / Researchers said this" — matches BuildOS's "thinking environment for people making complex things" positioning and segments by creator persona.
- **Comparison page must visually dominate.** When BuildOS publishes "vs Notion / vs ChatGPT / vs Obsidian" pages, the BuildOS column gets the larger card, distinct background, and the headline must be problem-led ("Notion doesn't compound your context — here's what does").
- **No section gets less love than the hero.** FAQ and contact must carry the Inkprint design language end-to-end. Default accordions and stock contact forms break the magic.

## Skill Draft Inputs

### Heuristics for `marketing-site-design-review`

These are layout-specific scoring criteria that complement the design heuristics from the sibling Pro-vs-Amateur DESIGN video:

- **Hero zone count.** Penalize 3+. Reward two-zone splits with real photography.
- **Grid uniformity vs. intentional break.** Portfolio, features, product = penalize rigid grids; reward floating compositions and bento layouts.
- **Asset realism.** Score the ratio of real (product, customer, work) imagery to stock/template imagery.
- **Section width consistency.** Testimonials, sub-heros, and primary product visuals should use full width.
- **Effort uniformity.** Sample FAQ and contact — if visual quality drops, score the whole site down.
- **Demo interactivity.** Static screenshot < video < embedded interactive product. Score accordingly.
- **Comparison dominance.** On comparison pages, check that "your" column is visually heavier.

### Heuristics for `landing-page-scorecard-funnel`

Per-section layout signals to score (binary unless noted):

- Hero: two-zone split, real visual asset, single message.
- Benefit: animated or interactive visual present; visuals match copy 1:1.
- Features: bento or varied-tile layout; long enough; CTA at end.
- Demo: interactive embed (vs. static or video).
- Product (e-com): full-width hero, multi-composition scroll, inline reviews.
- Comparison: visual dominance for owner column; problem-led headline.
- Testimonials: full-width, persona-segmented, sourced links.
- FAQ: brand-consistent, not default accordion.
- Contact: layout consistent with rest of site; ideally conversational form.
