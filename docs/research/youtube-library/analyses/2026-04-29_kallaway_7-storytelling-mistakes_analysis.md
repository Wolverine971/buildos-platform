## Core Thesis

Time compression has rewired audience attention. The traditional bell-curve story arc — slow setup, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution — was built for captive audiences (movies, books, the family fire). On the internet, viewers are not captive. They re-decide whether to keep watching every few seconds. Eighty percent of viewers will bounce before the rising action gets interesting if you use the old structure.

Modern storytelling is therefore not a single climb but a **rolling sequence of peaks and releases** — start at 70/100 intensity, spike to 90/100 within the first minute or two, release to 30/100, then re-peak every 2-5 minutes. The seven mistakes Kane catalogs are the seven ways small creators still tell stories as if their viewer were trapped in a movie theater.

For BuildOS, the translation is: a story-craft agent should not just suggest "add a hook." It should detect each of these seven specific failure modes in a draft script and surface concrete fixes — because knowing the rules is easy and applying them consistently is what separates 10x creators from everyone else.

## Operating Lessons

### 1. The Traditional Story Arc Is A Lie

**The mistake:** Building stories on the classic Freytag bell curve — background, initial conflict, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution. Starting at zero intensity and slowly building.

**Why it fails:** Time compression has sped up baseline processing. The traditional arc was built when audiences were captive (2-hour movies, 300-page books, drunk uncle around the fire). On the internet, viewers have infinite alternatives and re-decide every few seconds. Eighty percent bounce before the rising action gets interesting. None of the work after the first 5% is seen if they leave at 5%.

**The fix:** Use the "Kallaway story arc" — start the intro at ~70/100 intensity (not zero), spike to ~90/100 with a conflict or contrast moment in the first minute or two, release down to ~30/100, then re-peak around 75/100 on a 2-5 minute cadence. The biggest peak can be first (with subsequent peaks ramping down) or last (peaks building toward it). What changes vs. tradition: intros above zero, first conflict much earlier, many more peaks throughout.

**Agent behavior:**

- Score the opening 15 seconds for intensity. Flag if it reads as "scene-setting" rather than "above zero."
- Map a draft script onto an intensity curve. Reject any segment longer than 2-5 minutes without a peak.
- Detect "background-first" openers ("In 2019, I was working at...") and rewrite them to lead with stakes or contrast.
- Warn when a draft has only one climax — that pattern signals the old arc.

### 2. Jumbling The W's

**The mistake:** Building intros in the wrong order — leading with where/when, then who/what, then how/why. Setting the scene before establishing the stakes.

**Why it fails:** Leading with facts that don't matter to the viewer. "It was a long cold windy night in December, Billy was walking down the street, he fell because it was slippery" or "Last month I was at a conference in Miami, I saw the CEO of Disney speak, he is worried about the future of entertainment" both sound decent but waste the first sentences on details that don't tell the viewer why they should care. You will lose 50% of viewers in the first 30 seconds — every sentence in that window must do work. The viewer has an invisible internal timer; if it strikes zero before they understand what's in it for them, they bounce.

**The fix:** Restack the W's as **what + why → who + how → where + when**. Lead with what is happening and why it matters: "The future of entertainment is going to look super different." Then add credibility: "The CEO of Disney is really worried about it." Then add color: "He was talking about it at a conference in Miami." Where and when are the icing, not the cake.

**Agent behavior:**

- Parse the first 3 sentences of a draft. Tag each as W-type (who/what/where/when/why/how).
- Flag intros that lead with where/when as "jumbled."
- Auto-suggest a reordered opening: lead with what + why; demote scene-setting to position 3 or later.
- Score every sentence in the first 30 seconds for "does this answer 'what's in it for me?'" — if not, mark for cut or move.

### 3. Rebuys & Rehooking

**The mistake:** Getting the viewer to buy in with the initial hook, then never re-earning their attention. One peak, then coast.

**Why it fails:** The viewer is making a fresh decision to keep watching every few minutes. Once you resolve the initial conflict and release the tension, you have given them a natural exit. If you don't re-tee the stakes, they leave. Social algorithms push content largely based on **average view duration** — without re-hooks, AVD craters and distribution dies with it.

**The fix:** Treat attention like a poker rebuy (Casino Royale: Bond loses the 10M, rebuys, wins). After each peak-and-release, introduce a new pain point or stakes moment. The pattern: solve the first problem → "but the truth is, even if you solve that, X will undo it unless you also fix Y." Close existing loops while opening new ones. After 3-4 closed loops, the viewer feels like you solved 4 of their problems — but you really solved one and created three new ones to solve. They stay the whole time.

**Agent behavior:**

- Detect every resolution moment in a draft. For each one, check whether a new loop opens within 30 seconds.
- Flag scripts with only one loop as "single-rebuy risk."
- Suggest rehook bridge phrases: "but the truth is...", "the catch is...", "even with that solved, you still face..."
- Track loop-open vs. loop-closed count across the script. Warn if more than 2 loops are open simultaneously without resolution.

### 4. Heroes vs Villains (Missing Villain)

**The mistake:** Building a hero with no villain. A protagonist alone with no antagonist or contrast.

**Why it fails:** Stories live on contrast. The distance between hero and villain is bigger than the distance between hero and nothing. Without a villain, there is no tension to resolve and no reason for the audience to feel the stakes.

**The fix:** Pick the thing, person, group, or brand that is the antagonist of your hero and frame them as the villain. The villain doesn't have to be a character in the human sense — it can be Corporate America (for startup-founder content), the other career path (for career content), a competing methodology, a status quo, an industry incumbent. Use frames like "they do this, but we do that" to make the contrast explicit.

**Agent behavior:**

- Scan a draft for explicit antagonist framing. If no "they/them/the old way" appears, flag as "missing villain."
- Suggest 3 candidate villains based on the topic (e.g., for a productivity video: "the productivity-app graveyard," "context-switching," "task-list theater").
- Audit hero introductions — if the hero is described in absolute terms rather than against a contrast, rewrite as comparative.

### 5. Nobody To Root For

**The mistake:** No likable hero, no relatable situation, nothing for the viewer to invest in. The story has stakes for the creator but not for the audience.

**Why it fails:** Stories are great when the audience has someone to root for. Without that, the viewer is a spectator without a team. The more likable the hero or relatable the situation, the more invested the viewer is. Optimism — built from rooting — always wins.

**The fix:** Make the viewer see themselves as the protagonist (a character or a movement). Build common ground tactically: contextualize _why this story matters for the target viewer_. Kane's example: "I make these videos because I genuinely want entrepreneurs to make more money from content" → entrepreneurs hear that and root for him as the creator and for the frameworks he shares.

**Agent behavior:**

- Detect explicit audience-mapping statements in a draft ("for entrepreneurs," "if you're a solo founder," "for people who...").
- Flag drafts where the hero is the creator only and the audience has no on-ramp to identify.
- Suggest "common ground" insertions early in the script: name the viewer's situation, name why their stakes are also the hero's stakes.

### 6. Lacking Atomic Shareability

**The mistake:** Stories that cannot be compressed into a single passable unit. Ideas that require the original video to be understood.

**Why it fails:** Virality requires retellability. Kane's framing: the **Paul Revere effect** — "the British are coming, the British are coming" is a full story in four words; the whole town moves on it instantly. If your story can't be condensed to that atomic unit, it can't spread by word of mouth.

**The fix:** Cut all unnecessary fluff. Simple words, simple ideas, punchy sentences. Tell stories in a form others can retell after a single watch. **Tactic for complex/niche topics:** say each point twice — first explain it like you would to a colleague, then restate it as a metaphor like you would to a child. Double-encoding makes the atomic version stick.

**Agent behavior:**

- Generate a one-sentence atomic version of every script (a "Paul Revere line"). If it requires more than ~10 words, flag the script as too dense.
- Detect "colleague-only" explanations (jargon-heavy, no metaphor) and suggest a child-version restate.
- Score sentences for word count and complexity. Flag sentences over ~15 words for compression.
- Track which lines are most "passable" — quotable, repeatable, shareable — and recommend moving them to peak moments.

### 7. Not Painting The Picture

**The mistake:** Telling a story with words alone, leaving the viewer to construct the visual themselves. No graphics, no broll, no demonstration.

**Why it fails:** The human brain was designed for visual input. Forcing the viewer to imagine causes comprehension loss in most cases. Kane's frame: a picture is worth a thousand words; a video is worth a thousand pictures. Without visuals, the message degrades regardless of how good the writing is.

**The fix:** Pair every point with a visual — graphic, b-roll, demonstration, on-screen text, motion graphic. If you can't get visuals, you have to paint the picture completely with words (much harder, lower hit rate). Kane explicitly credits hiring strong editors (Charlie and Eek) as the single biggest leverage point for his story quality.

**Agent behavior:**

- Annotate each script line with a "needs visual?" tag based on whether the point is concrete or abstract.
- For abstract points without planned visuals, suggest a metaphor or analogy that _can_ be drawn.
- Generate b-roll briefs alongside the script — what should be on screen during each beat.
- Flag scripts that are >60 seconds of talking-head with no visual cues.

## Frameworks Or Templates

**The Kallaway Story Arc (replaces the traditional bell curve):**

- Intro: start at ~70/100 intensity (not zero), deliver context + characters at this level.
- First spike: ~90/100 within first 1-2 minutes — the first major conflict or contrast.
- Release: down to ~30/100 — tension exhales.
- Re-peak: ~75/100 — new conflict or head-fake.
- Cycle: peak/release on a 2-5 minute cadence until the end.
- Biggest peak can be first (descending pattern) or last (ascending pattern) — both work.

**The Modern Hook Stack (W-order):**

1. **What + why** — what is happening and why it matters.
2. **Who + how** — credibility (who is talking, how they know).
3. **Where + when** — color details only.

Old order (broken): where/when → who/what → how/why. New order: what/why → who/how → where/when.

**The Rebuy Loop:**

1. Open a loop (state pain or stakes).
2. Crescendo to the resolution.
3. As you deliver the fix, immediately open the next loop ("but the truth is, that only works if you also...").
4. Repeat 3-4 times across the video.
5. Resolution feels like 4 problems solved, but only one was real — the other 3 you created and then closed.

**The Atomic Shareability Test:**

Can this story be retold in one sentence after a single watch? If not, compress.

**The Double-Encode Tactic:**

For complex points: say it once for the colleague, say it again as a metaphor for the child.

## Anti-Patterns

This entire video is an anti-pattern catalog. Listed in compact form for skill ingestion:

1. **Bell-curve arc** — slow buildup, single climax, late stakes. Loses 80% before rising action.
2. **Scene-setting opener** — leading with where/when ("It was a cold December night..."). Wastes the 30-second window.
3. **Single hook, no rebuy** — initial peak, then coast. Average view duration collapses, algorithm punishes.
4. **No villain** — hero alone with no contrast. No tension, no stakes.
5. **No rooting interest** — viewer has no on-ramp to identify with the hero or the cause.
6. **Inscrutable density** — story can't be passed in a sentence; jargon-only; no child-version.
7. **All words, no visuals** — talking head with no graphic, b-roll, or demonstration. Comprehension loss compounds.

**Meta anti-pattern:** treating these as one-time fixes. Kane explicitly says knowing the frameworks is easy; applying them _consistently_ across every video is what separates winners. This is why he built Sandcastles — to bake the formulas into a tool that enforces them on every draft.

## Open Questions / Gaps

- **Genre transferability:** All seven mistakes are framed for short-form and mid-form content (sub-15 minute videos). How much carries to long-form (45+ minute YouTube), podcasts, or written essays? The dopamine-ladder cadence likely needs recalibration at longer durations — but the W-stack and atomic shareability seem universal.
- **B2B vs. consumer:** The villain framing (Mistake #4) is intuitive for consumer content but may need more nuance for B2B narratives where the villain is often a process, a metric, or a workflow rather than a brand or persona.
- **How to score "rooting interest" objectively:** Mistake #5 is the squishiest. An agent can detect explicit audience-mapping language, but measuring whether a viewer _actually roots_ requires engagement signals (comments, shares) post-publish.
- **Visual generation:** Mistake #7 assumes the creator has editor capacity. For solo creators using AI tooling, what is the minimum viable visual layer that captures the comprehension benefit without requiring a Charlie/Eek-tier editor?
- **Loop-opening fatigue:** Mistake #3 says open new loops continuously, but at what point does it feel manipulative? Is there a viewer-perception threshold where rebuys flip from "engaging" to "bait"?
- **Cultural/regional variance:** Time compression and attention norms vary across markets. Are the 70/90/30/75 intensity numbers calibrated to US/EN viewers, or do they generalize?
- **Interaction with platform algorithms:** Kane mentions AVD as the key algorithmic input. How do these mistakes map onto platforms that weight differently — Threads (replies), LinkedIn (dwell + comment), Instagram carousels (swipe-through rate), Twitter/X (replies + bookmarks)?
