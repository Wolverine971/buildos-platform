## Core Thesis

**Social media is no longer social. It is media.** The platform shift Kane locates in 2020 (TikTok-driven) collapsed the social graph as a primary distribution input. Every major feed — Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn, YouTube, Facebook, X — now runs on **audience matching**: an algorithm pairs videos with viewers regardless of follow relationship, optimizing for time-on-platform so more ads can run.

The creator's only real job in this regime is to **make the matching easy** for the algorithm. That requires brutal topic discipline, narrow audience targeting, and a refusal of broad virality. The platforms reward precision, not reach.

The macro-economic frame: there are three layers in the content stack — **platform / media / offering** — and value capture is correlated to risk. The media (creator) layer captures the least. AdSense and brand deals will not produce real money. Use media to generate attention; route that attention to owned products, services, or affiliate deals with uncapped upside.

Kane's mental model in one line: **a creator is an audience-matching utility for the platform; the platform is an attention market; money lives at the platform and offering layers, never at the media layer.**

The five "uncommon learnings" Kane lists are not parallel — they nest. Lesson 1 (matching) is the operating physics. Lesson 2 (on-target virality) is the publishing strategy that respects the physics. Lesson 3 (one platform) is the resource allocation that lets you execute the strategy. Lesson 4 (islands) is the architectural rule that prevents you from undermining yourself across platforms. Lesson 5 (value capture) is the macro-economic frame that decides why you are doing any of this. Read them as a stack, not a list.

## Operating Lessons

### 1. Train the algorithm with militant topic precision

The algorithm only needs two things to match: the topic of your videos and the profiles of viewers who engage. It infers topic primarily from **video transcription and caption** (the spoken words and what you write). It infers audience by lookalike-clustering viewers who positively engage.

This is Kane's most concrete tactical claim and the one most creators ignore. The matching layer is a text-classification problem disguised as a video-recommendation problem. Your transcript is the input. Your title and caption are reinforcement. Everything else (thumbnail, edit pace, music) is execution that affects engagement _after_ the topic has been classified.

Agent / creator behavior:

- Pick one topic per channel and stay there for 30–50 videos minimum.
- Write spoken scripts that contain the keywords the audience uses about its pain points (the algorithm reads the transcript).
- Audit your last 20 videos: are they the same topic, hitting the same viewer avatar? If yes and views are still flat, the issue is execution. If no, the issue is positioning and the algorithm cannot help you.
- Do not split sports, tech, and design across one account. Split accounts.
- Treat the first 30 videos on a new channel as **calibration spend** — you are paying the algorithm in content to teach it who you are.
- Do not pivot topic mid-channel. Each pivot resets the matching loop.

### 2. Reject pure virality. Aim for on-target virality.

Two virality modes:

- **Pure virality:** maximize the slice of 8 billion people who see the video. Generic topics, broad relatability. Produces ego, not income.
- **On-target virality:** pick a narrow audience slice and saturate it. High penetration within a defined avatar; intentional spillover ceiling.

Algorithms prefer precision to breadth. Cult-favorite creators with 100–300K followers monetize better than 7M-follower generalists because their match signal is clean. Pure virality confuses the algorithm by surfacing the video to too many disjoint groups, poisoning the next-video targeting.

Decision filter for every idea: **"Will my core audience avatar find this interesting, useful, valuable, or entertaining?"** If no, kill it — even if it would go broadly viral.

The mental shift is from _reach maximization_ to _category penetration_. Kane's framing inverts the default content-creator instinct: a 200K-view video that lands inside your avatar is a better business outcome than a 10M-view video that spans tech bros, real estate agents, and architects. The reason is downstream — the algorithm uses the engagement signal from the broad video to corrupt your next ten videos' targeting. You are paying for the dopamine hit with reduced future precision.

### 3. Fish where the fish are. One platform, six months.

There are six platforms where buyers actually live: Facebook, YouTube, LinkedIn, TikTok, Instagram, X. Pick the **one** where your customers cluster, add email, and run only that combo for six months. Kane explicitly rejects "be everywhere" advice as nonsense for solo / small operators.

Single-platform focus enables:

- Immersion in the platform's zeitgeist and sub-communities.
- Comment and DM engagement at saturation.
- Use of secondary formats (Stories, Notes, Spaces) that the algorithm rewards.
- Becoming a category staple instead of a generalist nobody.

If you don't know where your customers are, ask a small random sample of existing customers. If that fails, model it on where you yourself learn the most about your space.

All formats migrate to video. Whichever platform you pick, you make video.

### 4. Treat platforms as islands, not an ecosystem.

The "build a connected ecosystem; cross-promote everywhere" advice is wrong for a creator starting from scratch. Reasons:

- **Platforms penalize off-platform pushes.** Posting URLs in tweets, LinkedIn posts, or Instagram Stories nukes engagement. Meta does not want to send users to YouTube. YouTube does not want to send users to TikTok.
- **Users consume at platform-specific speeds.** TikTok at 50 mph, Instagram at 35, YouTube at 10. Forcing a viewer to switch platforms breaks consumption habits and they churn.
- **Cross-platform forced ramps reduce trust.** Subconscious dislike of being yanked from a familiar UX.

The only sanctioned ramp is **rented → owned**: Instagram → email, Instagram → private community, never Instagram → TikTok. If you want a unified brand presence, use **world-building** — visual cohesion, recurring imagery, consistent voice — so that when a viewer organically discovers you on another island, the brand feels continuous. Don't force the discovery. Earn it.

Practical implication for content repurposing: it is fine to take the same idea and produce a native version for each platform you operate on. It is not fine to post identical content with a "watch the full version on [other platform]" CTA. Native execution per island; world-building for cross-island recognition; rented-to-owned for actual conversion.

### 5. Value does not accrue at the media layer.

The content stack:

- **Platform layer** (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube): captures most of the value.
- **Media layer** (creators): generates the attention but captures very little of the value.
- **Offering layer** (products, services, brands): captures most of the rest.

Value capture correlates to risk. Starting media is cheap and easy; starting a brand or platform is hard. Therefore the rate paid to the media layer is structurally low.

Tactical implication: AdSense and brand deals will not produce sustainable income. Use the media layer to manufacture attention, then route that attention to:

- Owned products (digital, physical, courses, software).
- Owned services (agency, consulting, productized).
- Affiliate deals with **uncapped upside** (revenue share, not flat fees).

The 12-months-for-$52 AdSense moment Kane describes is the universal disillusionment. The fix is not more views; it is moving up a layer.

The corollary Kane does not state but implies: **the right number of monetizable creators on any given platform is small.** The platforms are not in the business of making creators rich; they are in the business of generating cheap inventory. A creator who internalizes this stops measuring success in views and starts measuring it in offering-layer revenue per attention dollar generated.

## Frameworks Or Templates

### The Audience-Match Loop

```
Topic discipline (transcript + caption) →
    Algorithm classifies the topic →
    Algorithm finds positive-engagement viewers →
    Algorithm builds lookalike cluster →
    Algorithm pushes to that cluster →
    Cluster engages →
    Loop tightens
```

Break any link (mixed topics, vague captions, off-target ideas) and the loop fails to close.

### The Virality Pie

- Pure virality = "slice of 8B".
- On-target virality = "saturate a chosen slice; refuse spillover".

Question to ask before publishing: _Will this idea engage anyone outside my avatar in a way that confuses the algorithm tomorrow?_

### Hero-Platform Decision Filter

1. Where do my existing customers spend the most time? (Ask a sample.)
2. Where do I learn the most about my own space?
3. Pick one. Add email. Six months. No exceptions.

### The Three-Layer Content Stack

| Layer    | Examples                   | Value capture | Why                              |
| -------- | -------------------------- | ------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Platform | IG, TikTok, YT             | Highest       | Highest barrier, network effects |
| Offering | Products, services, brands | High          | High risk, capital, fulfillment  |
| Media    | Creators                   | Lowest        | Low barrier, abundant supply     |

Where to spend a creator's hours: 30% on the media layer (just enough to manufacture attention), 70% on owned offerings the attention can flow to.

### World-Building vs. Ecosystem

- **Ecosystem** = forced cross-platform ramps. Wrong.
- **World-building** = visual / verbal / aesthetic cohesion across islands so organic discovery feels continuous. Right.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Mixed-topic channels.** Sports + tech + design on one account. The algorithm can't find your audience and won't push you.
- **Caption / transcript carelessness.** If your spoken words and captions don't name the topic in audience language, the matching loop never starts.
- **Chasing pure virality for ego.** A 10M-view hit that doesn't convert is worse than a 200K-view hit that lands inside your avatar — the broad hit corrupts your next-video targeting.
- **Be-everywhere strategy.** Spreading thin across six platforms with no team produces noise and burnout. Single-platform focus compounds; multi-platform fragmentation does not.
- **Forced cross-platform ramps.** "Watch my YouTube video" in an Instagram Story nukes the Story's reach and annoys viewers who came for Instagram-native content.
- **Posting external URLs.** Platforms suppress link-bearing posts. Treat link drops as paid expenses.
- **Relying on AdSense / brand deals.** The media layer's rate card is structurally low. Twelve months to $52 is not a personal failure; it's the layer.
- **Generic "value-add" content.** Broad / generic = broad / generic audience match = low monetization.
- **Aiming the algorithm correctly but executing badly.** Once the algorithm is aimed, you still need ideas, storytelling, scripts, and editing to perform.

## Cross-Source Notes

This is the critical section. Kane's mental model and the BuildOS interest-media manifesto (`social-media-is-dead-interest-media.md`) are working the **same diagnosis from opposite chairs**: Kane writes for the creator who must succeed inside the new regime; BuildOS writes for the human who must protect their mind from it. They name the same shift, then prescribe opposite responses.

### Where Kane and BuildOS agree

1. **The 2020 break is real and definitional.** Kane: "social platforms shifted away from social and toward media because of TikTok." BuildOS: the social web (Web 2.0) ended; what replaced it is interest media. Both reject the term "social media" as descriptive of the current product. The shift is not gradual or partial — both sources treat it as a category change with a roughly identifiable date, and both make that naming move the foundation of everything else they prescribe.
2. **The social graph is no longer the primary distribution input.** Kane: "media is matching content from anyone, followers or strangers." BuildOS: "the social graph is a minor input. The paid distribution budget is a major one. The platform's ranking model is the final arbiter."
3. **The algorithm's objective is time-on-platform, not connection.** Both treat the recommendation engine as a profit-maximizing matching system whose interests are not aligned with the user's.
4. **The "social" thing migrated to private spaces.** Kane gestures at this in lesson 4 (the only sanctioned ramp is rented → owned, Instagram → community). BuildOS makes it explicit: "Discord. Group chats. Signal. Private Slacks. The private, chosen, bounded spaces are where the social web actually lives now."
5. **Reach is not the same as value.** Kane: pure virality is a trap; 7M-follower generalists can't pay rent. BuildOS: viral reach is paid distribution arbitraged against your trust. Both reject "more views = more value."
6. **The creator's unit of work has shifted.** Kane: precision and topic discipline. BuildOS: chosen surfaces and reduced exposure to the loud half. Both demand intentionality where the platform encourages spray.

### Where Kane and BuildOS disagree (or operate at different altitudes)

1. **The fundamental question is different.** Kane asks: _"How do I succeed inside interest media?"_ BuildOS asks: _"How do I keep my mind intact while the public web becomes more synthetic each year?"_ Kane is operating _inside_ the regime; BuildOS is examining _whether to participate_.
2. **Kane is a participant; BuildOS is a critic.** Kane sells Sandcastles (an AI scriptwriting tool that helps you produce 30 scripts/day to feed the matching loop). BuildOS argues against precisely that style of input — generative content that "platforms will keep using until they're forced to build something better." The Kane playbook is a contributor to the synthetic-content wave the BuildOS essay names as the next stage.
3. **"On-target virality" still ends in the feed.** Kane's prescription routes attention from the feed → owned offerings (products, email, services). BuildOS asks whether the feed is the right place to be visible at all, given that "what gets distributed isn't exactly content. It's pre-decided importance." Kane optimizes the matching; BuildOS questions the legitimacy of the matching layer itself.
4. **Kane's "rented → owned" ramp is identical to BuildOS's "migrate to the quiet half" — but for different reasons.** Kane: own the relationship because the platforms underpay creators. BuildOS: own the surface because the public feed is a paid distribution mechanism with your eyes as inventory. Same tactical move, very different ethics underneath.
5. **Kane treats the algorithm as neutral terrain to be exploited.** It rewards precision; learn its preferences; win. BuildOS treats the algorithm as an active distortion of what counts as important — a mechanism that arbitrages user trust to whoever paid to be there. Kane's frame says "play the game well." BuildOS's frame says "the game is rigged in a way that calling it a game is part of the rigging."
6. **The "value layer" stack and the "importance as a product" frame are complementary, not contradictory.** Kane: value capture goes to platforms and offerings, not creators. BuildOS: importance itself is now a purchasable product, sold by the platform layer to whoever pays. Both name a hidden transaction; Kane names a money one, BuildOS names a trust one. They click together.
7. **What replaces it.** Kane's answer: better positioning inside the system (hero platform, narrow avatar, owned offering). BuildOS's answer: a third move Kane does not consider — _build a surface where the direction of the arrow is reversed._ A brain dump. A journal. A thinking environment. Kane prescribes how to be a better speaker into the firehose. BuildOS prescribes a place to be silent, and to be the author of your own input, at least once per day.
8. **The role of AI in the regime.** Kane treats AI tooling (Sandcastles) as an accelerant for the matching loop — produce more scripts, hit the topic harder, win more matches. BuildOS treats the AI content wave as the next stage of feed degradation — "generative models will pump out clips, posts, articles, and commentary at prices no human can compete with. The volume signal — already broken — becomes meaningless." Kane sees AI as a tool to play the game faster. BuildOS sees AI as the force that makes the game unwinnable. Both are right at different timescales: Kane is right for the next 18 months; BuildOS is right for what happens when everyone has Kane's tools.

### What this means for the combo

The "Algorithm-aware publishing" combo can use Kane straight as the operating manual for **distribution work the founder is going to do anyway**: BuildOS publishes blogs, posts, and clips. We need to know how the matching layer reads them, why pure virality hurts us, why we should pick a hero platform, and why our money will come from the offering layer (BuildOS subscriptions), not the media layer (views).

For BuildOS specifically: we already know we are operating at the offering layer (a SaaS product). The question Kane answers is _how to manufacture attention into that offering without becoming a content factory_. His answer is precision — fewer videos, narrower audience, higher saturation inside the avatar. That maps cleanly to BuildOS's anti-AI marketing strategy: we are not trying to "be everywhere," we are trying to be the obvious choice for _people making complex things_.

The "Ethical interest-media distribution" cross-link is sharper. Kane's playbook is **necessary but not sufficient** for BuildOS positioning. A founder who only reads Kane will optimize for the matching loop and treat the feed as neutral terrain. A founder who only reads the BuildOS essay will reject the feed and have no distribution. The combo's job is to teach a creator-founder to **execute Kane's playbook with BuildOS's diagnosis in mind** — meaning:

- Use the matching loop, but refuse to manufacture synthetic volume to feed it.
- Pick a hero platform, but build the rented → owned ramp as the actual product strategy, not a side benefit.
- Aim for on-target virality, but design the offering so it deserves the trust the feed is arbitraging.
- Treat platforms as islands and **never** rely on the feed as your only attention surface — invest in the quiet half (newsletter, podcast, RSS, long-form essays) as the base of the brand, with the loud half as ramp.

The two sources are doing different jobs at the same level of intelligence. Pair them.

The two sources do not contradict at the level of diagnosis. They contradict at the level of _what the diagnosis obligates you to do_. Kane is writing for a creator who has decided to make money on platforms. BuildOS is writing for a person who has not yet decided whether the platforms deserve their time. The difference is whether you are inside the regime trying to win or outside the regime trying to think clearly about it. A founder publishing for BuildOS occupies both positions simultaneously, which is why this combo exists.

## Open Questions / Gaps

- **Is the algorithm-aware playbook stable?** Kane's prescriptions assume the current matching architecture. As generative content saturates the feed (the BuildOS essay's prediction), platforms will likely change their ranking inputs. Topic precision via transcript-keyword may become a weaker signal once every transcript is AI-optimized. Kane does not address what happens when his playbook becomes table stakes for everyone.
- **How does this scale to a B2B SaaS founder, not a creator?** Kane's examples are creators, agency owners, and personal brands. BuildOS is a B2B-ish productivity platform. The "hero platform + six months + on-target virality" advice translates, but the audience-matching loop assumes a high publish cadence. What's the founder-publishing version of this?
- **What is the right hero platform for BuildOS?** The combo will need a recommendation. The candidates are X (founder presence, dev/PM audience), LinkedIn (operator audience), YouTube (long-form essays + clips), Instagram (anti-feed visual carousels), TikTok (short-form). Kane's framework demands one. We have not chosen.
- **Where does the BuildOS essay's "build a thinking surface" move fit in Kane's stack?** It's not the platform layer, not the media layer, not the offering layer. It might be a _fourth_ layer Kane doesn't see — call it the **author layer**. Worth exploring whether this is a real fourth layer or just the offering layer described differently.
- **Is "world-building over ecosystem" enough when the cross-platform brand is the actual product?** BuildOS is a software brand that lives across web, blog, social, podcast, email. World-building visual cohesion is a baseline; the question is whether Kane's "don't force cross-platform discovery" advice is too restrictive when each surface serves a different funnel stage.
- **Does on-target virality conflict with anti-AI marketing?** BuildOS leads with relief, not AI. Kane's matching loop rewards keyword-dense, pain-point-named content. The two are compatible in principle, but the temptation to over-optimize for the matching loop and lose the literary, founder-direct voice is real. Worth surfacing as a tension in the combo.
- **No discussion of paid distribution / clipping economy.** Kane's playbook is the organic-creator path. The BuildOS essay (via Devin Nash) names the paid distribution layer as the real game underneath. The combo should reconcile: Kane's organic optimization + Devin Nash / BuildOS's paid-distribution receipts = the full picture of the regime.
- **Sandcastles tension.** Kane sells an AI scriptwriting tool that produces 30 scripts/day. BuildOS argues against synthetic content volume. If we cite Kane in a public skill, we should be clear: we use the mental model, we do not endorse the volume-production tooling.
