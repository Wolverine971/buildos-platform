## Core Thesis

Viral scripting is not art, it is psychology executed in a fixed sequence. Every good script is the same machine: **expectations vs reality**. The creator's job is to make the title set an expectation, then let every following beat — packaging, intro, body order, transitions, outro, CTA — push reality slightly above that expectation, line by line.

The five-step order is non-negotiable:

1. Packaging (idea + title + loose thumbnail)
2. Outline (unique points before words)
3. Intro (5-part hook formula)
4. Body (2-1-3-4 ordering with Value Loops and rehooks)
5. Outro (Fortune Cookie recap)

Plus a Step 0 governing principle (expectations vs reality) and a Bonus layer (native CTA embeds).

For BuildOS, the translation is direct: an agent helping a founder write video scripts (or any narrative content — landing pages, threads, fundraising decks, demo flows) should not start at "draft me an intro." It should enforce the order, refuse to advance until uniqueness is proven at the outline step, and treat the title as a contract reality must beat.

## Operating Lessons

### 1. Expectations vs reality is the only metric that matters

Every line either raises reality above the title's promised expectation or it doesn't. Retention, likes, shares, and rewatches are downstream of that single gap.

Agent behavior:

- Before drafting, ask: "What expectation does the title set? What reality must the script deliver to beat it?"
- After each beat, score: did this raise reality, hold reality, or shrink reality vs the implied expectation?
- Refuse to ship a script where the body merely meets expectations — meeting is losing.

### 2. Write in this order, every time

Kane writes every script in the same order: packaging → outline → intro → body → outro. The order itself is a forcing function — it stops you from polishing prose before you have a unique point.

Agent behavior:

- Block forward motion until the prior step is checked. Do not let the founder start writing the intro before the outline exists.
- Treat order violations (writing pretty intros over weak outlines) as the most common script failure.

### 3. Click confirmation in the first three lines

The first three lines of the intro must (a) confirm the click — yes, this is the video the title promised — and (b) raise expectations slightly. Mr Beast example: title says "I spent 100 hours inside the pyramids," the first line says he's in the pyramids (confirm) plus "unprecedented access to areas never explored" (raise).

Agent behavior:

- Generate the title first. Then write the first three lines as a click-confirmation block.
- Audit those three lines for: does it name the topic from the title? Does it raise the stakes beyond what the title promised?

### 4. Title is mandatory before script. Thumbnail is loose.

Kane diverges from the YouTube orthodoxy of "lock title and thumbnail before writing." His rule: title locked, thumbnail loose. A perfectionist thumbnail process kills script momentum.

Agent behavior:

- Demand a final-or-near-final title before drafting.
- Allow a placeholder thumbnail brief; do not block on visuals.
- Flag if the founder is procrastinating on the script by polishing visuals.

### 5. Outline before prose — and the outline must prove uniqueness

The outline step exists to gut-check whether the body has anything new to say. If the bullet points are common knowledge, the script cannot beat expectations no matter how good the prose. Anyone can learn intro mechanics. Almost nobody invests in unique points.

Sources of uniqueness:

- The tip itself
- How it's distilled
- How it's phrased
- The example used to explain it

Agent behavior:

- After the outline, run a "is this unique" check. Compare against existing top videos / common takes in the space.
- If not unique, refuse to draft the script. Send the founder back to research.
- For each bullet, demand the **what / why / how**: what it is, why it matters, how it fits the larger story.

### 6. The 5-Part Intro Hook Formula

Kane gives a copy-paste intro framework. Order matters (parts 4 and 5 can swap):

1. **Immediate context.** Bluntly state what the video is about. Confirm the click in plain English.
2. **Common belief.** State the conventional take on the topic. The viewer's existing belief is the baseline expectation.
3. **Contrarian take.** Contradict the common belief. This is where you start beating expectations.
4. **Plan.** Give the ordered structure of what's coming (steps, list, framework name).
5. **Proof.** Establish credibility — why should they trust this contrarian approach.

Agent behavior:

- Generate intros as 5 distinct labeled blocks before stitching to prose.
- If any block is missing, refuse to ship. The most commonly skipped block is the **common belief** — without it, the contrarian take has nothing to push against.

### 7. The 2-1-3-4 Body Order (counterintuitive)

The single biggest novelty in the talk: do **not** put your best point first. Put your second-best point first, your best point second, then descend.

Why: human brains read patterns. Two ascending points train the viewer that value is increasing — they stay for point three. Lead with your best and the slope flips downward; the viewer subconsciously expects diminishing returns and bounces.

Music album analogy: the single is rarely track 1. It's track 3 or 4. Track 1 is intro, track 2 is strong, track 3-4 is climax — that ascending pattern is what holds listeners through the album.

Agent behavior:

- After the outline, **rank points by strength**, then reorder to 2-1-3-4-5.
- Justify the swap in writing — force the founder to confirm point 2 really is stronger than point 1 by the metric of "novelty + shock + uniqueness."
- For multi-section content (threads, decks, blog posts), apply the same pattern: second-strongest opens, strongest pays off, then descend.

### 8. The Value Loop: every body point has three components

For each point in the body, write:

1. **Context.** What is it. Stated as simply as possible.
2. **Application.** How to do it. Use as many concrete examples as the medium allows.
3. **Framing.** Why it matters. How it slots into the bigger puzzle.

This three-part loop matches how brains process information: surface fact → tactical implementation → zoomed-out meaning. The "wave" of zoom-in / zoom-out keeps comprehension and retention high simultaneously.

Agent behavior:

- Every body point must be checked for the three components. Missing any one weakens the loop.
- Check rhythm: does the script alternate big-picture and tactical? If the founder is stuck in tactics or stuck in abstractions, force a wave.

### 9. Rehooking between points

Between every body point, plant a **mini hook** — a one-sentence transition that creates curiosity about the next point. Pattern: "That point was important, but if you don't also pair it with this next one, the whole thing collapses."

Why: attention decays after a peak. Without a fresh micro-hook, the viewer bounces in the trough between points.

Agent behavior:

- Auto-generate a rehook sentence between every two body points.
- Score: does this rehook name a stake or consequence the next point resolves?
- If the rehook is generic ("Next, let's talk about…"), rewrite. Generic transitions are bounce points.

### 10. The Fortune Cookie Outro

The outro is not an afterthought. It is where likes, shares, comments, and rewatches are seeded. Pattern: summarize the points, restate the pain solved, leave the viewer with a high note.

Analogy: free chocolate at the end of a restaurant meal. Your dinner already happened — the chocolate's job is to recolor your memory of the dinner so you tip well, return, and tell friends.

Agent behavior:

- Write outros as: (a) recap of points, (b) re-statement of the pain solved, (c) emotional high note.
- Never end with "anyway, that's it." That is leaving algorithmic value on the table.

### 11. Native Embed CTAs (don't break flow)

Standard CTAs ("subscribe, like, hit the bell") break the script's spell. Kane's solution: identify points in the body where one of your free resources is the natural answer to the pain just raised, and embed the CTA inside the explanation.

Two flavors:

- **Open the loop with a pain, give your resource as the solve.**
- **Speed-run a framework so densely that the viewer needs your free download to actually keep up.**

In this very video Kane does it twice — for "virality blueprint" (after the intro framework) and "Sandcastles" (after talking about organizational overhead). Both are framed as solving a pain raised in the surrounding content, not as ad reads.

Agent behavior:

- During outline, mark which point each CTA belongs to.
- Write the CTA as a continuation of the point, not a break from it.
- Test: read the script with CTAs removed — does it still feel complete? If yes, the embed worked. If the script collapses without it, the CTA is too load-bearing.

## Frameworks Or Templates

### The Full Kallaway Killer-Script Skeleton

```
STEP 0 — PSYCHOLOGY (governing layer)
  Principle: Reality must beat expectations.
  Question every beat: does this raise reality vs the title's promise?

STEP 1 — PACKAGING
  Idea: one-sentence pain point or curiosity rabbit hole for ideal viewer
  Title: must trigger a curiosity click + set the expectation contract
  Thumbnail: loose, do NOT block the script on it

STEP 2 — OUTLINE (bulleted, before prose)
  Unique points list:
    - Point A — what / why / how
    - Point B — what / why / how
    - Point C — what / why / how
  Uniqueness check: are these new vs common takes in the niche?
    -> If no, return to research. Do NOT proceed.

STEP 3 — INTRO (5-part hook)
  (1) Immediate context + click confirmation
  (2) Common belief / conventional take
  (3) Contrarian take that breaks the common belief
  (4) Plan / ordered structure of what's coming
  (5) Proof / credibility (parts 4 and 5 swappable)

STEP 4 — BODY (2-1-3-4 order)
  Reorder ranked points: 2nd best, best, 3rd, 4th...
  For each point, run the Value Loop:
    - Context (what)
    - Application (how + examples)
    - Framing (why + zoom-out)
  Between points: insert a rehook (mini stake-raising transition)
  Rhythm: alternate zoom-in (tactic) and zoom-out (big picture)

STEP 5 — OUTRO (Fortune Cookie)
  Recap the points
  Restate the pain that was solved
  End on a high emotional note
  Goal: trigger likes, shares, comments, rewatches

BONUS — NATIVE CTA EMBEDS
  Tag 1-2 body points where your free resource = natural pain solve
  Embed the CTA as continuation of the point
  Avoid generic "subscribe / like / bell" breaks
```

### The 2-1-3-4 Order, Annotated

```
Rank your points by raw novelty + shock + uniqueness:
  Best: A
  2nd:  B
  3rd:  C
  4th:  D

DO NOT publish in order A, B, C, D.
Publish in order:    B, A, C, D

Why:
  - B opens strong (better than expected, but not peak)
  - A escalates (peak novelty -> pattern of ascending value)
  - C continues the pattern enough to retain
  - D wraps the arc

The pattern itself is the retention mechanism, not any single point.
```

### The Value Loop (per body point)

```
POINT N
  Context:     what it is, simplest possible statement
  Application: how to do it, multiple concrete examples
  Framing:     why it matters, how it slots into the bigger story

Then: REHOOK -> "that was important, but if you don't pair it with this next
                one, the whole thing collapses"

Then: POINT N+1
```

### Native CTA Embed Pattern

```
[Body point raises a pain]
   ↓

   ↓

   ↓
[Continue the script — do NOT pause for "and don't forget to subscribe"]

Test: if the script reads complete WITHOUT the CTA line, the embed worked.
```

## Anti-Patterns

The script-killing failure modes Kane explicitly or implicitly names:

- **Skipping the title.** Drafting an intro before the title is locked. The script has no expectation contract to beat, so click confirmation is impossible.
- **Skipping the outline.** Writing prose for points that turn out to be common knowledge. Beautiful sentences cannot fix unoriginal points.
- **Putting the best point first.** Trains the viewer that value is decaying. They bounce after point one.
- **Missing the common belief in the intro.** Without it, the contrarian take has nothing to contrast against and just sounds like an opinion.
- **Generic transitions between points.** "Next, let's talk about…" is a bounce-trigger. No rehook = decay = exit.
- **Treating the outro as an afterthought.** Skipping recap leaves likes, shares, and rewatches on the table. The free chocolate matters.
- **CTAs that break the spell.** Mid-script "smash that subscribe button" interrupts the expectation-vs-reality engine. Native embeds hide CTAs inside content.
- **Locking the thumbnail before the script.** Kane's heresy vs YouTube orthodoxy. Thumbnail perfectionism kills script momentum.
- **Summarizing existing knowledge.** If your reality matches the viewer's existing baseline, you cannot beat expectations. Regurgitation = retention collapse.
- **Writing the body before checking uniqueness.** The outline gut-check exists for one reason: to stop you from making a video that can't win.

## Open Questions / Gaps

- **How does 2-1-3-4 scale to 5+ points?** Kane implies you continue ranking down (2-1-3-4-5-6) but doesn't address whether mid-list peaks (e.g., putting the 3rd-best at slot 4) ever helps re-energize a long video.
- **Quantitative evidence for 2-1-3-4 over 1-2-3-4.** The music-album argument is suggestive but anecdotal. No A/B data shown. An agent applying this should treat it as a strong default, not a proven law.
- **Cross-platform compression.** The framework is presented for long-form. The 60-second TikTok version (covered in the walkthrough partner video) compresses each section heavily — but the rules for what gets cut (proof? common belief?) aren't fully spelled out here.
- **When uniqueness is genuinely impossible.** Some topics have been covered to death. Kane's answer is "go research more" — but for evergreen niches the realistic move is novel framing or novel example. The agent needs heuristics for "novel-enough" when "novel" is unattainable.
- **CTA frequency.** Two native embeds per ~20-minute video worked here. The agent needs guardrails for short-form (one embed max?) and for long-form (when does a third embed cross from native to ad-feeling?).
- **Voice and persona.** This video is structural. It does not address how the founder's voice interacts with the framework. Master Storyteller (`t5Z-Q1bg1tU`) is the missing piece — the agent should not produce voice-flat scripts that follow the structure but feel generic.
- **Failure recovery mid-script.** What happens when point 2 (the second-best) underperforms in the data? Does the framework recommend moving point 1 to slot 1 in the next video, or hunting for a new "second-best"? Open question for an iterative content system.
