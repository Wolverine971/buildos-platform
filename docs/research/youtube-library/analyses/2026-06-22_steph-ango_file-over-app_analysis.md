# ANALYSIS: Steph Ango (Obsidian CEO) — File Over App, Durable Tools, and Preserving User Agency

## Skill Combo Links

1. **Constraint-driven creative systems** — Ango treats self-imposed constraints (500-word essays, a 9-color palette, a 5-line manifesto, a 5-year recommendation rule, a 7-person headcount cap) as the engine of creativity and consistency, not its enemy.

## Core Thesis

Ango's worldview reduces to one move repeated across mediums: **deliberately constrain the system so the human stays in control of it.** "File over app" is the political/civilizational form of this — own your data in durable, plain-text, Lindy-format files so your work survives any single tool or company. "Don't delegate understanding" is the personal form — keep enough comprehension of the systems you depend on (your furnace, your money, your software) that you can still make choices instead of being farmed by "parasites." A tool, in his definition, is "something that converts what you can do into what you want to do"; the best tools conform to the human (and to the _changing_ human) rather than forcing the human to conform to them. Obsidian is the proof of concept: 7 people, ~3–4M users, no investors, local-first encrypted data, a plugin architecture that makes the tool malleable to each brain, run on a 5-point public manifesto.

## Key Insights & Takeaways

1. **"File over app" is a stance, then a constraint.** Ango is explicit there are "two totally separate topics": (a) file-over-app as "a political statement... societally important, civilizationally important... a rejection of how big tech... is built," and (b) the day-to-day engineering constraint that follows from respecting it. The philosophy is upstream; the product rules are downstream.

2. **The deal most software offers is "selling back access to your own data."** His sharpest operational line: "You're feeding a service data, and then it's selling you access to that data that you fed into it. We have sort of accepted that that is the deal, but it doesn't have to be that way." Obsidian inverts it: data lives on _your_ devices, sync is end-to-end encrypted, "we can't read it, we can't train on it."

3. **Durability comes from format age, not heroics (the Lindy rule).** "The older the format, the more likely it is to still be around in the future." His test: "If you want your writing to still be readable on a computer from the 2060s... it's important that your notes can be read on a computer from the 1960s." Plain text / markdown qualifies; proprietary binary formats do not.

4. **Links beat hierarchy as the core knowledge primitive.** The one thing Obsidian adds over pen and paper is cheap linking: "the most important thing about Obsidian is just being able to click links and connect things with links. It's that simple. All the extra stuff... is on top of that foundation." Folders/hierarchy are never named as the primitive — links are.

5. **Malleability is the agency mechanism.** "Obsidian is about malleability. It's about shaping the tool around your brain rather than the other way around. A lot of tools ask you to shape your brain around it." And it must flex over time: "your brain is going to be different... it also might be different from your brain a year ago." The decision rule is operational: "If there's an option to make the thing malleable and there's an option to make it non-malleable, we're always going to pick the malleable one."

6. **"Don't delegate understanding" — the parasite.** He frames outsourced comprehension as a parasite: "Let me take care of this hard thing for you... Instead of understanding it yourself, you choose to give the parasite control over your health, education, money... even your beliefs." The home-ownership story (watching the electrician, then doing the furnace/toilet repair himself from a YouTube video) is the antidote: understand enough to _choose_, then delegate deliberately, not by default.

7. **Delegate caloric energy, not understanding.** Counterweight to #6: from "Caloric Energy Is Precious," automation (now cognitive automation via LLMs) frees you to _choose_ what to spend effort on. He uses AI to ship throwaway personal tools — "I don't really care about the quality of the code" — so he can spend his caloric energy learning piano. The distinction is sharp: delegate the _tedious/mechanical_ (good); never delegate the _understanding that preserves your ability to decide_ (bad).

8. **Constraints are the source of creativity, deliberately self-imposed.** Winamp-2 12-pixel sliders, 500-word footnote-free essays, a fixed 9-color palette reused everywhere, the 5-year rule before he'll recommend a product. He "zigzags" two time-constraints — "wait a year and see how you feel" vs. "I gotta ship tomorrow, so cut everything" — to depersonalize attachment and force removal.

9. **"What can we remove?" as a standing question.** Removal is invisible like a cleaned beach: the user "will just enjoy the pristine beach" and never know trash was there. Great design "distills what this thing is for into something easy to understand," and "form following function" produces beauty (machinery, nature, "use it or lose it").

10. **Care is the moat; the factory kills it.** "In Good Hands" (omakase): you can tell within a minute whether the maker cared. The failure mode is the "factory environment" optimizing for "grow the number of users or improve retention," which "is probably not going to lead toward a thing that makes you feel in good hands." His recipe: "care more than someone else," "paint the back of the cabinet," and put in attention "that doesn't make sense from an allocation-of-resources standpoint."

11. **Small + principled is itself the strategy.** No investors, 100% user-funded, hard internal cap of 10–12 people (currently ~7), almost no meetings. The 5-line manifesto removed so many decisions that he "could disappear for six months and the team would do almost exactly the same thing." Constraints scale judgment without scaling headcount.

12. **Give the idea away to make it more powerful.** Treating "file over app" as non-proprietary multiplied its impact — Microsoft shipping markdown converters, "10 different startups" doing "file over app for X." "If we thought of it as a proprietary idea... it would be less powerful."

13. **Build for yourself, durably, across the "stadium of selves."** Everything he makes is "for a version of myself"; essays are written "to a younger version of myself." This is not narcissism but a durability and authenticity test — and "other people respond to it" because shared human needs barely change.

14. **He names the canon himself.** Ango explicitly recommends **Ivan Illich's _Tools for Conviviality_** ("Everyone who's listening to this, read that... it explains this problem really well") and ties his own essays to **Samuel Butler's _Erewhon_ / "The Book of the Machines."** This directly answers Gap #2 of the index's audit (no canonical philosophy-of-tools foundation) — the bridge to Illich is in the primary source, not invented.

## Extracted Principles (file-over-app as CHECKABLE RULES)

Each rule below is written so an agent could _enforce or refuse_ against it, not just quote it. "Pass" = honors the principle; "Fail" = violates it.

### Rule 1 — Own your data (the user's data is the user's, not the app's)

**Statement (grounded):** "You're feeding a service data, and then it's selling you access to that data that you fed into it... it doesn't have to be that way." Obsidian "doesn't store things in a big database in the cloud. It is all stored on your devices."
**Checkable test:**

- Does the user retain a usable copy of their content without the app running, the account active, or the internet connected? (Must be YES.)
- Is there a full, lossless export (not a degraded PDF/screenshot)? (Must be YES.)
- Can the vendor read or train on the content without explicit consent? (Must be NO.)
  **Refuse / flag when:** a feature makes the only authoritative copy of user data live in a vendor-controlled DB with no plain-export path; or sync is added without end-to-end encryption being at least an option.

### Rule 2 — Plain-text / Lindy durability (prefer the oldest format that still works)

**Statement (grounded):** "The older the format, the more likely it is to still be around in the future... if you want your writing to still be readable on a computer from the 2060s... it's important that your notes can be read on a computer from the 1960s."
**Checkable test:**

- Is the primary storage format human-readable in a 1960s-era text editor (plain text, markdown, CSV) rather than a proprietary binary blob? (Pass = YES.)
- If a new feature _needs_ a new format (his Canvas example), is that format open-sourced and documented so others can read it? (Pass = YES.)
  **Refuse / flag when:** durable user content is locked into an undocumented proprietary format, or a format chosen for vendor convenience over readability/longevity.

### Rule 3 — Links over hierarchy (connection is the primitive)

**Statement (grounded):** "The most important thing about Obsidian is just being able to click links and connect things with links... All the extra stuff is on top of that foundation." (He never names folders/hierarchy as the core primitive.)
**Checkable test:**

- Can any item reference any other item directly (bi-directional link), independent of where it sits in a tree? (Pass = YES.)
- Is the knowledge graph navigable by association ("your own rabbit holes"), not only by drilling a fixed hierarchy? (Pass = YES.)
  **Refuse / flag when:** a design forces strict tree placement as the only way to relate items, or buries linking behind hierarchy.

### Rule 4 — Tools outlive vendors (the app is replaceable; the data is not)

**Statement (grounded):** "If Obsidian goes out of business... you still have your data. If somehow the app gets deleted off your machine, all your data is still there. It's always in your control."
**Checkable test:**

- If this company vanished tomorrow, would the user lose any _content_, or only _convenience_? (Pass = only convenience.)
- Is there a documented path to take the data to a competing tool? (Pass = YES.)
  **Refuse / flag when:** a feature increases switching cost by making data unusable outside the product (lock-in dressed up as "integration").

### Rule 5 — Malleability over the user's lifetime (the tool conforms to the brain, including the future brain)

**Statement (grounded):** "Shaping the tool around your brain rather than the other way around... your brain is going to be different... also different from your brain a year ago." Decision rule: always pick the malleable option over the non-malleable one.
**Checkable test:**

- Can the user reshape the tool's behavior to fit how _they_ work (config → scripting → plugins/forking)? (Pass = YES.)
- When a build choice is "malleable vs. fixed," is malleability the default? (Pass = YES.)
  **Refuse / flag when:** an opinionated default removes a capability with no escape hatch, _and_ the capability is load-bearing for some users. (Caveat below — malleability is not unconditional.)

### Rule 6 — Don't let the user delegate understanding (preserve the ability to choose)

**Statement (grounded):** the "parasite" essay + the furnace story. Understand enough to decide, then delegate deliberately.
**Checkable test:**

- After the tool does the work, does the user understand _what happened_ well enough to make a different choice next time? (Pass = YES.)
- Does the automation increase the user's capability, or quietly outsource it? (Pass = increases capability.)
  **Refuse / flag when:** an automation hides its reasoning such that the user becomes dependent and _less_ able to do or judge the thing themselves (deskilling). Pair with Rule 7.

### Rule 7 — Delegate caloric energy, never understanding (the automation line)

**Statement (grounded):** "Caloric Energy Is Precious" — automate the tedious/mechanical so the human can spend effort on what they care about; he ships throwaway AI-coded tools so he can spend energy _learning piano_.
**Checkable test:** classify the work being automated — is it _mechanical drudgery_ (safe to delegate) or _the comprehension/judgment that preserves agency_ (Rule 6, do not delegate)?
**Refuse / flag when:** automation crosses from "saves keystrokes" to "removes the user's understanding of their own work."

## Agent-Behavior Implications (most important section)

How this changes what an AI agent inside a tool like BuildOS should **build** or **refuse**:

1. **Refuse to make the product the sole home of the user's data.** Any agent action that writes user content should assume an exportable, plain-readable representation exists. If an agent designs a feature whose only authoritative store is an opaque internal DB with no export, that is a Rule-1/Rule-4 violation and should be flagged, not shipped. _Concrete:_ an agent proposing a new "smart format" must also propose its export/open-spec.

2. **Build the explanation, not just the result.** Per "don't delegate understanding," an agent that performs a mutation (creates a project, reschedules tasks, rewrites a doc) should surface _what it changed and why_ in a form the user can inspect and override — not just hand back a finished artifact. The user must remain able to make a _different_ choice next time. An agent that silently optimizes away the user's comprehension is building the parasite.

3. **Classify every automation against the caloric-vs-understanding line before doing it.** Drafting boilerplate, transcribing, deduping, reformatting = delegate freely (caloric). Deciding _what the user's priorities are_, _what their project means_, or _what they should believe about their own work = do not silently delegate_; assist and return control. This is a literal pre-action check an agent can run.

4. **Prefer the malleable implementation, but not unconditionally.** When two designs are equally good, pick the one the user can later reshape (Rule 5). BUT see the counter-school caveat: most users do not want to program their tools, and opinionated defaults often serve agency better than infinite configurability. The agent's job is to offer an _escape hatch_, not to push complexity onto the user.

5. **Default to durable, Lindy formats for anything meant to last.** When an agent stores knowledge it expects to persist (notes, project context, brain-dumps), it should reach for plain text / markdown over proprietary structures, and treat any new structured format as something to document and open, not hoard.

6. **Treat "care" as a buildable constraint, not a vibe.** "Paint the back of the cabinet" and the omakase test ("in good hands") become a refusal trigger: an agent generating output that is _generic_, _factory-grade_, or optimized only for an engagement/retention metric should flag it as failing the care test. (This is where Ango overlaps with Dylan Field's "good enough is mediocre" and Linus Lee's "aliveness.")

7. **Removal is an agent action, not just an addition.** Agents are biased toward adding. Ango's "what can we remove?" makes _subtraction_ a first-class proposal: an agent reviewing a surface or a doc should be able to recommend deleting cruft, and should treat "we shipped everything we could think of" as a failure signal.

8. **Give the capability away where it compounds.** The "make it easy to copy / open-source it" instinct maps to agent-native design: anything a user can do, an agent (or a third party) should be able to do via an open interface. Lock-in is an anti-pattern even when it's commercially tempting.

## BuildOS Guardrails

Use this source to keep BuildOS from collapsing into **generic automation** — the exact job the index assigns this category. Ango gives BuildOS both a mirror and a set of tripwires.

**Where BuildOS already aligns (state honestly):**

- BuildOS's positioning is a _thinking environment_ ("turn messy thinking into structured work") — close to Ango's "tool for thought" and the "conversation with oneself" frame. Brain-dump is the malleable, build-for-yourself surface.
- BuildOS has an **export/data-ownership posture** worth pressure-testing against Rule 1 and Rule 4: confirm there is a _lossless_ export (markdown/JSON) of projects, tasks, and brain-dumps that a user could take to another tool, and that it works without the app running. If that path is degraded or missing, that is the first guardrail to fix — it is the single most load-bearing file-over-app test, and BuildOS is _more_ exposed than Obsidian because it is cloud/DB-first (Supabase), the very architecture Ango critiques ("a database in the cloud... you have no direct access to").
- BuildOS's data is in Postgres with RLS, not local-first plain files. That is a legitimate divergence (web-first, multi-device, agentic), but it means Rules 1/2/4 must be honored _by export and openness_, since they cannot be honored by local-first storage. **This is the guardrail that most needs an explicit decision.**

**Tripwires (where BuildOS could become the parasite):**

1. **Agentic chat must not delegate understanding.** Every write op (project create, task sync, change-set commit) should leave the user understanding what changed and able to reverse it. The staged-mutation / ChangeSetReview posture (commit-by-default + opt-in review) is exactly the right shape — keep it, and treat "silent commit with no inspectable diff" as a Rule-6 violation.
2. **Don't let "AI organizes your thoughts" cross into "AI thinks for you."** Caloric vs. understanding (Rule 7): BuildOS should automate the _structuring drudgery_ of a brain-dump, never the _judgment about what matters_. Keep the human as the chief-cohesiveness-officer of their own projects.
3. **Resist factory metrics as the design driver.** Ango's warning that "grow users / improve retention" produces software that doesn't feel "in good hands" is a direct caution for a product with a growth org. Craft/Inkprint polish and the care test are guardrails against optimizing the soul out of the product.
4. **Prefer durable, open representations for project context.** The Project Knowledge Layer (L0 outlines, knowledge maps, docs) should lean on plain markdown and documented structures so a user's accumulated context is portable, not trapped.
5. **Offer malleability with an escape hatch, not infinite config.** BuildOS is (correctly) opinionated. The guardrail is Rule 5's caveat: keep opinionated defaults, but never remove a load-bearing capability without a way out.

**Net:** Ango's value to BuildOS is as a _durability and agency auditor_. The highest-priority BuildOS action this source surfaces is a concrete audit of the export/data-ownership path against Rules 1 and 4, precisely because BuildOS's cloud-DB architecture is the one Ango argues against — which makes an honest export story the thing standing between BuildOS and the "selling back access to your own data" trap.

## Counter-School Caveat (for the index's Gap #4)

Ango is _not_ a pure malleability maximalist, and the transcript proves it — useful for the index's missing counter-school. He is "very opinionated about the fact that it should be so unopinionated," but also: the 5-line manifesto is _highly_ opinionated; he admits Obsidian is "not making it intuitive enough for newbies"; and he explicitly says none of this is "a prescription for everyone." So the honest reading is: **malleability for the power user, opinionated defaults and hand-holding for the newcomer.** That tension (malleable core + opinionated onboarding) is the real, defensible position — closer to the calm/opinionated counter-school than to "configure everything."
