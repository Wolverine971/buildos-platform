## Core Thesis

The hook is the 80/20 of short-form video. All winning hooks are doing one job: opening a curiosity loop by creating contrast between what the viewer believes (A) and what the video introduces (B). The bigger the contrast, the deeper the curiosity, and you have ~5 seconds to install it.

Across thousands of videos, the winning patterns reduce to **six hook archetypes**. Any video idea can be expressed through any of the six — the choice is a strategic decision about which archetype best fits the **key visual** you have available, because the viewer's subconscious processes video in a **visual → audio → visual** sandwich.

For BuildOS, the most important translations are:

1. Hook generation is a **slot-fill problem**, not a creative problem — pick an archetype, pick a key visual, write to alignment.
2. Selection of archetype is downstream of **what visual the founder actually has** (or can manufacture), not of the topic.
3. Comprehension across spoken hook, visual hook, text hook, and audio hook is the difference between 500 views and 500K views — agents must check alignment, not just cleverness.

## Operating Lessons (Hook Archetypes)

The six archetypes below are presented in Kallaway's order. Each is a different way to install the A → B contrast in the first five seconds.

### 1. The Fortune Teller

**Mechanism:** Positions the present against the future. Triggers the brain's built-in compulsion to predict and prepare for what comes next. Curiosity = "I need to know what's about to change so I'm not caught flat-footed."

**Formula:**

- A = current reality the viewer takes for granted.
- B = a future state implied or asserted by the video subject.
- Frame as a question or declaration teasing that future as inevitable or imminent.

**Example hooks Kane reads aloud:**

- "That new microwave is going to change cooking forever."
- "That little blue light is going to impact traffic moving forward."
- "We're witnessing one of the largest breakthroughs in marketing."
- "This is the future of animation."
- (Kane's own 15M-view example) "I think this is the future of how people are going to design their homes."

**Tactical steps:** (a) establish current reality, (b) figure out how the future shifts because of the video subject, (c) frame a question or statement teasing that future.

**Best fit:** Breaking news, product innovations, anyone building authority around predicting where a category goes.

**Agent behavior — when to pick this for a BuildOS founder:**

- The founder is publishing about a category-shifting tool, model, integration, or workflow shift (e.g., "agent-native", "context engineering", "anti-feed").
- The b-roll is something visual that _is itself a tease of the future_ (a working demo, a never-before-seen UI, a prototype).
- Avoid when there is no genuine forward-looking claim — Fortune Teller without a real prediction reads as clickbait.

### 2. The Experimenter

**Mechanism:** Peer-to-peer demo. One friend showing another what they tried and what happened. Contrast = "I used to do it this way; I tried a new method; here's what I'm doing now." The visual _is_ the demo.

**Formula:**

- A = baseline pain or default method.
- B = the experiment / new tool / new method, shown live.
- Walk through why the new way is different/better.

**Example hooks Kane reads aloud:**

- "Does anything look weird about this shot to you? I actually don't have a camera in my hands. I'm recording with these glasses on my face. These are the Ray-Ban Meta smart glasses, and they're about to change the way that we create." (Colin and Samir — 50M views; key detail: the glasses' camera is the subject _being filmed with_, so visual and verbal hook self-prove.)
- "This right here is the brand-new ChatGPT Agent that literally controls your browser and does tasks for you, and this is an 11-step process, and we're going to see if it does it."
- (Kane notes most MrBeast videos are "human experiments" using this pattern, plus elaborate outdoor build/cabin creators.)

**Tactical steps:** (a) establish the base pain point, (b) show that you solved it (or tried something) with an experiment, (c) explain why this new approach beats the default.

**Best fit:** Product demos, new framework launches, B2B "watch our tool work," elaborate process content.

**Agent behavior — when to pick this for a BuildOS founder:**

- Demoing BuildOS itself, a new feature, a context-engineering workflow, or a comparison test (BuildOS vs. ChatGPT vs. Notion).
- Live-screen recording of an actual run is available.
- The verbal hook should describe what is happening on screen so visual ≡ words ≡ proof.
- Avoid when there is no live demo footage — fall back to Teacher.

### 3. The Teacher

**Mechanism:** Same contrast spine as Experimenter, but framed teacher-to-student instead of peer-to-peer. The proof is _extracted lessons_, not a live experiment. Contrast = "you're failing at X; I found a way; here's how."

**Formula:**

- A = base pain point the viewer is failing at.
- B = a lesson or method that solves it (extracted either from your own work or from studying someone else's).
- Walk through the steps and why they work.

**Example hooks Kane reads aloud:**

- "Three things you can learn from Aritzia."
- "Here's how I got this shot."
- "I made this Ecom brand a million dollars in 12 hours using something called the product drop method."

**Tactical steps:** (a) establish the base pain, (b) reveal a learnings-based solve, (c) walk through the process and why it works.

**Best fit:** Authority-building, expertise-as-product, anybody monetizing know-how.

**Agent behavior — when to pick this for a BuildOS founder:**

- Founder is teaching a workflow, framework, or hard-won lesson (most "context engineering" content fits here).
- No live demo b-roll, but slides, diagrams, or talking-head footage exists.
- Especially good for the BuildOS "thinking environment for people making complex things" wedge — Teacher hooks position the founder as the trail guide, not the salesman.
- Avoid stacking too many Teacher hooks back-to-back — feed gets pedagogical and lecture-y.

### 4. The Magician

**Mechanism:** A _visual stun gun_. Not a contrast format on its own — a precursor or layer that forcibly stops the scroll using either (a) outlandish visuals, (b) directing language ("look at this", "check this out") that yanks the eye, or (c) a **visual pacifier**: a passive activity (makeup, cup-stacking, GTA gameplay split-screen) that holds enough subconscious attention to keep the viewer parked.

**Formula:**

- Either (i) atypical visual that visually pops (rapid match-cuts, costume rip-off, neon, signature object), or (ii) verbal stun ("check this out") tied to a directed visual, or (iii) visual pacifier underneath the spoken hook.
- Then layer one of the other five archetypes on top.

**Example hooks Kane reads aloud:**

- "Yo, check this out."
- "China-cha — what's up, MTV chis."
- "President Trump just ordered the resurgence of a 1980s plan for nuclear war in space — let me explain."
- The neon-sign creator who opens as Trump (wig + voice), rips off the wig, then talks about neon signs.
- Carl Shakur's "snap-click" signature.
- Alex Earl doing makeup while talking; the cup-stacking creator; GTA gameplay split-screens.
- Kane's own signature: "check this out" rolling into the first line, used on ~30 of his recent videos to create audible recognition.

**Tactical steps:** Find a visual, a sound, or a signature thing atypical enough to break the scroll, then attach one of the other five archetypes underneath.

**Best fit:** Always-on layer. Especially valuable for creators with weak topical b-roll — visual pacifiers compensate.

**Agent behavior — when to pick this for a BuildOS founder:**

- Treat Magician as a _modifier_, not a standalone choice. Pair it with one of the other five.
- For a solo founder with limited b-roll, suggest a recurring visual signature (a recurring object, signature jump-cut, or "check this" verbal tic) so the audience develops Pavlovian recognition.
- Visual-pacifier candidates for a BuildOS founder: typing in BuildOS, walking, whiteboarding, or working on a real project in the background while voiceover plays.
- Do not use Magician as the entire hook — without a contrast layer it is empty spectacle.

### 5. The Investigator

**Mechanism:** Contrast against an _unknown secret_. Curiosity loop = "today you don't know X; I show you X; now you know." The viewer is positioned as someone about to be let in on something.

**Formula:**

- A = viewer doesn't know the thing.
- B = creator has unearthed the thing.
- Tee up the existence of the secret, then frame it against the viewer's current ignorance.

**Example hooks Kane reads aloud:**

- "This is one of the sneakier marketing campaigns I've seen — Central Cee's brand sells out in seconds, and what's crazy is that it's not just because he's a rapper."
- "Check this out — this is a secret Japanese city built at the base of Mount Fuji, but it's not just any city."
- (Kane: most of his own personal short-form falls into Investigator — "unearthing some tech or AI thing about the future that people don't know about.")

**Tactical steps:** (a) tee up that there is a secret/finding most people miss, (b) anchor it against current reality (not knowing).

**Best fit:** Research-driven creators, "leading edge" positioning, anyone whose value is being early.

**Agent behavior — when to pick this for a BuildOS founder:**

- Founder is sharing original observation, market commentary, or a "nobody is talking about this" insight (anti-feed thesis, manufactured viral content critique, model-specific behavior, hidden BuildOS feature).
- Especially good for X / LinkedIn carryover content where the founder builds a reputation as someone who notices things first.
- Avoid Investigator when the "secret" is actually well-known in the founder's audience — it reads condescending and burns trust.

### 6. The Contrarian

**Mechanism:** The most direct contrast format. The creator immediately states a belief that opposes conventional wisdom. No hiding, no setup — A and B are stated explicitly.

**Formula:**

- Identify a belief that is widely held in the audience's space.
- State your opposite belief explicitly in the first line.

**Example hooks Kane reads aloud:**

- "I think this is probably the most important thing that you need to be doing if you run a brand that you are probably not doing — and this is having one-on-one conversations with your customers."
- "You have no creative ideas because your space sucks to live in."
- "This guy is a multi-millionaire from his clothing brand, and the weird thing is their best sellers are almost never in stock."

**Tactical steps:** Figure out what you believe that most people don't believe, then say it explicitly.

**Best fit:** Smarty-pants experts, anyone trying to differentiate in a crowded space.

**Agent behavior — when to pick this for a BuildOS founder:**

- Founder is publishing a POV piece (anti-AI marketing, anti-feed, "blogging is dead" rebuttals, "AI assistants are hype" takes).
- Best hook archetype for the BuildOS "thinking environment, not AI tool" positioning — Contrarian _is_ the anti-AI strategy in hook form.
- Use sparingly. Stacking Contrarian hooks turns the feed into a "hot takes" account and erodes credibility — pair with Teacher or Investigator hooks to balance.

## Frameworks Or Templates

### The 6-Archetype Catalog (decision matrix)

| Archetype      | Contrast spine                              | Best when you have...                                      | Risk if misused                       |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Fortune Teller | Present → future                            | A real forward-looking claim and a "future-feeling" visual | Empty trend-chasing                   |
| Experimenter   | Old method → new method, shown live         | Live demo b-roll, a real test, a tool to show              | Clout demos with no real result       |
| Teacher        | Failing at X → here's how to win at X       | Hard-won lesson, slides, talking head                      | Becomes a lecture, kills retention    |
| Magician       | Layer/modifier — uses visual or verbal stun | Atypical visuals, signature objects, visual pacifiers      | Spectacle without payoff              |
| Investigator   | Don't know → now you know                   | A genuine "secret" or under-noticed insight                | Condescension if secret is well-known |
| Contrarian     | Conventional wisdom → opposite belief       | A real opposing POV with proof                             | Becomes a hot-takes account           |

### Universality principle

> "Every single video idea could be manipulated to use any of the six hook formats."

Kane demonstrates with the same backpack-review video idea expressed six ways:

- **Fortune Teller:** "This backpack is going to completely change the way Millennials travel."
- **Experimenter:** "I just took this backpack 7,000 miles across the world but it has three major design flaws."
- **Teacher:** "If you're traveling internationally this summer, this is the best way to pack your backpack."
- **Magician:** Rapid match-cut of multiple backpacks + voiceover "Which of these backpacks is the best for a guy in their 20s?"
- **Investigator:** "I can't believe this company isn't marketing this one flaw in their bag."
- **Contrarian:** "Everybody loves this bag, but if you use it every day, it is so overhyped."

**Implication for an agent:** never pick the archetype from the topic. Pick it from the available visual + the specific contrast you can credibly make.

### The 4 Components Of A Winning Hook

Every hook is four aligned signals:

1. **Spoken hook** — what you say in the voiceover.
2. **Visual hook** — what is on screen.
3. **Text hook** — text overlay on the screen.
4. **Audio hook** — music / SFX in the background.

> "The difference between 500 and 500,000 views is unlocking max alignment between those four things."

Misalignment causes **comprehension loss**, which kills the curiosity loop before it forms.

### The Visual → Audio → Visual Sandwich

Subconscious heat map of how viewers consume the first 5 seconds:

1. Eyes process visual + read text overlay.
2. Ears catch up and parse the spoken hook.
3. Eyes return to the visual seeking confirmation of what they just heard.

> "Our eyes process 10 to 100 times more information per second than our ears — speed of light versus speed of sound."

**Therefore:** the **key visual** is the load-bearing element of the hook, not the words. Words exist to set up and confirm the visual.

### The 5-Step Hook-Writing Process (Kane's golden approach)

1. **Inventory the visual.** What b-roll, demo footage, motion graphics, or stock do you actually have? If the visual is weak, decide whether to manufacture one (motion graphics, prop, signature object) or kill the video idea entirely.
2. **Find the biggest contrast.** Of all the angles available, which produces the largest A → B gap? That contrast picks the archetype.
3. **Write the spoken hook.** Two to four lines following the **context-lean → contrast → contrarian snapback** pattern (cross-ref the "Irresistible Hooks" video).
4. **Layer the text overlay.** Reinforce the visual on the way in and on the visual-confirmation rebound.
5. **Gut-check max comprehension.** Watch silent → watch with audio → ask: when the viewer goes visual → audio → visual, do all four hook components agree? If not, **stop and rewrite, or kill the idea**.

### Live walkthrough — the winning hook (15M views)

Video: life-size projected floor plans you can walk through.

- **Key visual:** people walking on top of full-size projected floor plans in a warehouse.
- **Inherent contrast:** never been seen before — the visual alone _is_ the contrast.
- **Archetype chosen:** Fortune Teller (over Investigator) because "future of home design" is more shocking than "secret nobody knows."
- **Text overlay:** "the future of home design" (sliding in) + "life-size floor plans" with an arrow pointing at the key visual.
- **Spoken hook:** "Check this out [Magician layer] — these are life-size floor plans, you can literally walk through your exact home design before you build it [context-lean, perfectly aligned with what's on screen]. I think this is the future of how people are going to design their homes [Fortune Teller payoff]."
- **Why it worked:** every component pointed at the same key visual.

### Live walkthrough — the losing hook (~100K views or fewer)

Video: generative world model rooms.

- **Key visual:** rooms appearing magically from a single image.
- **Spoken hook:** "This is the future of storytelling. It's called a generative world model. It lets you turn a single image into a photorealistic cinematic world."
- **Why it failed:**
    - "Future of storytelling" is too abstract — the visual (rooms) doesn't visually _mean_ "storytelling."
    - "Generative world model" and "photorealistic cinematic world" are jargon — viewer can't comprehend in 5 seconds.
    - Visual ≠ words → comprehension loss → no curiosity loop.
- **Kane's retro:** "These rooms are the future of _commercials_" with footage of someone filming a commercial in the room would have aligned visual ≡ words. Or kill the video.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Picking the archetype from the topic, not the visual.** The topic is universal — six archetypes can express any topic. The visual is the constraint. Inverting this order produces hooks that read clever in text and fail on screen.
- **Magician without a contrast layer underneath.** Spectacle alone (jump cuts, signature object, "check this out") opens no curiosity loop. It must be paired with one of the other five.
- **Jargon in the spoken hook.** "Generative world model," "photorealistic cinematic world" — words the audience can't decode in 5 seconds destroy comprehension. The first line must be in the audience's working vocabulary.
- **Abstract verbal claim vs. literal visual.** "Future of storytelling" + footage of rooms = misalignment. Either the verbal claim must match what the visual literally shows, or the visual must be manufactured to match the claim.
- **Saving the strongest visual for after the hook.** The key visual must be in the first 3–5 seconds. Any later, and the hook works with a weaker proxy.
- **Making the video when the visual is weak.** Kane explicitly says: if you don't have a strong key visual, _kill the video_. Idea inventory is infinite; produced minutes are not.
- **Stacking the same archetype across the feed.** All Contrarian = hot-takes account. All Teacher = lecture channel. All Investigator = condescension. Rotate archetypes across content cycles.
- **Treating hook-writing as a creativity problem.** It is a _slot-fill alignment problem_ — visual chosen, archetype chosen, four components aligned. Cleverness is downstream.
