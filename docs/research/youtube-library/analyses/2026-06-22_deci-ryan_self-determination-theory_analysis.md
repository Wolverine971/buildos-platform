## Source

- **Primary:** Edward L. Deci & Richard M. Ryan — Self-Determination Theory (SDT), developed from ~1971 onward; consolidated in _Intrinsic Motivation and Self-Determination in Human Behavior_ (1985) and the _Handbook of Self-Determination Research_ (2002).
- **Canonical institutional source:** [selfdeterminationtheory.org](https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/) — the official SDT site maintained by Ryan, Deci, and the Center for Self-Determination Theory.
- **Source type:** Established empirical psychology theory (not a single video/interview). This is one of the most replicated frameworks in motivation research, with thousands of studies across education, work, health, sport, and technology.

> **Honesty note (carried through this analysis):** SDT's core need framework and the _direction_ of its motivation findings are robust and widely replicated. One specific claim — that extrinsic rewards undermine intrinsic motivation (the overjustification / crowding-out effect) — is real but **conditional and historically contested**. The [Critical Analysis / Contested Findings](#critical-analysis--contested-findings) section represents that nuance honestly rather than overstating it. No study details below are fabricated; where a finding is debated, it is labeled as such.

## Core Thesis

People have three innate psychological needs — **autonomy, competence, and relatedness** — and any environment, relationship, or tool either _supports_ those needs (producing intrinsic motivation, vitality, and well-being) or _thwarts_ them (producing controlled motivation, disengagement, and ill-being). Crucially, motivation is not a single quantity to be maximized; it has a _quality_ that ranges along a continuum from purely controlled (external pressure) to fully self-determined (intrinsic). The design implication is sharp: a tool that does more _for_ the user is not automatically better — if it removes the user's experience of autonomy and competence, it can crowd out the very motivation and capability it claims to serve.

## Key Concepts

### The Three Basic Psychological Needs

SDT holds these as innate and universal; their satisfaction predicts well-being and high-quality motivation, their frustration predicts the opposite.

1. **Autonomy** — "being the perceived origin or source of one's own behavior"; the experience of _volition_, that one's actions express one's own values and choices. **Autonomy is not independence and not "do whatever you want"** — you can autonomously choose to follow an instruction. The opposite of autonomy is _heteronomous control_ (feeling pressured/coerced into acting). Source: [Ryan & Deci, SDT Encyclopedia (2022)](https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/2022_RyanDeci_SDT_Encyclopedia.pdf); [SDT official theory page](https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/the-theory/).

2. **Competence** — the need "to engage optimal challenges and experience mastery or effectance in the physical and social worlds." It concerns building skill, achieving effective outcomes, and the felt sense of growing capability. Optimal challenge is key: tasks too easy or too hard both frustrate competence. Source: [Wikipedia: SDT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-determination_theory); [PositivePsychology: SDT](https://positivepsychology.com/self-determination-theory/).

3. **Relatedness** — the need "to seek attachments and experience feelings of security, belongingness, and intimacy with others"; feeling connected to and cared for by others and a sense of belonging in a community. Source: [Wikipedia: SDT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-determination_theory).

When all three are met, "people tend to be more self-motivated, feel more satisfied, and experience greater well-being." ([APA: SDT overview](https://www.apa.org/research-practice/conduct-research/self-determination-theory).)

### Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation

- **Intrinsic motivation:** "motivation that is based on the satisfactions of behaving 'for its own sake'" — doing something because it is inherently interesting or enjoyable.
- **Extrinsic motivation:** "behavior that is instrumental — that aims toward outcomes extrinsic to the behavior itself" — doing something for a separable consequence (reward, avoiding punishment, approval).

Source: [SDT official theory page](https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/the-theory/).

### The Organismic Integration Continuum (extrinsic motivation is not monolithic)

This is SDT's sub-theory **Organismic Integration Theory (OIT)**, which "addresses the topic of extrinsic motivation in its various forms." Extrinsic motivation falls "along a continuum of internalization" — the degree to which an external regulation has been taken in and made one's own. From least to most self-determined:

| Regulation type            | What it feels like                                                                        | Locus             | Quality    |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------- | ---------- |
| **External regulation**    | "I do it because I'll be rewarded / punished."                                            | External          | Controlled |
| **Introjected regulation** | "I do it to avoid guilt/shame or to feel worthy." Internalized _pressure_, not yet owned. | Somewhat external | Controlled |
| **Identified regulation**  | "I do it because I value the goal it serves."                                             | Somewhat internal | Autonomous |
| **Integrated regulation**  | "I do it because it fits who I am." Fully assimilated to the self.                        | Internal          | Autonomous |
| **Intrinsic motivation**   | "I do it because it's inherently interesting/enjoyable."                                  | Internal          | Autonomous |

Source: [SDT official theory page](https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/the-theory/) (OIT subtypes: "external regulation, introjection, identification, and integration"). The design takeaway: the goal isn't always to reach _intrinsic_ — many necessary tasks aren't fun — but to move regulation toward the **autonomous** (identified/integrated) end and away from controlled (external/introjected).

### Cognitive Evaluation Theory: how rewards and feedback move the needle

SDT's sub-theory **Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET)** "specifically addresses the effects of social contexts on intrinsic motivation, or how factors such as rewards, interpersonal controls, and ego-involvements impact intrinsic motivation." Its central mechanism: every external event has a _controlling_ aspect and an _informational_ aspect.

- When an event is experienced as **controlling** (pressuring you to act a certain way), it shifts the perceived locus of causality outward and **diminishes** intrinsic motivation.
- When an event is experienced as **informational** (giving competence-relevant feedback under conditions of autonomy), it **enhances** intrinsic motivation.

Source: [Wikipedia: SDT, CET section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-determination_theory) — "when external rewards are controlling… they diminish internal motivation… when external motivations are informational and provide feedback about behaviors, they increase internal motivation."

### The Overjustification Effect / Crowding-Out (the agency-relevant finding)

The classic demonstration: **Deci (1971)** had subjects work on an inherently interesting Soma puzzle across three sessions, with a "free choice" observation period in each. The paid (experimental) group got $1 per puzzle on day 2; the control group never got paid. The paid group "spent significantly more time… playing the puzzle… on day 2 when they were paid but significantly less on day 3 when they were not paid" — i.e., introducing a tangible reward _reduced_ spontaneous interest once the reward was removed. ([Overjustification effect — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overjustification_effect).)

**Lepper, Greene & Nisbett (1973)** extended this to children: preschoolers who already enjoyed drawing were promised a "good player" award for drawing; those promised the reward later showed _less_ spontaneous drawing than children who got no reward or an unexpected one. ([Overjustification effect — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overjustification_effect).)

This has many names — Deci's "hidden costs of rewards," Lepper & Greene's "overjustification effect," and Bruno Frey's economics framing, the **"crowding-out effect"**: external interventions aimed at boosting extrinsic motivation can _diminish_ intrinsic motivation. ([overjustification meta-search summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overjustification_effect).)

**For tools and automation, this is the load-bearing extension:** if a _reward_ can crowd out the motivation to do an interesting task, an _automation_ that removes the interesting/effortful part of a task can erode both the motivation to do it _and_ the competence built by doing it. Automation is, in CET terms, a powerful controlling event — it relocates the locus of causality from the user to the system.

## The Agency-Preserving Automation Test

This is the gap audit's proposed sub-skill (`agency-preserving-automation-test`) made concrete: a **pre-build rubric** to run on any proposed automation, feature, or agent action _before_ writing it. The question SDT forces: does this automation increase the user's _experienced_ autonomy and competence, or does it outsource and erode them?

Run all six checks. **Any FAIL = stop and redesign** (see verdicts below).

### Need 1 — Autonomy (volition, not coercion)

- **Q1 (Initiation):** Does the user _choose_ to invoke this automation, or does it run _on_ them by default? → FAIL if the system acts without the user being the perceived origin of the action.
- **Q2 (Reversibility & override):** Can the user see what the automation did, edit it, and undo it? → FAIL if the output is a black box the user cannot inspect or override. (An override the user can't find is not an override.)

### Need 2 — Competence (mastery, not dependence)

- **Q3 (Skill trajectory):** After repeated use, is the user _more_ capable at this task, or _more dependent_ on the tool to do it at all? → FAIL if the tool's value depends on the user never learning the underlying skill.
- **Q4 (Optimal challenge preserved):** Does the automation remove drudgery (good) or does it remove the _productive struggle_ where competence is actually built (bad)? → FAIL if it automates the part of the task that is the user's own thinking/judgment.

### Need 3 — Relatedness (connection, not replacement)

- **Q5 (Human connection):** Does this automation support or substitute for a relationship/connection the user values? → FAIL if it inserts itself between the user and people in a way that displaces real connection. (For solo-productivity tools this often reads N/A — mark it N/A explicitly, don't skip it.)

### Crowding-out risk (the CET / overjustification check)

- **Q6 (Controlling vs informational):** Is this automation experienced as _controlling_ (it pressures, nags, or takes over) or _informational_ (it surfaces feedback and leaves the choice with the user)? Would removing the automation later leave the user _less_ willing/able to do the task than before it existed? → FAIL if the automation, once removed, would crater the user's own motivation or capability — that is crowding-out.

### Verdicts

- **PASS (all needs supported):** Build it. The automation removes drudgery, the user stays the origin and the editor, and competence is preserved or grown.
- **CONDITIONAL (one FAIL, mitigable):** Build with a guardrail — e.g., make it opt-in, add an inspect/edit/undo surface, add a "show your work" view that teaches rather than hides. Re-run the test on the mitigated design.
- **REFUSE / REDESIGN (FAILs Q3, Q4, or Q6):** Do not ship as designed. An automation that makes the user permanently dependent, automates their own judgment, or would crater their motivation if removed is _agency-degrading by construction_. Redesign so the human stays the thinker.

## Agent-Behavior Implications

How SDT changes what an AI agent **builds or refuses**:

- **Refuse "do it all for them" by default.** When asked to build a feature that fully removes a cognitively meaningful task from the user, the agent should flag the competence-erosion risk (Q3/Q4) and propose an autonomy-supportive alternative (assist, draft, suggest — with the user as editor) rather than silently automating.
- **Default to informational, not controlling.** Per CET, the agent should design outputs as _feedback the user can act on_ (informational) rather than actions taken _on_ the user (controlling). Suggestions over auto-applied changes. Drafts over silent commits.
- **Preserve the perceived locus of causality.** The user should remain the perceived author. The agent surfaces _what it would do_ and _why_, then lets the user confirm — so the resulting action is experienced as the user's choice (autonomy), not the system's.
- **Make every automation inspectable and reversible.** No black boxes (Q2). Show the work; allow edit and undo. This is also what keeps the action _informational_.
- **Target the autonomy end of the continuum, not maximal automation.** The agent's job is to help the user move from external/introjected regulation toward identified/integrated — i.e., help them connect the task to _their own_ goals — not to make the task disappear so completely the user never internalizes its value.
- **Name the crowding-out risk explicitly when it exists.** If a proposed feature would make the user less able or willing to do the task after the tool is removed, the agent should say so out loud and treat it as a design defect, not a feature.

## BuildOS Guardrails

BuildOS automates _planning and structure_: it takes a stream-of-consciousness brain dump and extracts projects, tasks, and context; it generates daily briefs; it offers agentic chat and durable agent runs that can create/edit projects and tasks. The SDT risk is precise and uncomfortable: **the single most valuable cognitive act BuildOS could accidentally outsource is the user's own thinking and prioritization.** If the user stops thinking because the system thinks for them, BuildOS would erode the exact competence ("turn messy thinking into structured work") it sells.

Concrete guardrails, mapped to the test:

1. **Brain dump stays the user's thinking (Q4).** Brain dump is the user's own cognition; BuildOS _structures_ it, it must not _replace_ it. The extraction should reflect the user's words back as structure they recognize and can correct — not generate plans the user never thought. The "conversation with oneself" frame (see the [Cultured Code / Werner Jainek analysis](./2026-04-29_werner-jainek-cultured-code_things-design-philosophy_analysis.md)) is the SDT-correct frame: the user is the thinker, the tool is the mirror.

2. **Daily brief is informational, not controlling (Q6).** The brief should _reflect the user's own commitments back to them_ as feedback they act on — not nag, guilt (introjected regulation!), or auto-reschedule their life. A brief that shames the user for incomplete tasks is a controlling event that pushes toward introjected/external regulation and away from autonomy. Reflect, don't pressure.

3. **Agentic chat and agent runs default to propose-then-commit (Q1/Q2/Q6).** The shipped commit-by-default + opt-in review and staged-mutation (stage → proposal*ready → commit) pattern is already SDT-aligned for \_write* operations: it keeps the user as the perceived origin and gives an inspect/edit/undo surface. Guardrail: keep the _review_ and _undo_ paths first-class and discoverable for any high-impact change; an undo the user can't find is not autonomy support. The `ChangeSetReview` surface is the autonomy-support mechanism — treat it as load-bearing, not optional polish.

4. **Show the work, build the skill (Q3).** When BuildOS structures a brain dump or proposes a plan, expose _why_ (which phrases became which tasks), so the user learns the structuring move and gets better at thinking-in-structure over time — rather than becoming dependent on BuildOS to think at all. Over months, a healthy BuildOS user should be a _better planner_, not a more dependent one.

5. **Resist "auto-everything" feature pressure (Q4/Q6).** BuildOS will be pulled toward auto-prioritization, auto-scheduling, and "just tell me what to do today" automation. Run each through this test. Auto-scheduling drudgery (calendar slotting) likely PASSES; auto-deciding _what matters to the user_ likely FAILS Q4 (it automates the user's own judgment) and should be assist-not-replace.

6. **Watch the crowding-out trap in marketing alignment.** The anti-AI / "lead with relief, not AI" positioning is, under SDT, also the _correct product stance_: relief from drudgery (competence-supporting) is the promise; relief from _thinking_ (competence-eroding) is the trap. Keep the product on the right side of that line.

## Critical Analysis / Contested Findings

**The need framework is robust; the overjustification claim is conditional.** The three-needs model and the broad finding that autonomy-supportive contexts produce better motivation/well-being are among the most replicated results in motivation psychology. The _overjustification / reward-undermining_ effect is real but **not universal**, and was the subject of a genuine, sharp academic dispute that any honest skill must represent:

- **Cameron & Pierce (1994)** published a meta-analysis arguing the undermining effect was limited and overstated — that under many conditions rewards do _not_ harm (and can help) intrinsic motivation. This was a serious empirical challenge, not a footnote. ([Overjustification effect — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overjustification_effect).)
- **Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999)**, _Psychological Bulletin_ 125(6):627–668, responded with their own meta-analysis (128 studies) concluding the effect is real but **conditional on reward type**. The honest, replicated synthesis is:
    - **Undermines intrinsic motivation:** _tangible_, _expected_, and _task-engagement/completion/performance-contingent_ rewards (especially for already-interesting tasks).
    - **Does NOT undermine (and can support):** _verbal praise / positive informational feedback_, _unexpected_ rewards, and _task-noncontingent_ rewards. Rewards for uninteresting tasks, or for outperforming others, can even increase it.
    - Source: [Overjustification effect — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overjustification_effect); [Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999 (cited via search)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overjustification_effect).

- **The alternative-explanation critique:** Critics noted the post-reward drop "may be explained by a negative reaction when the reward was withheld" rather than a genuine loss of underlying interest — a measurement/ecological-validity challenge. Treat the magnitude of real-world crowding-out as uncertain, not settled.

**Implication for the skill — do not overclaim.** The test above leans on the _robust direction_ (controlling > erodes; informational > supports) and the _most-replicated condition_ (it's the **controlling, dependence-creating** character of an intervention that does the damage, not "any help is bad"). That distinction is exactly why the test centers on **controlling-vs-informational** and **dependence-vs-skill-growth** rather than a blanket "automation is bad." Verbal/informational support is _good_ under SDT; it's the locus-of-causality shift that's the risk. A skill that says "never automate" would misread SDT as badly as one that says "automate everything."

**Other limits:** SDT's universality claim (the three needs are innate and cross-cultural) is debated at the margins (relatively more contested across individualist/collectivist contexts, though SDT researchers argue autonomy-as-volition still holds). And "autonomy" in SDT is volition, _not_ the libertarian "more options = more freedom" — more configurability can actually _reduce_ experienced autonomy (this is the calm/opinionated-software counter-school's correct point, audit Gap #4). The test must not be misread as "always give the user more knobs."
