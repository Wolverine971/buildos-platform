## Core Thesis

Writing a viral short-form script is not a creative act first. It is a research-into-simplification act, then a rhythm act. Kane's process: take a raw idea you barely understand, do the research live until you find the one common plot line that will fit in 60-90 seconds, then write the middle first, then the hook, then the ending — iterating line by line for cadence (the "dance"), aggressive simplification of language, and ruthless cutting of every adjacent idea that doesn't serve the chosen plot line.

For BuildOS, the translation is: an agent helping a founder write short-form content should not start by drafting copy. It should walk the founder through a research-and-narrowing loop, force a single-plot-line decision, and only then write — with the agent re-reading the working draft aloud after every change so cadence is felt, not assumed.

## Operating Lessons

### 1. Start without understanding the topic — research is part of writing

Kane begins the video saying he literally has not read the source tweet, has not watched the linked TED Talk, and does not yet know what the company does. He researches _while_ writing. This is intentional: the writer's confusion is a proxy for the viewer's confusion, and the moments of "oh, that's the interesting thing" become the actual hooks.

Agent behavior:

- Do not require the founder to "have it figured out" before opening the script doc.
- Treat early confusion as data, not as a blocker.
- Capture the moment the founder's eyebrows go up ("damn, that'd be insane") — that is almost always the line.

### 2. Validate that there is a "there" there before you write

Before writing anything, Kane checks signal: tweet engagement, follower count of the source, prior viral context (his own South Park video). He decides whether the seed is worth pursuing. If a small account got 264K views on a tweet, there's a real underlying interest signal even if execution was bad.

Agent behavior:

- Ask: what's the proof this idea will resonate? (engagement on source, prior similar wins, friend tagged me, gut + receipts)
- If the founder cannot point to _any_ external signal, push back before writing.

### 3. Simplify by climbing layers of source material

Original tweet was too complex. Kane goes to a Forbes article that already simplified it. Then he simplifies further. The pattern: each source layer strips complexity; the script must strip more.

Agent behavior:

- Pull 2-3 sources at increasing levels of simplification.
- Identify the words the founder is using that are still too "heady" (Kane: "the simulation" is too woo-woo, rename it "AI Netflix").
- Keep renaming entities until a non-expert teenager would track it.

### 4. Pick ONE plot line — kill everything adjacent

This is the single most important decision in the video. Kane has many possible angles:

- Their own AI shows
- The simulation tech
- Past South Park demo
- Future Hollywood IP deals
- Fans creating new episodes

He explicitly says: "you can't answer everything in these shorts… create one common plot line and just hammer it." He picks: prior South Park demo → now interactive shows → fans generate episodes → business strategy take. Everything else gets cut, even though it's interesting.

Agent behavior:

- After research, force the founder to write down 4-6 possible plot lines.
- Ask: "which one of these is the most retellable in 90 seconds, and which one carries the strongest emotional payoff?"
- Reject combination plot lines. Pick one.
- Allow at most one "tease" reference to a cut angle near the end.

### 5. Write the middle first

Counter to most advice. Kane says he usually starts with beginning and ending, but for this one the middle came to him first because that's where his inspired lines landed during research. He writes those lines down raw, then engineers a hook that earns them and an ending that lands them.

Agent behavior:

- Capture inspired phrases verbatim as they emerge during research.
- Ask the founder: "what's the single best line you've said out loud in the last 5 minutes?" Drop it in the middle.
- Build hook and ending around the strongest middle line, not the other way around.

### 6. Layer the founder's POV on top of the news

Kane explicitly: "what's happening, then I give my take of like the business strategy angle." The video is news + me giving my perspective. That's why founders working alone can compete with news accounts: the take is the moat.

Agent behavior:

- After the "what is it" section, prompt: "what's your actual hot take on the business or strategy here?"
- Encourage taking a side. "It's a win-win for the IP holders." "This is going to be massive." Conviction reads as authority.

### 7. Treat each sentence as a beat in a dance

The most quoted craft principle. Kane: "When you're writing these videos it's kind of like a dance. You don't want every beat every sound every instrument to be on the same cadence." He varies sentence length intentionally — long sentence, then short interjection ("it's crazy"), then long again — like syncopation in music.

Agent behavior:

- After draft, mark each line by word count.
- Flag any 4+ consecutive lines of similar length.
- Insert short 2-3 word interjections ("it's crazy", "picture this", "well now") to create breathing room.
- Read every revision out loud. If it doesn't dance, it doesn't work.

### 8. Simplify language ruthlessly — every word audited

When Kane wrote "they created a generator," he stopped and said: "they created a generator… it's like, why am I using that extra complex word." Replaced with "this AI platform that generated new South Park episodes." Each pass, he interrogates one word at a time.

Agent behavior:

- After each draft, run a "complex word scan."
- Replace any word the founder wouldn't say in casual conversation.
- Replace abstract entity names with concrete renamings (the simulation → AI Netflix; their prototype platform → "their prototype using AI to recreate South Park").

### 9. Iterate by speaking, not by typing

Kane is verbalizing the script as he writes — that's why the video is 48 minutes for a 60-second script. He says a line, hears the rhythm, says it again with one word changed, says it again. Voice-first iteration is the actual mechanism.

Agent behavior:

- Encourage the founder to read the working script aloud after every meaningful edit.
- The agent should generate revisions in batches of 3 micro-variants and ask the founder to read all 3 to pick the best-feeling one.

### 10. Cut for time even when it hurts

Toward the end Kane realizes the draft is too long for 90 seconds. He cuts the "but it's coming as soon as one show creator lets it happen" line, cuts most of the "imagine an AI Netflix streaming platform" expansion, cuts a "thousands of new scenes" digression. He explicitly trades depth for fit.

Agent behavior:

- Estimate read time at ~150 wpm.
- If draft is over 90 seconds, force cuts before publishing.
- Cut from the "expansion" sections first, never the hook or the take.

### 11. The hook is the last thing you finalize

Kane spends most of the middle section writing the explanation. Only after the explanation is solid does he finalize the hook, because the hook has to _promise_ the explanation. He iterates the hook 8-10 times: "this company is building the AI version of Netflix where users can create their own episodes" → "where fans can create their own episodes" → "where fans can watch shows and create their own episodes."

Agent behavior:

- Don't lock the hook until the middle is real.
- Generate 5-8 hook variants once the explanation is set.
- Test each by asking: "if a viewer hears this hook, do they know exactly what they're going to learn in the next 60 seconds?"

### 12. Reference your own prior wins as social proof

Kane deliberately ties this video to his prior viral South Park video: "I know the video I made before went viral, I want to tie that in here because I think by showing it it gives social proof and social credibility." Reusing your own catalog as proof-of-authority is a multiplier.

Agent behavior:

- Ask the founder: "have you posted anything related to this topic before that performed?"
- If yes, weave it into the hook or first 5 seconds as a credibility anchor.

### 13. Lie a little about timing if it sounds better

Kane: "I'll probably say six months ago regardless of when it is just because that sounds better." (The actual prior video was July 25, ~16 months earlier.) Final script says "last year" — close enough, more rhythmically clean.

Agent behavior:

- Surface this tradeoff explicitly. "Six months ago" vs "last year" vs "a while back" — pick the one that lands rhythmically, not the one that's literally accurate to the day.
- Never invent claims, but allow for soft temporal smoothing.

### 14. The ending carries the verdict

Kane's chosen ending isn't a CTA. It's a verdict on what's coming: "This is going to be massive and could completely change Hollywood." The ending = the founder's gut prediction stated with conviction.

Agent behavior:

- Generate 3 ending variants: prediction, question, callback.
- Default to prediction unless the founder hates being declarative.
- Conviction beats hedging in short form. Cut "I think" and "maybe" from endings.

### 15. The "win-win frame" earns the take

Kane's POV section is structured as a deliberate frame: "it's a win-win for the original show." Then he enumerates: passive revenue, IP booster, fan engagement. This isn't free-flow opinion — it's a structured argument compressed into 4-5 sentences. The structure makes the take feel earned instead of preachy.

Agent behavior:

- After capturing the founder's hot take, ask: "in 3 bullets, why is this true?"
- Compress those 3 bullets into 3 sentences in the script.
- The take earns its airtime by being structured, not by being loud.

### 16. Surface the obvious objection and dismiss it

Kane explicitly addresses the counterargument: "the only downside would be if you think the AI episodes would dilute the original brand, but if all new episodes are contained on one AI Netflix platform then you still own premium distribution everywhere else." Naming the objection inoculates against it and signals confidence.

Agent behavior:

- Ask: "what's the strongest pushback someone could give to this?"
- Write a one-sentence dismissal that doesn't dismiss the concern but contains it.
- Place this just before the verdict ending.

### 17. Reading time vs character count vs feel

Kane never counts words or characters. He uses page length ("more than one page is too long") and time-on-clock ("longer than 90 seconds, need to cut"). The unit of measurement is _what the page looks like_ and _what the read aloud sounds like_, not formal metrics.

Agent behavior:

- Display read time prominently as the founder edits.
- Use page-length visual cues, not word counts.
- A quiet "you're at 1:42, target is 1:30" is more useful than "your draft is 287 words."

## Frameworks Or Templates

### Kane's Live Scriptwriting Process (extracted from this 48-min walkthrough)

**Phase 1: Source intake (0:00-6:07, ~6 min)**

1. Open the seed (tweet, link, screenshot from notes app).
2. Skim once, react out loud — capture confusion and "wait, what?" moments.
3. Validate signal: source engagement, prior viral hits in the same theme.
4. Pull 1-2 secondary sources (article, founder interview) to simplify.
5. Identify the _most viral angle_ among 3-6 possible plot lines.
6. Lock the single plot line. Verbally name what's getting cut.

**Phase 2: Middle first (6:07-13:55, ~8 min)**

1. Write down inspired lines verbatim as they come during research.
2. Draft the explanation: "here's what's crazy" + concrete examples (South Park, Pokemon, Rick and Morty).
3. Add the founder POV / business take after the explanation.
4. Don't worry about transitions yet.

**Phase 3: Hook (13:55-26:30, ~13 min)**

1. Open with subject + simplification: "This company is building the AI version of Netflix where fans can watch shows and create their own episodes."
2. Test rhythm: subject sentence, then short interjection ("it's crazy"), then setup.
3. Anchor with prior credibility: "Last year I made a video about their prototype…"
4. Iterate hook 8-10 times, reading aloud each pass.

**Phase 4: Iterate and pace (26:30-41:33, ~15 min)**

1. Read full draft aloud start to end.
2. Mark lines that don't dance — too long, too similar, too jargon.
3. Replace abstract entities with concrete renamings (the simulation → AI Netflix).
4. Add bridge phrases ("picture this", "well now they're back", "if you think about it").
5. Cut adjacent ideas creeping back in.

**Phase 5: Ending (41:33-47:22, ~6 min)**

1. State the verdict with conviction.
2. Optional: tease one of the cut angles ("there's so much more here including their 10 original shows").
3. Drop on a prediction line: "this could completely change Hollywood."

**Phase 6: Final read + cut for time (last pass)**

1. Time the read aloud against 90 seconds.
2. Cut expansions first, never the hook or the take.
3. Lock and record.

### Sentence cadence rule (the "dance")

Pattern that recurs in the final script:

- LONG (15-20 words: subject + simplification)
- SHORT (2-3 words: "it's crazy")
- LONG (the explanation)
- SHORT-MEDIUM ("well now they're back")
- LONG (the payoff scenario)
- SHORT ("how insane is that")
- LONG (the take)
- SHORT-MEDIUM (verdict)

### Word-by-word audit checklist

Run on every draft:

- [ ] Any word the founder wouldn't say in casual speech? → swap.
- [ ] Any abstract entity name? → replace with concrete renaming.
- [ ] Any sentence over 25 words? → break or cut.
- [ ] 4+ similar-length sentences in a row? → insert a short beat.
- [ ] Any "I think / maybe / probably" in the ending? → cut for conviction.
- [ ] Read time over 90 seconds? → cut from expansions.

### Hook iteration log (extracted from this video)

Kane's hook went through this sequence — useful as a template for an agent prompting iteration:

1. "This company is building the AI version of Netflix where users can create their own episodes."
2. "...where fans can create their own episodes."
3. "...where fans can create their own episodes in minutes." (cut)
4. "...where fans can watch shows and create their own episodes."
5. "...where fans can both watch H — it's getting…"
6. "This company is building the next generation of Netflix" (rejected as vague)
7. "...where fans can watch shows and create their own episodes." (locked)
8. (For final cut) "This company's building the AI version of Netflix." (no clause — cleanest)

Pattern: each iteration either adds a concrete verb pair ("watch shows AND create their own"), or strips a clause. The final aired version is the _shortest_ — Kane trusts that the next line ("Now last year I made a video…") will earn the explanation, so the hook doesn't have to do the explaining.

Kane verbally walked through and rejected each adjacent angle:

All you had to do is type in an idea for a South Park episode and their showrunner platform will do the rest. It autocreated the characters, the scenes, the plots, the voices, everything. And that video went nuclear. So nuclear that every movie studio in Hollywood reached out to them trying to make a deal. Well now they're back, and they're working on interactive TV shows. Now of course the only way this will be allowed is if the original show creators give the go-ahead, and they'll only do that if they're getting a revenue split from this new showrunner platform. But I promise you it's coming, 'cuz if you think about it, it's a win-win for the original show. - Cut "where fans can watch shows and create their own episodes" → just "AI version of Netflix" in the hook (lighter).
- Cut "it's crazy" interjection from hook (replaced with "Now last year").
- Kept "and that video went nuclear. So nuclear that every movie studio…" (the rhythm Kane explicitly engineered).
- Cut "imagine a world where AI Netflix creates a streaming platform just for fan episodes" expansion.
- Cut "this deepens fandom and creates a bigger cult following" line.
- Cut "but it's coming as soon as one show creator lets it happen, it will open the floodgates."
- Replaced "this new AI Netflix" with "this new showrunner platform" in one spot (more accurate, similar rhythm).
- Tightened ending from "this is going to be massive and completely change the animated show industry" to "this is going to be massive and could completely change Hollywood."

## Anti-Patterns

- **Trying to cover every interesting angle.** Kane: "there's just too many ideas to cover in 60 seconds." Picking 2 plot lines = the video flops. Pick one.
- **Writing the hook first when you don't know the explanation.** The hook has to promise something the rest of the video delivers. If the middle isn't real, the hook is a lie.
- **Using the company's official language.** "The simulation" is what the company calls itself. Kane renames it "AI Netflix" because the official name fails the teenager test.
- **Reading the draft silently.** Kane verbalizes constantly. Silent revision = bad rhythm.
- **All sentences the same length.** The "dance" requires variance. Punchy-punchy-punchy = monotone. Long-long-long = sleepy.
- **Hedging in the ending.** "I think this might be cool" kills conviction. "This is going to be massive" carries.
- **Assuming the founder needs to "be ready" before writing.** Confusion at the start of research is part of the process, not a blocker.
- **Using the founder's vocabulary unedited.** Founders default to their domain words ("ontology", "context engineering"). Audit every word as if a teenager is listening.
- **Defending word count.** If it doesn't fit in 90 seconds, cut. Period.
- **Treating the script as static after draft 1.** Kane iterates 8-10 times on the hook alone.

## Open Questions / Gaps

1. **How does Kane choose source seeds?** The video starts with "a friend tagged me on Twitter, I put it in my notes." We don't see his upstream curation system. Worth a separate analysis on his idea-mining workflow.
2. **What's the exact editing pass?** Kane says he records the script then edits in Premiere. The video stops at script lock — there's no coverage of b-roll selection, captions, pacing in post.
3. **How does this scale to non-news content?** Kane's example is a news drop with a clear external trigger. How does the same process work for evergreen / educational / personal-story content where there's no "tweet to react to"?
4. **What does Kane do when the rhythm just won't come?** He doesn't show a stuck state in this video. A real BuildOS agent will need to handle "I rewrote this 12 times and it still feels flat" — that recovery loop isn't documented here.
5. **How does he validate before posting?** In the video he just decides "this is good, let's record." No A/B test, no hook test, no preview audience. Is that always the case, or did he skip it for the demo?
6. **Founder voice vs Kane voice.** Kane has a specific cadence ("it's crazy", "well now they're back", "picture this") that's _his_. How much of the cadence framework transfers to a different founder's voice? An agent must learn the founder's natural interjections instead of forcing Kane's.
7. **Tool support.** Kane mentions Sandcastles (his AI tool that condenses this 50-minute process into 60 seconds). What does that tool actually do, and what would BuildOS's equivalent agent surface look like for a founder writing TikTok scripts in the brain dump flow?

**Translation to BuildOS agent behavior (summary):**

A BuildOS agent walking a founder through scriptwriting should run the founder through Kane's six phases — not generate a script in one shot. Specifically: (1) demand a source seed and validate signal, (2) pull simplification layers and force a single plot line, (3) write the middle from inspired lines captured during research, (4) iterate the hook 5-10 times with the founder reading aloud each version, (5) iterate cadence by varying sentence length and inserting short beats, (6) lock the ending as a conviction statement, (7) cut for 90-second fit.
