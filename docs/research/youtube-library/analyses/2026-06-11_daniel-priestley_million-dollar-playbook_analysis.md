## Core Thesis

A business is built in five sequenced steps: (1) productize a **proof story** into intellectual property with two products (a product for prospects + a core offering), (2) earn **attention** through the noticed → known → rated ladder, (3) convert attention into **signals of interest** via a four-element landing page, (4) convert signals into **sales** managed on a LAPS dashboard (Leads → Appointments → Presentations → Sales), and (5) **scale** by computing allowable cost per sale / per lead and ratcheting paid promotion while staying inside those numbers. Every step has a numeric threshold or a measurable conversion, which is why the library calls this the **KPI playbook**.

Two governing maxims: _"Your entire business is downstream from attention"_ and _"All business is downstream of lead generation."_

## Operating Lessons

### Step 1 — Product with proof

**Proof story criteria (all five required):**

1. Happened in the **last 5 years**.
2. You did **something special**.
3. It is of interest to a specific **ICP** (ideal customer persona).
4. The outcome is **quantifiable**.
5. The outcome has a **name**, and you can explain how you got it **step by step**.

The proof story _is_ the intellectual property: "It's your story with your emotions, with your steps, with your take on things." The name you give the outcome can be registered and tied to the business.

**Productize the IP into exactly two products:**

- **Product for prospects** — where people _learn_ the IP: webinar, online assessment, mini-course. Build spec: it needs an **online landing page**.
- **Core offering** — where people _get the IP delivered_: agency (done-for-you), software, physical product, or consulting. Build spec: it needs a **slide deck or brochure** that explains what gets delivered and how much it costs.

**Worked examples:**

- Priestley himself: outcome named "Key Person of Influence," a 5-step methodology → book → global accelerator (Dent).
- **Ronley Vaz:** restaurant ~$500k in debt, but a successful food podcast = proof story → audio marketing agency. First major clients signed within **30 days**; debt paid down within **6 months** with **$20k+ cash** in the bank after.

### Step 2 — Attention: the noticed → know → rated KPI ladder

> "In school they told you do not be an attention seeker. But in today's economy you must get attention. Your entire business is downstream from attention."

**Noticed (short-form):**

- Threshold: _"People notice you for the first time when they see you for the 11th time."_
- Window: the 11 impressions must occur **within 90 days** or the counter effectively resets.
- Cadence math: weekly posting = noticed at best every 90 days, and one missed post breaks it. **Daily posting = new people notice you every ~2 weeks.** Heroes you follow post 5–10x/day to be noticed more often.
- Format: short punchy posts, image carousels, short video — anything feed-skimmable.

**Known (long-form):**

- Threshold: **2–7 hours** of accessible long-form content.
- Formats ranked in the video: podcast guest appearances (called out as some of the best), ~1,500-word reports/long LinkedIn posts, long video, live workshops/keynotes, Zoom recordings, and a **book** ("there is a reason authors end up with fans all over the world").
- The mechanic: short-form gets them to notice; long-form must be _waiting_ for them when they want depth.

**Rated (association):**

- "Rated" = what people assume your rates are. Four bands: **discount, mass-market, niche, luxury**.
- Mechanic: people rate you by seeing you **aligned to 4 other brands**. (Rolls-Royce + private jet + Four Seasons + Dom Pérignon → luxury rating and luxury price assumption.)
- Decision rule: deliberately choose the 4 associations to match the band you want to be in. If you don't want to be a discount brand, don't let discount-brand cues accumulate.
- Worked example: **Annie Filipova** — ramped short-form + long-form + named the big brands she had worked with → one of the most influential LinkedIn voices in Greece.
- Priestley's own diagnosis: he had heavy long-form, weak short-form → "I wasn't getting noticed by enough people." He chopped existing long-form into short-form and "my following exploded" — noticed → known → rated, in order.

### Step 3 — Signals of interest: the landing page

A signal of interest = a person taking an action that says "I want to know more." The device is a landing page with **four elements in order**:

1. **Hook** — two preferred forms:
    - **Frustration hook:** "Are you frustrated that [outcome] isn't happening?" — names the frustration of _lacking your IP's outcome_.
    - **Readiness hook:** "Are you ready to [X, Y, Z result]?" — e.g. "Are you ready to launch your YouTube channel?"
2. **Value proposition** — what you can solve, improve, or measure; better/faster/cheaper/more emotional benefit than they currently know how to get.
3. **Credibility** — your story, background, headshot + bio, or research/statistics that back the value proposition. (Placed after the value prop because "when you explain a value proposition, people have doubts.")
4. **Call to action** — the button + form = the signal = the lead. _"All business is downstream of lead generation."_ Named CTA options: **start the assessment, register for the workshop/webinar, join the waiting list, join a discussion group.** Each routes the prospect into the product for prospects.

**Worked example:** **David Fraser / Bunky Life** (prefab cabins in Canada). Readiness-hook landing page — "Are You Ready for the Bunky Life?" — built on ScoreApp → "thousands and thousands of leads."

Tooling note: Priestley pitches **ScoreApp** (his product) — 150+ signal-of-interest templates plus a mini-CRM for lead-to-sale tracking; export to a Google Sheet LAPS dashboard or a CRM at scale.

### Step 4 — Sales conversions: the LAPS dashboard

**LAPS = Leads → Appointments → Presentations → Sales.** The presentation is what moves a prospect from product-for-prospects to core offering: collect the lead, book the appointment, present the education/insight needed to understand the core offering, then **ask for the business**.

- Worked baseline: **100 leads → 15 appointments → 10 presentations → 3 sales** ("11,5 10 3" conversions in his shorthand: 100/15/10/3).
- Operating cadence: the **"perfect repeatable week"** — review the dashboard weekly and brainstorm one improvement per stage: more leads, higher lead→appointment rate (15 → 20), higher show/presentation rate, higher close rate (3 → 5).
- Decision rule: the entrepreneur's recurring job _is_ improving the four LAPS conversion ratios week after week — not inventing new funnels.

### Step 5 — Scale up: allowable-cost math and paid promotion

The gate to spending money is two computed numbers:

1. **Allowable cost per sale (ACPS)** = core-offer price × the % you're willing to spend to acquire a sale.
    - Worked example: $5,000 offer, happy to spend 15% → **ACPS = $750**.
2. **Allowable cost per lead (ACPL)** = ACPS × lead→sale conversion (from the LAPS dashboard).
    - Worked example: 3 sales per 100 leads → ACPL = $750 × 3/100 = **$22.50**. (At 1/100 it would be $7.50.)
    - Benchmark: $22.50/lead is achievable on Instagram/Facebook/LinkedIn; "if you've got good content online, if you've got good intellectual property, you'll see that you can generate leads for **well under $10 per lead**."

**Decision rule:** _"Paid promotions only work when you know your allowable cost per sale and your allowable cost per lead and you can track the money you're paying back to your allowable cost per sale."_ As long as actual CPL ≤ ACPL, keep spending.

**Ratchet protocol:** spend what you can afford this month (e.g. $10k), verify ACPL and ACPS are holding, then step up: $12k → $15k → $20k — re-verifying at each step before increasing.

**Paid promotion menu (4 categories):**

- **Ads:** Google, Facebook/Meta, LinkedIn, X.
- **Joint ventures:** pay influencers or organizations to promote you.
- **Paid outreach:** pay for calls, DMs, or placement in someone's email campaign.
- **Sponsorships:** events, newsletters, YouTube channels.

## Failure Modes

- **Long-form without short-form:** Priestley's own pre-2023 mistake — deep content nobody noticed because the 11-impressions-in-90-days threshold was never crossed.
- **Posting weekly:** technically clears the 90-day window with zero margin; one missed post resets the prospect's counter.
- **Unmanaged rating:** if you don't choose your 4 brand associations, the market rates you by accident — often as a discount brand.
- **Landing page without a hook or with a vague CTA:** no signal of interest, no lead, nothing for the LAPS dashboard to measure.
- **Selling without a dashboard:** if you can't state your 100/15/10/3 (or whatever your numbers are), you can't compute ACPL and therefore cannot safely buy traffic.
- **Paid promotion before the math:** spending on ads without ACPS/ACPL is the explicit "only works when" violation — scaling losses instead of sales.
- **One product instead of two:** trying to sell the core offering directly with no product for prospects forces strangers to make a high-trust decision with no learning step.
