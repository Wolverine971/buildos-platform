## Core Thesis

The gap between an amateur marketing site and a professional one is small and mechanical, not a matter of taste or talent. Most amateur sites suffer from the same recurring section-level mistakes — cramped headlines, oversized navs, static screenshots where demo videos belong, generic templates, and graphics that fight the message. The pro fix is almost always a combination of (a) giving the hero room to breathe, (b) writing copy that names a specific customer and benefit, and (c) replacing decorative clutter with one strong visual asset and one strong piece of social proof.

## Per-Section Operating Lessons

### 1. Hero / Landing Section

**Amateur tells:**

- The hero graphic (often a static product screenshot) eats ~60% of the section, leaving the headline cramped.
- The logo and nav menu are visually larger than they need to be and compete with the headline.
- Empty whitespace sits where social proof should live.
- Copy describes what the product _is_ rather than the benefit it delivers.

**Pro tactics:**

- Center everything around the main headline so visitors know the problem being solved within the first second.
- Use a video or interactive demo instead of a still screenshot — "most software companies opt for a video or interactive demo instead of still images."
- Drop a small company-logo strip or testimonial line in the empty space below the buttons.
- Push the hero graphic _below_ the headline, peeking in from the bottom of the frame, with a play-button affordance.

**Decision rule:** The hero exists to answer "what problem does this solve, for whom, and what's the proof" — in that order. If your hero has a 60/40 split with the graphic winning, you have an amateur hero.

**Memorable quote:** _"Everything is centered around the main headline so visitors know immediately what problem they're solving."_

### 2. Headline Copy

**Amateur tells:**

- Vague taglines that could fit any company.
- Headlines that describe features instead of outcomes.
- No mention of who the product is for.

**Pro tactics:**

- Rewrite the headline to lead with the main benefit, not the product mechanism.
- Name the ideal customer somewhere in the hero ("we're an agency for creators").
- Use a display/quirky font for the headline at large sizes, then switch to a normal sans-serif for the subhead — display fonts collapse at smaller sizes.
- Set headline line-height to 95%–100% (not the default), and weight up to bold/black.

**Decision rule:** A pro headline can be screenshotted alone and a stranger will know (a) what the company does and (b) who it's for. If yours can't, rewrite.

**Memorable quote:** _"A lot of the times we forget that visitors don't know what we know about our business so we have to tell them exactly how we can help them."_

### 3. Navigation Bar

**Amateur tells:**

- Logo and nav items oversized.
- ALL CAPS menu items competing for attention with the headline.
- Too many links, including superfluous ones.
- Logo placed in two locations (nav + hero block).

**Pro tactics:**

- Make the nav smaller than you think — it should never compete with the headline.
- Drop ALL CAPS in favor of sentence/title case.
- Remove superfluous links; keep only what users will actually use.
- Make the final menu item a CTA button styled in the same family as the hero's primary CTA.
- If the brand has a tagline, decide whether it lives in the nav _or_ the hero — never both.
- Optional: add a subtle glass effect (white background at 25% opacity + background blur) for a modern feel.

**Decision rule:** Nav is supporting cast, not a co-star. If users can't ignore it while reading the headline, it's too loud.

### 4. Hero Imagery

**Amateur tells:**

- Static screenshots for software products.
- Logos or text plopped onto full-bleed photos, fighting the subject.
- Generic stock imagery that doesn't signal the niche.

**Pro tactics:**

- **For software:** video demo or interactive demo with a visible play button.
- **For personal brands:** big, face-forward photo — "for personal brands, a big face helps people know who you are and what you're like really quickly."
- **For full-bleed photo heroes:** add a directional dark gradient over the image (blend into a natural dark area like a punching bag or shadow) so headline copy reads cleanly without obscuring the subject.
- **For service/portfolio:** off-kilter rows of niche-specific image frames (Polaroid for wedding photographer; dot-grid + floating case-study cards for SaaS agency).

**Decision rule:** Choose the imagery format that signals the niche before a word is read. A wedding photographer's hero should not be reusable for a gym.

**Memorable quote:** _"Using a template is okay if you don't have design experience… but they're designed to be generic. I can see this layout also being used for a gym or a restaurant or a lot of different kinds of businesses."_

### 5. Social Proof

**Amateur tells:**

- Empty whitespace below the hero buttons.
- Testimonials buried far below the fold.
- No logos, no ratings, no numbers.

**Pro tactics:**

- Drop a logo strip directly under the hero CTAs.
- Use a ratings row (stars + count) under the buttons — "social proof in the first section of your website does increase conversion rates."
- For agency/portfolio sites: float case-study image cards around the hero with metric badge overlays (e.g., "+200K followers") so social proof and visual interest solve the same problem.

**Decision rule:** If the first 600px of your page contains zero proof signals, you are asking visitors to trust you on faith.

### 6. Benefit Section (Below the Fold Tease)

**Amateur tells:**

- Hard cutoff between hero and the next section.
- Benefits dumped in a generic 3-column grid with no visual hook.

**Pro tactics:**

- Tease the benefit section by letting three frames peek into the bottom of the hero, with a gradient transitioning from white into the next section's background.
- Each frame uses the same shadow recipe as case-study tiles (light drop shadow + heavier shadow as a soft border).
- Pair each benefit with a metric callout or icon — never just a paragraph.

**Decision rule:** The hero should imply more is coming. A flat horizontal line between hero and benefits wastes the scroll signal.

### 7. Case Study / Portfolio Tiles

**Amateur tells:**

- Plain text list of clients.
- Same template gallery layout that could fit any business.

**Pro tactics:**

- Image cards with rounded corners, a light drop shadow, and a heavier drop shadow stacked on top to act as a soft border.
- Float small metric badges (icon + number + tiny label) over the cards to combine social proof with visual interest.
- Use niche-specific framing (Polaroid frame: 18px top/sides, 72px bottom + cursive caption) when the niche has a visual vocabulary.
- Captions in a deliberately near-illegible cursive can add personality without distracting — "our brains naturally read from left to right and they read bigger bolder things first, so we can get this cool handwritten effect without detracting from our headline."

**Decision rule:** Case study tiles do double duty — they're proof _and_ design texture. Strip either function and you're back to amateur.

### 8. CTA Buttons

**Amateur tells:**

- One flat, brightly-colored button.
- No visual hierarchy between primary and secondary actions.
- Default text size and weight.

**Pro tactics:**

- Primary CTA: thicker text, slightly smaller than default, gradient fill (e.g., blue gradient), white text, rounded border, drop shadow.
- Add an inner white highlight from the top + dark shadow on the outside from the bottom — "that extra bit of realism that makes you want to click it."
- Secondary CTA placed beside the primary, either deep-color contrast variant or white-with-colored-border outline.
- Pick the CTA color from a highlight inside the hero image (e.g., orange CTA matching highlights in the photo) for color cohesion.
- Set 48px gap between button frames; group them with auto-layout.
- Optional: secondary CTA can be a popup trigger instead of a second button — "I've had a lot of success with popups."

**Decision rule:** Two buttons, two visual weights. Same shape, different colors. If your buttons look identical, the user can't tell which action you want.

### 9. Background / Texture

**Amateur tells:**

- Flat white background.
- Default template gradients.
- Decorative shapes that compete with content.

**Pro tactics:**

- Subtle dot grid behind the main hero container, masked by an oval shape so the grid fades rather than ending abruptly.
- Two large ovals with low opacity + 150 layer blur, one orange, one blue — creates a soft light gradient that complements case-study graphics without dominating.
- Off-white backgrounds for warmth ("brings out the white wedding dresses") or matched to the brand's existing color palette.
- For software: cool grid background as a subtle hero accent.

**Decision rule:** Texture is for adding depth, not personality. If your background draws the eye, it's wrong.

### 10. FAQ / Features / Demo / Comparison / Contact (extrapolated patterns)

The video does not deeply walk these as separate sections, but the same operating principles apply:

- **FAQ:** Even though the video does not feature an FAQ, the cross-cutting principle holds — every section is an opportunity to sell, not just inform. Don't let any section devolve into pure operational text.
- **Features:** Use the case-study tile recipe (rounded corners + stacked shadows + metric callouts) instead of a 3-column grid of icons + paragraphs.
- **Demo:** Use the same play-button-on-video pattern from the hero — never a static screenshot in a feature row.
- **Comparison:** Apply the "name your customer" principle — the comparison should make clear who _you_ are for, not just who you're not.
- **Contact:** The final menu item should already be the contact CTA. The contact section itself should mirror the hero's primary CTA styling, not introduce a new button design.
- **Testimonial:** Pair the quote with a face photo or company logo at minimum. Treat it as social proof real estate, not a decorative quote block.

## Failure Modes (Cross-Cutting)

Things to never do in marketing-site design:

- **Don't let the hero graphic eat 60% of the hero section** — headline-first, graphic-second.
- **Don't ship with a static screenshot** when the product is software — use a video/interactive demo with a play affordance.
- **Don't use ALL CAPS nav links** — they compete for visual attention with the headline.
- **Don't reuse a generic template** that could plausibly belong to a gym, a restaurant, _and_ a wedding photographer — a pro site signals its niche before any text is read.
- **Don't leave whitespace in the hero where social proof should live** — logos, ratings, or testimonial line.
- **Don't put your logo in two places** (nav + hero block). Pick one.
- **Don't ship one flat CTA button with no secondary** — pros always show primary + secondary side by side.
- **Don't drop text on a full-bleed photo without a directional gradient** — overlay must blend into a natural dark region of the image.
- **Don't use a display font for body text or subtitles** — display fonts collapse at small sizes; switch to a clean sans-serif.
- **Don't end backgrounds abruptly** — if you use a dot grid or texture, mask it with an oval/gradient so it fades.
- **Don't ignore color cohesion** — pull CTA colors from highlights already in your hero imagery.
- **Don't write a headline that doesn't name your customer** — the visitor should feel addressed, not pitched.

## BuildOS Application

Specific audits and pro tactics to apply to the BuildOS marketing/landing page:

- **Audit the hero graphic ratio.** If our hero screenshot or animation is taking >50% of vertical space, the headline is being crowded. Pull it below the headline, let it peek in from the bottom of the frame, and add a play affordance if it's video.
- **Replace any static screenshots with the existing brain-dump demo video.** The pro pattern is unambiguous: software heroes ship a demo, not a still. If the demo is too heavy, an interactive carousel or animated GIF beats a screenshot.
- **Add a social-proof strip directly under the hero CTAs.** Even a faint line of "Used by [N] thinkers" or a logo row from any inbound press / podcast appearances is better than empty whitespace. The pro pattern: proof appears in the first 600px.
- **Audit the nav for ALL CAPS, oversized logo, and link count.** Inkprint nav should be smaller than the headline by a clear visual margin. Final menu item should be a CTA button styled like the hero primary CTA.
- **Pull CTA colors from hero imagery for cohesion.** If the hero photo / illustration has a warm accent, the CTA should echo it — don't introduce a third brand color in the CTA.
- **Tease the next section.** Let the first benefits or "how it works" frames peek into the bottom of the hero with a gradient transition. The hard horizontal section break is an amateur signal.
- **Name the BuildOS customer in the hero copy.** Per the strategy doc, the audience is "people making complex things" / authors + YouTubers as primary wedge. The hero headline should put that customer name in the page (or in the subhead) — not just talk about "thinking environments" abstractly.
- **For testimonials and case studies, use the floating-tile + metric-badge pattern.** Don't ship a flat 3-card grid. Cards with rounded corners, light shadow + heavier shadow border, and metric callouts ("X projects organized," "Y hours saved per week") double as social proof and visual texture.

## Skill Draft Inputs

Section-by-section heuristics to feed into `marketing-site-design-review` and crossover with `landing-page-scorecard-funnel`:

### For `marketing-site-design-review` skill

For each section the agent reviews, run these checks:

1. **Hero ratio check:** Is the headline visually dominant over the graphic, or vice versa? Pro = headline-dominant.
2. **Hero asset format check:** Is the graphic a static screenshot? If yes and the product is software, flag for upgrade to video/interactive demo.
3. **Customer-naming check:** Does the headline or subhead name the ideal customer ("we're an X for Y people")? If no, flag.
4. **Nav noise check:** Logo + nav competing with headline? ALL CAPS? More than 5 links? Final link not a CTA button? Each is a flag.
5. **Social-proof-in-hero check:** Are logos, ratings, or testimonials visible in the first 600px? If no, flag.
6. **CTA hierarchy check:** Primary + secondary side by side? Two distinct visual weights? Same shape? If no, flag.
7. **Background fade check:** Does any texture/grid end abruptly, or is it masked? Abrupt end is a flag.
8. **Section-break tease check:** Does the next section peek into the hero, or is there a hard horizontal break? Hard break is a flag.
9. **Niche-specificity check:** Could this layout plausibly belong to a different industry? If yes, flag for niche-specific framing.
10. **Image overlay check:** If the hero is a full-bleed photo, is there a directional gradient blending copy area into a natural dark region? If no, flag.

### For `landing-page-scorecard-funnel` crossover

Score each section out of 10 along these axes:

- **Clarity:** Does the headline state what + for whom + the benefit?
- **Hierarchy:** Is the visual weight ordered headline → CTA → graphic → nav → social proof?
- **Proof density:** Logos, ratings, testimonials, metric callouts in the first viewport?
- **Action affordance:** Primary CTA visually distinct from secondary? Both styled the same way (gradient, shadow, inner highlight)?
- **Niche signaling:** Could a stranger guess the industry from layout alone within 1 second?
- **Texture discipline:** Backgrounds adding depth without competing?
- **Section flow:** Does each section tease the next, or is there a hard break?

A landing page scoring <7/10 average across sections is amateur-grade and should be rewritten section by section using the per-section pro tactics above.
