## Core Thesis

Research is not a luxury, a delaying tactic, or specialist work. It's a daily habit, a pre-condition for design, and something every team can do enough of to make better decisions. "Just enough" research means right-sized research that informs decisions without becoming its own bottleneck.

## Hall's Four Research Modes (canonical)

1. **Organizational research** — what does the team / organization actually believe, and where do those beliefs come from? Internal research before external research; you can't design for a market you don't agree about.
2. **User research** — who are the users, what are they trying to do, and what's getting in their way? Includes both generative (discovery) and evaluative (testing) modes.
3. **Topical / domain research** — what's already known about this problem space? Existing research, competitive landscape, prior art. Hall pushes hard against "we'll figure it out from first principles" hubris.
4. **Evaluative research** — does the design we built actually work? The Krug-territory of usability testing.

## Key Principles

- **Research is a verb, not a department.** "Anyone can do research." It's a posture, not a function.
- **Bias is unavoidable; it must be surfaced and managed.** Hall's chapter on cognitive bias is the strongest argument for structured methods even in lightweight research. Methods are anti-bias scaffolding.
- **The research question is the artifact.** Most failed research starts with a vague "let's talk to users" goal. A real research question — answerable, actionable — is the first deliverable.
- **Hire for hypothesis-testing, not validation.** A team that runs research to validate a decision already made is wasting the budget.
- **Conversations beat surveys.** Surveys give you what people say they think; conversations give you why. Hall is explicitly skeptical of survey-only research.
- **Just enough means just enough — not minimum, not maximum.** Right-sizing is its own skill; under-research and over-research are equally common failures.

## Hall's Method Catalog (widely cited)

- **Stakeholder interviews** before any user research; understand the internal landscape first
- **Contextual inquiry** — observe users in their actual environment, not in a lab
- **Semi-structured interviews** with open-ended questions and active listening
- **Comparative review / competitive teardown** — what does the existing landscape teach us?
- **Diary studies** for behavior over time (especially for habit / lifecycle work)
- **Card sorting** for IA decisions (open vs closed sort)
- **Heuristic evaluation** before usability testing — fix the obvious before paying for user time
- **Usability testing** — the Krug 3-user, monthly cadence

## What Hall Says That Differs from Other Voices

- **Against the "deliver an insight report" output.** Hall argues research should produce decisions and design changes, not document artifacts. A report nobody acts on is failure.
- **Against the "research team" silo.** Research as a craft owned by every designer, PM, and developer outperforms research as a specialist function bottlenecked behind a researcher.
- **Against "personas" as default.** Hall is skeptical of personas done badly (cf. Cooper "Defending Personas"). When in doubt, use real customer quotes from real interviews.
- **Pro speed.** Light, fast, repeatable research beats heavy, slow, occasional research.

## Application to BuildOS

- **Research as habit.** A solo-founder can run lightweight discovery weekly: 2 customer interviews, 1 stakeholder check-in (with self / advisors), and a small competitive teardown. Two hours a week beats one quarterly two-day sprint.
- **Anti-validation discipline.** Before any "user interview" for BuildOS, write down what we already believe; then ask questions that could disprove it.
- **The research question artifact.** Don't run brain-dump-experience interviews for "general feedback." Run them for one specific question (e.g., "what does relief look like when a brain-dump finishes?").
- **Pair with Krug for evaluation, with Cooper for archetypes, with Maurya for switching forces** (PRODUCT_STRATEGY index). Hall is the discovery / generative side; Krug is the evaluative side.
