# Karri Saarinen's 10 Rules for Crafting Products That Stand Out

## 1. Commit to Quality at the Leadership Level

Leadership must demonstrate that craft is the top priority, creating organizational permission to build quality into everything. Without a clear implementation plan and business rationale, quality becomes merely aspirational. Saarinen observed how design elevated Coinbase's approachability in cryptocurrency, illustrating how craft matters when pursuing mainstream success.

## 2. When It Comes to Building Teams, Go Small and Aim High

> "The more people you have, the more opinions and deliberation you introduce, which dilutes the quality."

Small, high-performing teams execute better work faster than large ones. Success requires hiring craft-oriented people who already demonstrate excellence — measuring quality by setting standards rather than output metrics.

## 3. Do Away with Handoff

Connected teams eliminate silos where product managers decide, designers visualize, and engineers build separately. When everyone shares responsibility for quality and understands implementation details, teams achieve higher standards. There should be no "handoff to dev" — accountability remains continuous.

## 4. Resist Creating Specialized Product Teams

Artificial product team structures create quality inconsistencies across the organization. Rather than assigning people to search permanently, rotating responsibilities keeps ideas fresh.

> "Users don't care about your org chart — they care about the experience"

## 5. Consider the Spec Your Baseline Minimum Viable Product, Not Your Goal

> "A door is a door, and if it opens, it technically functions. It's easy to meet the spec. It's harder to do the craft."

Excellence demands exceeding specifications. Treat requirements as starting points, not finish lines, investing extra care that competitors overlook.

## 6. Quality Is Not Perfection

All details must meet quality standards before public release, but perfection isn't required initially. Beta users help refine rough products toward polish. Success metrics remain qualitative — Saarinen measures success by "seeing Linear in organic conversations about quality solutions."

## 7. The Best Design Is Opinionated

Products designed for everyone become mediocre everywhere. Specific, purposeful design serves intended users better than flexible, general approaches.

> "Products need a clear value proposition to win a market and users' trust"

This requires deliberate choices about who benefits most.

## 8. The Simplest Way to Increase Quality Is to Reduce Scope

Teams struggling with quality typically attempt too much simultaneously. Building partial features exceptionally well, then iterating based on feedback, maintains standards better than rushing everything.

> "Quality isn't binary — it's about continuously refining a product to meet a standard."

## 9. Don't Get Locked into One Way of Doing Things

Linear establishes values and principles rather than rigid processes, empowering teams to think critically. Direct user access through shared Slack channels connects makers to real feedback. Teams receive responsibility and decision-making freedom within defined quality standards.

## 10. Data Can Be a Crutch

> "To design with craft, you must develop and trust your intuition."

Linear avoids A/B testing and data-driven decisions, instead relying on team expertise and user conversations. Mistakes become learning experiments.

> "You must surprise users. You can't expect data — or even people themselves — to tell you how."
