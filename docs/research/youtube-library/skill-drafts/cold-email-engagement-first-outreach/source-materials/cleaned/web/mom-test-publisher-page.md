# The Mom Test Publisher Page

Source URL: <https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/The-Mom-Test/Rob-Fitzpatrick/9798893312560>

Raw artifact policy: no raw HTML stored; this card preserves the useful extraction and source URL.

## Use In Cold Email Architecture

Strengthens customer-research outreach and OfferLab by clarifying how to avoid false positives when outreach asks for feedback, discovery, or market insight.

## Source Notes

- Author: Rob Fitzpatrick.
- Publisher/distributor page: Simon & Schuster.
- Note: page references a revised and expanded edition; use it for official publisher positioning, not as a substitute for the full book.

## Cleaned Extract

- Customer conversations can mislead when people give compliments, opinions, or hypothetical future behavior.
- Better questions uncover real problems, actual evidence, and false positives.
- Discovery should validate without pitching or selling too early.
- The cold email version is a research ask that makes the conversation about the recipient's real workflow, not the sender's idea.

## Cold Email Implications

- Customer-research mode should not ask "would you use this?" in the first email.
- OfferLab should collect evidence from past behavior, existing spend, current workaround, urgency, and recent switching attempts.
- Taste review should penalize research emails that secretly pitch.
- Reply OS should tag compliments as weak evidence unless paired with behavior or commitment.

## What Should Enter The Skill

- Real-problem evidence over compliments.
- Past behavior over future hypotheticals.
- Discovery ask separated from sales ask.
- False-positive warning for customer-research outreach.

## What Should Not Enter The Skill

- Treating positive sentiment as validation.
- Hiding a sales pitch inside a research request.
- Asking recipients to design the offer for us.
