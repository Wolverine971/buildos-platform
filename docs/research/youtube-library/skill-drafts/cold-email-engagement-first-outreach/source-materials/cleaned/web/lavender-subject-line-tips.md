## Cleaned Extract

### Length

- **"One to three is best. Two is optimal."**
- Going from a two-word subject line to a four-word subject line **reduces replies by 17.5%**.

### Negative deltas (what to avoid, with the page's numbers)

| Element in subject   | Effect (as stated on page)                 |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Question             | "lower your open rate by 56%"              |
| Numbers              | "reduces your chance of an open by 46.33%" |
| Punctuation (? or !) | "reduces the likelihood of an open by 36%" |
| Not using title case | "can reduce your opens by 30%"             |
| First name           | "12% fewer replies" (Salesloft data)       |
| 2 → 4 words          | "reduces replies by 17.5%"                 |

Also avoid: emojis, commands ("improve," "respond"), superlatives ("better," "improved"), clichés ("15 minutes?", "Quick Question").

### Positive guidance

- Neutral sentiment gets the most replies (per Salesloft research).
- "Make it sound like an internal email."
- Boring, descriptive, title-cased, 1–3 words.

### Good examples from the article

"Template Revisions" · "Reply Rate Question" · "Personalization Issue" · "Rep Writing" · "Ramp" · "Email Data Thoughts"

### Caveats

- Open-rate deltas (questions, numbers, punctuation, title case) measure **opens**; the length and first-name deltas measure **replies** — don't mix the units when citing.
- Title-case finding (−30% opens without it) conflicts with the casual-founder lowercase register used by some practitioners (e.g. Shepherd); resolve by mode register, not by data.
- Mixed attribution (Lavender + Salesloft) and no stated sample sizes per claim — keep directional-vendor.
