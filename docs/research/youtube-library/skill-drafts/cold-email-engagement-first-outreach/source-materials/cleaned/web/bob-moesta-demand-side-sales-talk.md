# Bob Moesta Demand-Side Sales 101 Talk

## Use In Cold Email Architecture

Strengthens ICP, signal design, OfferLab, and research anchors by shifting outreach from seller funnel language to the buyer's progress timeline.

## Source Notes

- Creator: Bob Moesta.
- Venue/source: Business of Software talk with transcript.
- Trust level: high for Jobs-to-be-Done and demand-side sales.

## Cleaned Extract

- Demand starts from a struggling moment. Cold outreach is strongest when it names a real moment of friction or switching energy.
- Sellers tend to think in supply-side categories: product, features, benefits, demo, funnel. Buyers think in demand-side context: what changed, what progress they want, what alternatives and anxieties are in the way.
- The buyer has a timeline, not just a sales stage. Outreach should not treat "not ready" as a failure; it should identify whether the recipient is passive looking, active looking, deciding, onboarding, or building habit.
- Questions can create space for a solution. The cold email version is a bridge that makes the recipient recognize a relevant tradeoff before the offer appears.
- Context creates value and contrast creates meaning. A strong cold email contrasts current approach, desired progress, and why this artifact/offer is useful now.

## Cold Email Implications

- Right moment should be defined as a likely struggling moment, not just a demographic trigger.
- Right reason should connect the signal to the buyer's progress timeline.
- OfferLab should frame artifacts as help making progress, not a seller-centered pitch.
- Reply OS should treat "not now" as timeline data and route it into nurture or recycle, not pressure.

## What Should Enter The Skill

- Struggling moment detection.
- Buyer timeline language.
- Progress-oriented offer framing.
- Questions that help the buyer orient, not questions that force a meeting.

## What Should Not Enter The Skill

- Sales funnel language as the main buyer frame.
- Feature-first copy.
- Treating every reply as a close opportunity.
