## System-Level Synthesis

The cleaned corpus reinforces the master outreach flow: right person -> right moment -> right reason -> right offer -> right ask -> right follow-up. The sources split cleanly into four jobs:

- Deliverability and compliance define whether sending is allowed and technically plausible.
- Compiler, taste, and sequence sources define how the message should feel in an inbox.
- ICP, offer, and learning sources define whether the campaign deserves to exist.
- Specialty-mode sources define how standards change for investors, recruiting, PR/podcast, and founder/creator outreach.

## Deliverability And Compliance

- Treat authentication, consent/identity, complaint rate, bounce handling, and unsubscribe mechanics as launch gates rather than copy preferences.
- Google, Yahoo, Microsoft, FTC, ICO, and CRTC are governing sources; vendor deliverability guides are useful for explanation and diagnosis, not policy authority.
- Operational recommendations based on provider/legal pages must be rechecked before use because sender requirements and compliance rules change.

## Compiler, Taste, And Sequence

- The compiler should package subject, preview, first line, proof, offer, CTA, and follow-up as one unit; weak packaging wastes otherwise good research.
- Vendor benchmarks are directional. Use them to generate hypotheses, then validate through campaign-specific replies, positive sentiment, bounces, complaints, and qualified conversations.
- Taste rules should penalize fake intimacy, meeting-first pressure, bloated proof, template smell, and copy that sounds more important to the sender than to the recipient.

## Specialty Modes

- Investor mode should stay factual, short, and disciplined; the evidence package matters more than persuasion language.
- Recruiting mode must be candidate-centered and avoid treating people like pipeline objects.
- PR and podcast mode should protect the recipient's audience: relevance to their beat/show is the offer, not the sender's desire for exposure.
