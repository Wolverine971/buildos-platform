# Operation American Independence: Complete Campaign Outline

> **Perspective**: This document outlines the Revolutionary War as if George Washington were organizing it in BuildOS - from his appointment as Commander-in-Chief (June 1775) through his resignation (December 1783).
>
> **Purpose**: Ensure the example project captures everything a historian would expect when viewing Washington's campaign.
>
> **Last Updated**: December 20, 2024 (Expanded Version)

## What Would a Historian Look For?

A serious historian examining "Washington's Revolutionary War Campaign" would expect to see:

### 1. **The Full Strategic Picture**

- Not just battles, but the political, diplomatic, economic, and social dimensions
- The constant struggle to keep the army fed, clothed, and paid
- Relations with Congress, state governments, and foreign powers
- Intelligence operations and counterintelligence
- The home front and civilian support

### 2. **The Human Element**

- Key personnel decisions (promotions, dismissals, assignments)
- Managing difficult subordinates (Charles Lee, Horatio Gates, Benedict Arnold)
- Building a command team
- Dealing with mutinies and desertion
- The role of his personal staff (aides-de-camp)

### 3. **The Evolution of Strategy**

- Learning from mistakes
- Adapting to circumstances
- The shift from conventional to Fabian warfare
- Integration of French forces

### 4. **The Complete Operational Picture**

- All major theaters (not just where Washington was)
- The war in the South
- The frontier war
- Naval operations
- The siege warfare

## Complete Goals Structure (Washington's Perspective)

### GOAL 1: Preserve and Build an Effective Continental Army

_"Without a respectable Army, we are nothing."_

This is Washington's most fundamental goal - everything else depends on it.

**Sub-Goals:**

- Maintain army in being (survival)
- Transform militia into professionals
- Establish officer corps
- Solve logistics crisis
- Manage pay and morale
- Inoculate against smallpox (revolutionary medical decision)

**Key Milestones:**

1. Take command at Cambridge (Jul 3, 1775)
2. Survive New York Campaign (Nov 1776)
3. Victory at Trenton restores morale (Dec 26, 1776)
4. Victory at Princeton (Jan 3, 1777)
5. Order mass smallpox inoculation (Feb 5-6, 1777)
6. Army survives Valley Forge (Winter 1777-78)
7. Steuben professionalizes army (Spring 1778)
8. Battle of Monmouth proves transformation (Jun 28, 1778)
9. Suppress Pennsylvania Line Mutiny (Jan 1781)
10. Suppress New Jersey Line Mutiny (Jan 1781)

### GOAL 2: Secure Foreign Military Alliance and Support

_"France is the power upon which we must depend."_

**Sub-Goals:**

- Prove American military viability to France
- Secure treaty of alliance
- Coordinate with French forces
- Obtain loans and supplies

**Key Milestones:**

1. Demonstrate capability at Trenton/Princeton (Dec-Jan 1776-77)
2. Victory at Saratoga proves viability (Oct 17, 1777)
3. Treaty of Alliance signed (Feb 6, 1778)
4. French fleet arrives (Jul 1778)
5. Failed Newport operation (Aug 1778) - lessons learned
6. French army arrives at Newport (Jul 1780)
7. Wethersfield Conference - plan Yorktown (May 1781)
8. Combined victory at Yorktown (Oct 19, 1781)

### GOAL 3: Defeat British Forces in the Field

_"We must take the field and achieve decisive victory."_

**Sub-Goals:**

- Expel British from Boston
- Defend the Middle Colonies
- Recover the South
- Win decisive battle to end war

**Key Campaigns/Milestones:**

**Boston Campaign (1775-1776):**

1. Siege of Boston (Apr 1775 - Mar 1776)
2. Knox Artillery Train (Winter 1775-76) - 60 tons, 300 miles
3. British evacuation of Boston (Mar 17, 1776)

**New York Campaign (1776):**

1. Battle of Long Island (Aug 27, 1776) - Near disaster, 2,000 casualties
2. Miraculous evacuation from Long Island (Aug 29-30, 1776) - 9,000 troops saved overnight
3. British capture New York City (Sep 15, 1776)
4. Fort Washington captured (Nov 16, 1776) - 2,800 prisoners
5. Retreat across New Jersey (Nov-Dec 1776)

**New Jersey Campaign (1776-1777):**

1. Crossing of the Delaware (Dec 25-26, 1776)
2. Battle of Trenton (Dec 26, 1776) - 900 Hessians captured
3. Battle of Princeton (Jan 3, 1777) - Marines present

**Philadelphia Campaign (1777):**

1. Battle of Brandywine (Sep 11, 1777) - British flanking, Lafayette wounded, 1,000+ American casualties
2. Paoli Massacre (Sep 20, 1777) - Wayne's troops surprised
3. British capture Philadelphia (Sep 26, 1777)
4. Battle of Germantown (Oct 4, 1777) - Bold counterattack fails in fog

**Saratoga Campaign (under Gates, but strategic importance):**

1. Bennington (Aug 16, 1777)
2. First Saratoga (Sep 19, 1777) - Freeman's Farm
3. Second Saratoga (Oct 7, 1777) - Bemis Heights, Arnold wounded
4. Burgoyne's surrender (Oct 17, 1777) - 5,900 troops

**Stalemate Period (1778-1780):**

1. Battle of Monmouth (Jun 28, 1778) - Army proves itself, Charles Lee disgraced
2. Stony Point raid (Jul 16, 1779) - Wayne's bayonet assault

**Southern Theater (Washington's strategic responsibility):**

1. Fall of Charleston (May 12, 1780) - DISASTER, 5,000 captured
2. Camden (Aug 16, 1780) - DISASTER under Gates
3. Kings Mountain (Oct 7, 1780) - Turning point, Ferguson killed
4. Appoint Greene to command South (Dec 1780)
5. Cowpens (Jan 17, 1781) - Morgan's masterpiece, 868 British casualties
6. Guilford Courthouse (Mar 15, 1781) - Cornwallis loses 25% of army
7. Greene's "winning by losing" campaign
8. Eutaw Springs (Sep 8, 1781)

**Yorktown Campaign (1781):**

1. Strategic deception (Aug 1781) - Fooled Clinton about New York attack
2. March to Virginia (Aug-Sep 1781) - 600-mile secret march
3. Battle of the Chesapeake (Sep 5, 1781) - French fleet victory
4. Siege of Yorktown (Sep 28 - Oct 19, 1781)
5. Assault on Redoubts 9 and 10 (Oct 14, 1781) - Hamilton leads bayonet charge
6. British surrender (Oct 19, 1781) - 7,000 troops

### GOAL 6: Win the Intelligence War

_"The necessity of procuring good intelligence is apparent and need not be further urged."_

Washington was a master spymaster. This is often overlooked.

**Sub-Goals:**

- Establish spy networks
- Conduct counterintelligence
- Deceive the enemy
- Protect operational security

**Key Milestones:**

1. Nathan Hale mission and execution (Sep 22, 1776) - Lesson learned: use civilians
2. Appoint Benjamin Tallmadge as intelligence director (Nov 1778)
3. Establish Culper Ring (1778)
    - Key members: Abraham Woodhull (Culper Sr.), Robert Townsend (Culper Jr.), Anna Strong, Caleb Brewster
    - Headquarters: Long Island and New York City
    - Code system: Washington = 711, New York = 727
4. Culper Ring prevents Clinton's attack on French at Newport (1780)
5. Detect Arnold's treason (Sep 1780)
6. Major John André captured (Sep 23, 1780)
7. André executed (Oct 2, 1780)
8. Yorktown deception operation (Aug 1781) - Convinced Clinton attack was on New York

**Intelligence Methods:**

- Invisible ink ("sympathetic stain")
- Coded letters and numerical aliases
- Clothesline signals (Anna Strong)
- Double agents
- Deliberate misinformation

### GOAL 7: Manage Logistics and Supply

_"An army marches on its stomach."_

This was Washington's constant nightmare.

**Sub-Goals:**

- Feed the army
- Clothe the army
- Arm the army
- Pay the army (or manage without pay)
- Establish supply chains
- Protect against disease

**Key Milestones:**

1. Appoint Thomas Mifflin as Quartermaster (Aug 1775)
2. Order mass smallpox inoculation (Feb 5-6, 1777)
    - Reduced mortality from 50%+ to less than 2%
    - First mass immunization in American history
    - "May have saved the Revolution"
3. Valley Forge supply crisis (Winter 1777-78)
4. Appoint Nathanael Greene as Quartermaster (Mar 1778)
5. French supply shipments arrive (1778-1781)
6. Robert Morris becomes Superintendent of Finance (1781)

**The Smallpox Decision - Detail:**

- Washington initially prohibited inoculation (May 1776)
- Reversed decision after seeing disease devastation
- Ordered Dr. William Shippen Jr. to inoculate all Philadelphia recruits (Feb 6, 1777)
- Used winter timing deliberately - recovery before spring campaigns
- Kept operation secret to prevent British exploitation
- Result: Disease controlled, army strength preserved

## Risk Register

What kept Washington awake at night:

### Military Risks

1. **British naval superiority** - CONSTANT, mitigated by French alliance
2. **Encirclement of army** - Long Island, nearly realized
3. **Destruction in pitched battle** - Avoided through Fabian strategy
4. **Loss of key cities** - NYC lost, Philadelphia temporary
5. **Southern collapse** - Charleston, Camden

### Personnel Risks

1. **Expiring enlistments** - Crisis every winter, especially Dec 31, 1776
2. **Desertion** - Constant problem
3. **Mutiny** - Pennsylvania Line (Jan 1-10, 1781), New Jersey Line (Jan 20, 1781)
4. **Officer incompetence** - Charles Lee at Monmouth
5. **Treason** - Benedict Arnold (Sep 1780)

### Logistics Risks

1. **Starvation** - Valley Forge, Morristown
2. **Lack of clothing** - Barefoot at Valley Forge
3. **Ammunition shortage** - Constant
4. **No pay** - Years without pay
5. **Disease** - Smallpox (mitigated by inoculation)

2. **States fail to provide troops** - Constant
3. **States fail to provide money** - Constant
4. **Peace faction gains power** - 1782
5. **Conway Cabal** - Attempt to replace Washington (1777-78)

## The Mutinies: A Crisis of Morale (January 1781)

### Pennsylvania Line Mutiny (Jan 1-10, 1781)

**Cause:** Unpaid wages, expired enlistments, deplorable conditions (no food, clothing, shelter)

**Key Facts:**

- ~1,500 soldiers of 2,400-man Pennsylvania Line mutinied
- Marched from Jockey Hollow near Morristown
- Board of Sergeants negotiated with state representatives
- Only one casualty: Captain Adam Bitting

**Resolution:**

- General Anthony Wayne led negotiations
- ~1,250 infantrymen and 67 artillerymen discharged
- Congress struggled to fund settlement
- Relatively bloodless outcome

### New Jersey Line Mutiny (Jan 20, 1781)

**Washington's Response:** DIFFERENT from Pennsylvania

**Key Facts:**

- Washington ordered military suppression, not negotiation
- Major General Robert Howe commanded suppression force
- Feared "contagion of rebellion" spreading

**Significance:**

- Revealed fundamental morale crisis
- Army held together by ideology, not pay
- Demonstrated Washington's limits of patience

## Benedict Arnold: From Hero to Traitor

### The Heroic Phase (1775-1778)

- Fort Ticonderoga capture (May 10, 1775)
- Quebec expedition (1775-76) - Wounded in leg
- Battle of Valcour Island (Oct 1776)
- Battle of Saratoga (Oct 1777) - Wounded in same leg, decisive contribution
- One of Washington's most capable battlefield commanders

### The Grievances (1778-1779)

- Passed over for promotions multiple times
- Investigated by Congress for expenses
- Court-martialed 1779 (minimal punishment)
- Growing debt and resentment
- Married Peggy Shippen (1778) - Connection to British

### The Treason (1779-1780)

- **May 1779:** Begins secret correspondence with Major John André
- **August 1780:** Appointed commander of West Point
- **Plan:** Surrender West Point for £20,000 and British commission
- **September 21, 1780:** Secret meeting with André at Joshua Hett Smith's house
- **September 23, 1780:** André captured by three militiamen with incriminating documents
- **September 25, 1780:** Arnold flees to HMS Vulture

### The Consequences

- **October 2, 1780:** Major John André executed as spy
- Arnold commissioned as British Brigadier General
- Led British raids against former countrymen
- Washington: "Treason of the blackest dye was yesterday discovered!"
- West Point preserved - critical strategic position saved

## Key Battles Deep Dive

### Battle of Cowpens (January 17, 1781)

**Commander:** Brigadier General Daniel Morgan
**Opposition:** Lieutenant Colonel Banastre Tarleton

**The Setup (Morgan's Genius):**

- ~1,900 Americans vs ~1,100 British/Loyalists
- Three-line defensive formation
- Militia given LIMITED objective: fire two volleys, then withdraw
- Cavalry hidden behind ridgeline

**The Execution:**

- Militia sharpshooters killed 15 officers in minutes
- Planned "retreat" deceived Tarleton into thinking rout
- Cavalry emerged to strike right flank
- Reformed militia struck left flank
- Double envelopment achieved

**Casualties:**

- British: 110 killed, 200 wounded, 500 captured (868 total - 86% of force)
- American: 12 killed, 60 wounded

**Significance:**

- Destroyed Tarleton's Legion
- One of most tactically perfect battles in military history
- Morgan: "I have given [Tarleton] a devil of a whipping"

### Battle of Guilford Courthouse (March 15, 1781)

**Commander:** Major General Nathanael Greene
**Opposition:** General Lord Cornwallis

**Forces:**

- American: ~4,440 men
- British: ~2,000-2,100 men

**Greene's Defensive Lines (following Morgan's example):**

1. North Carolina militia with artillery - fire two volleys, fall back
2. Virginia militia - receive retreating NC militia
3. Continental regulars with artillery - final defense

**Outcome:**

- British tactical victory - held the field
- British casualties: 25-27% of force (~500+ killed/wounded)
- Cornwallis's army crippled

**Charles James Fox (House of Commons):** "Another such victory would ruin the British army."

**Strategic Result:** Cornwallis forced to Wilmington, then Virginia → Yorktown

### Battle of Kings Mountain (October 7, 1780)

**Opposition:** Major Patrick Ferguson (only British regular present) vs Overmountain Men

**Forces:**

- British/Loyalist: ~1,100 militia
- American: ~1,400 frontier riflemen

**The Battle:**

- Ferguson threatened to "march over the mountains, hang their leaders"
- Americans surrounded hilltop position
- Ferguson killed by riflemen while attempting breakout

**Casualties:**

- Loyalist: 157-200 killed, 160 wounded, 700 captured
- American: 28 killed, 62 wounded

**Significance:**

- Thomas Jefferson: "The turn of the tide of success"
- Destroyed British Loyalist strategy
- Forced Cornwallis to retreat from Charlotte
- Led directly to Cowpens, Guilford Courthouse, Yorktown

### Battle of Monmouth (June 28, 1778)

**Significance:** Proved Valley Forge transformation

**Context:**

- Army just left Valley Forge after Steuben's training
- British evacuating Philadelphia, retreating to New York

**The Crisis:**

- Charles Lee ordered to attack British rearguard
- Lee retreated without authorization when faced with Cornwallis's counterattack
- Washington confronted Lee on battlefield: "You ought not to have undertaken it unless you intended to go through with it"

**Washington's Response:**

- Rallied retreating troops
- Organized new defensive line
- Fought British to standstill

**Casualties:**

- British: 147 killed, 170 wounded, 64 missing
- American: 106 killed, 161 wounded, 95 missing

**Aftermath:**

- Charles Lee court-martialed (Jul 4 - Aug 12, 1778)
- Suspended from command for one year
- Never held field command again
