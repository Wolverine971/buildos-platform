# YouTuber Archetype Research - 2026-07-02

This folder expands the research base for `docs/marketing/brand/worldbuilding/ARCHETYPE_YOUTUBER.md`.

The existing archetype file was not edited. These notes are raw material for a later pass on the archetype map.

## What This Research Is For

The current YouTuber archetype is already pointed in the right direction: a solo or tiny-team creator with ideas everywhere and no durable pipeline. The research here tries to make that world feel more native to YouTube creators by grounding it in the language they use, the places they gather, the tools they buy, the stories they repeat, and the anxieties they carry.

The central finding: YouTubers do not only struggle with "making content." They struggle with keeping a living channel memory while the feed pressures them to turn every idea into an upload before the idea has matured.

## Iteration Ladder

### Iteration 1 - Broad Scan

Sources scanned:

- Official YouTube Help and Creator documentation.
- Reddit communities including `r/NewTubers`, `r/PartneredYoutube`, `r/Creator`, `r/CreatorServices`, and `r/VideoEditing`.
- Creator education and operator sources such as Creator Science, TubeBuddy, and Colin and Samir.
- Creator economy reporting from The Guardian, Axios, Vogue Business, Business Insider, and IAB coverage.
- Academic papers on YouTube comments, creator earnings, affiliate disclosure, accessible editing, and generative AI video workflows.
- Existing BuildOS docs and Reddit community profiles already in the repo.

Broad read:

- Creators speak in production language: hook, script, title, thumbnail, retention, AVD, CTR, impressions, rough cut, B-roll, Shorts, long-form, sponsor, clipper.
- Creators also speak in folklore: 100 videos, the algorithm, 1 of 10, 10 of 10, first monetized month, video that wakes up months later, the six-month gap, the comeback.
- The official YouTube docs are much less mystical than creator discourse. YouTube frames recommendations as matching viewers with videos, not creators fighting an algorithm god.
- The strongest emotional language comes from burnout essays and creator forums: constant output mode, input mode, hamster wheel, the fear of disappearing, the dashboard as a mirror.

### Iteration 2 - Language, Pain, Cast

After the broad scan, the high-signal cluster was not "growth hacks." It was the operating gap between:

- The craft world: ideas, research, point of view, story, trust, audience promise.
- The platform world: packaging, retention, upload cadence, CTR, impressions, monetization, policy, analytics.
- The business world: sponsors, editors, thumbnails, clips, taxes, disclosures, audience diversification.

This produced deeper documents on:

- Vocabulary and name concepts.
- Terms creators currently struggle with and terms BuildOS can redefine.
- Burnout, the idea graveyard, cringe first videos, plateaus, pivots, and comeback arcs.
- Cast, settings, objects, collaborators, villains, and background institutions.

### Iteration 3 - BuildOS Synthesis

The most useful BuildOS reframes:

- Consistency is not cadence. It is pipeline health.
- An idea bank is not a notes dump. It is an idea maturity system.
- Analytics are diagnostics, not identity.
- The first 30 seconds start in the idea bank, not in the edit.
- A break is not channel death. It is an audience warmup problem.
- AI is strongest when it helps preserve context and handoff work, weakest when it looks like inauthentic slop.
- The comeback arc is not "post more." It is "return with a different operating rhythm."

## High-Signal Findings

1. YouTube creators distrust advice that sounds abstract, AI-first, or outsider-coded. The credible language is specific to production and channel operation.

2. The real enemy is not just "the algorithm." The enemy is statelessness: every upload starts from scratch, every idea loses its context, every break feels like disappearance, and every dashboard refresh becomes a referendum on the creator.

3. "Input mode" is probably the best new concept to bring into the archetype. It names the upstream phase creators lose when they are trapped in output mode.

4. "Ripe queue" is stronger than generic "idea bank." Creators already have notes. They need a way to know which ideas are ready to script, film, package, clip, pitch, or shelve.

5. The cast should grow beyond editor and thumbnail designer. The YouTube creator world includes clippers, sponsor reps, Discord peers, commenters, policy reviewers, analytics dashboards, tax/accounting helpers, and the comparison ghosts of creators who scaled too fast or blew up earlier.

6. The comeback arc should be gentler and more operational. The creator does not come back by becoming a machine. They come back by protecting input mode, rebuilding a queue, warming the audience, and lowering the cost of starting again.

## Open Questions For The Archetype Rewrite

- Should this archetype focus on the 5k-500k subscriber solo creator, the monetized partnered creator, or the pre-monetized creator trying to escape the first plateau?
- Is BuildOS aiming at video essayists, educators, gaming creators, documentary creators, tutorial creators, or all YouTube-native workflows?
- Should "idea bank" remain the flagship phrase, or should the archetype graduate to "ripe queue" / "channel memory" language?
- How much should the archetype explicitly name AI? The research suggests "context," "source vault," "huddle," "brief," and "handoff" are safer than leading with AI.
