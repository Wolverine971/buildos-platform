# Writer / Author Persona — Research Dump

A working brief for synthesizing the BuildOS writer persona. Research-grade detail with real names, real tools, real Reddit-tier complaints, real URLs. Not a finished persona doc. DJ will distill.

The persona we are biasing toward is **the indie / hybrid / Substack-serial / multi-WIP author who lives in their own tool stack**, not the literary-establishment trad-pub-only author. We cover the trad midlist for completeness but the strategy is to win the indies first because they:

1. Already pay for their own stack (low friction to add BuildOS).
2. Live in public on Substack/X/Reddit/Discord (we can find them).
3. Operate like solo businesses (BuildOS pitch maps 1:1 to their pain).
4. Talk about their tools out loud (the source of every pain quote in this doc).

> **Corpus role — Tier 3 (Evidence).** This is the canonical **deep-research / evidence base** of the writer corpus — the hard layer the lighter docs can't supply: ~100 named tools with prices, community sizes, the full 15-stage self-pub ops pipeline, sourced verbatim pain quotes, and a ranked outreach pipeline. It pairs with the July **community-voice** research at [`../brand/worldbuilding/research/writer-archetype-2026-07-02/`](../brand/worldbuilding/research/writer-archetype-2026-07-02/) and feeds the persona ([`persona-writer.md`](./persona-writer.md)) and archetype ([`../brand/worldbuilding/ARCHETYPE_WRITER.md`](../brand/worldbuilding/ARCHETYPE_WRITER.md)). Hub: [`writer-corpus.md`](./writer-corpus.md). Keep it — but treat it as **evidence, not positioning**: its value-prop framing predates the 2026-07-06 memory-hero ruling, so use `persona-writer.md` for current copy.
>
> **⚠ Perishable facts — verify before quoting (freshness pass owed):** tool prices (Vellum $249–499, Atticus $147, dev-edit $1.5–5k/book, BookBub deals $200–1.5k) and especially **BookFunnel "doubling 2026"**; community counts (20BooksTo50K / Author Nation ~70k, Wide for the Win ~18k, subreddit sizes); **Joanna Penn's annual theme** ("2025: Leverage" has almost certainly moved); Substack Fiction leaderboard ranks; rebrands (Kit ← ConvertKit, D2D ← Smashwords, Spotify Audiobooks for Authors ← Findaway); and the **§6 outreach send-status** vs. the current skip list. ## 1. Persona archetypes — the indie / scrappy writer

Six concrete sub-archetypes. Each is real, each has a named example, each has a different stack and a different pain shape.

### 1a. The self-publishing series-fiction author ("20Booksto50K / Wide for the Win" crowd)

- **Genre:** Romance subgenres (paranormal, romantasy, contemporary, MM), urban fantasy, cozy mystery, military sci-fi, LitRPG, progression fantasy.
- **Output target:** 4–12 books/year. "Rapid release" is the verb. Lindsay Buroker publicly produces 8–10 novels per year.
- **Distribution:** KDP Select (Kindle Unlimited) or "wide" (Apple Books, Kobo Plus, Barnes & Noble Nook, Google Play, library distribution via IngramSpark + Draft2Digital).
- **WIPs in flight:** Almost always 2+ simultaneously. The pattern Lindsay Buroker described on the Creative Penn: finish a draft, hand it off to her editor, start the next book while the prior one is in editing.
- **Day-in-the-life:** 90-minute morning writing sprint (Sarra Cannon's HB90 method — quarterly planning broken into 90-day blocks with daily/weekly planner pages). Afternoon: Amazon Ads + Facebook Ads dashboards, KDP/Kobo/Apple sales dashboards, ARC team management, newsletter writing.
- **Stack:** Scrivener (drafting) → Plottr or Scrivener corkboard (plotting + series bible) → ProWritingAid (self-edit) → BetaBooks / Google Docs (beta) → professional editor → Vellum (formatting) → KDP + IngramSpark + Draft2Digital (distribution) → BookFunnel + StoryOrigin (ARCs + newsletter swaps) → Amazon Ads + Facebook Ads + BookBub (paid).
- **Community:** 20BooksTo50K Facebook group (now Author Nation conference, formerly 20Books Vegas). Wide for the Win Facebook group (~18K members, founded by Erin Wright, Mark Leslie Lefebvre is comms director). The Sell More Books Show podcast. Creative Penn podcast.

### 1b. The Substack serialist (fiction or longform nonfiction, weekly cadence)

- **Examples:** Simon K. Jones (Tales from the Triverse, 4-year serial, 12K+ subs, ranked #67 in Substack Fiction); Elle Griffin (The Novelleist, $19K her first year, 42-week serialization at $10/month or $50/year); Susan Dennard (Misfits & Daydreamers writing-advice Substack, "tens of thousands" of subs).
- **Output target:** One chapter or essay per week, every week, often for years. Simon K. Jones explicitly described his closing-stretch reference-compilation pain on Substack Notes — see Section 10.
- **Stack:** Substack (publishing, payments, community Chat, Notes) → Scrivener or Obsidian (drafting buffer; most serialists pre-write 6–10 chapter buffer ahead) → BookFunnel ($100–$400/year, doubling 2026) → Canva / Affinity (cover art) → ConvertKit / Beehiiv (some authors run a side newsletter on a non-Substack ESP for portability).
- **Day-in-the-life:** Pre-dawn writing (Elle Griffin: 6 AM for two hours before her full-time job). One ship-day (usually Wednesday or Friday). The rest of the week: ahead-buffer drafting + Notes engagement + reader replies + Substack Chat moderation.
- **Pain shape:** Continuity drift across 100+ weekly chapters. "What did I establish about X in chapter 14?" four years ago. Source-of-truth problem: drafts live in Scrivener, published chapters live on Substack, world rules live in Obsidian or Notion or a hand-written notebook. Three different surfaces, one story.

### 1c. The trad-pub midlist novelist (1–4 books out, agent-rep'd, mostly solo)

- **Examples:** Susan Dennard (NYT/USA Today bestselling YA fantasy, Witchlands series at Tor, 14+ years of writing-advice newsletter — straddles trad and indie); Valorie Castellanos Clark (debut nonfiction at Chronicle Prism).
- **Output target:** 1 book every 12–24 months (publishing-house pace), but with 2–3 WIPs in various stages: current contract book, next-pitch book, side-Substack content.
- **Stack:** Scrivener or Word (publisher requires Word docs for delivery), Track Changes for edit letters, QueryTracker (if still querying), Notes/Apple Notes for capture, reMarkable 2 + Boox Mira (Susan Dennard's distraction-free combo — see her "My Top Writing Tools of 2026" post), Voice Memos for shower-thinking.
- **Pain shape:** Marketing-yourself-alone. Trad publishers do less and less promotion, so a midlist author runs their own newsletter, Substack, social media, and ARC strategy on top of writing the actual book. Often the most-overwhelmed archetype because they don't have the indie author's "this is my business" mental model yet.

### 1d. The indie nonfiction / expert author (one book + course + newsletter + speaking)

- **Examples:** Joanna Penn (The Creative Penn, indie nonfiction + fiction under J.F. Penn pen name, theme of 2025 "Leverage"); Ryan Holiday (Daily Stoic empire); Tim Ferriss (multiple books); Monica Leonelle (WriterMBA, USA Today bestseller, Growth Hacking for Storytellers series sold 50K+ copies).
- **Output target:** One book every 2–3 years, plus a perpetually-running newsletter (Penn's free podcast biweekly + paid resources), plus a course or membership.
- **Stack:** Scrivener for the book, Substack or Beehiiv or ConvertKit for newsletter, Teachable / Maven / Podia / Circle for courses, Canva for visuals, Descript for podcast, Anchor/Captivate for podcast hosting.
- **Pain shape:** Recurring research re-cited. The same Harvard Business Review article ends up cited in the book, the newsletter, the podcast, the course module, and the talk slides — but those five surfaces don't share a source-of-truth. (Compare to Darin Soat / How Money Works in DJ's existing outreach list — same pattern, different format.)

### 1e. The narrative nonfiction / research-heavy author (Robert Caro descendants)

- **Examples:** Valorie Castellanos Clark (Unruly Figures — 20+ historical profiles per book/podcast, "extremely carefully cited and obsessively researched"); Anne Helen Petersen (Culture Study Substack — "pieces that sometimes take weeks to research and report").
- **Output target:** One major book per 3–5 years, but the in-flight Substack/newsletter funds the research time.
- **Stack:** Zotero (academic citations), Readwise Reader (highlights from PDFs/articles), Obsidian or Notion (notes), Scrivener (drafting), reMarkable 2 (for archive reading), Google Scholar + JSTOR + Internet Archive for sources, voice memos for interview field notes, Descript or Otter for transcripts.
- **Pain shape:** Source-of-truth across years of research. Where is that one quote from that one Pinkerton-archive document I read in 2024? Did I already use it in episode 12 of the podcast? Did I re-cite the same source in two different chapters? Re-finding > re-reading > re-re-summarizing the same source three times because the highlight didn't travel from Readwise → Obsidian → Scrivener.

### 1f. The ghostwriter / book coach (manages multiple client manuscripts in parallel)

- **Examples:** Tim Grahl (Story Grid CEO + author + coaches writers); typical Reedsy ghostwriter or Gotham Ghostwriters contractor working 2–3 client manuscripts simultaneously; book coaches at Scribe Media or independent.
- **Output target:** 3–6 client manuscripts in flight, each at a different stage (interview phase, draft phase, revision phase, copy-edit phase).
- **Stack:** Google Docs (collaboration with client) + Scrivener (own working draft) + Otter / Rev (interview transcripts) + ClickUp / Notion / Asana (project management across clients) + DocuSign (contracts) + QuickBooks / Stripe (invoicing).
- **Pain shape:** Context-switching tax. Each client's voice, world, expert vocabulary, and Chicago/AP style preference is different. Forgetting whose POV you're in. Bleeding voice between manuscripts. Per Bernoff's "How to manage a book project with many diverse contributors": complex projects often include "a ghostwriter, author, communications person, project manager, assistants, researchers, designers, and subject-matter experts." The ghostwriter is the integrator. They are the BuildOS user.

## 2. End-to-end workflow

Granular pipeline. Each stage names real tools the persona actually uses.

### Stage 1: Idea capture → premise development

- **Capture surfaces:** Apple Voice Memos (Susan Dennard explicitly cites this: "The simple act of translating a problem into words that I then articulate out loud frequently reveals my solution"), Apple Notes, Notion daily-note, Obsidian inbox, a physical notebook, scribbled-on-receipts.
- **Premise development:** "What if" docs, Reedsy plot generator, ChatGPT/Claude as a brainstorm partner.
- **Market validation (indie-specific):** Publisher Rocket (KDP keyword + category research), K-lytics genre reports, PublisherRocket competitor analysis, scrolling Amazon top-100 for the target subgenre, scrolling TikTok BookTok for emerging tropes (cozy academia, "morally grey" romantasy, monster romance, "fated mates," etc.).

### Stage 2: World-building / outlining / series bible

- **Outlining:** Plottr (visual timeline + character + tag system), Scrivener corkboard, Save the Cat Beat Sheets, K.M. Weiland's structure resources, Story Grid Five-Leaf Genre Clover.
- **World-building:** World Anvil (wiki-style, more popular with TTRPG/DnD crowd than authors), Campfire Write (author-focused), Notion templates ("Fantasy Bible," "Magic System Tracker"), Obsidian + custom-linked notes, LegendKeeper (D&D/worldbuilding crossover), hand-written notebooks for many old-school plotters.
- **Series bible:** Plottr's series-bible feature (carries characters across multiple book projects), NovelCrafter's Codex (auto-detects references in the manuscript, builds inline preview), Scrivener research folder, separate Google Doc.
- **Timeline:** Aeon Timeline (custom calendars for fantasy/sci-fi, syncs with Scrivener).

### Stage 3: Drafting

- **Primary drafting apps:** Scrivener (still dominant, especially Mac), Microsoft Word (trad-pub required for delivery), Google Docs (collaboration, ghostwriting), Ulysses, iA Writer, Reedsy Studio (free), Dabble, NovelPad, Novlr, NovelCrafter (AI-integrated), Sudowrite (fiction-specific AI), Atticus (formatting + drafting hybrid), Plottr's writing module, Obsidian + Longform plugin.
- **Distraction-free hardware:** reMarkable 2 + Type Folio (Susan Dennard's setup), Boox Mira e-ink monitor, Freewrite Traveler, AlphaSmart Neo (still beloved by some authors because of zero connectivity).
- **Voice drafting:** Dragon NaturallySpeaking, MacWhisper, Otter, MacOS built-in dictation, walks-with-Voice-Memos.

### Stage 4: Self-editing

- ProWritingAid (most popular indie pick), Grammarly (more business/general audience), AutoCrit (genre-specific style targets), Fictionary (story-structure analysis), Hemingway Editor (readability).
- **Self-edit pass order varies but typical:** Big-picture structure → scene/chapter sequence → POV/character voice → line edit → copy edit → proofread.

### Stage 5: Beta readers / critique partners

- BetaBooks (Andrew Burleson's tool, organizes beta reader feedback, $34.99/mo at top tier, free for readers); BetaReader.io; Google Docs comments; Discord servers (Sci-Fi & Fantasy Writing Community, WritingNovels); manual email management.
- "Where did Beta 3's feedback go?" is a universal complaint. Feedback comes back marked up in Google Docs comments, threaded emails, voice memos, BetaBooks, and Discord DMs — all at once.

### Stage 6: Professional editing pipeline

- **Developmental edit** ($1,500–$5,000 per book): Reedsy marketplace, EFA (Editorial Freelancers Association), direct hire.
- **Line edit** ($500–$2,000): same channels.
- **Copy edit / proofread** ($300–$1,500): same channels, plus PerfectIt for the editor's own consistency check.
- **Tools managing the editor relationship:** Tracked Changes in Word, Google Docs suggesting mode, sometimes back-and-forth in Scrivener but most editors return Word.

### Stage 7: Cover commission

- Reedsy ($300–$2,500/cover), Damonza ($200–$1,000), Miblart, 100 Covers, Get Premade Covers, Fiverr (low end). Specialist designers known by name within genres (e.g., Christian Bentulan for paranormal romance, Aero Gallerie for romantasy).

### Stage 8: Formatting

- **Vellum** ($249 for ebook-only, $499 for ebook+print — Mac-only, gold-standard typography, one-time fee). The Substack post "Vellum vs. Atticus 2026" notes Vellum is "the gold standard in indie publishing" but Mac-only and pricier.
- **Atticus** ($147 lifetime, cross-platform Mac/Win/Web, full writing environment included).
- **Reedsy Studio** (free), Scrivener compile, Affinity Publisher (advanced typographers), InDesign (for very custom print).

### Stage 9: Print proof

- KDP print proof copies ($3–$5 + shipping), IngramSpark proofs ($30+), BookVault (UK), Lulu.

### Stage 10: ARC distribution

- **BookFunnel** (~$100–$400/year, doubling 2026 per Simon K. Jones — quoted: "more reliable ROI than spewing money into Facebook or Amazon ads"), **StoryOrigin** (freemium, list-swap focused), **BookSirens** ($10/book ARC distribution), **NetGalley** ($450+/title — trad-pub favored), **Hidden Gems**.

### Stage 11: Launch

- **Newsletter:** Substack, Beehiiv, ConvertKit, MailerLite (price-sensitive indies favor this), Kit (formerly ConvertKit), Ghost (technical/hybrid), AWeber (legacy).
- **ARC team management:** StoryOrigin or BookFunnel + private Facebook group + private Discord.
- **Launch ads:** Amazon Ads (AMS), Facebook Ads, BookBub Featured Deals (the holy grail — $200–$1,500 per deal, can move thousands of copies in a day), BookBub Ads (cheaper, ongoing), TikTok Shop.

### Stage 12: Post-launch ad management

- Amazon Advertising Console (AMS), Facebook Ads Manager, BookBub Ads Manager, ScribeCount (cross-platform sales aggregation), Book Report (KDP sales dashboard analytics), Bookreport.io, Readerlinks (ad campaign tracking).

### Stage 13: Series planning / next book

- Plottr series bible carries character/world tags into Book N+1.
- Sarra Cannon's HB90 method: quarterly planning. Each quarter you pick the projects you can fit and break them down.

### Stage 14: Backlist marketing / going wide

- Draft2Digital (D2D — aggregator to Apple Books, Kobo, Nook, etc.), PublishDrive, Smashwords (now D2D).
- Kobo Plus (Kobo's KU equivalent), Apple Books promotions, Spotify Audiobooks for Authors (formerly Findaway Voices), Spotify Audiobooks in Premium.

### Stage 15: Audiobook

- **ACX** (Amazon-owned, Audible distribution, $150–$300 per finished hour for professional narrators, or royalty share). **Findaway Voices / Spotify Audiobooks for Authors** (wider distribution: Apple Books, Walmart, Nook, Chirp, Scribd, Storytel). **ElevenLabs** (AI narration, controversial). **Lovo**, **Speechki**. Pro narrators at $250+/PFH still dominate quality bar.

### Where the stack fragments (the canonical pain quotes)

**Susan Dennard's "My Top Writing Tools of 2026"** lists ten separate tools she pays for: reMarkable 2, Scrivener, Voice Dream Reader, Becca Syme's resources, Voice Memos, Boox Mira, Canva, Desk Cycle, Dumb Phone app, Freedom. She describes herself as "a circling writer" who goes "through the same parts of my manuscript many, many times." After 13+ years on Scrivener she admits she "ignores 95% of the toggles, switches, menus, or options." The stack is real and the depth of usage is shallow on purpose.

**Dominic de Souza's "I Tested 23 Writing Tools"** — tested Milanote, Obsidian, Scrivener, Novelcrafter, Chapterly, Speare, Lattics, Anytype, Craft, FirstDraftPro, Novlr, Talers, Motif, Novelize, Sudowrite, AutoCrit, Storywriter Pro, Evernote, Reedsy Studio, Bear, UpNote, Pensive. Verbatim quotes: _"I don't want a brand-new system that makes me get a master's degree in someone else's method." "I already have a workflow. I've got habits. I've got needs." "I'm a design snob — I love things that look good and get out of the way." "Pricey subscriptions when I might not even be writing for six months." "The moment I open Chrome, I'm Pavlov'd to open new tabs."_ He says outright: he's looking for "an upgrade. A better tool. Something that feels like an extension of how I think and write."

**Simon K. Jones' "2026 tech stack for serial fiction writers"** (Substack post) — covers Scrivener (£55), Canva, Affinity, Clip Studio Paint Pro, Audacity, Substack, IngramSpark, Bookshop.org, BookFunnel ($100–$400/year doubling), Google Drive, Remarkable Paper Pro (£559+), Obsbot Meet 2 webcam (£129), Sennheiser Profile mic (£88), external hard drive. Quote: _"Scrivener doesn't make me a better writer, but it definitely makes me a less stressed writer."_ That's the BuildOS thesis from a writer's mouth — the tool that takes off cognitive load.

## 5. Aspirational / apex authors (writer-equivalent dream clients)

The "if BuildOS works for them, every indie author follows" tier.

### 5a. Brandon Sanderson — apex of "indie-hybrid mega-author"

- **Format:** Multi-series epic fantasy (Stormlight Archive, Mistborn, Cosmere). Hybrid: Tor for trade, his own Dragonsteel for special editions.

## 8. How they research, take notes, manage scenes, manage tasks

Concrete habits per archetype.

### Series-bible chaos (multi-book indie series)

- Characters: Plottr's character list, Scrivener character templates, Notion character database, hand-drawn character sheets.
- World rules / magic systems: Notion or Obsidian wiki, Campfire Write articles, World Anvil wiki entries.
- Cross-book continuity: this is where the drift happens. By Book 4, the magic system has accreted three contradictory exceptions across 1,200 pages.
- Authors with 10+ books in one universe (Sanderson, Buroker, Howey, Aaron) typically have a _human assistant_ whose job is series-bible maintenance. Sanderson's team includes a continuity editor.

### Worldbuilding wiki sprawl

- World Anvil → Notion → scene cards in Scrivener → corkboard → magic-system tracker in Plottr → "the one I actually use" (usually the simplest one).
- The "Worldbuilding for Fiction Writers" Substack post by Quill&Steel notes that "knowing how to structure worldbuilding can save writers from endless disorganized notes and lost ideas" — the canonical pain.

### Scrivener corkboard + index cards

- Many writers (Brandon Sanderson included, before he moved to Word Outline mode) used physical index cards before going digital. The corkboard view in Scrivener is the digital-native equivalent. Plottr's timeline view + tag system is the heavier-weight version.

### Voice memos in showers / on walks

- Susan Dennard, Joanna Penn, Tim Ferriss all publicly recommend voice-memo-first capture for the moments where ideas surface (shower, walk, drive).
- Otter / MacWhisper / Apple Voice Memos with Apple Intelligence summary → captured. Most authors do not have a reliable pipe from voice memo → next-action.

### Beta reader feedback consolidation

- BetaBooks is the purpose-built tool but only ~30% of indie authors use it; the rest use Google Docs comments + email + Discord.
- The merge problem: "Reader 1 said this, Reader 2 said the opposite, Reader 3 said nothing about this scene, Reader 4 highlighted the wrong line."
- Many authors hand-tag feedback (P1/P2/P3 priority, or "consider/ignore/fix"). Lindsay Buroker and Sarra Cannon both teach feedback-triage methods.

### Research files for nonfiction

- Zotero (academic citations), Readwise Reader (highlights), Notion (databases of citations), Obsidian (markdown notes with backlinks), DEVONthink (heavy-archive nonfiction researchers).
- Anne Helen Petersen: "Pieces that sometimes take weeks to research and report" + crowdsourcing from readers. Her source-of-truth is her Substack archive itself.
- Cory Doctorow: 20+ years of daily blogging _is_ his note system. "Long-form essays are dividends from 20+ years of daily blogging, with short pieces available to search and reference in longer, synthetic pieces."

### Long-tail series authors (Sanderson tier)

- Series bible maintained by an assistant (sometimes a paid continuity editor).
- Public-facing fan wiki (Coppermind for Cosmere, Tolkien Gateway for Middle-earth) often becomes more reliable than the author's own notes — a sign of the canonical pain.

### Substack serialists (weekly cadence)

- Pre-write 6–10 chapter buffer ahead (advice consistently given on Substack-for-fiction posts).
- Maintain a "chapter index" post with all published chapters linked.
- Track world rules in a separate Obsidian vault or Notion DB.
- Sub-pain: "Where did I publish that line about X? Was it in chapter 14 or chapter 41?"

## 11. BuildOS angle per sub-archetype

Concrete, anti-hype, matches BuildOS voice. The point is consistent across archetypes: **BuildOS is the shared context layer underneath your existing stack.** Scrivener stays. Plottr stays. Substack stays. ChatGPT stays. BuildOS sits below them and remembers what they each forget.

### For 1a. The self-pub series-fiction author (20BooksTo50K crowd)

_Your stack is already a business — Scrivener for drafting, Plottr for the series bible, ProWritingAid for the self-edit, ScribeCount for sales, BookFunnel for ARCs. BuildOS doesn't replace any of them. It's the place where book 4's revision tasks, book 5's plot bible, and the beta feedback on book 3 share one brain. So when ChatGPT helps you outline scene 12, it actually knows what the assassin already drank in book 1._

### For 1b. The Substack serialist

_Pre-writing a six-chapter buffer means you're juggling four versions of your world at any moment — the live published chapter, the buffered chapter, the open draft, and the world rules you keep "in your head." BuildOS is the project that holds all four at once. World rules, character status, chapter dependencies, next-up tasks — one workspace. Substack still does the publishing. Scrivener still does the drafting. BuildOS keeps you from compiling references by hand at the end of year four._

### For 1c. The trad-pub midlist novelist

_Your publisher does less promotion than they used to. You're running your own newsletter, your own social, your own ARC team, your own next-pitch deck — all while drafting book two on a contract deadline. BuildOS is the layer where the current book, the next pitch, and the marketing-yourself-alone work live as one project. So you stop losing the line you wrote in your notes app at 2 AM that was the whole reason book three is going to work._

### For 1d. The indie nonfiction / expert author

_Your stack is books + newsletter + course + podcast + speaking — five surfaces off the same underlying research. The same HBR article ends up cited in the book, the email, the episode notes, the lesson, and the keynote. BuildOS is the shared archive your AI tools can read from. Newsletter draft, episode prep, course module, book chapter — they all see the same source-of-truth so you stop re-citing yourself and start compounding._

### For 1e. The narrative nonfiction / research-heavy author

_Three years of source material is too much for any one tool. Zotero has the citations, Readwise has the highlights, Obsidian has the notes, Scrivener has the drafts — and you still spend an hour finding the quote you want to use today. BuildOS holds the project's brain. Every source, every highlight, every draft revision in one place. When you ask Claude to help reframe chapter 6, it already knows what you wrote about the same archive in chapter 2._

### For 1f. The ghostwriter / book coach

_Three client manuscripts, three voices, three sets of expert vocabulary, three style guides. The context-switch tax is the job. BuildOS gives every client their own project — interview transcripts, voice notes, draft revisions, edit-letter response tasks — so when you sit down with Client A's chapter today, your AI tools and your own notes are already in Client A's voice and world. Less bleed. Faster onboarding the next time you take a new manuscript._
