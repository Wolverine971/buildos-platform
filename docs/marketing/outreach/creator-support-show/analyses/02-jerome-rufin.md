# Jerome Rufin — Golf "Scratch to Scratch"

## Profile

- **Channel:** [@JeromeRufin](https://www.youtube.com/@JeromeRufin)
- **Background:** Former lead engineer at a top tech company. ML / data science background.
- **Niche:** Golf. Recruiting world's best golf coaches to mold him to scratch level.
- **Volume:** Posted **1,200 videos in 2 years**. First 136 days = 136 daily long-form vlogs (~1hr each), plus 6 shorts/day.
- **Current performance:** 150K-200K views per video. Wants 400K-1M.
- **Team:** Has a team. ("My team and I went literally...")
- **Sponsorship economics:** Implied — has coaches worth $100K+ in lessons documented as channel content.

## What he's building

A scratch-golfer journey channel that scales by _replicating elite coaching for an audience that can't access it_. ("The value add for my channel is no one can really take these lessons just because they're like the best golf coaches in the entire world. What I really wanted to do is democratize that to the general public.") He's already pivoted strategy two months prior to the episode and 5x'd viewership. Now wants to reduce risk on experimental videos and crack 400K consistently.

## The on-air problem (verbatim)

> "About two months ago, I pretty much pivoted and restructured everything on my channel. And through that new strategy, I've been able to, you know, accumulate around 150,000 to 200,000 views every 30 to 60 days. But now I'm trying to really understand kind of two things — how do I get to that next level of like 400,000 or 1 million."

And, crucially:

> "I edit every — now I don't edit, so I like critique and like make comments on every single thing. Like you'll see in this frame I like made like 300 different comments riddled with — **it took me um like six hours.** And so I do this for every video right now."

## Colin & Samir's diagnosis

1. Reduce filter density in the hook. Don't pile on 4 storylines (left job + 18 lessons + scratch golfer + 5000 hours).
2. Lead with personal stakes ("you left your job to become a pro golfer"), drop in lessons gradually.
3. Build a "list of expert coaches I'm trying to work with" as a side narrative — knock off each in successive videos.
4. Identity-Emotion-Action exercise to clarify why people watch.
5. "Why not me" as a tagline / channel mission.
6. Write the headline you want Men's Health to write about you at year-end — work backwards.

## Verbatim pain quotes

1. **"It took me six hours [to make 300 frame-by-frame comments]. And so I do this for every video right now."** — workflow pain, on-camera.
2. "My methodology is aggregate as much data as humanly possible and make it a large enough sample size so that you could create long-term decisions from it."
3. "I'm so precise about so many of these things."

## Tool / workflow signal

- **Engineer mind.** Treats YouTube as a data problem. Posted 1,200 videos as a sample-size exercise to learn patterns.
- **Reviews every cut frame-by-frame** with ~300 written comments per video, 6 hours per pass. Outsources editing but micromanages it through written feedback (probably Frame.io or similar).
- **Team-driven** ("my team and I went literally"). Multiple stakeholders consume the same research.
- **Story-research overlap.** He's planning a Paris video and an upcoming series of expert coaches — each guest needs a research arc.
- **Same coaches surface across multiple videos.** Each coach is essentially a recurring character. Their gear, advice, drills should compound.

## BuildOS angle

Jerome is the **dream BuildOS user** — a former engineer who already treats YouTube as a data system, who runs a team, and whose pain is literally "I spend 6 hours per video writing 300 comments on every frame." That's not a creator problem, that's a _context-handoff_ problem. Each coach he features is a project that lives across 5+ videos. Each video is itself a project with research, hook variants, editor notes, sponsor placements. He explicitly wants to "long-term be able not to do this anymore" — i.e., move the 6-hour frame review into the system instead of his head.

**Pitch shape:** "You already think like an engineer about your channel. BuildOS holds the project — one coach, the lessons they taught, the videos they appear in, your editor's notes, your audience reactions — so that the next video reads from what the last one learned. You stop being the 6-hour bottleneck."
