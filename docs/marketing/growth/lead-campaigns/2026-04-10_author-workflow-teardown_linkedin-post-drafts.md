## What's in here

Three drafts, all following the structure from the campaign brief (hook → scene → transformation → CTA). They differ in hook angle:

1. **Draft A -- "I fixed that yesterday"** -- direct, concrete, specific-number hook
2. **Draft B -- "trying to remember what I already figured out"** -- friend-observation hook, softer entry
3. **Draft C -- "your manuscript is not the only thing you're holding"** -- thesis hook, leads with the pain frame

Pick whichever lands most like DJ's voice. All three follow the rules from the campaign brief: no "AI-powered," no "productivity tool," no "check out BuildOS," no "launch," no tagging people hoping they share it.

## Draft A -- "I fixed that yesterday"

My chapter revision notes lived in four places. I fixed that yesterday.

Google Doc. Phone lock-screen note. A sticky on my monitor from a beta reader call. A voice memo I was never going to transcribe.

Every time I sat down to revise, the first 20 minutes were spent remembering what I had already figured out. The real work -- the actual chapter -- never started until I was already tired.

This is the part nobody talks about when writers describe the muddy middle. The manuscript is not the only thing you're holding. You're also holding every decision you've made about the manuscript, scattered across tools that were never designed to remember for you.

Yesterday I dumped the whole mess into one place. The scattered revision notes. The continuity problem with Marcus in chapter 12. The setting change in the warehouse scene that might contradict chapters 3 and 4. Two minutes later I had a revision plan with the continuity issues flagged, the scene-level tasks laid out, and one clear place to come back to tomorrow.

The project started remembering with me instead of demanding that I remember it.

## Draft B -- "trying to remember what I already figured out"

A writer friend told me her revision process is "trying to remember what I already figured out."

That is the whole problem.

The manuscript is the thing everyone sees. But the manuscript is not the only thing the writer is holding. There's the revision comment from a beta reader. The timeline fix you wrote on your phone last week. The continuity issue with a side character you clocked at 2am and meant to come back to. The subplot thread you changed three weeks ago that now contradicts chapter 4.

None of that lives in the manuscript. It lives in your head, or in notes scattered across five tools, or worse -- it doesn't live anywhere. You solved the problem once and then lost the solution.

The muddy middle is not a motivation problem. It's a memory problem. The project should be remembering with you, not demanding that you remember it.

I spent yesterday dumping a messy revision pass into one place -- scattered notes, continuity issues, scene-level tasks, the whole pile. Two minutes later I had a structured revision plan with the problems flagged and the next moves obvious.

## Notes for recording the hero asset

Whichever draft DJ picks, record the hero asset using the exact brain-dump content from the campaign brief:

> "Chapter 7 needs a complete rewrite. The pacing is off after the reveal in chapter 5. Sarah's motivation doesn't connect to what happens in chapter 12 anymore. I have notes about this in my Google Doc but I also wrote something on my phone last week about fixing the timeline. The beta reader said the middle section drags. I think I need to move the confrontation earlier but that breaks the subplot with Marcus. Also I changed the setting of the warehouse scene and I need to check if that contradicts anything in chapters 3 and 4."

That way the LinkedIn post language, the video narration, and the before/after screenshot pair all tell the same story: Marcus, the continuity issue, the warehouse scene, the revision plan. Repetition is the point -- it makes the proof legible.

## Reply guide (for comments on the post)

Anticipate these common reply shapes and have a 1-2 line answer ready. Do not pitch the product in replies; offer the teardown.

| If they say...                 | You say...                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "This is exactly my problem."  | "If you want, send me a rough description of your current workflow and I'll sketch what a cleaner version could look like. No pitch."                                                                                           |
| "What tool is this?"           | "It's called BuildOS -- I built it. Happy to show you how I'd use it on your specific workflow if you DM me a rough outline of how you're working today."                                                                       |
| "I love Scrivener."            | "Good -- Scrivener is great for the manuscript itself. This is about the _stuff around_ the manuscript -- the notes, the feedback, the fixes you're holding in your head. Happy to talk through where that pile lives for you." |
| "I use Notion for everything." | "Notion can absolutely do this if you've built it right. If you want, I'll look at your setup and tell you what I'd change or leave alone. Free teardown."                                                                      |
| "I write in Google Docs."      | "Me too, sometimes. The question isn't the doc -- it's where the decisions about the doc live. Want me to take a look at your current setup?"                                                                                   |

Goal in every reply: move to a DM or a short call, not a signup.
