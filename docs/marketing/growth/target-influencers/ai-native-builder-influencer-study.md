You’re looking for a very specific class of person:

**Not “AI celebrities.” Not lab CEOs.**
You want the people who are close enough to the frontier that labs give them early access, invite them to demos/hackathons, or watch what they build — but who are still independent enough that you could potentially work with them.

My read: the best targets are **AI-native builders with distribution**. They are building demos, tools, courses, newsletters, agents, prompt guides, coding workflows, and “proof-of-future” projects in public.

## The core names to study first

### 1. Pietro Schirano — design + AI app generation

Pietro is one of the clearest examples of what you mean. He became known for viral GPT-4 demos, then built **MagicPath**, and was featured around OpenAI’s GPT-5 launch. He also published widely shared GPT-5 prompting guidance based on weeks of usage. ([LinkedIn][1])

**Why he matters:** he sits at the intersection of design, AI coding, product demos, and model-release interpretation. He is not just commenting; he is turning model capability into product metaphors.

**Project angle for you:** “BuildOS for AI-native design/build workflows,” “context systems for designers using agents,” or detailed teardown pages of MagicPath-style workflows.

---

### 2. Riley Brown — AI coding / agent workflow educator

Riley Brown is a strong target because he is deep in **Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, OpenClaw, iOS apps, and agentic workflows**. Recent posts show him documenting months of hands-on agent work, including what he got wrong using Claude Code, Cursor, and Codex; he has also created Codex explainer content with significant reach. ([LinkedIn][2])

**Why he matters:** he is exactly in the “highest leverage” zone: one person teaching thousands how to use AI agents as a software team.

**Project angle for you:** create a “Riley Brown agent stack map” or “Claude Code/Codex/OpenClaw workflow benchmark” and pitch it as a living resource.

---

### 3. Nick St. Pierre — AI creative direction / image-model frontier

Nick St. Pierre is one of the better-known AI creative/image generation people. His X bio is explicitly about playing with AI and sharing learnings, and he runs AI-for-creatives education; his client list includes Meta, Google, Ogilvy, Oliver Wyman, and Nike. ([X (formerly Twitter)][3])

**Why he matters:** labs need people like this to show what new image/video models can do. He translates raw model capability into taste, visual culture, and workflows.

**Project angle for you:** “AI creative operating system,” “brand asset memory,” “real-world creative workflow library,” or case studies of how elite AI creatives use model releases.

---

### 4. Allie K. Miller — business AI influence / enterprise education

Allie K. Miller is a large AI-business voice, commonly listed among top AI influencers, with a broad cross-platform audience and enterprise/advisory positioning. A 2025 newsletter item also quoted her as saying she had early access to OpenAI’s GPT-4o image-generation release. ([hyperwriteai.com][4])

**Why she matters:** she is less “vibe coder” and more “AI business adoption translator.” She is useful if you want BuildOS/content projects aimed at executives, operators, and teams.

**Project angle:** build “AI operating playbooks” around her frameworks; offer research-backed, visual, boardroom-ready explainers.

### 5. Rowan Cheung — AI news distribution machine

Rowan Cheung, founder of **The Rundown AI**, is not necessarily the deepest builder, but he has major distribution in AI news and is repeatedly listed among top AI accounts. ([Twitter Influencer Database][5])

**Why he matters:** he controls attention. If you build a useful research project or interactive AI timeline, people like Rowan can amplify it.

**Project angle:** “daily AI product-release intelligence,” “model-drop cheat sheets,” “who got early access and what they found.”

### 6. Swyx / Shawn Wang — AI Engineer movement builder

Swyx is one of the most important non-lab connectors in the AI engineer scene. He runs **Latent Space** and **AI Engineer**, and describes his work as fostering “the Rise of the AI Engineer.” His AI Engineer events are highly curated and bring together AI engineers, founders, and frontier-tool builders. ([swyx.io][6])

**Why he matters:** if you want the real room where frontier AI builders gather, Swyx is part of that room.

**Project angle:** contribute highly useful research/maps to the AI Engineer ecosystem: “agent engineering primitives,” “context engineering taxonomy,” “AI coding stack comparisons.”

### 7. Simon Willison — independent technical authority

Simon Willison is one of the most trusted independent technical voices in AI. He speaks at AI Engineer events and is known for practical, skeptical, technically grounded analysis of LLMs and tools. ([Mastodon hosted on simonwillison.net][7])

**Why he matters:** he is not hype-first. He is credibility-first. If Simon links to something, engineers trust it.

**Project angle:** build genuinely useful, reproducible technical artifacts: evals, demos, benchmarks, “here is exactly how this model behaves.”

### 8. Hamel Husain — evals, applied AI engineering, production discipline

Hamel is a high-leverage practitioner because he focuses on **LLM evals**, one of the highest-value bottlenecks in real AI products. His background includes Airbnb and GitHub, including early LLM research used by OpenAI for code understanding. His AI evals material and course are widely referenced. ([Hamel's Blog - Hamel Husain][8])

**Why he matters:** as AI gets noisier, evals become status. People who can prove AI works in production become extremely valuable.

**Project angle:** BuildOS could have an “eval layer” for project agents: measure whether agents are actually improving projects, not just producing output.

---

### 9. Harrison Chase — LangChain / agents infrastructure

Harrison Chase is CEO/co-founder of LangChain, and LangChain’s Interrupt conference is explicitly positioned around builders learning what works in production with agents. The 2026 event was marketed as bringing 1,000+ developers, product leaders, researchers, and founders together around agents. ([interrupt.langchain.com][9])

**Why he matters:** he is not an “influencer” in the TikTok sense, but he is an infrastructure node. If you care about agent workflows, LangChain’s ecosystem matters.

**Project angle:** “BuildOS as long-term project memory for agents,” “context persistence layer for LangGraph/LangChain teams.”

---

### 10. Andrej Karpathy — highest-signal bridge between research and builders

You said not Sam Altman, but Karpathy is different. He is a frontier researcher with massive builder influence. He popularized “vibe coding,” has huge educational pull, and just joined Anthropic’s pretraining team, according to Reuters. ([Reuters][10])

**Why he matters:** Karpathy shapes the language of the whole scene. When he names something, the ecosystem starts using that frame.

**Project angle:** do not try to “work with him” first. Instead, build artifacts around his frames: vibe coding, software 3.0, agentic engineering, AI education.

## The “one-person billion-dollar business” people

The phrase mostly traces back to Sam Altman’s prediction/bet about a future one-person billion-dollar company. Multiple outlets have covered that prediction, and Forrester notes Altman made the prediction in early 2024. ([Forrester][11])

The current real-world examples people point to are:

### Maor Shlomo — Base44

Maor Shlomo built Base44, a vibe-coding/app-builder startup, and sold it to Wix for about **$80 million** after roughly six months. It was widely framed as a “solo founder” or “one-man startup” story, though later reporting/comments note the company did have employees by the exit period. ([ctech][12])

**Why he matters:** this is the cleanest “AI-native solo founder” case study.

### Matthew Gallagher — Medvi

Matthew Gallagher / Medvi became another “tiny team, giant business” example. Business Insider reported Medvi had two employees, $401 million in prior-year business, $65 million in profit, and projected $1.8 billion in sales — while also raising serious concerns about AI-generated ads, misleading affiliate marketing, and regulatory/compliance issues. ([Business Insider][13])

**Why he matters:** this is the darker version of the one-person-billion-dollar story: AI leverage plus aggressive marketing in a regulated market. Study it, but do not blindly emulate it.

## My blunt take

The highest-leverage people are **not generic AI influencers**. They are people who do one of four things:

1. **Turn model drops into demos** — Pietro, Nick, Riley.
2. **Turn demos into workflows** — Riley, Hamel, Simon.
3. **Turn workflows into ecosystems** — Swyx, Harrison Chase.
4. **Turn AI leverage into company outcomes** — Maor Shlomo, Matthew Gallagher, Pieter Levels-style indie hackers.

For your goals, I would not start by asking to “work with” them. I would build **public research assets about them**:

**“The AI-Native Operator Index”**
A living database of frontier builders: their stack, workflows, model preferences, products, distribution, launch history, and leverage pattern.

That is exactly the kind of thing BuildOS could credibly own. It would attract the people you want because it helps them look legible, important, and ahead of the curve.
