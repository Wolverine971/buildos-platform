# Paddy Galloway — YouTube Packaging Strategy (CCN, TAM, Phases)

## Skill Combo Links

This source contributes to these multi-source skill combo indexes:

- [Marketing And Content Skill Combos](../../../../research/youtube-library/skill-combo-indexes/MARKETING_AND_CONTENT.md): YouTube channel craft for founders; SaaS social distribution system

> **Core thesis:** YouTube success is **non-linear**: a 20% better title/thumbnail can produce 100x more views. The framework that decides what to make is **CCN fit (Core, Casual, New)** — every video must work for niche enthusiasts, casual viewers, and people new to the topic. Strategy is sequenced by phase: Establishment (30–50 videos, just consistency) → Improvement (layering strategy) → Optimization (analytics-driven). Skipping phases wastes years.

Paddy Galloway is the YouTube strategist behind videos with **10+ billion views**, including work for MrBeast and major brands. This analysis extracts (1) the CCN fit framework, (2) the packaging non-linear-returns insight, (3) the three-phase channel-build sequence, (4) idea filtering, and (5) what BuildOS should learn before any founder-channel attempt.

---

## 1. The CCN Framework (The Foundational Filter)

> "Are we hitting the more casual viewers... the new viewers? So those are viewers... maybe aren't into the niche."

**Every viral video must work for three audience tiers:**

- **Core** — dedicated niche enthusiasts who already follow you.
- **Casual** — occasional-interest viewers in adjacent niches.
- **New** — people completely unfamiliar with the topic.

If a video idea works only for Core, it will hit 10K. If it works for all three, it can hit 1M+. The math is simple: the Core audience is finite; Casual + New is the entire YouTube user base.

**The CCN test:** before producing, ask "would my mom (a New viewer) understand the title and want to click?" If no, the packaging filters out 90% of potential viewers.

For BuildOS: the equivalent is "would a founder who has never heard of BuildOS click this?" Most BuildOS-relevant content is too Core. Examples that pass CCN:

- "How I run a one-person startup without losing my mind" (Casual + New friendly).
- "Why I deleted ChatGPT from my workflow" (curious + contrarian + universal).

Examples that fail CCN:

- "Brain dump → context tree workflow in BuildOS" (Core only).
- "Project ontology classification for solo founders" (Core only).

## 2. The Total Addressable Market (TAM) Calculation

Galloway dismisses the "my niche is too small" excuse:

> "30,000,000 people play golf every weekend."

His method:

1. Search your niche on YouTube.
2. Filter by video length (remove Shorts).
3. Sort by most-viewed.
4. Look at the ceiling — what are the top 10 videos doing?

If videos in your niche regularly hit 1M+, the market is there. The constraint is execution, not market size.

For BuildOS: "founders building things" is enormous on YouTube. The constraint is whether BuildOS-style content can be packaged for that audience, not whether the audience exists.

---

## 3. The Non-Linear Returns Of Packaging

> "A 20% better title could mean double, triple, quadruple... a hundred times more views."

This is the single most important quantitative claim in YouTube strategy. The algorithm is exponential, not linear:

- Better title → higher CTR → more impressions → more views → more subscribers → algorithmic boost on next video.

Each step compounds. A small improvement at the input stage produces an outsized effect downstream.

**Implication:** budget shifts from production to packaging. Most small creators spend 95% of their time filming/editing, 5% on titles + thumbnails. The leverage is the inverse — at least 30–40% of effort should go to packaging.

> "There's not a single video I've worked on... that we got 100% of views we could have got."

Even Galloway, the top YouTube strategist, doesn't claim to fully optimize packaging. The lesson: **packaging is a perpetual optimization, not a one-time decision.**

## 4. Thumbnail Best Practices

### The Glance Test

> Viewers spend "under one second" deciding on a thumbnail.

The thumbnail must communicate in milliseconds. Anything that requires reading, interpreting, or context is too slow.

### The 3-Element Rule

Limit visual focus to **1–3 key elements maximum.** Cluttered thumbnails fail the glance test. Common elements: person's face, one prop, one piece of text.

### Simplicity + Exaggeration

Galloway's tactical rule: keep design simple, but **exaggerate one element** (shine, contrast, scale, expression). The exaggeration is what stops the scroll without crossing into misleading clickbait.

### Cultural Adaptation

Asian markets click more text-heavy thumbnails than Western audiences. Localization matters if you're optimizing globally.

For BuildOS: a founder-channel thumbnail should pass the glance test. A face + one prop + one bold text element. Skip clever visual metaphors.

## 5. The Four Traits Of Viral Videos (Cross-Niche)

Galloway identifies four traits that show up in viral videos regardless of category:

1. **CCN fit.** Works for Core, Casual, and New simultaneously.
2. **Unique novelty value.** Genuinely new or freshly framed. Not "another video about X."
3. **Easy title/thumbnail conveyal.** _"You don't have an amazing idea then"_ if you can't thumbnail it.
4. **Genuine excitement.** Test the idea on your "YouTube council" — friends/peers. If they're not excited, it's not the right video.

Trait 3 is a useful pre-production filter: **if you can't sketch the thumbnail before filming, the idea isn't ready.**

## 6. Studying Outlier Videos (The Comparative Method)

> "Why did this video get 150,000 views while similar ones got 10,000?"

Galloway's analytical method:

- Compare two videos in the same niche with very different view counts.
- Use ViewStats (or similar) to check if the concept was novel or borrowed from another niche.
- Check **demographics** (age, geography shifts).
- Check **device type** (TV viewers behave differently).
- Check **new vs. returning** viewer ratio.
- Check **view trajectory** (rapid spike vs. gradual build).
- Check **traffic sources** (search vs. recommendations vs. external).

> "Data... can start to color in some of the picture, but you still need to zoom out and look at the full picture."

Data narrows the hypothesis; intuition completes it. Pure-data optimization without taste fails.

## 7. CTR Limitations

CTR is **only predictive in the first hour or first 24 hours.**

> "There's a very strong correlation between first hour CTR and long term video performance."

After that window, CTR naturally drops as impressions grow, making it unreliable as a long-term metric.

Implication: **track first-hour CTR religiously**, ignore lifetime CTR after week one.

---

## 8. The Idea Funnel

Galloway uses a structured idea-filtering system:

**Inputs (three sources):**

- Internal — what worked on your channel.
- External — what worked for competitors / other niches.
- Innovation — completely new approaches.

**Five elimination filters:**

1. Does it have CCN fit?
2. Is it feasible to execute?
3. Can you create a title + rough thumbnail?
4. Are you genuinely excited?
5. Top 10 ideas → develop into "1-page ideas" (thumbnail sketch, title, 1–3 reasons it works, logline).

**Final step:** gather feedback, prioritize 3–4 videos, ship.

> "I can do 10 one-page ideas in like an hour or two."

The 1-page idea doc is a cheap artifact that prevents the more expensive mistake (filming a video that doesn't pass CCN).

For BuildOS: this is portable to any founder content operation. A weekly "1-page idea" doc for the next 3–4 pieces of content (newsletter posts, videos, tweets) is a discipline that prevents wasting production budget on the wrong ideas.

## 9. The Three Phases (Establishment → Improvement → Optimization)

This is the most contrarian Galloway insight: **strategy doesn't apply equally at every stage.**

### Phase 1 — Establishment (30–50 videos)

- Focus on **consistency only** (1–2 videos/week).
- Build fundamental skills: editing, thumbnail design, storytelling.
- **Create for passion, not data.**
- _"Make whatever calls to you."_

Trying to do strategy in Phase 1 is wasted effort. You don't have the reps to know what works for your channel yet.

### Phase 2 — Improvement (after consistent posting)

- Layer in strategy and ideation processes.
- Develop brainstorming systems.
- Study retention curves and data.

### Phase 3 — Optimization

- Fine-tune based on detailed analytics.
- Apply sophisticated frameworks (CCN, TAM analysis).

> Attempting Phase 3 tactics before Phase 1 completion wastes time and causes overwhelm.

For BuildOS specifically: if BuildOS launches a YouTube channel, the first 30–50 videos should be "DJ shipping ideas as he has them" — not strategically packaged content. Strategy comes after consistency is real.

---

## 10. When To Broaden vs. Stay Narrow

**Expansion strategy:** start narrow, then expand outward — like monkey bars connecting overlapping adjacent niches.

> Never reverse direction (broad → narrow loses viewers).

**Example:** Noah Kagan moved from niche business tools (Slack guides) to broader entrepreneur advice. The reverse — starting broad and narrowing — fails because you train the audience on a wide topic and then disappoint them.

For BuildOS: the channel should start as narrow as "DJ's solo founder thinking-environment workflow" and expand outward to "solo founder operations" → "knowledge work in the AI era." Reversing this (starting with "AI productivity tips" and narrowing to BuildOS) would lose audience.

---

## 11. Small Creator Mistakes

Galloway's diagnostic of why small creators fail:

1. **Using niche size as excuse.** Blame execution, not the market.
2. **Overthinking without deadline.** Time-box titles/thumbnails to 1–2 hours max.
3. **Skipping execution for strategy.** Reps matter more than frameworks early on.
4. **Chasing meta trends.** _"There is no meta."_ MrBeast-style videos are 5–10% of viral content even at peak trend; YouTube is too diverse for a single playbook.

For BuildOS: a founder channel's biggest risk is over-strategizing the first 20 videos. Phase 1 is reps; resist the urge to optimize.

---

## 12. The "No Meta" Hot Take

> "There is no... one way... one meta to do YouTube."

Simultaneously thriving on YouTube right now:

- Long-form storytelling (60+ minutes).
- Short, minimal-editing videos.
- Fast-cut retention tactics.
- Slow-burn, conversational formats.

All coexist because YouTube's scale is immense. **Pick a format that fits _your_ identity, not the trending format.**

This is liberating: a founder doesn't need to MrBeast-ify their content. Slow, calm, opinionated long-form is a valid format. The CCN test still applies — but the production style is up to the creator.

For BuildOS: this is permission to publish slow, calm, anti-AI content on YouTube without copying the AI productivity channel format. There's no "right" YouTube; there's only "the right format for your identity."

## Top Takeaways for BuildOS

1. **The CCN test is the single best content filter.** Every BuildOS content asset (newsletter post, blog, video) should be runnable through "does this work for Core / Casual / New simultaneously?" If it only works for Core, it caps at the Core audience size.

2. **Packaging is not optional.** Whatever BuildOS creates, **at least 30% of the time** should go to title + opening hook + thumbnail/preview image. Most BuildOS marketing under-invests at the packaging layer.

3. **First-hour CTR is the only metric that matters.** If BuildOS launches a YouTube channel, ignore lifetime CTR. Track and optimize first-hour CTR religiously.

4. **The 1-page idea doc is portable everywhere.** Apply to newsletter posts, blog drafts, X threads, ads. A weekly batch of 10 one-page ideas → pick the top 3 → ship → repeat. This is a content operation, not a content calendar.

5. **Phase 1 is reps, not strategy.** If BuildOS starts a YouTube channel, the first 30–50 videos should be the founder shipping content as ideas come. Strategy applies after consistency is established.

6. **Start narrow, expand outward.** A founder channel should begin as "DJ's solo-founder thinking-environment workflow" and expand to broader topics over time. Never reverse.

7. **No meta.** BuildOS doesn't need to copy MrBeast or Ali Abdaal. Slow, opinionated, anti-feed long-form is a valid YouTube format. Pick the format that matches the brand DNA, not the trend.

## Critical Notes / Caveats

- **Packaging-first mindset has limits.** A perfectly packaged bad video still underperforms a decently packaged great video. Don't read "packaging is non-linear returns" as "production doesn't matter."
- **CCN fit is hard for B2B.** The framework was developed for broad consumer YouTube. For SaaS founder channels, the "New" tier is harder to define — every clickbait-y "New" hook risks attracting unqualified leads. The trade-off is real.
- **"There is no meta" is partially true.** Different formats coexist, but at any given moment specific formats are _easier_ than others. Founders should pick a format that fits their identity _and_ is currently performing on the platform.
- **Phase 1 advice ("just be consistent") is the hardest to execute.** Most founders either over-strategize Phase 1 or never publish at all. Galloway's framework doesn't solve the consistency problem — it just acknowledges it.

## One-Line Summary

> Pass the CCN test (Core, Casual, New) on every video, invest 30%+ of your time in packaging because returns are exponential not linear, do 30–50 reps before applying strategy, start narrow and expand outward, and pick the format that fits your identity rather than chasing the meta.
