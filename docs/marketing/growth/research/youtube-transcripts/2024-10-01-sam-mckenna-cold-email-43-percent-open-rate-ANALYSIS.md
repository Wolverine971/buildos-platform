# Sam McKenna — "Show Me You Know Me" Cold Email Method

> Sam McKenna built an 8-figure business (Sam Sales) on a single trademarked idea: do enough research that the prospect can tell the email was written for _them specifically_. Her stated results: **43% open rate, 20% reply rate** — vs. industry average of 6% open / 0.9% reply.

---

## The Core Thesis

Cold email is broken because everyone optimizes for **scale and brevity**. Sam argues the opposite: **fewer, longer, hyper-personalized emails win** because executive inboxes are flooded with generic "personalization at scale" garbage. The opportunity is enormous because the bar is on the floor.

**Mantra**: Quality over quantity. Goal: 20 emails/week, not 200.

## The 4-Part Structure

Every email has four pieces, in order of importance:

1. **Subject line** (most important — determines if it's opened)
2. **First sentence** (combines with subject to form preview text — determines if it's read)
3. **Value proposition** (the body — determines if they reply)
4. **The close / CTA** (determines if they actually book)

---

## 1. Subject Line — Tips & Tricks

### The "Make No Sense to Anyone Else" Rule

Your subject line should be **so specific to the recipient that nobody else on earth would understand it**. If a stranger reads it and is confused, you did it right.

**Real example that worked:**

- Sent to the Chief Logistics Officer of Tory Burch (multi-billion-dollar company)
- He replied in **under 10 hours**: _"Your email stood out in a sea of emails I get every single day."_

### Subject Line Tactics

- **Use plus signs (`+`) to break up multiple personalization hooks** — e.g., `Switzerland + Liip cheeseburger + [Company name]`
    - "Switzerland" → Sam was born outside Geneva
    - "Liip cheeseburger" → DC restaurant she loves
    - "[Company name]" → recipient's company
    - To anyone else, this is gibberish. To her, it's irresistible.
- **Shorter is NOT better.** Ignore the "keep it under 4 words" advice. Use as many words as it takes.
- **Combine 2–3 personal hooks**, only one of which has to relate to your offer.

### How to Scale It (The Cheat Code)

Once you've written one hyper-personalized subject line, look for **other executives who share that same context** — same fraternity, same alma mater, same hometown, same dog breed, same cause they support. The same subject line works on all of them and _still feels written just for them_.

This is how you reconcile "personalization" with "volume."

## 2. First Sentence — Tips & Tricks

The subject line + first sentence together form the **preview text**. That preview is what actually decides whether the email gets read after opening.

### Two Opening Templates That Work

**Template A — The "Yet to be Introduced" Opener (Sam's go-to):**

> "Hi Bill, we have yet to be properly introduced, but I'm Sam McKenna…"

Conventional wisdom says intros waste space. Sam disagrees: when paired with a research-loaded subject line, this phrasing **signals "we maybe should know each other"** — which is flattering and earns the read.

**Template B — Straight into Show-Me-You-Know-Me:**

> "Bill, I saw your recent post about your adorable furry companion Sprinkles…"
> _(then tie back to your own dog / pet / shared experience)_

### Why Pets Work (and Why You Should Look for These)

High-level executives **frequently post about their dogs** on LinkedIn. It's one of the rare genuinely personal signals an exec puts on a public feed. Use it. Same goes for: kids' sports, marathons, alma mater sports teams, philanthropy.

---

## 3. Value Proposition — Tips & Tricks

### Reframe What "Value Prop" Means

Most cold emails describe **what the sender does**. That's a marketing email, not a sales email.

> Your value proposition is the **challenge you solve** for that specific buyer — not your product features.

### Longer Emails Win Here (Contrarian Take)

The whole industry says "short, short, short." Sam says **longer is better in the value-prop section**, because:

You must convince the buyer that **time spent with you will be well spent**. You can't do that in one line.

### The "Pre-empt the Objection" Move

The structural trick most people miss:

1. State the challenge you solve.
2. **Anticipate their immediate next objection.**
3. Rebut it inside the same email — before they've even thought of it.

**Worked example:**

> _"If you're anything like our clients, you see your LinkedIn Sales Navigator usage rates hover around 5% of your licenses. We can train your teams and bring that number up."_

Sam knows the most common objection: _"Eh, we have a customer success manager at LinkedIn — we don't need this."_ So she pre-empts it in the same email:

> _"You likely have a customer success manager at LinkedIn, but rather than having them teach you what buttons to push, our team will teach you how to use this platform to sell — the same way she did when she was an executive at LinkedIn for her teams."_

The buyer goes from skeptical → "huh, maybe there is something here" — without ever raising the objection.

**Action**: Before writing any value-prop line, list the top 1–2 objections and write the rebuttal into the email itself.

## 4. The Close / CTA — Tips & Tricks

This is where almost every cold email fails. Sam's rules:

### ❌ Don't Do These

- **Don't paste a calendar link.**
    - Presumptuous, slightly rude.
    - Puts the scheduling burden on the prospect.
    - Makes them work around _your_ calendar instead of you working around _theirs_.
- **Don't ask "Do you have 15–20 minutes tomorrow?"**
    - Timeline is too aggressive.
- **Don't ask "Would Monday at 1pm work?"**
    - Too specific → easy "no" → you're stuck chasing.

### ✅ The Magic Closing Line

> "Do you have time over the next week or two to learn more? Let me know what works for you and I'll send a calendar invite along accordingly."

Why this works:

1. **Nobody writes like this** → automatic differentiation.
2. **Respects their calendar**, not yours.
3. **You retain control** of when the call gets scheduled.
4. **You can rearrange your own calendar** to accommodate them, which feels white-glove.

## Critical Analysis (My Take)

**What's strong:**

- The "make no sense to anyone else" subject-line heuristic is genuinely novel and falsifiable — you can self-check it instantly.
- Pre-empting objections inside the first email is the highest-leverage idea in the video. Most reps don't do this even after 10 years.
- The close line is replicable today, no skill required.

**What's hand-wavy:**

- The 43% / 20% numbers are self-reported with no methodology. Likely real but inflated by a small, hand-curated list of senior execs at name-brand companies. Don't expect those numbers your first month.
- The "scale via shared affiliation" trick (frat brothers, alumni) is real but narrow — it works for Sam because she sells into senior sales leaders, a tight network. Less applicable selling SMB.
- "Longer is better" is true _only when the longer length earns the time_ via specificity. A long generic email is worse than a short one.

**How I'd apply this to 9takes / blog outreach:**

- Subject line formula for outreach to journalists/podcasters: `[Their last article topic] + [oddly specific 9takes angle] + [their name]`.
- First sentence: open by referencing a _specific line_ from their last published piece — proves the read, not just the search.
- Pre-empt the objection most journalists have: "I don't cover personality content." Rebuttal embedded: tie the Enneagram angle to the beat they actually cover.
- Close: never a Calendly link. Always "let me know what week works and I'll send the invite."
