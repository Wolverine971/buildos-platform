# Analysis: "Blogging is DEAD, Do THIS instead." — Caleb Ulku

## Core Thesis

**Blogs ≠ rankings for local service businesses.** Caleb Ulku argues that "publish weekly blog content" is dead advice for local SEO. Blog posts attract out-of-state, non-converting traffic on informational queries. What actually ranks (and converts) is a **Core 30** service-hub website structure built directly from your Google Business Profile.

A receipt he leans on: a client paying $8K/mo for blogs had 80K visits but ~zero local calls. After replacing blogs with the Core 30 structure, local calls **tripled in under a month** and they started showing up in ChatGPT / AI Overviews.

He claims he's used this across 200+ local businesses since 2016 with a >90% hit rate in markets under ~500K people.

---

## Tips & Tricks

### 1. Mental model: Google = filing clerk

- Blog post "5 tips for hiring a plumber" → filed under generic plumbing advice (useless for local intent).
- Page "Emergency plumber in Houston" with structured info → filed under Houston plumber (rankable).
- Same logic applies to ChatGPT, Perplexity, and AI Overviews — they need _structured, specific_ info to cite a business.

### 2. Stop doing keyword research — use your GBP

- Your **Google Business Profile categories** = top-level pages.
- Your **GBP services list** = individual service pages.
- "Google is literally telling you what it wants to see." No brainstorm, no SEMrush, no service-entity research needed.

### 3. Match intent to page type

- Quick proof: search the term yourself.
    - "black dress" → shopping carousel (transactional).
    - "how to style a black dress" → articles, blogs, YouTube (informational).
    - "plumber Houston" → service-provider pages and the map pack (local commercial).
- Google has already decided what wins each query. Don't fight it with a blog when it wants a service page.

### 4. The Core 30 structure (the actual blueprint)

Three-tier hierarchy, ~30–40 pages total, **no blog posts**:

- **Tier 1 — Home page**: targets `[primary GBP category] + [city]` (e.g., "Plumber Houston").
- **Tier 2 — Secondary category pages**: one per GBP secondary category + city (e.g., "Drainage Service Houston", "Water Heater Installation Houston", "Heating Contractor Houston").
- **Tier 3 — Individual service pages**: one per GBP service + city (e.g., "Tankless Water Heater Installation Houston", "Sewer Line Cleaning Houston", "Gas Line Repair Houston").

### 5. Interlinking (where most agencies fail)

Mirror the GBP hierarchy in your link graph:

- Home → links _down_ to every secondary category page + 2–3 priority service pages.
- Secondary → links _down_ to all related service pages.
- Service pages → link _up_ to their parent secondary.
- Secondary pages → link _up_ to home.
- Without this, you have "30 loose pages" — Google sees no hierarchy, no depth.

### 6. The three on-page elements that move rankings

For every service-hub page:

1. **Title tag** — must include `service + city`.
2. **H1** — service + city, _and_ speak to the searcher.
3. **First paragraph** — answer the query immediately. This is "goal completion."

### 7. Goal completion > dwell time

- Google now optimizes for "did the user get their answer in 5 seconds," not 5 minutes on page.
- Bad first paragraph: history of your business, definitions, generic platitudes.
- Good first paragraph: directly addresses the searcher's pain, says why to call you, what makes you different.

### 8. Copy pattern from #1-ranked pages

- Example: Zehl & Associates ("personal injury lawyer Houston") leads with "Experienced, undefeated personal injury lawyers… billions won. Why hire our injury lawyers… [social proof] [testimonials]."
- Caleb literally couldn't remember the firm's name — that's _fine_. The page is for the searcher, not the brand.

### 9. Quick competitive audit

Pull up any mid-size city + service. Most local competitors have:

- 5–6 pages total (home, about, contact, generic services).
- Title tag literally says "Home" or just the business name (a study of 100 local sites confirmed this).
- Stock/AI photos + "committed to excellence" platitudes.
- No category pages, no service pages, no interlinking.

That's your bar. The Core 30 obliterates it.

### 10. Where this works

- Markets ≤ ~500K people: works **over 90% of the time** because competitors aren't doing it.
- Bigger markets still work but face stiffer competition.

### 11. AI search visibility is a free byproduct

- The same structure that wins the map pack also gets cited by ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews.
- Blog-heavy competitors are invisible in AI answers because there's nothing structured to "file."

### 12. Build with AI, not by hand

- 30+ locally specific pages is a lot to write manually. Caleb plugs his own AI tool that ingests GBP data and outputs the full Core 30 (pages, interlinks, title tags). End-of-video CTA.

## Anti-Patterns He Calls Out

- Writing weekly informational blog posts for a local service business.
- Default WordPress/Wix title tags ("Home" or just the business name).
- Generic homepage paragraph: "We're committed to excellence."
- First paragraph that opens with company history or a definition.
- 5–6-page websites with one catch-all "Services" page.
- Burying social proof / video testimonials below the fold.
- Optimizing for time-on-page instead of fast goal completion.

## What This Means for 9takes (quick read)

9takes isn't a local service business, so the Core 30 isn't a literal fit. But the underlying principles port over:

- **Match content type to query intent** — `/personality-analysis` (DB-driven hub) already does this better than MDsvex blogs do for "[celebrity] enneagram type" queries. Lean further into the hub.
- **Goal completion in the first paragraph** — answer the type/question immediately, not after a Hook → Insight → Action setup. Worth A/B testing on top traffic pages.
- **Hierarchical interlinking** — type pages → trait pages → individual person pages, with links going both directions, mirrors Tier 1/2/3 logic.
- **Title tag + H1 + first paragraph trio** — easy audit to run across `blogs_famous_people` to find pages where any of the three are weak.
- **Caveat for top traffic pages** — for `enneagram-and-mental-illness` and similar high-traffic posts, only light-touch first-paragraph edits; don't restructure.
