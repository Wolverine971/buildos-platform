## Core Thesis

Etiquette is having a moment because tech is no longer a "cute sideshow" — when you're asking people to trust you with their data, jobs, and businesses, the soft signals of trustworthiness compound into hard outcomes. Sam reframes etiquette away from arcane rules and toward a single operating goal: **show up in any room with a low heart rate.** That means swapping a scarcity mindset ("this is my one shot") for an abundance mindset ("this is part of the story, not the entire story") so you stop projecting Energizer-Bunny desperation. Most of "good etiquette" is just signaling: I am calm, I am paying attention, I respect the room, and you can trust me. Silicon Valley has been telling founders none of this matters and only the product matters — Sam thinks that advice is dishonest and is costing founders deals.

## Mental Models & Frameworks

- **Low heart rate.** The single north star. Every rule in the book is downstream of "do you look calm and in control?" If you're flustered, late-running, over-questioning, or over-tipping conspicuously, your heart rate is showing.
- **Abundance vs. scarcity.** Walk in like this is _one_ of many opportunities, not your only shot. Even if it actually is your one shot, project the calm of someone with options.
- **Give-to-get / ping-pong.** A conversation is a ping-pong rally, not single-player. Index toward asking questions, but never hit 6 questions in a row — that's an inquisition. Hit the ball back and let them hit it back.
- **Signal the effort, not just the outcome.** What matters is that the other person can _see you trying_ (eye contact, name repetition, tailored fit, clean closet behind you on Zoom) — even imperfect execution earns credit.
- **Plausible deniability for bad etiquette.** Many tactics work by giving you and the other person an out. The "let it hang" name trick (below) is the canonical example.
- **B&D trick.** Bread on the left, Drinks on the right — make the lowercase letter "b" and "d" with your hands to find your bread plate and water glass at a crowded table.
- **Know the rules so you can break them on purpose.** Sam's "tuxedo Tuesdays" at Bain. Breaking a rule with cultural awareness is a power move; breaking it accidentally just looks classless.
- **Etiquette as invisibility.** The whole point is to _get out of the way_ of the conversation — like Kindle hardware disappearing so you can read. If your napkin placement, tip size, or fragrance is memorable, you've lost.
- **TCP handshake / modem crash analogy.** Small talk is the protocol handshake — annoying noise that has to happen before real bandwidth opens up. Don't skip it.

## The Tips & Tricks

### Introductions & Entering a Room

- **Be early — but not weirdly early.** Five minutes late spikes your heart rate; thirty minutes early makes you look desperate. Buffer enough to _let them keep you waiting_. If you do run late, just apologize once and move on — don't make it a five-alarm fire.
- **Strong handshake, don't crush.** "Practice on your friends." Firm, not bone-grinding.
- **Repeat names back immediately.** _"Hey Lenny, it's great to meet you."_ Forces you to encode the name and signals "you're not just a number to me."
- **Use "great to see you," never "nice to meet you."** Works whether or not you've met before. Sam's own wife caught him on this before they were dating. People aren't dumb — they'll quietly grant you the cover.
- **The "let it hang" partner intro for forgotten names.** When you can't remember someone's name and your partner is with you: _"Jessica, I want to introduce you…"_ — make eye contact with the stranger and let the silence hang. Their etiquette training kicks in: _"Hi, I'm Bob, nice to meet you."_ You recover the name without exposing the gap.
- **Always introduce your partner first.** Bring them into the conversation rather than parking them on the side.
- **If someone else is late, do not make them feel bad.** Don't be the indignant founder. You will burn the next 25 minutes setting the wrong dynamic.
- **Eye contact is a respect signal.** Especially valuable in a screen-fragmented world. If you're neurodivergent, the _effort_ still reads — just make it.
- **Don't be sycophantic with famous people, but don't pretend you don't know who they are either.** "Hi I'm Sam, who are you?" to Mark Zuckerberg is ridiculous. "You're the most important person I've ever met" is worse. Treat them like a person, play the iterative game, let _them_ offer their email or phone.

### Engaging Conversations

- **Welcome people into the conversation; don't form scrums around big names.** Power-dynamic flocking is a low-status tell.
- **Ask questions — but cap it at ping-pong cadence.** Six questions in a row feels like the Inquisition. The best conversations are a give-to-get rally.
- **Match vocabulary to the room.** Don't drop into J or jargon with a professor; don't go full PhD with a 12-year-old. Mirror their level so they relax.
- **Leave them wanting more.** Goal: they walk away thinking _"I'd love to hear from them again"_ — even better, walk across the room and tell someone else _"you should talk to Lenny."_ Don't dump your life story.
- **Have a small file of crowd-pleaser stories.** Short, build-on-able, capable of triggering a back-and-forth — not a 10-minute monologue.
- **Internalize "this is part of the story, not the entire story."** Even if it really is your one shot, project the calm of abundance. Listen to Eminem's "Lose Yourself" _before_ you walk in, not during.

### Hygiene & Dress

- **Your scent should not be noticeable in any direction.** "Don't smell like \_\_\_\_, but don't douse yourself either." Fragrance should never be memorable.
- **Don't be covered in schmutz.** Basic — but skipped more than you'd think.
- **Anticipate the room's dress level and don't violate it in either direction.** Tuxedo to a business-casual meeting is as bad as showing up disheveled. Both say "I have no respect for this room."
- **Dress one level up — not two, not three.** Easy way to win. You can drop down a notch (take off the jacket) but you can't dress _up_ mid-meeting.
- **Fit > brand > price.** A well-fitting $20 shirt beats a misfitting $500 one. Most people can intuit fit; nobody can price-check your blazer.
- **No Rolex on a startup founder.** Classless. Reads as trying too hard, signals you're not aware of the room.
- **When in doubt, just ask the dress code.** Asking shows confidence and humility. NYC vs SF have different defaults — you're not expected to know everything.
- **If you don't dress well: find one person you know who does and ask them.**

### Dining

- **Don't order the most expensive thing on the menu.** The investor doesn't care about the cost — they care that you're the type of person who's "truly insensitive to what things cost."
- **Order middle-of-the-pack to last.** Let someone else set the tone (starters? wine? long meal?) and match it.
- **Always offer to pay** — within reason. Expect to be turned down. Don't offer to _split_; offer to _pay_. Skip this if it's a $10K dinner or they ordered a $1K bottle.
- **Even with billionaires, offer to pay on a normal meal.** They'll decline, but the gesture reads as "I treat us as equals" — which almost nobody does.
- **If you do pay for a famous/wealthy person, tip the way they would.** They'd tip ~100%. Otherwise the gesture rebounds badly.
- **B and D for bread and drinks.** Make the letters with your thumbs and forefingers. B for bread (left), D for drinks (right).
- **Knife blade points inward when you set it down.** "You don't want to stab your partner next to you."
- **Napkin in your lap. Not your collar, not the side.** Don't make your napkin placement memorable in any way.
- **If you order a nice wine, offer the sommelier a taste.** Generous, low-cost, classy.
- **Split the bill evenly when peers; don't itemize "who got the flambe."**

### Tipping

- **Tip a lot. 20% is the floor; 30% is fine; >50% is silly and memorable.**
- **Don't let your tip be memorable in either direction.** "I have stories where someone tipped so much I remember it a decade later — that's the _only_ thing I remember about the night."
- **For the holiday/service-worker tipping tangle (gardener, shades installer, etc.):** he doesn't have a clean answer — punts on it as the joke "next book" / AI tipping app idea. Net advice: when in doubt, err high; you don't tip the shade installer if you already paid a project price.

### B&D Trick

See above — bread left, drinks right, lowercase b/d with your hands. Single tactic, single section.

### Humor

- **Humor proves social mastery — but only if it lands.** Telling a joke that's _one degree_ over the line is the ultimate "I'm comfortable here" signal. A joke that nobody laughs at is a high-cost failure.
- **Calibrate the joke to the room.** No knock-knocks with adults; no off-color sex jokes until you're confident in the room.
- **Keep a ranked file of jokes.** Sam keeps one in a Bear (formerly Evernote) note, "loosely ranked from least offensive to most offensive." Useful because you forget jokes when you need them.
- **Don't be remembered as "the comedian."** Humor is salt, not the meal.

### Self-Deprecating Humor

- **Make fun of yourself, not other people.** Self-deprecation is always low-risk. Making fun of others requires deep familiarity and reads as disparaging the second the wavelength is off.

### Winding Down Conversations

- **When the conversation is over, leave gracefully.** Don't loiter. The worst is the awkward standstill after the energy drops.
- **"I'm going to go grab a drink" is not an invitation to follow them to the bar.** Read the signal.
- **Hand them off to someone else as a graceful exit:** _"Have you met Steve? Let's go meet Steve."_ Then peel away.
- **Give the other person plausible deniability that you're winding down.** Don't be too overt; let everyone keep face.

### Scheduling

- **Default is NOT Calendly.** If you're the less senior or less busy person, ask the other person when _they_ are free and bend your schedule. Calendly defaulting up the hierarchy is disrespectful — Sam ranted hard enough about this once that he became Calendly's biggest organic growth driver of the month.
- **If you must send availability, give real options — three minimum, all genuinely workable.**
- **The reverse-pattern script Lenny uses (Sam endorses):** _"Do you have a Calendly or something I could use to book? (If not, here's mine in case it's easier.)"_ — puts the work on you, not them.
- **Reschedules: maximum notice + maximum flexibility.** Once you're the one rescheduling, you bend to _their_ available slots, not yours.
- **Time zones — actually check.** Don't propose 4am EST.
- **Respect EAs and PAs like equals on the team.** Thank them, follow up, don't be transactional. The story: when his company sold to Airbnb, the dealmaker deliberately built relationships with the Airbnb EA/office manager — gatekeepers move things.
- **Don't overdo the EA charm offensive.** Showing up with flowers for the EA is its own bad look unless you're very confident.
- **Sam's contrarian admission on his own calendar:** he _does_ move his calendar more than most people, and disagrees with the "all meetings are equal" flat-hierarchy view. If Obama wants to meet at 4am, you make it work.

### Communication & Email

- **Proofread. Get to the point. Assume the recipient is busy.**
- **You owe at least an acknowledgment quickly.** Sam disagrees with the "email is not a contract" crowd. Not a 12-page reply — but don't leave people hanging.
- **Re-read your email as if you're receiving it.** Ten paragraphs feels like a forced 10-minute monologue.
- **No emojis in business email.** Treat them like jokes — high-risk, culturally loaded, often imply "AI-generated this." If they send a smiley, you can match. Custom-color emojis or custom-built iOS emojis read as "this person spends time on emoji packs instead of finding product-market fit."
- **Order of recipients matters.** First name in the To: line is who it's really for. CC is "have a copy, no immediate response expected." If the EA is first and the CEO is fourth, you've done it wrong.
- **Reply order should follow CC order.** If you're the fifth CC, you don't reply first.
- **NEVER put someone you're talking _about_ in the To: field — even just to check the spelling of their name.** Sam's worst-ever faux pas. Just... don't.
- **Never reply-all on a BCC.** Outs the BCC. Bad.

### Meetings (Physical)

- **Arrive 10–15 minutes early. NOT an hour.** If you're an hour early, walk around the block — don't sit "six coffees deep with the receptionist."
- **Open with small talk, then flip to business.** Modem-crash handshake. Even when transparent, it signals "I'm a normal person before I'm a counterparty."
- **Stand to shake hands.** Don't shake hands seated.

### Virtual Meetings

- **Camera on.** Sam admits he violates this, but knowingly — and pays the cost.
- **Dress appropriately for video, not just from the waist up in theory.**
- **Have an appropriate background.** Make the bed. _Close your closet_ — founder pet peeve. He doesn't love virtual backgrounds either; he'd rather see a real (tidy) room.

### Cleaning Up

- **Always offer to take your coffee cup / Diet Coke to the kitchen — even when staff will obviously clean it.** Sam's partner Kevin Collard is "maniacal" about this with LPs. The signal: I'm aware there's a mess and I'm not too important to deal with it.
- **Founders pitching at VC offices: don't leave the cup on the table without asking where to put it.** Apparently Garry Tan-flavored Sam-bait.

### Exiting & Follow-up

- **Stand when others stand to leave.** Stand to shake hands.
- **Send a thank-you note.** Short. Specific. _"Thanks for taking the time, here's what I got out of it."_ Don't write an essay.
- **Don't take phone calls during the wind-down.** If a call comes in, hit "I'll call you right back" — don't pick up and wave.
- **Don't make a production of leaving.** In any large group, the **Irish goodbye is often best** — thank one person (the host) on your way out and disappear. Mass hugs/farewells are too much.
- **If you accidentally accepted a meeting with the wrong person (name off by one letter, etc.) — do the meeting anyway.** "Once you're committed to the meeting, you're doing the meeting."

### AI Corner

- Sam built **lettermeme.com** — a personal news aggregator that ingests all the newsletters he doesn't have time to read and turns them into a daily grid of cartoons, plus an email digest. Built first via vibe-coding (Cursor + DigitalOcean + Cloudflare), then friends with real engineering chops cleaned it up.
- His take on vibe-coding: great for v1, doesn't scale — eventually you need real engineers.

### Contrarian Corner

- **"Seed VCs investing in companies branded as AI companies are going to lose an impossibly large amount of money."** AI businesses are too capital-intensive for seed math (the seed investors in OpenAI made ~25x at a $500B valuation — _terrible_ return for the defining company of the era).
- **AI is infrastructure, not a category.** Not using AI in your startup is like not using cloud in 2010 — but "we are the AI [X]" companies are going to zero.
- **Every generation craves a "Terra Nova" myth** — the Frontier, the Internet, mobile, crypto, now AI-as-god. AI fits the cultural appetite for belief; that's why narrative-driven, capital-consumptive plays still get funded. They mostly won't return.
- **What he actually invests in:** companies enabled by AI's _cultural_ implications — e.g. Sublime Security, Outtake (the "Voight-Kampff problem": real or bot?). And businesses that will be disrupted _by_ AI but aren't themselves AI businesses.

## Contrarian Takes

- **Etiquette matters more, not less, in 2026.** SV's "just build product" line is dishonest advice that costs founders deals.
- **Default scheduling tool should NOT be Calendly.** Especially when you're the junior/less-busy party — bend your calendar to theirs.
- **All meetings are NOT equal.** Sam openly disagrees with flat-hierarchy scheduling — his calendar moves more than most people's, and that's correct.
- **No emojis in business email** — they're high-risk, culturally loaded, and increasingly read as "AI-generated."
- **AI seed investing is a value trap.** Even OpenAI was a "middling-at-best seed investment." Run away from companies branded as AI companies; invest in companies enabled by AI's downstream cultural implications.
- **Most "is this AI useful?" hype is religion.** Every generation manufactures a Terra Nova narrative.
- **Make fun of yourself, never others.** Standard, but the framing — that disparaging humor is a _high-risk maneuver_ requiring deep familiarity — is sharper than the cliché.
- **Tip a lot, but don't make tipping memorable.** Big tips that get noticed are vanity, not generosity.

## What Founders Should Steal (BuildOS context: investor meetings, user calls, sales)

1. **Adopt "low heart rate" as the operating principle for every external conversation.** Investor calls, user research, sales demos — your single job is to not look like the Energizer Bunny. Internalize "this is part of the story, not the entire story." This _one_ reframe re-prices your behavior on every other tip.
2. **Ping-pong, not interrogation.** On user calls especially, founders default to a question-list checklist. Sam's framing — "the second it feels like the Inquisition, you've lost" — is the single highest-leverage adjustment for discovery calls. Hit the ball back; share a small piece of context or a hypothesis; let them react.
3. **Leave people wanting more.** Investor meetings: don't dump the full story. Get them curious enough to want a second meeting. Same for sales convos with prospects — under-deliver on monologue, over-deliver on listening.
4. **Email discipline: short, no emojis, recipient order matters.** As a solo founder you're sending a lot of cold and warm intros — the To/CC ordering and length are subtle status signals. Always assume your reader is busy; always reply with at least an acknowledgment.
5. **Camera on. Closet closed. Bed made.** For a remote-first founder doing Zoom investor pitches and user calls, this is the cheapest possible upgrade in perceived professionalism.
6. **Always offer to pay; tip generously but invisibly; clean up your own coffee cup.** Especially with LPs/VCs/advisors. Sam's partner Kevin's "maniacal" cup-clearing habit is exactly the kind of unconscious signal that compounds over years.
7. **Default away from Calendly when you're the one asking.** When you're cold-emailing investors or partners, ask _their_ availability and bend yours. Reserve Calendly for inbound and for symmetric peer relationships. The Lenny script — _"Do you have a Calendly? If not, here's mine in case it's easier."_ — is the right hedge.

The deepest takeaway for a founder doing 5–10 external conversations a day: **etiquette is not a separate skill — it's the bandwidth tax you pay so the actual content of the conversation can land.** A founder running with a high heart rate is leaking trust the entire meeting; the product never gets a fair hearing. The 30+ tactics are downstream of that one mechanic.
