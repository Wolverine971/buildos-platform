# Ash Maurya — The One Customer-Interview Question

## Skill Combo Links

This source contributes to these multi-source skill combo indexes:

- [Product Strategy Skill Combos](../../../../research/youtube-library/skill-combo-indexes/PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md): Customer discovery through switching forces

> "Don't measure what customers will do in the future, because that's unreliable. Instead, ask them what they did in the past."
> — Ash Maurya

After running 1000+ customer interviews across seven products, Maurya argues 90% of the questions founders ask are useless. He has distilled it down to a single question that flips the entire frame from opinion to behavior — and that question maps cleanly to the four customer forces (push, pull, inertia, friction) from Jobs-to-Be-Done.

---

## 1. Why Most Customer Interviews Fail

Founders default to questions that feel natural but produce noise:

- "What features do you want?" → wish list of features they'll never use
- "Would you pay for this?" → lowballed answers
- "Would you use this?" → polite lies

The case-study founder did 47 interviews, got positive answers to all of them, burned $80k, launched, got 3 sign-ups. The diagnosis: she optimized for validation ("tell me I'm right"). **Validation does not predict behavior. Compliments don't convert to revenue.**

The golden rule:

> Don't ask what they will do in the future. Ask what they did in the past.

**BuildOS relevance:** Most BuildOS user research instinctively asks "would you use…" / "what do you wish BuildOS did." Reframe interview scripts around past behavior with adjacent tools (Notion, Apple Notes, Roam, Things, paper notebooks) before asking anything BuildOS-specific.

---

## 2. The One Question

> **"Walk me through the last time you encountered this situation and tell me where you struggled."**

Why it works: when you put someone inside a specific recent situation and ask them to play back what actually happened, they stop generalizing and rationalizing. They give you facts as a series of events where one thing led to the next. Your job is to build a mental model of the four forces:

| Force        | What it reveals                                                         |
| ------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Push**     | What broke in their old solution / triggered the search                 |
| **Pull**     | What they're trying to accomplish with the new solution                 |
| **Inertia**  | What's keeping them with the status quo (sunk cost, habit, familiarity) |
| **Friction** | What's making the switch hard (time, money, anxiety, learning curve)    |

You can only learn these by asking about real past behavior, not hypotheticals.

---

## 3. The Worked Example: Bad vs. Good Question

| Question                                                              | Type | Answer quality                                 |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| "What feature would you want in a CRM?"                               | Bad  | Wish list — features they'd never actually use |
| "Walk me through the last time you switched CRMs. What triggered it?" | Good | Story with all four forces visible             |

Sample good answer (CRM):

> "Our sales team hit 10 reps and our spreadsheets completely broke. We lost a $50,000 deal because two reps called the same prospect. I had 48 hours to fix it or my VP was going to quit."

What that answer encodes:

- **Push:** Lost $50K deal + VP threatening to quit
- **Pull:** Need to scale to 10+ reps without chaos
- **Friction:** 48-hour deadline + anxiety of learning something new
- **Inertia:** Spreadsheets were free and already familiar

This single answer gives you stakes, criteria, and constraints. You now know how to design, price, and pitch.

## 4. The Three-Step Interview Structure

### Step 1 — Search for the trigger

Open with: _"What happened right before you started looking for a solution?"_
Reveals: the **push** — the moment the old way stopped working.

### Step 2 — Map the journey

Walk them step by step from the trigger to the buying / using moment.
Reveals:

- **Pull** — what they researched / what they wanted
- **Friction** — what almost stopped them
- **Inertia** — what they had to overcome to switch

### Step 3 — Extract hiring criteria

Ask: _"What made you choose Solution X over Solution Y?"_
Reveals what they actually value (vs. what they say they value).

> **The hiring criteria is the spec for the product that wins.** If your product over-delivers on those criteria, it gets hired across competitive alternatives.

## 5. Maurya's Own Lean Spark Pivot Example

Bad question: _"Would you use AI for business model design?"_

Good question: _"Walk me through the last time you tried to validate a business idea. What happened?"_

Real answers:

- "I took a $2,000 course, got a 60-page workbook, never finished it — too overwhelming."
- "I hired a $5,000 advisor. They gave me a 20-page strategy deck. Still sitting on my shelf."

Forces extracted:

- **Push:** Real problem in their startup needing fixing
- **Pull:** Wanting systematic guidance, not more theory or guesswork
- **Friction:** Already paid for education/consultants but couldn't implement the advice
- **Inertia:** Just keep doing the same thing, hoping for a lucky break

That reframed Lean Spark from "another course" → "an interactive AI co-founder that diagnoses, prioritizes, and helps implement." Launched January 2026 with ~100 early adopters.

---

## 6. The Hidden Time Cost

> **20–30 hours to properly analyze 10 interviews — and that's if you know what you're looking for.**

It's easy to miss real signals. Example: hearing "I need better reporting" → you build a dashboard. But the real push might have nothing to do with reporting and everything to do with data analysis. Different feature, different product.

Maurya's three pillars of good interview analysis:

1. Build a mental model that captures the key insights
2. Audit insights against **facts** the customer said (not opinions)
3. Find actionable patterns across many interviews

(He uses Lean Spark to compress this loop, but the principles are tool-agnostic.)

## Top Takeaways for BuildOS

1. **Replace "would you use" / "what do you wish for" with "walk me through the last time."** Every BuildOS user-research script should default to past-tense, behavior-anchored questions.

2. **Document the four forces for each interview.** Use push/pull/inertia/friction as the standard frame. Not feature requests. Not feature feedback. Forces.

3. **Hiring criteria → product spec.** Whatever the customer says made them pick their current tool over alternatives is the spec BuildOS must over-deliver on. (For BuildOS, common existing alternatives: Notion, Apple Notes, Roam, Obsidian, Things, paper.)

4. **Bias toward stakes-rich examples.** Look for interviews where the push has real money or real consequences attached ("lost a deal", "VP quit", "missed a deadline"). Stories without stakes don't predict purchase.

5. **The activation/onboarding implication.** If the entry point of BuildOS doesn't relieve a recent push the user just experienced, the product won't get hired. Onboarding should ask for the recent triggering event, not for "your goals."

6. **Consider an interview-script template in `docs/marketing/`.** Codify the 3-step structure (trigger → journey → hiring criteria) so anyone running a BuildOS user interview produces compatible data.
