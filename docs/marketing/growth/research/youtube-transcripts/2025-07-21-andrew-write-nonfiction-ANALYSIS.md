# Analysis — "I'm Politely Begging You to Write Nonfiction"

**Speaker:** Andrew (Writing with Andrew / Goldfinch Media) — creative writing teacher, primary specialty in poetry, regular essayist.
**Premise:** Creative nonfiction is the most overlooked of the "big three" genres (fiction, poetry, nonfiction). Andrew makes the case that writers should add it to their practice — and breaks down what it actually is, common objections, and what you gain from writing it.

---

## Core Thesis

> "Where fiction tells us stories and poetry shares lyrical experiences, nonfiction puts us in contact with a human being trying to make sense of the world we're in and the lives we live."

Creative nonfiction is **a mind at work** on real material. It's not a Wikipedia entry, not a research report, not a chronological dump of your last two years. It's lived experience filtered through a unique, compelling perspective.

The word "essay" itself comes from the French for "to attempt / to try." The heart of the form is **the attempt to think through something** — not plot, not imagery.

---

## The Distinction That Matters Most

| Form                    | What it shows                                                       |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Fiction                 | What imagined humans do in imagined high-stakes scenarios           |
| Poetry                  | How humans experience and feel in response to life                  |
| **Creative nonfiction** | **How a specific person encounters and processes their experience** |

> "The hallmark of the personal essay is its intimacy… essays show us a mind at work." — Philip Lopate (paraphrased by Andrew)

**Wikipedia-style writing is nonfiction. It is not creative nonfiction.** Creative nonfiction shares a _perspective on being a person_, not just information.

---

## The Three Objections (and Andrew's Answers)

### Objection 1: "Real life is boring. I want to escape into fiction."

**Counter:** Have you lived real life? It's not boring. One of his favorite essays is literally about a father digging a hole in the backyard.

- **Tip:** You can write about boring things in engaging ways and still produce a compelling piece.
- "Nothing is more interesting to people than other people."
- Escape isn't only into imagination — escape into the life of someone unlike you and come away affirmed by your shared humanity.

### Objection 2: "I'm not comfortable writing about myself."

**Two-part counter:**

**(a) Readers aren't experiencing it the way you fear they are.** They're forming judgments about the _writing_, not about you. Experienced readers don't take it as confession.

**(b) The "you" on the page is a constructed persona, not the flesh-and-blood you.** This is the most actionable insight in the video:

> "The essayist is making a character of themself… positioning those traits already in us under the most clearly focused, sharply defined light. It's a subtractive process. You need to cut away the inessentials and highlight just those features in your personality that most quickly characterize you, preferably those that lead to the most intense contradictions and ambivalences." — Lopate

**Practical move:** When discussing autobiographical essays, say _"the speaker"_ — not _"you."_ This isn't pedantry; it's a working separation that gives you plausible deniability and lets you write more honestly.

**Bonus:** Nonfiction doesn't even require writing about yourself. He cites an essay about a famous insurance-company spokesperson as an example — your unique mind on something external still counts.

### Objection 3: "You want me to write a research paper?"

**Counter:** No. Creative nonfiction is doing creative things on a foundation of reality.

Concrete prompts Andrew offers as examples:

- Tell the story of your first remembered Thanksgiving but weave in reflections on how miserable you were in that town downwind of the turkey farm.
- Use the mannerly format of an academic article to write a wild, undisciplined meditation on your favorite chain restaurant — complete with absurd footnotes and ridiculous citations.
- Rant about hangnails for seven pages.

## The Three Benefits (Why You Should Write It)

### 1. It develops your **voice**.

- Most fiction in the last 100 years celebrates "minimally invasive narration" — readers want story, not the author's personality.
- Nonfiction is the opposite: **personality and voice are exactly what readers come for.**
- "An essay with an absent speaker is likely to break. Your voice is the hook."
- If you "still need to find your voice," it may be because you've been writing exclusively from omniscient third with no personal investment. Nonfiction forces voice into the foreground.

### 2. It develops your **style**.

- Fiction readers will call elaborate prose a "distraction" or "purple."
- Poetry's compression pushes sentences in specific directions.
- **Essays give you pages of meandering thoughts to play with** — room for stylistic experimentation other genres don't permit.
- Style commentators (in Andrew's two style courses across two schools, plus his own research) keep holding up _nonfiction sentences_ as the best of English prose: Wendell Berry, E.B. White, William Hazlitt.
- Why: where the selling point is the speaker's character, a strong stylistic personality is essential.

### 3. It develops your sense of **form and structure**.

- Fiction has its story-structure schemes. Poetry has its set forms.
- Essays have always been freer — "more determined by the needs of the piece than the received tradition."
- Forms to experiment with:
    - **Braided essay**
    - **Hermit crab essay** (essay that borrows the shape of another non-essay form — recipe, FAQ, instruction manual)
    - **Meandering diatribe**
    - **One-paragraph micro essay**
- The only constraint: the piece follows the contours of your thoughts in a productive way. _How_ it does that is up to you.

## Tips & Tricks (Extracted)

### Craft Tips

1. **Treat yourself as a character, not a confession.** Dramatize the persona; subtract the inessentials; sharpen the contradictions.
2. **Lean into contradictions and ambivalences** — they're the most characterizing material, per Lopate.
3. **Refer to the on-page self as "the speaker."** Maintains the working separation between writer and persona.
4. **Pick boring subjects on purpose.** A hole in the backyard. A trip to the pediatrician. The migratory habits of a warbler. The skill is in the rendering, not the topic.
5. **Read widely in contemporary essays** before deciding nonfiction "isn't for you." Reservation usually comes from school exposure to dry textbooks, not living essayists.
6. **Use form as a creative material.** Borrow the shape of an academic paper, a recipe, footnotes, citations — and break their tone on purpose.
7. **Let your sentences live.** Nonfiction tolerates — even rewards — the stylistic flourishes fiction readers reject.
8. **Voice ≠ finding it. Voice = exercising it.** You already have a voice; nonfiction is the genre that makes you _use_ it.

### Reading Recommendations (linked in the video description)

- **Eric LeMay — "Hole"** (River Teeth Journal) — the father-and-son backyard hole essay.
- **Richie Zaborowske — "It's About the Size of a Clenched Fist"** (Brevity) — the single-paragraph "last hangover" essay.
- **Amy Leach — "Warbler Delight"** (Identity Theory) — the warbler meditation.
- **Philip Lopate** — anthology / introduction to the personal essay tradition.
- Style examples: **Wendell Berry, E.B. White, William Hazlitt.**

### Hidden Benefits Andrew Lists at the End

- Process your own experiences.
- Find meaning where you thought there wasn't any.
- Reckon with family histories.
- Articulate parts of yourself previously unexpressed.
- Play with ideas; learn how to think through things.

## What's Worth Stealing (For 9takes / Blog Voice)

Several of Andrew's points map directly onto how 9takes blogs already work — and a couple sharpen the brand voice further:

1. **"Speaker" vs. "you" framing.** When writing celebrity analyses or community posts, the persona on the page is a constructed lens — already true for 9takes' tone. Naming it explicitly ("the speaker," "the take") gives plausible deniability for sharper opinions.
2. **Contradictions are the most characterizing material.** This is a direct upgrade for personality-analysis posts: don't sand off a person's contradictions — sharpen them.
3. **Hermit crab and braided forms** are underused in personality/Enneagram blogging. Worth experimenting: a Type 5 essay shaped like a research paper, footnotes and all. A braided essay that interleaves a celebrity's interview quotes with the writer's own observations.
4. **Voice is the hook, not the topic.** The "see the emotions behind every take" tagline already implies this; the video gives explicit permission to lean further into it.
5. **Boring on purpose.** The "digging a hole" essay is the proof. Mundane Enneagram-flavored situations (a Type 6 in line at the DMV) can outperform big dramatic setups if the rendering is strong.

## Summary in One Sentence

Creative nonfiction is the genre where **your voice, style, and a constructed-persona version of yourself** are the entire point — and it's the fastest way to develop all three because the form is built to demand them.
