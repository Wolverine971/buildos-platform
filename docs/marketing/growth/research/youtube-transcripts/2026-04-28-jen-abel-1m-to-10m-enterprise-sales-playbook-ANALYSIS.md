# Jen Abel — $1M → $10M Enterprise Sales Playbook

Analysis and tip extraction from Lenny's Podcast interview with Jen Abel (co-founder JJellyfish, ex-founder-sales coach to early-stage startups, now GM Enterprise at State Affairs).

This is a sequel to her first Lenny appearance, which covered $0 → $1M ARR (founder-led sales). This conversation covers the *next* phase: $1M → $10M ARR in enterprise.

---

## Core Thesis

Selling to enterprise is a fundamentally different game from selling to SMBs. There is no "mid-market." The biggest mistake founders make is **bleeding the two games together** — SMB pricing, SMB hires, SMB deal motions applied to enterprise prospects (or vice versa). Pick a lane. Almost everything in this playbook flows from that.

Underneath that: enterprise sales is **art, not science**. It's deal-crafting, vision-casting, and relationship-building — not playbook execution. The founder's job is to learn this game well enough to teach it before handing it to a salesperson.

---

## The Big Counterintuitive Bets

These are the takes that cut hardest against conventional advice:

| Conventional advice | Jen's take |
|---|---|
| Don't go after Walmart/Stripe/Tesla — they're slow | Go after them *first*. Tier-one logos are the early adopters because they have to keep their #1 spot. |
| Land cheap ($10-20k), expand later | $10-20k pricing will trap you. Land at **$75-150k** or you'll never escape the math. |
| Sell to the problem | Sell to the **gap / opportunity** — vision cast. Problem-selling is "playbook selling" and feels salesy. |
| Build product-only, no services | **Sell services first.** It's what enterprises know how to buy. Use service revenue to get in the door, then guide them to product. |
| Channel partners (Deloitte, Accenture) will scale you | They won't. They're consultants, not visionaries. There are 100 of you on their list. |
| Hire a senior VP of Sales from a big company | Their old brand did the trust-building. At your stage you need someone who can "cosplay a founder." |
| Use Apollo/Clay/Outreach for outbound | At enterprise ACVs, **manual outreach beats tooling**. AI tools all pull from the same databases. Take the back door. |
| Don't bother prospects on weekends | Email a chief legal officer at a hedge fund on a *Saturday* — one sentence — and they'll respond. |

## Tips & Tricks (organized by topic)

### 1. The Myth of the Mid-Market

- **There is no mid-market.** Anyone who claims to sell mid-market is either selling upper-end SMB or lower-end enterprise. Force yourself to pick.
- **Selling to a 100-person company ≠ selling to a 1,000-person company.** No hybrid approach exists.
- **SMB is marketing-led; enterprise is sales-led.** That dichotomy drives every downstream decision (pricing, hiring, motion).
- **Power laws apply to company size.** The Fortune 1000 dwarfs the so-called middle. There is almost nothing in the gap.

### 2. Target Tier-One Logos First (Counterintuitive)

- **Tier-one logos = the actual early adopters.** Walmart, Tesla, Nvidia, McDonald's, Exxon, UnitedHealthcare — they *have* to keep their #1 spot, so they're hungry for any alpha.
- **Their #2/#3/#4 competitors copy whatever #1 does** — so winning the leader unlocks the rest of the industry as referenceable demand.
- **Tier-one logos attract the best executives**, who individually love trying new tech.
- **VCs love tier-one logos.** Closing one shifts your fundraising story and your hiring story.
- **Get the founder involved fast.** Everyone wants to talk to a founder.

### 3. Vision-Casting > Problem-Solving

- **Sell to the gap, not the problem.** Problem-selling is technical and feels like every other salesperson. Vision-casting sells the future.
- **Use the "Mario on Blast" frame** (Kathy Sierra): don't sell the mushroom, sell the customer becoming a superhero.
- **Lead with alpha**: "Right now you can do X. We give you access to Y so you can move further upstream / faster / smarter than competitors."
- **Cursor example:** Don't pitch "your team will be more productive." Pitch "the 10x engineers won't even join your company unless you use Cursor." Talent attraction > productivity gain.
- **In the AI era, vision-casting is easier than ever** — every enterprise is asking "what's our AI strategy?" so opportunity-framing is what they're already buying.

### 4. Pricing — Don't Anchor Too Low

- **Land at $75-150k.** That's the band enterprises are used to spending on a first contract.
- **The expansion math breaks at $10k anchors.** Going $10k → $15k is a 50% jump that feels normal. $10k → $100k requires 15x value justification — almost impossible to defend, especially when AI is now reading procurement contracts.
- **A $10k deal can take 9 months to close** because procurement still gets involved. Same procurement pain, 1/10 the revenue.
- **Land contained, not cheap.** Sell $150k for a defined scope, not "the farm." Plant seeds for year 2 and year 3 expansion.
- **Discounting is a qualification signal — in reverse.** If they're nickel-and-diming you, they're not bought in. The best clients won't grind you on price.
- **One $100k client > ten $10k clients.** The ten will distract you and won't pull product roadmap forward.
- **Reframe pricing so you "co-author" it.** "$150k engagement, but I can flex bigger or smaller in year one if year two steps up — how do we get this done so when you go to bat, it's a win?"

### 5. Don't Play the SMB Game With an Enterprise Buyer

- **If Walmart shows up asking for 3 PLG seats — don't sell them 3 PLG seats.** You will trap yourself at SMB pricing forever.
- **Document pricing only at the level you want to expand from.** Anything cheaper sets a permanent reference point.
- **AI procurement tools will catch the gap.** "You charged Lenny $1k last year and now want $100k — defend it." Almost impossible.
- **Even if your land is small, structure it like enterprise**: senior exec sponsor, written value plan, contained scope.

### 6. Design Partners — Use Them Right

- **Design partners are the *hardest* logos to upsell.** Don't budget them as your million-dollar pipeline.
- **Best design partners = mid-cutting-edge tech companies in the Fortune 1000.** They get experimentation and they want startup-vibe energy.
- **Set framing on day one:** "You'll get a 30% concession in perpetuity for being in the trenches with us. Here's where pricing is going. Here's the year-2 trajectory."
- **Take a little skin in the game** — even small dollars — so they prioritize you internally.
- **80/20 rule on design partner feedback.** 80% is noise (the old way). 20% is the gold. Founder's job is to filter.
- **Tell them what you can't do.** Builds trust. "Here's where we are today. Here's what we will let you do in 6 months. It's ugly. Want to be on this journey?"
- **A design partner who converts to full rollout is a flag for product-market fit** — it's the hardest customer to convert in the whole funnel.

### 7. Enterprise Sales is Art, Not Science

- **No two enterprise deals look the same.** Deal-crafting is the actual skill.
- **Bundle "expensive-to-them, cheap-to-you" extras**: a custom integration you'd build anyway, a speaker slot at your event, a co-authored case study.
- **The product is not just the product.** It's pricing + framing + opportunity + access + relationship.
- **Avoid becoming "one of three."** As soon as the buyer is comparing you against alternatives, you've lost. Differentiation > comparison.
- **Reframe, don't argue.** When buyer says "we already use X," agree → "X is great for that. Here's where we take you upstream from X."

### 8. Services Are the Fastest Way In

- **Enterprises buy services constantly** — it's their largest external budget item and their procurement teams know how to do it.
- **Sell the service; technology powers it on the back end.** They don't need to know at first.
- **Forward-deployed engineers (Palantir model)** are the same idea. Engineer in their office solving problems → that becomes the spec for the product.
- **Once they're paying for the service, guide them to the product.** "You're spending $X on us doing this — let's move you to the tool that's been powering it."
- **Even free services can build trust** (early OpenAI educating CTOs before any product was sold).
- **Pro-rate services into a monthly equivalent** so the buyer sees a $10k/month rhythm vs. a $120k bill.

### 9. Channel Partnerships Don't Work

- **Deloitte / Accenture / McKinsey are not your channel.** They have 100 startups on their list.
- **Consultants are not vision-casters.** They sell the service, not your future.
- **Don't expect them to disseminate you to their clients.** That is not a workable strategy.

### 10. Hiring Your First Enterprise Salesperson

- **Time it to ~$1M ARR with 7-10 customers** so you have pattern recognition to teach.
- **Hire two people simultaneously** (Jason Lemkin's advice Jen endorses) — failure rate is 50%+, comparison helps you see who's good.
- **Profile to look for, in order of preference:**
  1. **Former founder.** Already sold investors and employees. Knows the cosplay.
  2. **Engineer / deep product person with no sales experience.** Doesn't trigger sales-allergy in buyers.
  3. **Experienced rep — but only if they pass the "do you want to buy from this person" test** (Jason Lemkin's pen-test).
- **Avoid:** senior VP of Sales from a giant company. Their previous brand was carrying the trust. Your founder + product can't replicate that yet.
- **Avoid:** junior reps selling to executives. Executives want to talk to peers. Unless the rep is a unicorn, the buyer-rep mismatch kills the deal.
- **The "cosplay a founder" test:** can this person sell the vision, run through walls, deal-craft, and get a future employee excited? If not, pass.
- **Validate with the founder on the first 5 calls.** After 5 calls you know.

### 11. Sales Comp Structure

- **50/50 base/OTE split** is standard for enterprise.
- **8-12% commission rate on tech deals**, ~10% as the round number.
- **Incentives drive enthusiasm.** When a rep can see how much money is possible on a single $250k-$500k deal, energy follows. Don't underprice the deal — you can't attract a great rep with $10k ACVs.
- **Build product that can carry a $250k-$500k price tag.** Your hiring quality follows your deal size.

### 12. Cold Outreach in the AI Era

- **Manual beats tooling for enterprise outbound.** AI SDRs all draw from the same databases — buyers see the same Apollo-pulled email 100x a day.
- **Take the back door, not the front.** Find prospects no one else is hitting.
- **Three-sentence cold emails.** Don't give away the farm. Sell the call, not the product.
- **Say something counterintuitive in the email.** Make the buyer feel they'll learn something on a 15-minute call.
- **Customize the *frame*, not the opening sentence.** AI-customized first lines are a tell. Customize subject line, tone, register, length.
- **Match register to seniority.** "QQ" for peers, tighter and more formal for senior executives.
- **Visual cues from LinkedIn matter** (photo, role tenure, company tenure). Use them to read who they are before writing.
- **Off-hour sends work.** A one-sentence Saturday email to a busy executive can get a reply because there's no inbox noise.
- **You don't need a CRM playbook to start.** Pattern recognition + intuition + reading the news for adjacent prospects is enough at this stage.
- **It's emotional and unteachable** — Jen herself can't fully articulate it. Best salespeople "just have it."

### 13. Relationship Mechanics

- **Enterprise deals close over text, not email.** Once a rep has the relationship, the channel shifts to texting/calling.
- **When a customer calls, you pick up.** That responsiveness is the trust compounder.
- **Co-author the deal with the buyer.** Their reputation is on the line for picking you. Make them feel like they got a win out of you.
- **Buyers buy a new tool once every 2-5 years.** No one likes a new tool unless it changes everything (Figma, Slack). Make sure you're in the "changes everything" frame.
- **Accept "no" fast.** Disqualify on the first call: "I'm getting the vibe this might not be a fit — am I wrong?" Saves the relationship and your time.
- **Ask the questions you're afraid to ask.** "Honestly, are we getting this deal done this year?" Gets you the truth.

### 14. Picking the Right Early Customer

- **Look for "startup-friendly" tier-ones** — companies that have an experimentation muscle.
- **The individual matters more than the logo.** Are they excited to give feedback? Do they buy your vision? Are they OK using a janky tool?
- **Don't oversell.** Most reps over-promise on initial design partners → churn / cancellation. Tell them what you *can't* do — that's what builds trust.
- **The founder's job is to filter feedback.** If a design partner pulls you toward the old way of working, hold the line. 20% of feedback is gold; 80% is the past.

### 15. Enterprise vs. SMB — Founder Self-Assessment

When picking which game to play, ask: **"What game does the founder best understand?"**
- Great marketer with audience instincts → SMB / marketing-led.
- Operator who understands large corporations and can deliver $100k+ value → enterprise.
- Some products start startup-y to grow with their customers (Linear is the canonical example). That works when the product is technical and the bet is "let's help these companies become large together."

### 16. Be Different, Not Better

This is Jen's closing meta-tip:
- **Everyone commoditizes themselves** by mimicking the latest pattern (e.g., everyone now branding their own "forward deployed engineer"). Rename it. Differentiate the language.
- **The whole enterprise game is about "this feels different."** That's what buyers want access to.
- **Don't be afraid to be cringe in service of being different.** Sending 15 individualized notes to high-value prospects is not cringe. Mimicry is.

## How This Applies to BuildOS

A few of these tips are directly relevant to BuildOS's growth context (creator wedge, anti-AI marketing, mostly self-serve PLG today):

- **The "no mid-market" rule cuts both ways.** BuildOS is currently SMB / individual-creator. If we ever try to sell to companies, don't try to "land 3 seats at a tier-one logo" — either commit to a $75-150k enterprise motion or stay SMB-marketing-led. Don't bleed the games.
- **Vision-casting > problem-selling** translates directly to the "thinking environment for people making complex things" positioning. We already lead with the gap (you can't think clearly anymore) instead of the problem (you forgot to write something down).
- **"Be different, not better"** is the entire anti-AI marketing thesis in three words. Worth pinning to the brand-guide one-pager.
- **Manual outreach beats tooling at high ACVs** lines up with the cold-outreach playbook the brand discipline already prefers — don't ship Apollo-templated emails to creators.
- **Services-first as a wedge** is interesting if BuildOS ever needs a high-touch offering for power users / agencies — sell the service, run the platform under the hood, graduate the buyer to product.
- **The "tier-one logos as early adopters" inversion** has a creator analog: the most-followed/most-respected creators in a niche are often more open to early tools than mid-tier creators chasing them, because they have to keep their position. Worth testing in WS09/WS10 outreach.
