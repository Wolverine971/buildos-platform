# Ash Maurya — Talk to Customers, Not Users

## Skill Combo Links

This source contributes to these multi-source skill combo indexes:

- [Product Strategy Skill Combos](../../../../research/youtube-library/skill-combo-indexes/PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md): Customer discovery through switching forces

> "Users and customers are not the same. Not all users become customers and most users have no intention of ever buying a product. The irony is that they often don't know this — and so they think they're trying to help by giving you feedback, but instead they're sending you off on wild goose chases."
> — Naval Ravikant (relayed by Ash Maurya)

The contrarian title masks a precise nuance: Maurya isn't saying don't talk to people — he's saying **most founders talk to the wrong people**. The fix has three components: (1) target paying customers, not free users, (2) avoid the word "problem," (3) anchor on past behavior in specific recent situations.

---

## 1. The Origin Story: Naval's Coaching

Maurya's first SaaS had thousands of free users / month and only a few dozen paying customers. He tried talking to users → got laundry lists of features → built them → users still didn't buy → got new laundry lists. Classic wild-goose chase.

Naval Ravikant pointed out the bug: **"You've been talking to users, not customers."**

The fix:

- Maurya called paying customers
- One was an attorney who told him: _Stop building features. Raise your prices instead._
- He liked the product so much he was afraid the company would go out of business if it didn't charge more.
- Other paying customer conversations sounded similar.

Result: Maurya repositioned the product (no feature changes), 10×'d pricing, doubled the number of paying customers. Free users complained — but they weren't customers anyway.

> "Free users were asking for more and didn't buy when I built it. Paying customers were asking for less and wanted to pay more."

---

## 2. The Three Objections (and Why They're Wrong)

### 2.1 "Customers don't know what they want" (the Henry Ford / faster-horses myth)

Even the misattributed Ford quote contains a customer problem statement. "Faster horses" = "I'm dissatisfied with the performance of my existing alternative for the job of transportation." That's actionable.

> Given the right context, customers can clearly articulate their problems. But it's not the customer's job to know what they want. **That's your job as the entrepreneur.**

### 2.2 "Talking to customers takes too long — let's just run a survey or A/B test"

Surveys assume:

- You already know the right questions
- You already know the right set of possible answers (multiple-choice)

> At the earliest stages of a product, we don't know what we don't know. So surveys only confirm what you already know — they can't uncover the breakthrough insight.

Landing pages assume you already know how to pitch the product. When initial pitches miss, traffic abandons silently and you can't tell _why_. You're stuck running multivariant guessing games — bad headline? bad copy? wrong visuals? wrong price? Each test costs traffic and time.

> Far more efficient to ask one customer face-to-face why they didn't buy.

### 2.3 "I am my own customer"

Scratching your own itch is a fine starting point, but:

- You can't be objective about pricing while role-playing both founder and customer.
- If you have deep prior experience, _use it in customer conversations_ — describe their problem better than they can. They'll grant you authority and treat you like an expert (the doctor effect — accurate symptom diagnosis = instant trust in the prescription).

---

## 3. The Innovator's Bias (The Real Risk)

> "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself, and you are the easiest person to fool." — Feynman

Innovator's bias = love for the solution. You'll lead customers to the answers you want to hear or unconsciously misread responses to justify what you wanted to build anyway.

The cost:

- You build on a false premise
- Months later, no one buys
- Expensive detour

---

## 4. The Three Tips to Sidestep Innovator's Bias

### 4.1 Target customers, not users or active buyers

> Most users still don't know what they're looking for. Active buyers are searching but haven't bought yet. **Customers have already bought** — your own or your competitor's.

You can occasionally get lucky with a user who becomes a customer, but it takes far more conversations to filter signal from noise. **Faster path: model after customers, not users.**

### 4.2 Don't use the word "problem"

"Problem" is a red-flag word. Three reasons:

1. **Spotlights one issue** — customer exaggerates its importance relative to other issues in their life.
2. **Surface-level answers / fabrication** — customers may not understand the root cause of their problems and either give you the surface symptom or make something up to sound smart.
3. **Vulnerability avoidance** — they may not want to admit they have a problem in front of someone they don't yet know, like, and trust.

### 4.3 Anchor on a recent specific instance

Instead of asking generally about problems, ask about _how they get the job done today_. Home in on a recent instance when they used your product or a close existing alternative:

- What caused them to start using it?
- What were they trying to accomplish?
- Walk me through every step from the triggering event to the job being done.
- Were they satisfied? Was the process enjoyable? What were the obstacles?

> "Customers are more alike than unalike. **Starting with just 10 conversations is often enough to extract 80% of the most actionable insights** — if you script your interviews well."

---

## 5. The Free-Users Liability (the deeper insight)

The single most under-appreciated point: **free users actively sabotage your product strategy.** Not maliciously — they think they're helping. But they:

- Tell you to build things they'll never pay for
- Don't price-anchor honestly
- Optimize you toward feature creep instead of value clarity

If your roadmap is shaped by free-user feedback, your roadmap is wrong.

## Top Takeaways for BuildOS

1. **Audit who BuildOS user interviews are actually with.** If most are with trial users, free users, or "people who signed up but never converted," you're collecting wild-goose-chase data. Heaviest weight should be on **paying customers and recently-churned-paid customers.**

2. **The Naval move for BuildOS:** call your top paying customers. Ask:
    - Why did you start paying?
    - What would make you stop?
    - **What should we charge?** (this is the surprising one — the attorney case study suggests paid customers will sometimes tell you to raise prices because they fear you'll go out of business)
    - What features would you remove?

3. **The "stop building, raise prices" hypothesis is worth testing.** Maurya's case study: 10×'d pricing on the same product, doubled paid customers. BuildOS pricing should be tested against what current paying customers say BuildOS _should_ cost relative to alternatives.

4. **Strip "problem" from BuildOS interview scripts.** Replace with:
    - "Walk me through the last time you sat down to plan a project."
    - "What did you try first?"
    - "Where did it break down?"
    - "What did you do next?"

5. **Authority via diagnosis.** Maurya's tip on using prior experience: if you can describe a creator's struggle better than they can, they grant you expert status. The BuildOS landing page / sales pitch should do this — describe the brain-dump → forgetfulness → guilt loop _more accurately than the user can articulate it themselves_. Then they'll trust the prescription.

6. **The free-user / trial-user filter.** If BuildOS publishes / curates feature requests from free trial users, treat that input as marketing/UX signal, not roadmap input. Roadmap input should come from people who have already converted to paid.

7. **10 conversations is enough.** This is the key timing point — a BuildOS quarterly research cadence of 10 carefully scripted interviews with paying customers (per segment) is a higher-leverage activity than a 200-respondent survey.

8. **The dangerous self-flattery loop:** if BuildOS roadmap decisions are validated by your own use of the tool ("I am my own customer") + free-user feedback → that's the perfect storm for innovator's bias. The corrective is structured customer (paying) interviews on a recurring cadence.
