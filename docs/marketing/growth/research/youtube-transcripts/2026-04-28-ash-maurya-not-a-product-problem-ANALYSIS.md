# Ash Maurya — Most Startups Don't Have a Product Problem

## Skill Combo Links

This source contributes to these multi-source skill combo indexes:

- [Product Strategy Skill Combos](../../../../research/youtube-library/skill-combo-indexes/PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md): Customer discovery through switching forces

> "If you skip discovery, your MVP is just a smaller version of building the wrong thing."
> — Ash Maurya

Despite the title, this video is really about **two-stage problem discovery** (broad match → narrow match) and the local-maxima trap that kills founders who jump to building. The case study is Steve, a VR founder who walked in thinking he was building a CAD competitor and walked out building a _sales tool_ for architects.

---

## 1. The Discovery Trap

Most founders treat MVP as step one: build something small, ship it fast, see what happens. That's wrong if you skip discovery — you're just shipping the wrong thing faster.

The instinct that kills:

- Founder has a clear product vision
- Lean Canvas filled in cleanly
- Pricing model decided
- Wants to go pitch the product

> "The danger with rushing to pitch a particular product or business model is prematurely climbing the top of a small hill and missing a much bigger mountain of opportunity. **This is the local maxima trap.**"

---

## 2. Broad Match Discovery (Stage 1)

**Goal:** Use a radar to scan the opportunity landscape — find which mountain is actually the biggest before you start climbing.

**Framing:** Learning, not pitching.

**Steve's example:**

- Original ICP: architecture firms wanting VR walkthroughs
- Broad match filter: any architect who currently uses CAD or 3D in their work — _not_ architects who want VR, just architects with 3D in their workflow
- Source list: scoured local architecture firm websites for portfolios featuring 3D renderings → cold-emailed those firms
- Result: dozen interviews with commercial, residential, and boutique firms

**The reframing question Steve had to learn to ask:**

- ❌ "Would you use 3D more if it were easier and cheaper?" (leading the witness, "puts the answer in their mouth")
- ✅ "What do you do today to close clients?" (open-ended, surfaces real workflow)

**The discovery:**

- Architects only use 3D when budget justifies it OR when client explicitly requests it. Otherwise too expensive and slow.
- Steve's whole product was solving a problem nobody had.

**But the redirect:**

> "If cost weren't an issue, one place where these walkthroughs would actually be useful is at the sales stage before the client commits. That's where we tend to lose them."

Steve almost missed it — he was so focused on the rejection of his original thesis that he nearly didn't hear the redirect. Mary's coaching: _Don't pitch. Don't lead. Follow the thread._

**The pivot insight:**

- Real competition isn't Autodesk. It's a 2D sketch + a sales pitch.
- Bar isn't "better than CAD." Bar is "better than a 2D sketch a client can't read."

---

## 3. Narrow Match Discovery (Stage 2)

**Goal:** Find out how deep the pain goes — measure the size of the mountain.

**Steve's narrow match:**

- 10 interviews, residential architects building custom homes
- Focus entirely on early sales process
- Question: "Walk me through how you win a new client from first contact to signed contract. What works? What doesn't? Where do you lose them?"

**The stories that emerged:**

- One architect lost 3 projects in a row, invested $3,200 in a professional rendering for next pitch ($450K project), won it — client said "I could see myself making coffee in this kitchen." But $3,000/pitch × 5–10 prospects/month is unsustainable.
- Another architect described showing 2D plans to first-time home builders. The wife started crying: "I don't understand any of this and we're about to spend our life savings on this project."

**The narrow-match output:**

- Customer segment: custom-home residential architects
- Trigger: losing projects in early sales pitches
- Existing alternative: 2D sketches + verbal walkthrough
- Constraints any solution must meet:
    - Works with 2D sketches (not 3D models — architects haven't built 3D yet at this stage)
    - Same-day delivery (not weeks)
    - Cheap enough to use _before_ client commits
    - Client experiences it with zero friction (no app downloads, no headsets)

That's a tight problem statement, a clear segment, and **a contrarian insight** — the industry assumes VR is too expensive and complex for the early pitch stage; Steve's bet is that you can make it cheap and fast enough.

---

## 4. The Two-Stage Discovery Pattern

| Stage            | Question                         | Output                                           |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| **Broad match**  | Which mountain is actually here? | Pivot signal — where the real pain clusters live |
| **Narrow match** | How deep does this pain go?      | Tight problem statement + MVP constraints        |

Both stages use the same interview technique (past behavior, customer forces) — only the **filter** differs.

---

## 5. The Three Anti-Patterns This Video Names

1. **Leading the witness** — "Would you use X if it were cheaper?" puts the answer in the customer's mouth.
2. **Tunnel vision after rejection** — when your thesis gets shut down, you're so focused on the rejection that you miss the redirect.
3. **Building before discovering** — filling out a Lean Canvas is not validation; it's a hypothesis.

---

## Top Takeaways for BuildOS

1. **BuildOS is past the broad-match stage.** Product exists, users exist, retention exists. But there may be a "Steve moment" hiding — a real-world use of BuildOS that wasn't the original thesis. Worth a fresh broad-match study with creators who recently switched any thinking/productivity tool, not just BuildOS evaluators.

2. **The mountain check.** ADHD-creators is a hill (small but real). "People making complex things in parallel" is a possible mountain. The current positioning pivot is exactly this kind of broad → narrow re-scoping. Make sure the narrow you're committing to is actually the biggest mountain in the radar scan.

3. **Steve's reframe applies to BuildOS.** "Better than other note-taking tools" might be the small hill. The mountain might be: **better than the brain-dump → forgetfulness → guilt cycle.** The "competitor" isn't Notion — it's the unstructured way creators are currently coping.

4. **The questions that kill BuildOS interviews:** "Would you use BuildOS for X?" / "Do you wish BuildOS did Y?" / "What features would make you upgrade?" All three are leading-the-witness questions Maurya specifically warns against.

5. **The redirect-listening discipline.** When a BuildOS interviewee shuts down a feature direction ("no I wouldn't use that") — what comes after that rejection is often the real signal. Train yourself (and team) to ask: "What _do_ you do today to handle [adjacent thing]?" instead of defending the original thesis.

6. **Codify a two-sprint discovery cadence.** Every quarter (or every major positioning shift), run:
    - 1 broad-match sprint (10 interviews, no BuildOS pitch, focused on creator workflow generally)
    - 1 narrow-match sprint (10 interviews on the strongest signal from sprint 1)
      The output is updated MVP/positioning constraints, not a list of feature requests.

7. **Connect to BuildOS positioning docs.** This video justifies the framing in `docs/marketing/strategy/buildos-marketing-strategy-2026.md` — the move from features-out to triggers-in. The two-stage discovery is the engine that keeps positioning honest.
