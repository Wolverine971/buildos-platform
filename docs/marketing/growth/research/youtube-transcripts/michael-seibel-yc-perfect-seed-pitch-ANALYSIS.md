# Michael Seibel (YC) — How To Perfectly Pitch Your Seed Stage Startup

## TL;DR (one paragraph)

Michael Seibel has run 2,000+ YC interviews and just finished helping 200+ companies pitch at Demo Day. His thesis: at seed stage, **you stand out by being concise and easy to understand — not by Pizzazz, Sizzle, or Shark-Tank energy.** Most founders are taught the wrong thing. The job of a pitch is to (a) get the investor to know what you do inside the first slide, (b) earn the next two minutes by leading with your most impressive point, and (c) get the investor talking — because investors don't get convinced, they convince themselves. The pitch has 7 elements: **What you do → Team → Traction → Unique Insights → Market Size → Ask** (+ a flexible middle order based on what's strongest). Every section has a specific failure mode that founders repeat. And — crucially — **you must actually ask for the money,** because ~10% of angels would write a check just to avoid the awkwardness of saying no, but only if you ask.

---

## Core Thesis

> "You stand out by being concise and easy to understand."

> "If I don't know what your company does, I can't fund you. The number one barrier to that is **you** — not me, not the world, not your users, not your advisors."

> "Goals should be 80% accurate, 100% clear — not 100% accurate, 50% clear."

The whole talk is an unlearning exercise. Most fundraising advice founders absorb (storytelling, Sizzle, designer-built decks, life-story openings, fancy market-size numbers) actively works against them at seed.

---

## The 7-Element Pitch Framework

The skeleton of every seed pitch:

1. **What do you do** — first, always
2. **Team**
3. **Traction**
4. **Unique Insights**
5. **Market Size**
6. **The Ask** — last, always

Middle 4 elements (Team / Traction / Insights / Market) **should be ordered by what's most impressive.** If traction is your strongest card, traction goes second. If team is, team goes second. **Don't bury the best slide.**

> "You earn every two minutes of that meeting. If the last two minutes sucked, that investor is checking their email."

---

## Element 1 — "What Do You Do"

### Rules

- **Two sentences max.** Plus one **specific** example.
- **Investors are not your users** → use plain language, not customer jargon.
- You need **two different answers**: one for customers (your homepage), one for investors. If you're using the homepage version with investors, you're doing it wrong.
- **80% accurate, 100% clear** beats 100% accurate, 50% clear.
- The example should illustrate the **problem AND solution** in one story — no separate "problem slide / solution slide."
- **Investors must know what you do before slide 2.** Period.

### The Airbnb Example (Seibel's gold standard)

> "Airbnb lets any home or apartment owner rent out their apartment online. They collect the payment online and take a 15% fee for every booking."

Then the example:

> "Imagine you're a waiter who lives in Washington DC. It's 2009, the Obama inauguration is happening, every hotel room is booked. You can make $2-4K renting your apartment out — that's two-three months of rent. All you do is take photos and put a listing on Airbnb…"

**Why it works:** specific role (waiter), specific city (DC), specific moment (Obama inauguration), specific dollar figure, specific outcome (rent paid). Reader can _see_ it.

### Common Mistakes

- ❌ Using customer jargon on investors
- ❌ Trying to be 100% accurate at the cost of clarity
- ❌ Vague examples ("imagine someone in a place with a big event") — strip out the specifics and the story dies
- ❌ Separate problem/solution slides — bake them into the example
- ❌ First slide is just a logo + tagline → investor sits through 5 more slides not knowing what you do
- ✅ **Pro move:** after the description, ask: _"Now that I've explained what we do, do you understand, or would another example help?"_

---

## Element 2 — Team

### Rules

- Cover: **who's on the team**, **what they do (titles!)**, **impressive accomplishments/credentials**, and ideally **how they personally experienced the problem.**
- Personal problem-experience reframes you as the expert. (Airbnb founders couldn't pay their rent → hosted 3 people from a design conference → that's the team slide right there.)

### Common Mistakes

- ❌ **No titles.** Investor doesn't know who the CEO is. They're trusting the CEO with money.
- ❌ Not saying **who writes the code.**
- ❌ **Life stories.** "There is no opportunity in your pitch to tell your life story unless the investor explicitly asks — and you should double-confirm they want it."
- ❌ **Burying credentials out of modesty.** Seibel had a founder who literally _built software for the Mars rover_ and didn't mention it on the team slide. Two months in, Seibel found out by accident.

> "If you've done something cool, just say it without the life story. Just say you did the software for the Mars rover. It'll be impressive."

---

## Element 3 — Traction

### Rules

- A **clear explanation of what you've accomplished since you started.**
- Only use a **graph if it's up and to the right.**
- **Always include time elapsed.** "100 users in TestFlight" means nothing without "in 1 month" vs "in 2 years."
- **Investors at seed are looking for momentum, not revenue.** Do you ship fast?
- **Pre-launch can have traction.** "100 users in TestFlight in one month" is real traction.
- **No traction? Skip the slide.** A bad traction slide is worse than no traction slide.

### Common Mistakes

- ❌ Listing accomplishments without timelines
- ❌ "Fake work" — advisor surveys, signed LOIs from friends, wishful pipeline
- ❌ Including a traction slide in week 2 of the company. Just don't.

---

## Element 4 — Unique Insights

> "What are the non-obvious things you've learned about the problem, the customer, or the potential solution?"

### Rules

- **Earn the right to deliver this.** Investors only care about your insight _after_ they understand what you do and trust the team.
- **Truly non-obvious** — if 50%+ of the room would already agree with the statement, it's not unique.
- **Tell the story of how you learned it.** Brian Chesky learned Airbnb's payments insight by going to SXSW on his own platform, forgetting cash, and having an awkward standoff with his host who thought he was getting ripped off.
- **Quote numbers and facts.** Especially for insights from user research.

### Common Mistakes

- ❌ "Insights" that are common knowledge
- ❌ Vague insights without a concrete story
- ❌ No numbers, no data points

### Airbnb Example

> "All the products before Airbnb that listed spaces didn't process payments. In a low-trust environment where host and guest have never met, processing payments is scary — it prevents hosts from hosting and guests from booking. A third party in the middle is more trusted than either party trusts each other, which makes the marketplace work."

---

## Element 5 — Market Size

> "Everyone thinks the _number_ is what's important. The reality is **how you calculate the number** is what's important."

### Rules

- **Bottoms-up, not top-down.** Number of potential users × price per user × why that price.
- **Show the math.** Literally show the math.
- **Use comparable products** to anchor pricing. "Figma charges $X. We'll charge $Y because we create $Z more value."
- **Teaching the investor turns you into the expert.** Investors fund experts.

### Common Mistakes

- ❌ "JP Morgan says the e-commerce market is $120T growing 20%" — who cares, you taught them nothing.
- ❌ Hiding the math
- ❌ No comparable product anchor — investor has no frame for your price

---

## Element 6 — The Ask (the killer section)

> "In your pitch, you actually have to ask for money. You'd be shocked how many founders don't. ~70% of pitches end with 'any questions?', the investor says no, the meeting ends, no money was ever asked for."

### The 3-Mindset Investor Model

When an investor listens, they fall into one of three buckets:

1. **Will never fund** — most common
2. **Will definitely fund** — least common, extremely rare
3. **"It will be uncomfortable to say no, so I'll write a check"** — the hidden ~10%

> "I am hoping the founder doesn't ask me for money, because I largely suspect that instead of saying no, I'll say yes — because the pain of saying no is way worse than writing a $25,000 check. **The only way you tap into that is you ask.** Especially with angels. You gotta ask."

### Rules

- **Ask explicitly.** Amount, terms, timeline.
- **Social proof.** Who's already invested, how much you've raised so far.
- **Tie the ask to milestones, not headcount.** Not "we'll hire 4 engineers." Instead: "We'll hit $X ARR / Y users by month 18-24." Hiring is a means; revenue/usage is the end.

> "If you don't have a goal of where you want to be 18-24 months from now, you're not going to be good at this slide. So make the goal. Make it something people would be excited by. Then ask."

### Common Mistakes

- ❌ Not asking
- ❌ No social proof
- ❌ Listing planned hires instead of milestones
- ❌ No 18-24 month vision attached to the dollar amount

## Meta-Principles (The Delivery Layer)

These are arguably more important than the slides themselves.

### 1. Order by impact, not by template

- "What do you do" → first.
- "Ask" → last.
- Everything in the middle: **most impressive thing first.**
- Don't bury the lede because someone told you decks have a "standard order."

### 2. Earn the next two minutes

> "An investor isn't going to give you 30 minutes of their time. You earn every two minutes. If the last two minutes sucked, they're checking email."

### 3. Don't deliver a book report — start a conversation

> "You're not really convincing an investor to invest. **They're convincing themselves to invest.** The more they talk, the more they convince themselves."

Sales analogy: in a sales call recording, if the _customer_ talks 50%+ of the time, they're far more likely to buy. Same with investors.

**Tactics:**

- When you see a spike of interest, _run with it._ "You looked interested — have you had a similar experience?"
- If they ask about slide 7 while you're on slide 3, **skip to slide 7.** Don't make them wait. Don't say "I'll get to that." That's "fuck you" energy.
- Get them contributing ideas, making their own connections — that's how they sell themselves.

### 4. Read the investor's face

Most investors are **not trained poker players.** Their faces are open books on Zoom. Watch them. Adapt in real time.

### 5. Visually boring slides on purpose

> "If your slides are visually interesting, that means I'm not focused on **you**, the person I want to give money to. I'm focused on some designer's work."

> "If you need a designer to build a slide deck, you're doing it wrong."

Designers also don't know what your most important points are — they'll emphasize the wrong things, because it's not their business. **Boring is the move. Clear and concise, not sexy and Sizzle.**

## Worked Example — Seibel Pitching YC Live

He demonstrates the framework on stage by pitching YC itself:

| Element                          | His pitch                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
| -------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **What do you do** (2 sentences) | "We're a startup accelerator. We fund early-stage startups with $500K standard deal, and we've invested in some of the best tech startups in the last 18 years including Stripe, Airbnb, Dropbox, Coinbase, Instacart, DoorDash, etc."          |
| **Specific example**             | His own Justin.tv/Twitch story: applied online, 10-min interview, funded, 3-month program, demo day, raised money, new friend group, new top speed of productivity.                                                                             |
| **Traction**                     | "YC has funded ~75 companies that generate over $100M in revenue."                                                                                                                                                                              |
| **Team**                         | "YC group partners are people who have started, funded, hired, fired, and done everything you're going to do as a founder — and they've all participated in YC themselves. Most investors have never been in your shoes; every YC partner has." |
| **Unique Insights (3)**          | (1) Online application without warm intros = best deal flow in the world. (2) Funding companies in batches → batch-mates support each other. (3) Demo Day is a fundraising auction → YC companies raise at higher valuations / less dilution.   |
| **Market Size**                  | "Since YC started, ~40+ VC-backed tech IPOs per year generate ~$10B in returns per year."                                                                                                                                                       |
| **Ask**                          | "Apply."                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        |

> "That's the YC pitch. Not much Sizzle. Clear, concise. That's the game."

This is the most useful artifact in the talk — a complete, working pitch in ~150 words, demonstrating every principle.

## All Tips & Tricks (Single Master List)

2. **Concise and easy to understand** beats Pizzazz/Sizzle every time.
3. **80% accurate + 100% clear** beats 100% accurate + 50% clear.
4. **You** are the bottleneck, not the investor or the market.

5. Two sentences max + one specific example.

7. **Bake problem and solution into the example** — no separate problem slide.
8. Investor must know what you do **before slide 2.**
9. Specifics > generality. Real city, real role, real dollar figure, real moment.
10. Pro move: ask "Did that land, or want another example?"

### Team

11. **Always include titles.** Especially "who's CEO" and "who writes code."
12. Pull out concrete impressive accomplishments — don't be modest, **don't bury credentials.**

22. Tell the **origin story** of how you learned the insight (Chesky's SXSW payments story).
23. Quote numbers and quote facts from user research.

24. **How** you calculate the number > the number itself.

27. **Anchor against comparable products.** "Figma charges X, we charge Y, here's why."

### The Ask

30. **You must ask for money.** ~70% of pitches don't.

32. **Tie the ask to milestones**, not headcount. "We'll hit $X / Y users in 18-24 months," not "we'll hire 4 engineers."
33. Add **social proof** — who's invested, how much raised so far.
34. If you can't define an 18-24 month milestone, your ask slide will suck — start there.

### Order & Structure

35. "What" first, "Ask" last — non-negotiable.
36. Middle slides ordered by **most impressive first.**
37. Don't bury the lede because of "standard format" thinking.

### Delivery

38. **Earn every two minutes.** No 30-minute attention spans.
39. **Get them talking.** Investors who talk 50%+ are buying themselves.
40. **Skip ahead** if they ask about a later slide. Never say "I'll get to that."
41. **Watch their face on Zoom.** Investors aren't trained poker players.
42. **Run with spikes of interest** — turn them into conversation.
43. Pitch ≠ book report. Pitch = conversation starter.

### Slides

44. **Visually boring on purpose.** Distracting slides pull eyes off you.
45. **Don't hire a designer.** They don't know what's important; they'll emphasize the wrong things.
46. Clear and concise, not sexy and Sizzle.

## What's Distinctive About This Advice

Most pitch advice on the internet is performance theater — Shark Tank energy, hero's-journey storytelling, designer-built decks. Seibel is explicitly **anti** all of that for seed stage. The framing that stuck out:

- **"Investors convince themselves."** Reframes the entire job from "perform a polished pitch" to "design a conversation that lets them sell themselves."
- **"You earn every two minutes."** Forces you to abandon the linear-deck model.
- **"~10% of angels will write a check just to avoid the discomfort of saying no."** Brutally honest insight you don't see in the standard playbook.
- **"Your designer has no idea what your most important points are."** Inverts the conventional "make it look great" advice.
- **The full live YC pitch.** Most coaches _describe_ the framework. He performs it. Worth re-reading the YC pitch a few times — it's the cleanest demonstration of "boring on purpose" in the wild.

## Direct Application to BuildOS Fundraising

If/when BuildOS pitches a seed round, this framework cleanly maps onto its current positioning. Concrete reminders for that work:

### "What do you do" — two sentences

A draft using Seibel's grammar:

> _"BuildOS is a thinking environment for people making complex things. Users dump their messy thoughts in, and we structure them into projects, tasks, and daily briefs — turning stream-of-consciousness into executable work."_

Plus one specific founder/creator example with names, hours saved, dollars made — _not_ "imagine someone with a complex project."

### "Two different answers" rule

- Customer-facing version (homepage): "Turn messy thinking into structured work" / anti-AI relief framing.
- Investor-facing version: explain the AI-extraction layer, the daily-brief retention loop, the calendar-integrated execution surface, the ontology-driven graph.
- **Do not pitch investors with the homepage copy.** That's the customer version.

### Team slide

- Title: who's CEO, who writes the code.
- Lead with **personal problem experience.** Founder built BuildOS for himself first — that's the team slide.
- Don't bury technical credentials.

### Traction

- Always include **time elapsed**: users in N months, brain dumps processed per week, retention curves.
- If a metric isn't strong yet, omit the slide rather than fake it.

### Unique insights — possible candidates

- The anti-AI positioning thesis (lead with relief, not AI).
- The "thinking environment" category framing vs. "AI productivity tool."
- Specific user-research insights from creator/founder interviews — _with quotes and numbers._
- Each insight needs a story of how it was learned (analog to Chesky's SXSW payments story).

### Market size — bottoms-up

- Number of complex-thinking creators × subscription price × why that price.
- Comparable anchor: Notion / Linear / Sunsama / Roam — what they charge, why BuildOS commands more.
- Show the math on the slide. Don't hand-wave with "the productivity software market is $XXB."

### The Ask

- Define the **18-24 month milestone** before drafting the slide. (e.g., $X ARR, Y paying creators, Z retention.)
- Include social proof (existing angels, advisors, design partners).
- Frame dollars as "what we'll achieve," not "who we'll hire."
