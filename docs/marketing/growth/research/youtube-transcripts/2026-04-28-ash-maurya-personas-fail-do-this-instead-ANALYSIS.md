# Ash Maurya — Switching Triggers Beat Personas

## Skill Combo Links

This source contributes to these multi-source skill combo indexes:

- [Product Strategy Skill Combos](../../../../research/youtube-library/skill-combo-indexes/PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md): Customer discovery through switching forces

> "The art of good customer segmentation isn't chasing the most distinguishing characteristics — it's the _fewest_ distinguishing characteristics that cause someone to buy from you."
> — Ash Maurya

Personas are overkill for early-stage products and can be actively harmful. Maurya's argument: replace persona attributes with **switching triggers** — events that change something in a prospect's mind, breaking their existing solution and prompting them to look for a new one.

---

## 1. Why Personas Fail (Three Reasons)

### 1.1 Demographics chase correlation, not causality

- Most ICPs list age, gender, household income, role, etc.
- Just because most of your customers are male and in their 20s doesn't mean 20-year-old males _cause_ buying.
- **If an attribute does not cause someone to become a customer, it doesn't belong in your ICP.**
- Getting to causality requires lots of data — which startups don't have. Personas make sense at scale, not at the start.

### 1.2 More attributes shrink your TAM

- Each added attribute makes targeting harder and more expensive.
- More importantly, **you shrink your total addressable market** — opposite of what early-stage products want.

### 1.3 The local-maxima trap (the most dangerous one)

You narrow down → you find a few customers who fit → you think you've validated.

> "You might end up focusing on climbing a small hill and totally miss the neighboring mountain of opportunity because you weren't looking for it."

Example: "Startup founders = 20-something guys in a Silicon Valley garage." Go look there, you'll find them. Miss the _much bigger_ market of 100M+ entrepreneurs worldwide who transcend age, gender, geography.

---

## 2. The One Distinguishing Characteristic All Customers Share

> The **switching trigger** — an event that changes something in the prospect's mind, breaking their existing solution and prompting them to look for a new one.

The mattress-company case study:

- A mattress company asked: "When's the best time of day to run an ad?"
- Common guesses: morning (people just woke up sore), evening (about to go to bed).
- Both wrong. **Optimal time: 2–4 a.m. — when people can't sleep.**
- Real customers were buying mattresses 30 minutes after their mid-night ad encountered them.
- The competitors were sleeping. Literally.

Why it worked: 2 a.m. is a switching trigger event. The customer isn't impulsive — they just hit the moment when their existing solution (current mattress) audibly breaks.

---

## 3. The Three Types of Switching Triggers

| Trigger type               | Description                                         | Mattress example                                  |
| -------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| **Bad experience**         | Existing solution stops working                     | Can't sleep at 2 a.m.                             |
| **Change in circumstance** | Life context shifts                                 | Going from single → couple needs a bigger bed     |
| **Awareness event**        | New information arrives that reframes the situation | Doctor recommends a therapeutic mattress for back |

> Notice that none of these criteria have any demographic attributes.

---

## 4. The Job-Based ICP (3 Attributes)

Replace persona attributes with three Jobs-to-Be-Done attributes:

1. **Switching trigger** — what event caused them to start looking
2. **Desired outcome** — why they bought, what they wanted to achieve
3. **Chosen solution** — what they actually picked for the job

These three attributes are the foundation of a good ICP.

> The trigger shapes the kind of solution someone buys.

---

## 5. How to Earn These Insights

> "Good insights are like magic tricks — hard to see initially, simple and obvious in hindsight. You can't simply guess at insights. They have to be learned or earned."

The path:

- If you have customers → interview them
- If you don't → interview customers of your closest competitors / existing alternatives
- **10–20 conversations** is enough for patterns to emerge (assuming you script the interviews well)

## Top Takeaways for BuildOS

1. **The BuildOS positioning has been mid-pivot from ADHD-heavy → "thinking environment for creators." This video is the playbook for that pivot.** Don't let ADHD become a demographic-style ICP. Treat it as one _of several_ triggers.

2. **Map BuildOS's three trigger types explicitly:**
    - **Bad experience triggers:** "I lost a project in my Notion graveyard." "I started a Roam database that died after week 2." "My Apple Notes is 4,000 entries and I can't find anything." "I missed a deadline because the task lived in a different tool than my note about it."
    - **Change in circumstance triggers:** "I went from employee → solo creator." "I started a YouTube channel and now have 6 parallel projects." "I started writing a book and need to think across chapters."
    - **Awareness triggers:** "Saw a tweet that 'AI assistants need context not commands'." "Got recommended BuildOS by another creator." "Read a thread about brain dumps."

3. **The mattress / 2 a.m. equivalent for BuildOS:** when _do_ creators decide they need a thinking environment? Likely candidates: Sunday night planning, the moment a new project lands, the moment they realize a project they cared about quietly died, the moment they have too many tabs open. Run ads / publish content **at those moments**, not at "creators in general."

4. **Stop adding to the persona spec.** If the BuildOS marketing brief lists "creators 25–45, makes complex things, has ADHD tendencies, uses Notion, etc." — that's a persona, not an ICP. Replace it with: trigger × outcome × current solution.

5. **The interview script Maurya recommends maps to question 3 of #1 in the companion video** — the "what made you choose Solution X over Solution Y" → switching-trigger surfacing.

6. **The TAM-shrink risk is real for BuildOS.** "Creators with ADHD" is much smaller than "people building complex things who have a thinking-environment switching trigger." Don't accidentally validate the small hill.
