## TL;DR

Jerome posted ~1,200 videos in 2 years (long-form + 6 shorts/day) using a data-science "aggregate-everything" approach and grew to 150–200K views per video. Colin & Samir's diagnosis: he's optimizing the **quantitative** levers but ignoring the **qualitative** ones — personal story, brand identity, and a memorable phrase people can spread word-of-mouth. The fastest way from 200K → 400K isn't a better algorithm play; it's becoming a "household name" by leaning into his own journey, not just his expert lessons.

The emotional one-liner Jerome lands on at the end: **"Why not me?"**

## Jerome's Setup (the facts)

- **Series:** _Scratch to Scratch_ — went from never playing golf to chasing scratch-golfer status (less than 1% of golfers).
- **Backstory leverage:** Quit a lead engineering role at a top tech company, moved 3,000 miles from family, has trained for 5,000+ hours with elite coaches.
- **Cadence:** 1,200 videos in 2 years. First 136 days = 136 long-form (~1 hr) videos. Then layered in 6 shorts/day (6:1 shorts:long ratio).
- **Why so much volume:** Background in ML/data science. Treated YouTube like a dataset — aggregate enough samples to make long-term decisions.
- **Current ceiling:** 150–200K views per video (up from ~35K six months ago).
- **Goal:** 400K–1M views per video; reduce risk on experimental videos.
- **Pain point:** Edits every video himself by leaving 300+ in-frame notes for editors — takes 6 hours per video.

## Tips & Tricks (Pulled From the Conversation)

### 1. Don't dump every value-prop in the first minute

Jerome's intro stacked **multiple storylines at once**: quit job, scratch golfer mission, $100K+ coaching access, 18 lessons, breaking down an elite course. Colin's note:

> "Don't give them to me all at once. I can't choose which one I care most about. So give me one storyline."

**The fix:** Drip storylines across the video. Open with **one** hook (the most universal one — "I left my job to become a pro golfer"), then drop new storylines later in the video as fresh re-engagement points.

### 2. Lead with the most universal version of your story

"Scratch golfer" / "zero handicap" filters out non-golfers in the first 30 seconds.

> "My mom understands 'I left my job to become a pro golfer.' That's universal."

**Rule of thumb:** The opening should be legible to people _outside_ your niche. Reduce filters in the first minute. You can re-introduce specificity once they're invested.

### 3. Filters are good — but only _after_ the hook lands

Colin defends specificity: "tight-knit, purpose-driven audiences" are valuable. The point isn't to be vague forever — it's to **delay the filtering** until people care about _you_, not just the topic.

### 4. The "Identity × Emotion × Action" worksheet

Colin & Samir's framework for shaping titles, thumbnails, edits:

| Column                | Question                                                                                        |
| --------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Audience identity** | Who is watching? (serious golfers, casual golfers, people unhappy in their jobs, dream-chasers) |
| **Emotion**           | What do you want them to feel? (inspired, capable, hopeful)                                     |
| **Action**            | What do you want them to do? (play a round, pursue their own dream)                             |

Use this on the channel as a whole, then on each individual video. Then ask: _Does my title/thumbnail/edit serve this combination?_ If not, cut it.

> "Seven hard lessons golf taught me about life" and "Can I get my handicap to X" target **different identities and different emotions** — they shouldn't share a strategy.

### 5. Lessons are commodity. _Your relationship to lessons_ isn't.

Colin's sharpest critique:

> "Golf lessons are everywhere. What's missing from that intro is **your perspective** on what the experts are saying."

Sustainable strategy = bring expert content **filtered through your POV as the everyday/aspiring golfer.** That POV is the moat. You can't sustainably keep finding new world-class coaches forever, but you can always be _you_ responding to them.

### 6. Build a "decentralized sitcom"

Colin's framing of why the YouTube golf vertical exploded (Bryson, Good Good, Brian Bros, etc.):

> "It's a decentralized sitcom where everyone is connecting to their own character."

People don't return to a channel for gameplay (gameplay is everywhere). They return for the **personality** they've bonded with. Bubby's Tourette's video on Good Good (~800K views) changed how viewers _experience him hitting a shot_. The personal story makes the gameplay matter.

### 7. The "knock them off the list" structure

Instead of randomly featuring coaches:

> "Communicate a list of the experts you're trying to work with. Knock them off in each video."

This gives the audience a **broader mission** to follow. Make the coaches into recurring characters (Ryan Trahan-style: "make big characters out of everyday people"). Add a side-quest mechanic: at the end of each lesson, ask the current coach, _"Hey, do you know [the holy-grail coach]? How do I get to them?"_ — a story-within-a-story.

### 8. Slow intros work _if_ you've earned the trust

Jerome was scared to use a slow, atmospheric opener (deep breath, swing, then voiceover). Colin's read:

> "It's easy to teach because the value exchange is clear — they know they'll learn something. It's scary to say, 'My story is why you're here.'"

But once you've built the audience relationship, slower openings work because viewers stay _for you_.

### 9. The "stakes" question

> "If no one knows anything about you, do they care at all where the ball goes?"

Before showing yourself in action (a swing, a putt, any high-skill moment), seed the why and the risk. Without that, every shot is just a shot. With it, every shot has weight.

### 10. The "future headline" exercise

Colin's go-to homework for clarity:

> "Write the headline of an article about you, from the future. Pick a publication — say Men's Health. What's the headline at the end of this year?"

This forces you to articulate the brand you're actually building. Bonus: it doubles as the answer to _"Why should someone tell their friend about you?"_

### 11. Word-of-mouth > algorithm hacks

The most under-rated growth lever:

> "Sometimes we think way too much about the metrics of YouTube and way too little about someone going to coffee with someone and saying, 'Dude, I've been watching this YouTube channel...' If it's hard for them to explain, it's not going to spread."

**Test:** Can your viewer describe your channel in one sentence to a friend? If not, your positioning isn't tight enough. Fix the positioning, then bake the phrase into the content (tagline, sign-off, recurring visual).

### 12. The "tagline" hook

Colin's example: ending every video with _"Whatever you're pursuing in your life, impossible is possible."_ Doesn't have to be that exact line, but the principle holds — a repeated phrase trains viewers (and word-of-mouth) on what you stand for.

Jerome lands on **"Why not me?"** during the conversation — Colin's reaction: "Write that down. That might be crazy." He coaches Jerome to end the upcoming Paris video with it.

### 13. Vulnerability isn't manufactured — but it is structural

Colin is careful here:

> "I'm not suggesting any sort of manufactured vulnerability. I'm suggesting that what's happening in YouTube golf is the _deep connection_ to the golfers."

Translation: don't fake a sob story. Do put the personal-journey beats _inside_ the video instead of burying them at the end. Jerome admitted he buries personal beats at the end because he's afraid: _"What if people don't care?"_ Colin's answer: front-load anyway, even when scary.

### 14. Volume buys you data — but data isn't strategy

Colin's most generous read:

> "If you don't take anything we said, you'll be at 400–600K views by next year just from time and reps. The question I'd push you on is the qualitative side: _does my brand grow? Do I become a household name?_"

Reps deliver **growth**; positioning delivers **identity**. They're not the same lever, and identity is what compounds into business outcomes (sponsors, products, courses, IRL audience).

### 15. Quantitative + Qualitative = success point

Jerome's anecdote: an elderly stranger flew from SF to San Diego after watching his channel because Jerome's pursuit pulled him out of a depressive state. Jerome's takeaway:

> "The intertwine of the qualitative and the quantitative is what I see as a good success point."

The numbers matter _because_ they multiply the depth of those moments. But the qualitative is the why — and lacking a why is what makes 1,200 videos feel hollow.

### 16. The hidden cost of in-frame editor notes

Jerome leaves ~300 written notes per video for his editor — 6 hours per video. Colin and Samir both recognized this:

> "This is one of my least favorite things about being a creator — writing notes in frame and then having those notes misunderstood because I didn't explain it well."

**Implied tip:** Document a style bible / shot-language so you don't re-explain the same micro-decisions every video. Push toward **systemized editor language** so you can stop being the bottleneck on every cut.

### 17. The Paris video diagnosis

Jerome opens his Paris (Le Saint-Cloud / French Open course) draft with a fast hook because he's used to fast hooks. Colin's pushback: he's a _strong enough story_ now that he can risk a slower, voiceover-led open. The conventional fast-hook is a habit, not a rule. Re-evaluate it once your audience knows you.

## The Diagnostic Pattern (Reusable)

If you transposed this into a checklist for any creator stuck at the next plateau, it looks like:

1. **Are you front-loading too many storylines in the hook?** → Pick one. Drip the rest.
2. **Is your opening legible outside your niche?** → If not, write the universal version first.
3. **Does an Identity × Emotion × Action exercise on your last 5 videos return _consistent_ answers?** → If they're scattered, your positioning is scattered.
4. **What's _your_ relationship to your topic?** → If your videos are interchangeable with the experts you feature, you have no moat.
5. **Could a fan describe your channel to a friend in one sentence?** → If not, you don't yet have a tagline-shaped identity.
6. **Where in your videos is the personal story?** → If it's at the end, move it forward. If it's missing, find the version you can tell honestly.
7. **What headline would Men's Health (or your equivalent) write about you in 12 months?** → Reverse-engineer your content from that line.
