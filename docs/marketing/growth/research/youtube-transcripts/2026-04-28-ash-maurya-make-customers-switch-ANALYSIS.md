# Ash Maurya — The Switching Story Behind Every Purchase

## Skill Combo Links

This source contributes to these multi-source skill combo indexes:

- [Product Strategy Skill Combos](../../../../research/youtube-library/skill-combo-indexes/PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md): Customer discovery through switching forces

> "Every time someone buys your product, they're firing something else. The reason they fire the old thing matters more than the reason they hire your product."
> — Ash Maurya

A switching story has three acts: trigger → search → outcome. Inside that story sits the exact spec for an MVP customers can't refuse: **must-haves, performance features, and trade-offs they've stopped hoping for**. The trade-offs are where the real opportunity lives.

---

## 1. The Sarah Bag Story (Worked Example)

Sarah needs a new bag for an executive training program — must hold a laptop, look professional in a boardroom, double as a purse at networking events.

**Act 1 — Trigger (the push):**
Her existing Luca Bella bag (worn straps, items falling out, can't double as a purse) breaks under the new context. The training opportunity isn't impulse — it's a specific shift in her reality that broke her existing solution.

> "Most founders obsess over how great their product is, but the smartest founders study the push. Without a strong push, no amount of product appeal will move the customer."

**Act 2 — Search:**
She auditions and fires multiple candidates:

- Elisa bag → too cheap/plasticky → fired (quality)
- Kuba bag → over $500 → fired (price)
- Four others → also fired

Each rejection has a **firing criterion** with a different weight. Some are deal-breakers; some are minor annoyances.

She picks a Cole Haan because by the end, only two things mattered: laptop fit + professional/purse hybrid look. Those are her **hiring criteria**.

**But:** she made trade-offs.

- Cole Haan has two straps, she wanted one
- Not padded → laptop concern
- A bit too large to truly double as a purse

She accepts these trade-offs. Her satisfaction lands at _neutral_. She uses the bag for travel, not daily.

## 2. The Four Customer Forces and Their Math

| Force        | What it is                                                 |
| ------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Push**     | The trigger that broke the old solution                    |
| **Pull**     | The attraction toward something new                        |
| **Inertia**  | Sunk costs / familiarity / habit pulling toward status quo |
| **Friction** | Anxiety / cost / learning curve of switching               |

**The switch formula:**

> A switch only happens when **push + pull > inertia + friction**.

And per loss-aversion research, **losses weigh ~2× more than gains** in the customer's mind. So friction and inertia carry **double weight**.

**Implication:** incremental improvements don't trigger switches. You need a 10× step change to overcome the loss-aversion math.

---

## 3. The Three Types of Criteria → Three Types of Product Insight

| Criteria type       | What it tells you about the product                                     | Kano archetype       |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| **Firing criteria** | What you must get right or you'll be fired too (table-stakes)           | Must-haves           |
| **Hiring criteria** | Where to compete — the dimensions where more is better                  | Performance features |
| **Trade-offs**      | Compromises customers are currently living with — where to delight them | Delighters           |

> Most founders only obsess over hiring criteria. They try to outperform competition on the obvious dimensions. **The real leverage is in the trade-offs.**

When you solve a trade-off:

- You're not just meeting expectations
- You're delivering something the customer **stopped hoping for**
- That creates word-of-mouth, not just satisfaction

---

## 4. The Switching Timeline (Emotional Curve)

Maurya describes the timeline shape:

1. Starts neutral
2. Drops when the trigger hits and the old way breaks
3. Dips further during the search as candidates get fired
4. Rises when the new solution is hired
5. Dips slightly again for the trade-offs
6. Settles at the satisfaction level

The shape of that curve tells you whether you're solving a real problem (deep dip) or a small annoyance (shallow dip), and whether you're delighting (high recovery) or just satisfying (neutral).

---

## 5. From Story → MVP

After analyzing many interviews, Maurya recommends extracting:

- **Must-have axes** (from firing criteria) — table stakes
- **Performance axes** (from hiring criteria) — pick the top one to compete on
- **Delighter axes** (from trade-offs) — pick one to delight on

→ Two primary axes: **one performance, one delighter.** Those become the foundation of MVP design.

> "Not a feature list, not a product roadmap — two specific dimensions where you can win. One where you can outperform, and one where you can delight."

## Top Takeaways for BuildOS

1. **The BuildOS switching story is the highest-leverage research artifact you can produce.** For each interview with a creator who recently picked a thinking/productivity tool (or attempted to), capture:
    - The trigger that broke their last setup
    - Every tool they auditioned and the _specific_ reason each one was fired
    - The criteria that made them pick the winner
    - The trade-offs they accepted (the gold mine)

2. **The trade-offs creators are accepting _right now_ with Notion / Roam / Apple Notes / Things are the BuildOS opportunity.** Likely trade-offs (worth validating):
    - Notion: power but no agentic structure / no daily synthesis
    - Roam/Obsidian: graph but no project execution
    - Apple Notes: capture but zero structure
    - Things: tasks but no thinking
      Each of these is a "stopped hoping for" axis BuildOS could turn into a delighter.

3. **Push + Pull > Inertia + Friction × 2.** The BuildOS "free trial" framing fights inertia (familiarity with current tool) and friction (learning a new system). The math says BuildOS can't win on incremental improvement — it needs a 10× step change on at least one axis.

4. **Identify the one performance axis + one delighter axis.** This is the single most important strategic exercise for the BuildOS landing page and onboarding. Examples worth testing:
    - **Performance axis:** Speed from messy thought → structured project (vs. Notion's blank-page tax)
    - **Delighter axis:** The morning daily brief that surfaces what you'd otherwise forget (the trade-off creators accept with every other tool)

5. **The "neutral satisfaction" trap.** Sarah ended up neutral on her Cole Haan bag and only uses it for travel. That's what most freemium signups look like — they "use" the product but don't love it. If BuildOS retention is high but love is neutral, you're hitting must-haves and performance but missing the delighter trade-off solution.

6. **Operationalize this.** Maurya does it in Lean Spark; for BuildOS you can do it manually. Suggested artifact: a `docs/marketing/growth/switching-stories/` folder with one file per real-user switching story, structured into trigger / firing criteria / hiring criteria / trade-offs.

7. **Connect this to the Mafia Offer.** Acts 1–2 of the Mafia Offer are exactly the trigger + status quo break from this video. Act 3 is the demo of the delighter that solves a trade-off. Same architecture, different surface.
