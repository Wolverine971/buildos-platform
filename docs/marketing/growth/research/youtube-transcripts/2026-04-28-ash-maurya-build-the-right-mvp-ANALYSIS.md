# Ash Maurya — Build the Right MVP, Not the Minimum One

## Skill Combo Links

This source contributes to these multi-source skill combo indexes:

- [Product Strategy Skill Combos](../../../../research/youtube-library/skill-combo-indexes/PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md): Right MVP and offer design

> "A bare minimum gets a lukewarm response. Nobody cares that you can do what your competitors already do. They want to know what you can do that's different and new."
> — Ash Maurya

The pop-culture MVP myth is "ship the smallest viable thing." Maurya argues that's how you get a **maximally undifferentiated product (MUP)**. The right framing: minimum _valuable_ product. Use the Kano model to mix delighters + performance + must-haves; innovate on _one_ delighter; use Wizard-of-Oz tactics to fake the must-haves.

---

## 1. Why "Minimum" Is Misunderstood

Two failure modes when founders try to build MVPs:

### 1.1 The bare-minimum trap

A truly minimal product gets a lukewarm response. People don't switch for incremental better — they switch for _different and new_.

### 1.2 The basic-features explosion

You can't ignore what competitors already offer. A car needs brakes, headlights, wipers — table stakes. People don't buy a car _because_ of brakes, but they won't buy one without them.

So a founder building a new car has to:

- Build all the basic features
- AND create something new and different
- At the same time

This is what kills MVP velocity.

---

## 2. The "V" Should Be Valuable, Not Just Viable

Most people interpret "viable" as: a complete product customers will pay for. They build all the basics first, then sprinkle differentiation on top.

> "This lands you back in the world of not building an MVP, but rather an **MUP — a maximally undifferentiated product.**"

Maurya redefines V = **Valuable**, which has three dimensions:

| Dimension        | Question                      |
| ---------------- | ----------------------------- |
| **Desirability** | Will customers want this?     |
| **Viability**    | Will they pay for it?         |
| **Feasibility**  | Can you build it fast enough? |

**Desirability** comes from a unique value proposition — different and better than alternatives.
**Feasibility** comes from speed of execution. (Given infinite time you can build anything; competitors don't wait.)

---

## 3. The Kano Model — 5 Feature Archetypes

Plotted against customer satisfaction × feature sophistication, features fall into 5 categories:

| #   | Type                     | Behavior                                                      | Car example                    |
| --- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| 1   | **Must-haves (basics)**  | Absent → very dissatisfied. Present → flatlines satisfaction. | Brakes                         |
| 2   | **Performance**          | More is linearly better.                                      | Engine horsepower              |
| 3   | **Delighters**           | Unexpected; create disproportionate happiness.                | GPS / park assist / FSD        |
| 4   | **Zone of indifference** | Don't move the needle either way.                             | (any feature nobody asked for) |
| 5   | **Reverse features**     | More is _worse_.                                              | Emissions                      |

Two important properties of delighters:

- True delighters aren't gimmicks — they solve a real existing problem in a new and different way.
- Over time, real delighters become must-haves. (Rear-view cameras: delighter at launch → mandatory in US for vehicles >10K lb after 2018.)

**The MVP cocktail prioritization:**

> Delighters first → Performance → Must-haves.

But: delighters are hard (innovation), so you can only afford a few. And must-haves can drain resources because you need them but they alone can't trigger a switch.

---

## 4. The 4-Step MVP Cocktail (Tesla Case Study)

### Step 1 — Build your UVP around ONE delighter

For Tesla in 2006, the delighter was **sustainability** (zero emissions). Cars at the time had emissions = a reverse feature. Tesla didn't promise lower emissions — they promised **zero**.

> "This positioning wasn't just about being better than other cars. It was about being categorically different. Everyone else was claiming the best gas mileage. Tesla was claiming our car has zero emissions."

That's the move: not "10% better than the alternative." A new benefit category customers care about.

### Step 2 — Don't forget minimum performance metrics

Tesla didn't invent electric cars — they made them mainstream. Concept electric cars before Tesla were "souped-up golf carts" that went 30–50 miles. Not credible alternatives.

Tesla's internal mandate: an all-electric, zero-emissions car that goes **at least 200 miles on a single charge.** That was the minimum performance threshold to be taken seriously as a switch from combustion.

### Step 3 — Layer in a second axis of better

Once Tesla nailed delighter + minimum performance, they added a second delighter: **acceleration** (0–60 fast, thanks to instant torque from no transmission).

They didn't bury this in a feature list. They built an electric _sports car_ and claimed _the fastest and greenest car money can buy_. Publicity stunts: Tesla Roadster vs. supercars on a track.

> Two axes of better is more powerful than one. Position yourself in the top-right quadrant of a 2×2 differentiation matrix — a category of your own making.

### Step 4 — Innovate around basic features (Wizard-of-Oz)

A typical car takes 10 years from concept to road. Tesla did it in 2.5 years.

How? They didn't try to build a full car from scratch. They licensed a pre-built **Lotus Motors chassis** and dropped their battery into it. That freed up 100% of their innovation resources to focus on **battery technology** — the one thing they had to win.

> This is the Wizard-of-Oz MVP design pattern: cobble together the basics from existing solutions; innovate only on the differentiating axes.

## 5. The Steve VR Worked Example

Steve's MVP cocktail:

| Step | What he did                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| ---- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1    | Delighter: turn a 2D architectural sketch into a 3D VR walkthrough in **30 seconds** by snapping a few phone photos. (Other VR platforms required tons of training.)                                     |
| 2    | Minimum performance: realized models didn't need to be perfect to the inch — just close enough to close the homeowner client.                                                                            |
| 3    | Second axis (stumbled into): a reusable catalog of furniture/appliance components → more personalized walkthroughs → became his moat.                                                                    |
| 4    | Wizard-of-Oz the basics: instead of building advanced CAD, built export plug-ins that pushed approved VR models _into_ the architects' existing CAD tools. **Turned competitors into channel partners.** |

> Before this, Steve was drowning in must-haves he thought he needed to build. After repositioning as a sales tool (not a CAD tool), he reduced scope and gained instant differentiation.

## 6. The Demo-Sell-Build Note

Maurya mentions he's currently using "demo-sell-build" — pre-selling Lean Spark before fully building it. He notes the technique was polarizing in earlier comments. The MVP cocktail framework + demo-sell-build are complementary: figure out the right cocktail, _then_ sell the demo before you finish building.

## Top Takeaways for BuildOS

1. **The MVP-as-MUP risk.** BuildOS is past MVP, but every new feature decision recreates the same trap: spend resources reaching parity (must-haves) instead of compounding the delighter axes. Audit the current roadmap — what % of features are must-have parity vs. delighter / performance differentiation?

2. **What is BuildOS's "zero emissions"?** The Tesla move was a categorically different benefit — not 10% better than incumbents. Candidates worth testing for BuildOS:
    - "Brain dump → structured project in seconds" (vs. Notion's blank-page tax)
    - "AI-generated daily brief that surfaces what you'd forget" (vs. every other tool's silence about your past work)
    - "Agent-native architecture: anything you do, an agent can do" (vs. every other tool's UI-only access)
      Pick _one_ that hits "categorically different" — not "incrementally better."

3. **What's BuildOS's 200-mile-range threshold?** Tesla had to clear a credibility threshold (200 miles, not 30). For BuildOS, the equivalent threshold is the bar at which a creator with an existing setup will say "okay, this is real — I'll switch." Worth identifying explicitly. Hypotheses:
    - Brain dump → project must complete in < N seconds
    - Daily brief must be useful X mornings out of 7
    - First brain dump → first useful brief loop must close in day 1, not week 1

4. **The second axis.** Tesla's second axis (acceleration) was a _side effect of the first innovation_. What's BuildOS's? Possible: the _agent-native_ property is a side effect of having structured ontology — and could become a second axis of better that fewer competitors can match.

5. **Wizard-of-Oz the must-haves.** This is the most actionable engineering takeaway. For every must-have feature on the BuildOS roadmap, ask: _can we route this to an existing tool instead of building it?_
    - Calendar → Google Calendar integration (already done — good example)
    - Tasks → could route to existing task tools instead of building heavier task UI
    - Long-form writing → route to user's existing writing tool, not build a doc editor
      The principle: **only innovate on the delighter and performance axes.** Plug in the rest.

6. **The "delighter becomes must-have" timeline matters.** BuildOS's current delighters (e.g. brain dump → structure, daily briefs) will become must-haves as competitors copy them. The roadmap needs a continuous delighter-discovery process or BuildOS becomes commoditized in 18–24 months. The customer-discovery videos in this batch (especially the trade-offs / "what they stopped hoping for") feed exactly this process.

7. **Connect to the Mafia Offer.** Step 1 (delighter as UVP) = Act 3 of the Mafia Offer (different and better demo). The two frameworks plug into each other — discovery surfaces the trade-off → MVP cocktail turns trade-off into delighter → Mafia Offer pitches it. End-to-end loop.
