# 250 Calls From a FREE Google Trick Nobody's Talking About — Analysis

## TL;DR

Andy Walker (CEO of Stryker Digital) breaks down how his agency set up **six Google Business Profiles (GBPs) for one cleaning company**, doubling its revenue from $500K → $1M in 12 months while taking it from ~60 leads/month to ~250 inbound leads/month. The whole "trick" is to **legally** clone your local presence across the city by stacking DBAs + real offices + unique phone/URLs at locations 10–15 miles apart, then flooding each new profile with real reviews, citations, and hyper-local content.

It's not a hack. It's how multi-location franchises have always operated, applied to a single small business.

---

## The Core Insight (the "why")

> **One Google Business Profile only ranks within a 10–15 mile radius of its pin.**

No amount of reviews, SEO, or "best in town" status will push a single GBP outside that bubble. If a searcher is 30 miles from your pin, you're invisible — even if you're the highest-rated business in the metro. The fix is geographic coverage, not optimization.

This single mechanical fact is what makes the whole strategy work. Most home service owners are trying to rank harder; Andy is telling you to **rank wider**.

---

## The Three-Part Framework

### PART 1 — Location Strategy (where to put the pins)

Goal: cover your entire serviceable revenue map without overlapping.

**Steps:**

1. **Pull existing GBP data.** Look at where your current calls are physically coming from — those clusters tell you where your one profile already ranks.
2. **Overlay an income/demand map.** Identify high-income, less-price-sensitive pockets in your service area where you currently get _nothing_.
3. **Place new pins 10–15 miles apart.** This is the constraint. Closer than that and Google sees overlap/spam. Farther than that and you create dead zones (especially in dense/competitive markets).
4. **Treat the city like a coverage map.** Profile 1 keeps its original area; profiles 2–6 fill the dark spots.

**Math from the case study:** if one profile in one pocket gets 40–60 calls/month with $0 ad spend, six profiles covering six pockets gets you ~250 calls/month — same input, 4–5x output.

> "Profile one gets to keep its own original area, while profile two goes right here to cover this part of town. Profile three right here, and so on up to six Google Business Profiles."

---

### PART 2 — Verification Strategy (how to not get suspended)

This is where 99% of people screw this up. Andy is explicit: **do it by the books or both profiles get nuked.** Three rules.

#### Rule 1: Get a DBA ("Doing Business As")

- Register through your state website (~$50–$100).
- **Don't** reuse your main business name. Same name across profiles = duplicate flag = suspension risk for _both_ profiles.
- **Embed your service keyword AND the location into the DBA name.**
    - ❌ "Andy's Cleaning Company"
    - ⚠️ "Andy's House Cleaning"
    - ✅ "Andy's House Cleaning of Lewisburg"
- This double-duty wins: it satisfies Google's "different legal entity" requirement _and_ exact-matches local search queries — one of the top GBP ranking factors.
- **Don't keyword-stuff.** It has to look natural to a human. Blatant stuffing gets caught eventually.

#### Rule 2: Rent a real office

- A co-work or shared office at **$300–$500/month** is enough.
- **Required:** physical sign on the door with the DBA name, and a lease in the DBA's name.
- During verification, Google makes you do a video walk-through showing the space + the legal docs (DBA paperwork).
- **Hard NO list:** P.O. boxes, virtual offices, UPS Store mailboxes. They will eventually get caught and suspended.
- ROI math: $6K/year rent vs. 20 jobs at $300 ticket = breakeven, and you'll usually clear that in the first month.

#### Rule 3: Unique phone number + unique website URL per profile

- **Phone:** different number for every profile. Even just changing the last digit is fine. Use Google Voice / OpenPhone / GoHighLevel / Quo — all of them can forward to one main line. Same number across two profiles ≈ 80% chance of duplicate-flagging both.
- **Website:** you do _not_ need a separate domain. Use a **location-specific page** on your main site (e.g., `andy.com/lewisburg`). This is exactly how franchises have always handled it.

> "Get a DBA with the keywords in the business name, check. Rent a real office, $300–$500. Use a unique phone number and a unique website URL on the profile, check."

---

### PART 3 — Ranking Strategy (how to actually crack top 3)

Verification just gets you on the map. Top-3 ranking is where revenue lives. Three levers, executed in parallel:

#### Lever 1: Treat reviews like fuel

- Export your existing customer list.
- **Split it geographically into N chunks** (one per new profile).
- Email each customer **only one** review link — the one for the profile closest to where they live.
- Goal: get every new profile to **100 reviews ASAP**.
- Why this works: real customers + real geographic distribution = Google sees legitimate local activity, not a review farm.

#### Lever 2: Citation speed-run (500 per profile)

- Citations = directory listings: Yelp, Angi, Facebook Business Page, BBB, YellowPages, Foursquare, etc.
- Andy's agency builds **~500 citations per profile** as fast as possible.
- Why: when Google crawls the web for info on "Andy's House Cleaning of Lewisburg," it should find consistent NAP (Name/Address/Phone) data across hundreds of sources. Consistency = trust = ranking.

#### Lever 3: Hyper-local content on the website

- Build a dedicated location page for each new GBP, down to the neighborhood level.
- Include a **hyper-local FAQ section** — your normal customer questions, but rewritten with the location baked in.
    - Example: instead of "How much does a carpet clean cost?" → **"How much does a carpet clean cost in Lewisburg?"**
- Why: Google loves locally-scoped FAQ content; it's lightweight to produce; you already know the questions because customers ask them daily.
- Make the page's NAP (name/address/phone) **exactly match** the GBP for that location. Story alignment = trust.

## Critical Analysis / Caveats

1. **Andy is selling a service.** This is a content-marketing video for Stryker Digital. The strategy is real, but he's also routing viewers to a sales call — read the case study with that in mind.
2. **The "sample size" is N=1 (Rob).** One cleaning-company case study is illustrative, not statistical. Results will vary heavily by:
    - Local competition density
    - Existing brand equity
    - Service category (some have lower GBP saturation than others)
    - Operational capacity to absorb leads
3. **Google's tolerance changes.** What's "by the books" in 2026 may not be in 2027. The DBA + real office route has been stable for years, but Google's algorithm and human reviewers can shift posture (especially around suspected "lead-gen networks"). Watch for changes in Google's [GBP guidelines](https://support.google.com/business/answer/3038177).
4. **Operational cost is real.** 6 profiles × $300–500/mo office = **$1,800–$3,000/mo in rent** before you generate a single extra lead. Plus 6 DBAs ($300–600 setup), 6 phone lines, citation-building labor, content production. Andy hand-waves this with "you'll easily clear it" — true at scale, but a real cash-flow consideration for $500K-revenue operators.
5. **"Speed-running 500 citations per profile" is non-trivial.** That's either a paid service (BrightLocal, Whitespark, Yext) or a lot of manual labor. Worth budgeting.
6. **The "treat reviews like fuel" tactic depends on having a real customer list.** Newer businesses without 500+ past customers will struggle to seed 6 profiles to 100 reviews each.
