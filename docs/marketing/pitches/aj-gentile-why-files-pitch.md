# AJ Gentile — The Why Files

## Profile

- **Channel:** The Why Files
- **Subscribers:** ~6M+
- **Content:** Mysteries, conspiracy theories, unexplained phenomena — presented with a skeptical, research-heavy lens
- **Style:** Entertaining storytelling with a goldfish sidekick (Hecklefish), but genuinely rigorous research underneath
- **Contact:** @ajgentile on Instagram/Twitter, ajgentile@gmail.com, aj@betterbox.tv
- **Website:** thewhyfiles.com

## Why He's a Fit

AJ already brands himself as the guy who _actually does the research_. His whole value prop is separating signal from noise on fringe topics. But his research lives and dies inside YouTube videos — once an episode drops, the sources, timelines, and evidence chains are buried in a 40-minute video. He's built a reputation on rigor, but his web presence doesn't reflect that.

## Suggested Subject Line

**Your research is too good to live inside YouTube**

## Pitch (Email)

Hey AJ — I've been a Why Files fan for a while and what sets your channel apart is obvious: you actually do the work. The research, the source-checking, the timelines — it's a cut above.

But here's something I've been thinking about:

All that research essentially disappears once the episode goes live. YouTube is great for distribution, but it's terrible for _reference_. Nobody can cite a timestamp. Nobody can link to your source chain. The depth you put into each episode doesn't compound — it just scrolls away.

I've been building something that fixes this.

I create **research companion pages** for podcast episodes that organize:

- sources and primary documents
- timelines and event sequences
- key claims with supporting evidence
- entity maps (people, organizations, connections)
- fact checks and counterarguments

Basically turning episodes into **permanent, citable research hubs**.

The idea is simple — your videos stay the main event, but these pages become the **credibility layer** underneath. When a journalist, blogger, or fellow researcher wants to reference your work, they have something real to point to.

A few things this does:

- your episodes stay discoverable via search for years, not weeks
- journalists and researchers can actually cite your work
- your audience gets a deeper way to engage with the material
- it positions The Why Files as a **research resource**, not just a podcast

I'm not trying to sell you anything — I built this because I think your content genuinely deserves it. If it looks interesting, I'd love to explore building this out with you.
