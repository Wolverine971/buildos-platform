## Chapter 1: Origins (Birth - High School)

### The Basics

**Where were you born? Where did you grow up?**

I was born in Columbia, South Carolina. We moved to Ocala, Florida when I was like one year old. We lived in Ocala for a bit, then Middleburg (south of Jacksonville) for a little bit, then Winter Haven for about three years. When I was about 13, we moved up to Maryland, and I stayed in Maryland until I got out of high school. I went to community college and then joined the Marine Corps — boot camp on Parris Island, South Carolina, then stationed in Twentynine Palms, California. That's kind of where I grew up and where I've been.

**What was your family like?**

I have a brother who's 18 months younger than me and a sister a bit younger than that. My parents were Christian; my dad was a pastor. It was a good household, a lot of fun. No complaints about my childhood — I feel like I had a great childhood. The only thing that was rough was moving; it made things hard for me a little bit. The one big thing: my senior year of high school, we found out my dad had cancer.

### The Kid You Were

**What kind of kid were you?**

I was very social, extroverted. Athletic but a little nerdy. I wasn't the class clown — my brother was the class clown; he was really funny. I'm an Enneagram 8, so I was outgoing and boisterous, but I would pay attention in class. When I moved, it was a setback — I was the new kid, and that happened a few times. I'd say I was a quiet, athletic, kind of weird kid. I didn't have a lot of friends, but I did have good friends I kept in touch with.

**What did you care about as a kid? What obsessed you?**

When I was really young, I was obsessed with football and video games. I really got into lacrosse, and when wrestling hit, I was super passionate about wrestling and got super into working out. I got in really good shape — that kind of helped me get into the sniper platoon later. I also played chess as a little kid and liked it; I liked thinking methodically like that.

**What was school like for you? Did you fit in?**

I don't think I fit in, really. When I was really young in lower school in Middleburg, I was like the golden child, the star. When we moved to Winter Haven, to a new school, I wasn't the best guy anymore. I struggled a little bit, but I also wasn't afraid of being different. I wanted to be popular, sort of, but I was okay with not being popular.

**Was there a formative childhood moment?**

A few. I got held back in second grade and put in a special program for delayed learning — a "discovery class." They gave us brain exercises. One was rhythmic writing, where you draw figure eights and call out what you're doing as your arms move — it's supposed to connect the right and left hemispheres of your brain. I was good at math but not good at English, and they said it was procrastination, but really I was just too much in my head — I needed to get words out on paper. There was this thing called a blue book that helps you with tricky words. I think all those little mental exercises helped me out.

The other thing is my dad was a pastor, and we would have these deep theological conversations and go down the rabbit hole. So I'm an extrovert deep thinker — Enneagram 8 vibe — who got to athletics by having to grit and grind his way through.

### The Teenage Years

**What were you like as a teenager?**

Typical teenager, but I was a pastor's kid. At my church I was like a rock star — really friendly and extroverted, with a really close friend group at the church. Not that close at school; I kind of faded into the background there, and my only outlet was sports. One cool thing: I went and worked on a farm for a summer.

**Who were your friends?**

My wrestling buddies, mostly. By senior year I was one of the captains of the wrestling team and got to help out some dudes, so I'm really good friends with all my wrestling guys.

**Were you ambitious, aimless, rebellious, or something else?**

I was ambitious but I didn't know what I wanted to do with the rest of my life. I wanted a scholarship but didn't put much thought into how to get one, and my parents weren't pushing college — it was kind of "figure out what you want to do." I wasn't rebellious. I just really tried to be a hard worker in general.

**What did you think you wanted to do with your life?**

I didn't know. I wanted to be a professional athlete — join the NFL, or be a wrestler. I was mostly focused on sports and didn't think about much else.

**Was there a moment in high school that matters to your story?**

I didn't make the JV soccer team, and a buddy said, "You should try out for wrestling — there's no cuts." Wrestling is brutally demanding, and my whole strategy was just don't quit. I remember so many times on the bus to practice having to psych myself up: I just need to get through this practice. That was a real growing thing for me. By senior year I was a team captain, a starter on the lacrosse team, and I scored a goal in the championship game. It was a comeback. _(Consolidated with the 9takes founder interview, 2026-06-11.)_

## Chapter 2: Before the Marines (Post-High School)

### The In-Between

**What happened after high school?**

I went to community college for two years and played college lacrosse, which was cool, but I wasn't a starter and it kinda sucked. The weird thing is I had great conditioning and I was really fast — I was just undersized and didn't quite shine. And I didn't connect with my teammates; they were all partying and doing drugs, and I was like, "Don't you want to do something meaningful with your life?" I remember feeling that tension. I also worked landscaping. This was my first time partying and drinking after high school — I'd gone to a private Christian school, and after graduating I got a car and a lot more freedom. But I didn't want to continue with college.

**The programming class — what happened there?**

I took a programming class, and I just remember them talking about objects and functions and parameters, and it didn't make sense to me at all. It was very theoretical, not hands-on, and I just didn't like it. I dropped it.

### The Lead-Up

**What was your life like before you enlisted?**

I was working hard, partying, but also aimless. I was like, "I need to do something big with my life" — and joining the Marine Corps was something big. I was super pumped to join.

**Were you searching for something? Running from something?**

I was searching. I felt like I didn't have the discipline to do what I needed to do. I was burnt out of school — I didn't want to go to school anymore. And my dad had cancer, and I remember thinking: I can't put my life on hold thinking about what could or couldn't happen. He was stable, but I couldn't stay around and put my life on pause.

**What made you start thinking about the military?**

I didn't even think of it as an option until my brother was flirting with the idea of joining. I was like, "Wait, yeah — I want to do that. I totally want to join the military."

### The Decision

**Why the Marines specifically?**

I knew I wanted to be special forces, so I actually went to the Army recruiter first and took the ASVAB — scored pretty good. But the Army recruiters didn't impress me. I walked in and they were eating chicken, like I was interrupting their lunch break. They didn't give me the time of day. I was like, "These guys suck." As I was walking out, a poolee — a Marine waiting to go to boot camp — stopped me and said, "Hey, you should try the Marines." I walked in the Marine door and said, "Why should I join the Marines? Aren't they a bunch of roided-out jocks?" This Gunnery Sergeant came up to me and flipped the script: "Why should I let you join my Corps?" I was like, okay, the Marines are badass. I told them I wanted special forces, and they said there's no joining special forces directly — you work your way up. They gave me a recon contract.

**What did you hope to find or become?**

I just wanted to do something badass. I knew I needed to get out of there and do something else, and I wanted to be around people who were intense and motivated. And boy, did I get that — at least in boot camp. I was surprised after I joined that not everyone pours their heart and soul into the Marines. A lot of people just suck — they do it for the T-shirt, do it for the clout.

**How did people react?**

My dad was hit hard by it — "Wow, I can't believe my son's joining the Marines" — but also stunned and impressed. My mom was surprised too, but she was like, "Okay, that makes sense." My brother and sister were probably inspired a little bit, but also like, "DJ's crazy."

**What did it feel like to make that commitment?**

I was just excited. Really excited. I honestly had the attitude of: bring it on. I'm ready for a challenge.

## Chapter 3: The Marines (2012-2017)

### Boot Camp & Early Days

**What did boot camp do to you mentally?**

It was really hard mentally. Just... like brainwashing, because you don't have your phone, you don't have anything. You're doing everything they tell you to for three months. You don't move anywhere without being told to, you're always in formation, you have to ask permission to use the head. It's really strict — but good. I was a squad leader, and then everything went to shit the last two or three weeks where I lost my squad leader position. There's a story there, but I don't want to get into it.

**Was there a moment you almost quit?**

No, I didn't almost quit at all. I was crushing it in boot camp.

**What surprised you about yourself?**

Nothing really. Boot camp wasn't the hard part — infantry training, recon, and scout sniper school were the hard parts.

### Infantry & Scout Sniper

**What drew you to infantry initially?**

I wanted infantry because I wanted to be special forces — that was the path. I joined with a recon contract, which meant after infantry training I got to go to recon.

**What made you pursue the sniper path?**

I was in MART — Marines Awaiting Recon Training — where they work you out twice a day and basically beat you down, but you also get stronger, faster, a better swimmer. I was in the recon training pipeline for about seven months and classed up twice for the actual recon course. The first time I failed on land nav. The second time I made it past land nav, made it past pool competency... and then I failed the knots test. (The full story is in the 9takes founder interview: the knot was a figure-eight loop with one pigtail tied off in the wrong direction — and the day before, I'd flown around Camp Pendleton spy-rigged to a helicopter on that exact knot. They kicked me out anyway.)

After I failed, I was assigned to the needs of the Marine Corps, and I went before the monitor who reassigns your job and said, "Please let me stay infantry." He had mercy on me. I got assigned to 29 Palms, and I was in a line platoon — those guys sucked. Then I saw a buddy at the armory doing sniper stuff and asked, "How do I do that?" He said you've got to do the indoc. So I did the indoc — really hard — and made it through. I wasn't the top performer, but people were quitting and I was one of the few who didn't quit.

**What was sniper training like? What sticks with you?**

There's a whole arc here. I got into the sniper platoon and it was freaking amazing — hard like recon, and me as an Enneagram 8, I was loving it. Went through pre-sniper, and then right before deployment the battalion commander said they had to cut one of the four sniper teams — and they cut mine. I wanted to deploy, so I went back to my old infantry line platoon commander and said, "Sir, I really want to deploy. Do you have any spots for me?" He said, "DJ, I'll make a spot for you," and I'm forever grateful. I deployed to Afghanistan with them as a regular line-platoon dude — driver on some stuff, point man on some stuff. Uneventful deployment.

When we got back, I had to do the sniper indoc all over again to get back into the platoon. It was such a humbling thing — it's three or four days where they basically try to break you — but I knew what was coming and I was already seasoned. I got back in, crushed pre-sniper this time, got a slot to sniper school, and passed. The closest thing I almost failed was land navigation, but I did good on the shooting and the stalking — the two parts a lot of people fail. That whole comeback — getting cut, earning my way back, passing sniper school — is the thing that sticks with me. Failing and trying again and coming back. I'm not afraid of failure.

### The People

**Who were the Marines you served with?**

A lot of good guys — a lot of good guys in the sniper platoon. (And some terrible ones. That's the Marines.)

**Was there a leader or mentor who shaped you?**

Several, but the one person was this guy named Mayman. He was shorter than me, and he also failed recon. He was extremely smart and overlooked — he knew everything, was an extremely good sniper, didn't have that alpha-male mentality, but he was better than everyone else. When I got kicked out of the sniper platoon and it was devastating, Mayman was the one who said, "Hey, you've got to try out again and get back in." So I did, and I got to be with Mayman. He went off and did MARSOC things. He's an extreme introvert — an Enneagram 5 — and I love that guy.

**What did you learn about people?**

Some people suck. Honestly, I didn't really get into _learning about people_ until I got married — that's when I got into personality tests and the Enneagram deeply, which is why I created 9takes. But that's a whole separate thing.

### MARSOC & Getting Out

**What made you try out for MARSOC?**

I was frustrated with the Marine Corps after my second deployment. I hadn't gotten to put my training to use — it was just a bunch of training. Not that I _wanted_ to shoot anyone, but I didn't want to train for nothing. So it was: go do special forces, do MARSOC, or get out.

**What was it like not getting selected?**

I should have prepared more. Not getting selected was devastating, but I was also like — moving on. In retrospect, I should have prepared more, but the fact that I didn't get selected... I think it was fate. Just got to move on.

**What made you decide to get out?**

I said I was either joining MARSOC or getting out. I didn't get MARSOC, so I got out.

**What were you thinking about the future?**

I wanted to start my own business. (I literally bought the domain wayneenterprises.enterprises one night and thought I was going to run multiple businesses. It went nowhere — but it was the first founder move.)

### Lasting Impact

**What do you still carry today?**

A tolerance for pain, ambiguity, and hardship. Nothing is going to be as hard as what I did in the Marines, at least physically. Mental resilience and toughness in a general sense.

**What did you have to unlearn?**

Hard work doesn't get you where you want to go all the time. You need to manage relationships and work closely with others. You can't just do hard work in a silo.

**How do you think about that chapter now?**

I remember it with bittersweetness. I really miss the community and the guys. But the military is a shit show — there's a bunch of good guys and a bunch of bad guys, and a bunch of bureaucracy that I do not miss.

## Chapter 4: Becoming Technical (Post-Marines)

### The Transition

**What was it like becoming a civilian again?**

It was a short transition — I got my EAS papers leaving the Marine Corps March 25th, and I started coding bootcamp April 1st. Only a few days. And I was newly married.

**How did you end up reading Tim Ferriss and deciding to get technical?**

My dad actually told me about Tim Ferriss. I followed him, listened to his podcast a ton, read his books. He talked about startups, and I was like, "I want to do that." And his line stuck: if you're not technical, you're at the mercy of the people who are technical. So I needed to be technical, so I could build my own ideas and not rely on other people to build them.

**Why a coding bootcamp? Why Sabio?**

I met a guy who was a Marine who went through the bootcamp, graduated, and went back as an entrepreneur to have the bootcamp coders build his app idea. He was making moves, and I was like, "I want to do what you do." Sabio was also great because they partnered with Antioch University so my GI Bill could pay for it. It ended up being a great choice — I learned a ton there.

### Learning to Code

**What was bootcamp like after being a scout sniper?**

It was great. Intense mentally — you're there eight, ten hours a day coding, working on stuff. That set the pace for what I was going to do.

**Did any of the military skills transfer?**

Yes. Being thoughtful and methodical. And I loved the idea that I could get paid to think — the Marines had no creativity, and I was like, "I can figure things out and get paid for it? That's a skill? I love that." The other thing: as a scout sniper you brief missions to commanders — you talk to generals — so I was used to presenting and teaching. The bootcamp was full of introverted people, and I was the extrovert who loved demoing what we built. That became my thing.

**Was there a moment when it clicked?**

Just the gratifying feeling of creating something. That was great. Loved that.

### Early Career

**What was it like getting hired by Sabio after graduating?**

It was a relief off my back, because I didn't have to search for a job immediately. But part of my job was coding and another part was sales — they'd secretly hired me more for my sales potential and my military network. They wanted me to recruit other military dudes. They didn't really care about my coding. And I sucked at it — my plan was to get on base and talk to people, and I couldn't even get on base. So it didn't work out, and they let me go after a few months.

**Why did you move back to the East Coast?**

We didn't have enough money to pay LA rent after I lost the Sabio job. So we had a Chevy Tracker, I attached a hitch, we got a U-Haul, threw all our stuff in, and did a four-or-five-day road trip back to the East Coast, back to Maryland, and restarted life there.

**The Census Bureau? The lawyer platform? The Air Force document system?**

The Census Bureau (through a company called MetroStar) was very comical — bureaucratic, literal cubicles. "Wow, this is what it's like." I was good at my job, we did a bunch of Agile stuff, very corporatey, but it was right — that started my legit coding career. The Marine Corps lawyer platform was a shit show, very scrappy. That and the Air Force document system were my first remote jobs. The Air Force system was an intelligence document ingest system — a graph database linking pieces of documents together, before LLMs. The problem was only one or two dudes understood how it worked and they didn't teach anyone. If you're going to have a system, it needs to be understood by everyone so everyone can build on it. Looking back, it was people monetizing being smart instead of building something useful.

What I realized across all of them: these companies aren't that great. I need to do something better — which is why I got super excited when I got the opportunity to work for a Y Combinator-backed startup called Curri.

### The Thread

**What connects these early career experiences to what you're building now?**

I was a scrappy person who could build stuff, prototype stuff, and demo it. That's the thing that carried through to Curri and to now.

## Chapter 5: Curri (The Inflection Point)

### Joining Curri

**How did you end up at Curri?**

I put my resume in the Y Combinator work-at-a-startup portal and didn't hear back for a long time. Then out of the blue, at the right time, the founder of Curri hit me up. It was awesome. The coding challenge wasn't algorithm-based — it was building a web scraper, and I'd already built a scraper to collect Enneagram content for what became 9takes. So I did great with it and got the job. _(Detail from the 9takes founder interview, 2026-06-11.)_

**What was the company like? What was your role?**

It was the best company I ever worked for. My role was software engineer. Really good team vibe — everyone was great, interesting, young, and motivated, all excited to work at Curri.

**What excited you about working at a YC-backed startup?**

I knew it was legit. A Y Combinator-backed startup receiving investment was a major step above all my other experiences, and I was working with really great people.

### What You Built

**The exception system**

This was brought to me as a design — "DJ, you're going to build this." A PM had been reviewing our service-ops people's workflow and realized they had a static dashboard of deliveries they were responsible for, and they'd just be changing and tweaking things to stay busy. He said: they need to not just stare at the same list — we need a system where they get pinged to deal with issues. So we built a queue system that fills up with issues people need to triage. I set everything up — full-stack stuff, a lot going on there. It was great.

**The integrations work (Uber, Lyft, DoorDash)**

This was what I was mainly known for — DSP work, delivery service provider work. Basically me reverse-engineering these delivery providers' APIs and plugging them into our driver-search algorithm, so if the proper conditions were met we could dispatch a delivery to Uber or Lyft or whoever. I worked directly with the founder of Curri on this — he had the rough idea and said, "DJ, go make it work," and I made it work. Our first prototype was with Uber, then we expanded to Lyft and DoorDash and a few smaller ones. That was something I owned, and it was great.

**What project are you most proud of?**

Probably the DSP integrations. The other project I built — that no one freaking used — was the sales map. Sales reps trying to grow their accounts wouldn't know who's booking what; they'd have to do SQL queries to get information on what's going on. I built this map where you could search through account structures and see geographic locations — situational awareness about what's happening with an account. The salespeople who saw it thought it was amazing. An extremely interesting project that didn't get picked up. I'm still frustrated about it today.

### The Frustrations

**The Linear frustration**

I hated Linear. It was a way for my manager to get context on what I was doing, but it didn't help me in my job in any way.

**Other things that bothered you about how work was organized?**

Honestly, not really — I really liked so many of the people I worked with. I butted heads with one manager. One thing I loved about Curri was that it was engineering-led in the beginning: we'd write design specs and problem statements, go figure out the problem, create the documents, then go build. Then it became more bureaucratic, a middle-manager kind of deal — I started building things PMs had spec'd instead of doing my own design documents. The big frustration was the sales map initiative: I'd heard from multiple sources that growth always came back to sales, so I dove into the sales process and built the map tool in our admin panel. The salespeople I demoed it to wanted their hands on it, and the founder said it was really interesting — but my manager was against it and wouldn't let me merge the code. It sat in my branch. That pissed me off to no end. I had a disagreement with my manager, and he basically won.

**When did you start thinking "there has to be a better way"?**

After leaving Curri, the realization was: I put so much work and effort into Curri and building stuff, and no one cared about the thing I built. I was like, "I'll just build my own thing."

### Leaving

**What made you finally leave Curri? What did you think you'd do next?**

It came to a head over the map-tool initiative — the disagreement with my manager — and my time at Curri ended in March 2025. My learning: you really have to work with people; you can't just trust that your work speaks for itself. After that I was like, "What should I do? AI is becoming a thing. I should deep-dive into AI and pour all my effort into starting my own company, my own business." _(Public framing per DJ's ruling: "I left Curri and moved on to BuildOS" — see FOUNDER_DEEP_CONTEXT.md Sensitive Index.)_

## Chapter 6: BuildOS (The Present)

### The Origin

**When did the idea for BuildOS crystallize?**

After leaving Curri (started building May 2025). I had this idea for context engineering that was the origin of BuildOS: you have context, and you repeat yourself across all these agentic AI chat threads. I was having many chats and saving documents about certain things just to re-paste them, and I realized I need to create this thing where I don't have to recreate the starting point every time — I can build off what I talked about last time. This was before AI started remembering things about you. I wasn't that big into RAG or anything; I just knew I wanted to build context. The idea was that your context will compound. You just need to store the context, and as the AI agents get better, they'll be able to take more advantage of it.

And one more thing that matters: I wanted the memory of the AI agent to be transparent. With the big AI companies, you don't know what they know about you or what they've stored. BuildOS should have that information be transparent — you can see what the agent knows about you, and you should be able to export it. No hidden stuff.

**What were the first versions like?**

The first version was just based around brain dumping information — I brain dump and store it in a project, kind of a one-way conversation. Then I added more of an agentic chat. Then I added unneeded complexity with this whole ontology thing — I overengineered it — and I was like, "Okay, we need to stick to the basics," and backed off some of that.

**What have you learned about the problem you're solving?**

I do have a decent solution, but it's more about getting users and growing the product with the users. That's one thing I'm realizing.

### The Journey So Far

**What have been the highs?**

So many highs — I have learned so many things. I was doing context engineering before it became trendy, and I was building a harness before the idea of a harness became trendy.

**What have been the lows?**

The collaboration side hasn't landed yet — BuildOS today does project sharing, building context and then working with people, but I haven't gotten teams to work with it, and I'm trying to figure that out.

**What surprised you about building this?**

It's just crazy to witness the capabilities of the LLM models. They just keep getting better.

### Right Now

**Where is BuildOS today?**

Project sharing — building context and then working with people. The solution is decent; the work now is users.

**What are you most excited about?**

The idea of collaboration would be really impactful, and the idea of skills. And one of the main things with BuildOS is being able to use cheaper models to do work. If I can use cheaper models, I can have these models running endlessly. It enables people who aren't technical to do crazy stuff — if you can do a lot more with cheaper models, you're punching above your weight class. That's the end goal.

**What keeps you up at night?**

Somebody building something similar to what I'm doing — so many people are doing context engineering or harnesses, but they haven't landed on exactly my thing. I constantly think about: is BuildOS easy to copy? _(Internal — do not publish; see FOUNDER_DEEP_CONTEXT.md Sensitive Index.)_

### Who You Are
