## 2. The Chapters

### Chapter 1: Pastor's Kid `[human]`

Born Columbia, SC. Dad was a pastor — youth pastor in Middleburg, FL, first pastorate in Winter Haven, FL, then a church in Glen Burnie, Maryland when DJ was ~13 (younger congregation, more room to lead; the Florida church was "full of old people who didn't really like what he was doing").

Five-person family: dad, mom, DJ (oldest), brother ~18 months younger, sister younger still. Good childhood, no complaints — the hard part was the moves. "Every move was a setback where I was the new kid, and that happened a few times."

**Living life on hard mode:**

- Held back in second grade; placed in a special program for delayed learning. They did "rhythmic writing" — drawing figure eights while calling out the motion, supposed to connect the brain hemispheres. Good at math, behind in English/reading. The diagnosis was "procrastination" but really: "I was too much in my head. I just needed to get words out on paper."
- Top of his class his first year at the Maryland private school — then it was too expensive, so mom homeschooled him for 7th–8th grade. "We didn't really learn anything." By high school, the kids he used to outscore were in the advanced classes and he was in the beginner ones. C student, barely skipping by.
- Didn't make the JV soccer team. A buddy said: **"Try out for wrestling — there's no cuts."** Wrestling became the crucible: "I would have to psych myself up on the bus — I just need to get through this practice." Senior year: team captain. Also lacrosse all four years, starter, scored a goal in the championship game.

**The dad relationship (load-bearing for everything):**

- "My relationship with my dad was amazing... We would have these really deep intellectual conversations. I went to him with everything."
- "He didn't talk down to me. He explained things to me. He treated me like a man when I didn't even deserve it."
- Dad's only fault: didn't sort out the kids' fights ("Figure it out") — so sibling grievances lingered. DJ had an anger problem as a kid; the brother was "schemey" with words, DJ would lash out and get in trouble. Formative scene: mom laughing while DJ raged about an injustice → "I can never lose my shit. I can never be in a place where I need someone to give me justice." `[internal]` (family dynamics — per 9takes ruling)
- **Dad introduced DJ to Tim Ferriss.** The chain that leads to everything: dad → Tim Ferriss → "if you're not technical you're at the mercy of whoever is" → bootcamp → engineer → founder.
- Senior year of high school: dad diagnosed with cancer. He died right after DJ passed sniper school (~2015/16). "The highest high of my life and the biggest loss, at the same time. I don't think I ever fully processed it." `[internal]` — **DJ ruling 2026-06-11: do not use his dad's death in public content.** It's okay, it was a while ago, but it's sensitive — never for clout. The _living_ dad material (intellectual conversations, Tim Ferriss introduction, "he treated me like a man") is fine.

Kid stuff: obsessed with football and video games, played chess ("I liked thinking methodically like that"), wanted to be Batman or a soldier. Extroverted but quiet at school — "rock star" at church, athletic-but-nerdy, "okay with not being popular." Worked on a farm one summer. `[human]`

### Chapter 2: The Aimless Years (Post-HS) `[human]`

Two years of community college lacrosse — great conditioning, undersized, not a starter. Didn't connect with teammates: "They were all partying and doing drugs, and I was like, 'Don't you want to live a life of purpose?'"

Landscaping: "Breaking my back, sweating my butt off... there's no reward for being tough doing landscaping. The hardest workers got paid the same as the guys coasting. If I'm gonna break my back, I want to break my back doing something that's gonna get me somewhere."

Took an intro programming class — "objects and functions and parameters... very theoretical, not hands-on" — hated it, dropped it. (The same person later thrives at a hands-on bootcamp. The lesson he draws: it was bad, abstract teaching, not him.) `[buildos]` — useful for "learning styles / show don't tell" content.

The push: dad had cancer. "I can't put my life on hold thinking about what could or couldn't happen... I can't stay around here and put my life on pause." His brother was flirting with joining the military — "Wait, yeah. I want to do that."

### Chapter 3: The Marines (~2012/13–2017) `[human]` — military is "a nice little treat," never the headline

**The recruiting scene (great story, cleared by usage history):** Wanted special forces. Went to the Army recruiter first, scored well on the ASVAB — but the recruiters were eating chicken and wouldn't give him the time of day. On his way out, a poolee said "try the Marines." DJ walked in hot: "Why should I join the Marines? Aren't they a bunch of roided-out jocks?" The Gunnery Sergeant flipped it: **"Why should I let you join my Corps?"** Sold. Recon contract.

**Boot camp (Parris Island):** Squad leader most of the way, "crushing it." Demoted in the final weeks (the vending-machine incident — drill instructors turned on him, took his original eagle-globe-anchor from the Crucible). `[internal]` — in the 2026-06-11 interview DJ said "there's a story there but I don't want to get into it." Treat as do-not-push.

**The recon gauntlet:** MART holding platoon ~7 months ("they try to drown you in the pool and run you to death — but you survive and get stronger"). Failed BRC attempt #1 on land nav. Attempt #2: passed land nav, passed pool competency, then **failed the knots test** — a figure-eight loop tied with one pigtail facing the wrong direction. The day before, he'd flown around Camp Pendleton spy-rigged to a helicopter **on that exact knot**. Kicked out anyway. Begged the monitor to keep him infantry; the monitor had mercy.

**29 Palms:** Told he was going to "29 Palms" — "Huh, sounds tropical." It's the California desert. Line platoon sucked; he'd just survived recon training so the fleet hazing bounced off ("whatever you're doing to us is nowhere near what recon just did to me"). Saw a buddy rucking at the armory — "I'm with snipers, you should try out." Made it through indoc the way he makes it through everything: **"I wasn't the top performer, but people kept quitting and I was one of the few who didn't."**

**Cut, and the comeback (the defining arc):** Right before deployment, battalion cut one of four sniper teams — his. He went to his old infantry commander and begged: "Sir, I really wanted to deploy. Do you have any spots for me?" — "DJ, I'll make a spot for you." ("I am forever grateful.") Deployed to Afghanistan as a regular line-platoon dude (driver, point man; uneventful, ~2013/14). Came back, had to do the brutal sniper indoc **all over again** — did it, got back in, crushed pre-sniper, earned a sniper school slot, and passed (almost failed only land nav; aced shooting and stalking, the two big killers). "A final triumphant thing... an amazing comeback story for me."

**Then his dad died.** Right after sniper school. (See Chapter 1; `[internal]` — ruled out of public content.)

**Deployment #2 (~2016):** Kuwait, TRAP force (recover downed pilots/aircraft/drones). Two snipers on base; swapped partners after friction, loved the second guy; both made honor grad at NCO course. But: nothing happened. Two deployments, never fired a round. "What's the point of training so hard when you don't do what you're trained to do?"

**MARSOC and out:** "I'm either going special operations or I'm getting out." Didn't prepare — "I'm a sniper, I'm tough, bring it" — and survived rather than thrived. Completed selection but wasn't picked (~200 start → ~50 finish → ~20–30 selected). Newly married, a little distracted, mostly already disenfranchised. "I took it as a sign from God that it's not meant to be." `[internal]` — per 9takes ruling: note, don't publicly push the MARSOC non-select.

**What the Marines left him:**

- "A tolerance for pain, ambiguity, and hardship. Nothing is going to be as hard as what I did in the Marines."
- What he had to unlearn: "Hard work doesn't get you where you want to go all the time. You can't just do hard work in a silo. You have to manage relationships." (Foreshadows Curri.)
- Snipers ≠ shooting: "It's mostly mission planning. If you plan a mission well, then you get the opportunity to deploy your rifle."
- Stalking: "In the beginning it feels like luck. As you get really good, it feels like disciplined execution. A really good plan executed well — that's what makes you a good sniper."
- Mentor: **Mayman** (spelling confirmed by DJ, 2026-06-11) — small, not physically imposing, smarter and better-planned than everyone, a great teacher, an Enneagram 5 introvert who told DJ to try out for the platoon again after he was cut. "I love that guy."
- Mixed feelings: "I remember it with bittersweetness. I miss the community and the guys. The military is a shit show... bureaucracy I do not miss."
- The brief he got in trouble for: a JAG asked how you tell hostile act from hostile intent. DJ, half-asleep from a training mission: **"You don't know until you try."** The room was disgusted. It's now basically his life motto — and his answer for what his tombstone would say. `[human]` — great self-deprecating story.

How she'd describe him: "weird, quirky, tough, kind, loving, stubborn, not a good listener but also a good listener."

Enneagram dynamic (8 + 7): he learned to make space for her fear instead of saying "don't worry about that"; she learned to let his anger dissipate instead of fighting it. This is the origin of the entire 9takes journey. `[9takes]` — the full version lives in the 9takes founder story.

### Chapter 5: Becoming Technical (~2017–2022) `[buildos]`

Six-day transition: EAS March 25, bootcamp April 1. Met a former-Marine roommate at a meetup; VA/GI Bill paperwork came through (Sabio partnered with Antioch University so the GI Bill paid).

**Why it worked when college didn't:** "They built you up in an intuitive way with practical, hands-on learning... In college it was abstract, lecture-based. Bootcamp was 'we'll show you how to do everything.'" Eight to ten hours a day coding — "that set the pace for what I was going to do."

**What transferred from sniping:** Concentration, being methodical — and **briefing**. "As a scout sniper you brief missions to commanders, you talk to generals. So when it came to demoing what we built, I loved it." The extrovert who can code _and_ explain code became his career edge. The moment it clicked: "Just the gratifying feeling of creating something."

The big realization: **"I can get paid to think."** (As a sniper, all that planning didn't matter if you never deployed. As an engineer, thinking is the job.)

**Sabio hired him, then let him go:** They wanted his network and military background for recruiting, not his code. He couldn't get on base to do presentations, sales flopped, he was out in ~3 months. Rent in LA was impossible → hitched a U-Haul to the Chevy Tracker, drove 4–5 days back to Maryland with Anna. `[internal-ish]` — the firing itself is fine to mention lightly (per 9takes: don't push), but the U-Haul scene is good color.

"The best company I ever worked for... really good team vibe, everyone young, motivated, excited."

**What he built:**

Worked directly with the founder ("he had the rough idea — 'DJ, go make it work' — and I made it work"). Prototype with Uber, then expanded. **"I was doing agentic tool use before it was called that."**
3. **The sales map tool (the wound)** — sales reps couldn't see what was happening in their accounts without SQL queries. DJ took initiative, built a map for searching account structures geographically. Salespeople loved it; the founder said "this is really interesting"; **his manager blocked it and the code died in his branch, never merged.** "That pissed me off to no end... I'm still frustrated about it today." `[internal]` for the manager conflict specifics; the _lesson_ is public-safe.

**The Linear frustration (content gold, already in use):** "It was a way for my manager to get context on what I was doing, but it didn't help me in my job in any way. Just look at my pull requests."

**The cultural arc:** Engineering-led at first — problem statements, design docs, go build. Then middle managers arrived and he was building PMs' specs instead of writing his own design docs. Then they let him go — "you're distracted" — over the map tool initiative. His learning, stated twice across interviews: **"You really have to work with people. You can't just trust that your work speaks for itself."**

**Public framing (DJ ruling, 2026-06-11):** Say **"I left Curri and moved on to BuildOS."** Avoid "let go" / "fired" in public content — not hiding it, just being sensitive about it. The frustrations (Linear, the map tool lesson) are fine to use; the departure mechanics are not.

### Chapter 8: BuildOS (May 2025 – present) `[buildos]` — THE chapter for marketing

**The origin, in his own words (new, 2026-06-11 — this is the best version on record):**

> "After leaving Curri, the realization was: I put so much work and effort into Curri and building stuff, and no one cared about the thing I built. I was like, 'I'll just build my own thing.' So I was like, 'I'm gonna experiment with AI.' I had this idea for context engineering that was the origin of BuildOS: you have context, and you repeat yourself across all these agentic AI chat threads. We need a project where we can do stuff. I wasn't that big into RAG or anything — I just knew I wanted to build context. The idea was that **your context will compound. You just need to store the context, and as the AI agents get better, they'll be able to take more advantage of it.**"

That last line is the crispest BuildOS thesis DJ has ever stated. Pair it with the earlier origin texture (Jan 2026): chats he never went back to, pasting the same documents into ChatGPT, "I wanted a thing I could just talk to, and it understands where I'm coming from."

**The origin scene (added 2026-06-11):** He was running many agentic AI chat threads and loving it — but he kept **repeating the same information to every chat**, and kept saving documents about certain things just to re-paste them. The realization: "I need to create this thing where I don't have to recreate the starting point every time. I can build off what I talked about last time." Crucially, **this was before the AI companies added memory** — he was solving the problem before the platforms acknowledged it existed.

**Transparent memory (core principle, first stated 2026-06-11 — not in any other doc):**

> "I wanted the memory of the AI agent to be transparent. These big companies that do AI agents — you don't know what they know about you. You don't know what they stored. BuildOS should have that information be transparent: you can see what the agent knows about you. We're not doing any hidden stuff. You should be able to see all the context that BuildOS has on you, and you should be able to export it."

This is a differentiator _and_ a trust pillar: visible memory + exportable context vs. the black-box memory of big AI platforms. It pairs naturally with the anti-AI "lead with relief" stance — no AI magic happening behind your back.

**What BuildOS refuses to become (DJ, 2026-06-11):**

> "There's so much AI stuff, and I want BuildOS to be a **tool for people**. I don't want it to have the AI magic that does everything for you. It should be a **thinking environment** — you brainstorm, go into a project, go deep. BuildOS helps you clarify your thinking and get organized so you can keep moving forward."

Red lines, stated plainly: no black-box memory, no hidden context, no "AI does everything for you" magic. The user thinks; BuildOS clarifies and organizes. (This is the founder-voice version of the thinking-environment positioning in `docs/marketing/strategy/` — now it's _his words_, not just strategy copy.)

**Product evolution (his honest version):**

1. v1: brain dumping into projects — "kind of a one-way conversation"
2. Added agentic chat — "the agent knows shit too"
3. Built the ontology — **"I added unneeded complexity... I overengineered it"** — then backed off to basics. (Building-in-public gold: the founder admitting the overengineering detour.)
4. Current: project sharing / collaboration, skills, and the cheaper-models thesis

**Where he is now (June 2026):**

- "I do have a decent solution, but it's more about getting users and growing the product with the users." Hasn't gotten teams using collaboration yet; figuring that out.
- Highs: "I was doing context engineering before it became trendy, and I was building a harness before the idea of a harness became trendy."
- What surprised him: "It's crazy to witness the capabilities of the models. They just keep getting better."
- **Most excited about:** collaboration, skills, and: "One of the main things with BuildOS is being able to use cheaper models to do work. If I can use cheaper models, I can have these models running endlessly... It enables people who aren't technical to do crazy stuff. If you can do a lot more with cheaper models, **you're punching above your weight class.**"
- **What keeps him up at night:** "Somebody building something similar... so many people are doing context engineering or harnesses, but they haven't landed on exactly my thing. I constantly think about: is BuildOS easy to copy?" `[internal]` — do not publish the copyability fear.
- Money is tight; survival mode; freelancing on the side. `[internal]`

**"Don't tell them, show them."** DJ used to keep this written on paper on his wall: "I'd rather show people what I can do and what I am about through my actions rather than trying to convince them with my words." — This is the anti-AI show-don't-tell marketing strategy, stated as a personal value years before the strategy doc existed. Use it as the bridge between founder identity and brand doctrine. `[buildos]` `[human]`
