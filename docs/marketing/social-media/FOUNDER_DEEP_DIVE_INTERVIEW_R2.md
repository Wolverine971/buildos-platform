## Section 1: Before the Marines - The Foundation

### Early Life & Family

1. Where did you grow up? Describe the place - what was it like?

Where did I grow up? I grew up in Florida. Actually, I was born in Columbia, South Carolina. We moved to Florida. My dad was a pastor. He was a youth pastor in a place called Middleburg, Florida, and then he got his first pastor gig in a place called Winter Haven, Florida. I grew up as a pastor's kid. When I was around 13, we moved up to Glen Burnie, Maryland, where there was another pastor, and I went to school at private Christian schools. I was also home schooled for two years in seventh and eighth grade.

2. What was your family situation? Parents, siblings, dynamics?

Family situation? We have five people in my family: dad, mom. I'm the oldest. I have a younger brother who's 18 months younger than me, and my sister is a little bit younger than that. We had a good dynamic; however, my dad passed away after I joined the Marine Corps, it must have been around 2015. And after he passed away, my siblings and I got married at different times. My brother got married first, then I got married, and my sister got married, and also my mom remarried. We're all kinda spread out right now.

Among the siblings, we had fierce competition. I had an anger problem, and I would lose my temper, and I would beat my brother and sister up. Not so much my sister, but me and my brother, because we were very close in age. I got held back in second grade, and we were so close together. He would annoy me, and then I would lash out with violence. That was kind of our dynamic. He was the smart mouth; I was the dumb person who would lash out.

Growing up, it was a battle for me to control my anger. It wasn't all bad with me and my brother; we had some good times, but it was marked by competition, I would say.

3. What was your relationship with your dad like? Your mom?

My relationship with my dad was amazing. I love my dad so much. We would have these really deep intellectual conversations; it's very stimulating. I went to him with everything. My mom, I love my mom, but me and her weren't that close, and since my dad's death we haven't been that close because she's sort of moved on, remarried, and we're just not as close.

4. Were you a good student? A troublemaker? The quiet kid? The class clown?

Okay, so I was a really good student, but when we moved to Maryland, I went to a private Christian school and I was doing great in all the classes, but it was too expensive. I got home-schooled for two years, and in those two years I didn't really learn anything. I really got held back, because when I went to ninth grade into high school, all the kids that I was smarter than were in better classes than me, and I was like behind the pack. The two years of being homeschooled really hurt me.

I mean, I like to think that I'm pretty decently smart, but school-wise I was definitely struggling. As far as that, I was pretty athletic. I did soccer for two years and then played football my senior year. I did wrestling all four years and I did lacrosse all four years. But I wasn't that social; I was more of the quiet kid.

5. What did you want to be when you grew up - like, when you were 10?

When I was like ten, I wanted to be Batman or some superhero, maybe a soldier. I don't know.

6. Was there a moment in childhood that you look back on now and think "that shaped who I am"?

OK, in a negative light, I have a negative experience that I think about, where my siblings and I, there was fierce competition. So one time my brother was always better at words than me. He would always just manipulate the situation to get the better upper hand with me and on a lot of things. If I would always just rage because things weren't fair, he would make me mad, and then I'd punch him, and then I'd get in trouble and he'd get his way. That was the kind of dynamic that we had growing up. I wasn't schemey at all, but he was very schemey, as I think about it.

Anyway, one time he did something where he lied or manipulated the situation, and I was losing my shit and I was screaming at him. My mom was like, "What's going on?" I was like, "He did this, he did this," and she kind of started laughing and she's like, "DJ, you're losing your mind." I was like, "It infuriated me even more, cuz I was like I was trying to say that he was doing something that wasn't fair, and then my mom was laughing at my face." I was like, "I just felt like I couldn't defend myself or get justice for the situation, and my mom laughing at me losing my stuff just made me realize I can never lose my shit. I can never be in a place where I need someone to give me justice." That's kind of what I'm saying.

It's a negative example, but I would say positive examples. I had really great intellectual theological talks with my dad. It's weird because he's passed away now, but in my memory he was just the best. He didn't talk down to me. He explained things to me. He treated me like a man when I didn't even deserve it.

I feel like my dad's only fault was that he didn't discipline us kids that much. He didn't sort out our fights, and we would fight. He'd say, "Figure it out," so I feel like that kind of gave us a gap where our conflict kind of lingered and grievances built up. We never really learned how to figure out and sort out our grievances we had with each other.

7. What were you into as a kid? Sports, games, hobbies, obsessions?

Yeah, when I was a kid, I was pretty smart and athletic, and then I said the 2 years of being home-schooled really put me back. I did wrestling and lacrosse, and it was good.

## Section 2: The Decision to Join the Marines

### The Why

14. Why the Marines specifically? Not Army, not Navy, not Air Force - why the Marines?

Well, I wanted to be Special Forces, and I walked into the Army Special Recruiting thing, and they did not impress me. I was interrupting them. I went in hot to the Marines, and a gunny came up to me. He said, "I said to him, 'Why should I join the Marine Corps?' He told me, 'He flipped it on me and he said, 'Why should I let you join my Corps?'"

And basically they convinced me; they gave me a recon contract. They said it was basically like special forces, so I was like, "Okay, let's do it."

15. How old were you when you enlisted? What was going on in your life at that moment?

It was around 24 years old.

16. What were you hoping to find or become by joining?

I just want to become more disciplined, and I just wanted to do something badass, and I wanted to be respected, and I wanted to fight and kill bad guys, and I needed to get out of Glen Burnie. I needed to go see the world and get out of where I was.

17. Did anyone try to talk you out of it? What did your family think?

My family was really supportive, but my dad, I think, was surprised that I was joining the Marine Corps, but he was also like stunned and impressed too.

18. Were you scared? Excited? Numb? What did it feel like making that decision?

I was just mostly excited and just ready for the challenge, actually. I was in good shape, and I was just like, "Bring it on."

19. Was there a specific moment or conversation that pushed you over the edge to actually enlist?

So my brother actually signed up to go to the Army, and I was like, "Okay, if you're going to join the military, I'm gonna join the military." I started talking Marines, and I was just like, "Heck yeah, let's do this!"

### The Transformation

20. What was boot camp like? Not the physical stuff - the mental/emotional part.

Yeah, boot camp was really hard. Psychologically, I feel like I was willing to do my best and I was giving my best, but there are games being played, psychological games that are hard to deal with when you don't have a lot of real-world experience. It was okay; it was good. I feel like some people, at boot camp, were kind of unfazed by the intensity. They knew it was a game and they knew to monitor their energy. Me, I was 100% the whole time, yeah.
It was both good for me, but also I didn't really have perspective on what I was going through. I don't think a lot of people do.

21. Was there a moment in boot camp where something "broke" or shifted in you? A defining moment?

So boot camp, I was doing really good. I was a squad leader. I was doing pretty good, but at one point I messed something up, and my drill instructors turned on me. I was a squad leader throughout the whole boot camp, but towards the end I got mixed up on some instructions where they basically said, "We're going to treat you like a marine now."

During one, when we were getting our pictures taken, I went to go get a snack out of the vending machine because I thought they were done fucking with us. One drill instructor came after me and was like, "Who do you think you are, blah blah blah?" I think, because he highlighted me, all the other drill instructors highlighted me, and so they put me on fire watch and they demoted me. I went from a top performer to being treated like crap, and that really sucked. They took away my original eagle, globe, and anchor that I got from the crucible, and that really fucked me up.

22. What surprised you most about yourself during that time?

Nothing really surprised me about myself. I was surprised about the intensity because it's like they can fuck with you pretty hard, and I never got fucked with, but that was fine.

23. Did you almost quit at any point? What kept you going?

No, I didn't almost quit at any time. The boot camp was actually light work compared to after boot camp when I went to infantry training. Infantry training was really hard because it was rucking, like carrying weight on your back for long distances. I was a really good runner, but the weight on my back was like a grind and I didn't prepare for that. That just really sucked and was very humbling. I did good on everything else, but that was very just brutal. And then the other thing is, after that I went to recon training, and recon training again was really freaking hard. The running part of recon was something I was pretty good at, and the swimming part of recon I wasn't as good at. I was already a decent swimmer, but they just basically kill you in the pool, so it was really hard to survive. Over time I was with the lead pack that was doing good, but I eventually ended up failing recon and getting kicked out, and that really sucked.

After that I went to the fleet. In the fleet, as a boot for a little bit, I got squad leader, and then I went for sniper school or the sniper platoon tryout. I got into that, and it was great.

## Section 3: The Marine Corps Years (2012-2017)

### Infantry & Scout Sniper

24. You went Infantry (0311) then Scout Sniper (0317) - what drew you to the sniper path specifically?

Ok, I went to sniper school, or scout sniper path, because I failed out of Recon. When I went to the Breacher Infantry, only some of the dudes were good, but not all of them were great. Some of them really sucked, so I wanted to be with an elite group of dudes again, and the Scout Snipers were the elite group of dudes.

25. What was sniper training like? What did it demand of you mentally?

Okay, so you have to earn your way to sniper school. I was a boot, and I was a boot for a while, and then there's a whole story here, but basically I ended up doing all the sniper stuff.

Right before deployment, there was some drama, and basically they had to cut one of the sniper teams, and the team that I was on got cut. We weren't going to be able to deploy, so I went back to my old platoon commander from when I was in the infantry, regular infantry line platoon, and begged that commander to let me deploy with him. I did.

That deployment happened, and then when I got back from deployment, I had to try out again to get back into the sniper platoon. After I tried out again, I made it, and that showed a lot of grit, and I did really good.

Basically, I then ended up going. I was with a new group of guys, and the new group of guys was amazing. I ended up getting a slot to sniper school. I went through sniper school and completed it, and it was awesome.

Yeah, so there's a lot to talk about with sniper school. You have to be methodical with what you do. There's a whole process to learning to shoot long-range precision rifles, and you have to be calm under stress and do a bunch of little things right. The same thing for stalking: you have to do a bunch of little things right and pay attention.

At this time, when I went through sniper school, I was extremely prepared because we had done a bunch of training before that. I was pretty prepared, and it's not that it was a cakewalk, but I felt just very prepared. It was extremely hard, but I didn't fail anything. The closest thing I almost failed was land navigation, but I did good on the shooting part and I did good on the stalking part. Those are two very hard things to do that a lot of people fail in sniper school.

26. You mentioned "stalking requires patience and concentration" - can you tell me about a specific stalk that sticks with you? What did it teach you?

OK, stalking is like hunting, but it's graded. What's interesting is when I first got into the sniper platoon, we would do stalk lanes and we would always fail and we'd always get caught, and it was mostly a hazing thing.

Over time, you learn how to plan your stalks, plan your routes. Check your movement so that you're never exposing yourself. You also learn to actually camouflage in a legit way, where once you make it down the stalk lane to where you need to go, it's also a lot about planning your route and stuff like that. You get down to where you want to go, final firing position, and you actually camouflage up.

In the beginning, you feel like you don't really know what you're doing, and it feels like luck. As you get really good, it doesn't feel like luck. It feels like disciplined execution. That's kind of what it was. It's like a really good plan executed well; that's what makes you a good sniper. That's what makes you good at stalking.

27. What's something about scout snipers that civilians don't understand or get wrong?

I think the main thing is snipers. It's just a lot of hard work; it's not a lot of shooting. Shooting is only part of it; most of it is mission planning. In the sniper platoon, you're mostly observing, at least when I was in with Afghanistan. At the tail end of Afghanistan, I didn't shoot people; I didn't get the opportunity to, but yeah, it's just a lot of hard work to do to plan missions really well. If you plan a mission well, then you get the opportunity to deploy your rifle and shoot bad guys.

28. Did you see combat? How did that shape you - or not?

No, I did not see Combat, and that was a bummer. It was kind of like, I mean, it was a bummer, but maybe it was also a blessing. I felt like the movie, the guy in the movie Jarhead, where he works real hard and he never gets the opportunity to shoot his gun. It's like really sucks for him. That's how I felt.

29. Who was the best Marine you served with? What made them great?

There was a bunch of really great marines. Okay, so there's a bunch of great marines and a bunch of terrible marines, but yeah, they're some of the best marines. One of them was my team leader. After I got cut in the sniper platoon, his name is Maimon. This guy was short. He wasn't physically imposing, but he was smart as hell. Because he was small and stuff, he had to make up for it by being smarter than everyone else. He was, and he planned better than everyone else. He just knew the equipment and everything. He knew the tricks of the trade better than everyone else, and he was a great teacher, and I love that guy.

30. Who was a mentor or leader who shaped you during those years? What did they teach you?

Yeah, so there were a few good mentors. Mayman was definitely one. There was another one who basically gave me a shot, believed in me, and so I think I was great. I thought to myself many times, being disappointed by leadership. I was more disappointed by my leaders than I'm impressed by my leaders, but we put it to you that way.

31. Was there a moment of failure or screwing up that taught you something important?

Yeah, I failed a bunch of times in the marines. I failed recon one time, and then I got to try again and I passed that. I passed it, and I failed out a second time during recon, so that really sucked.

Then I got into the sniper platoon, and that was great. Then I got kicked out of the sniper platoon, and then I had to earn my way back into the sniper platoon. It was a final triumphant thing when I finally got back into the sniper platoon and got a chance to go to sniper school. I made it through. That was all an exhilarating, amazing experience, an amazing comeback story for me.

And then after I became a sniper and got deployed as a sniper, I was in Kuwait and didn't see combat. I just spent six or seven months in Kuwait as a QRF, quick reaction force. Nothing happened, and that just really sucked. I was just like, "I don't want to waste more of my life training to be a sniper and never actually shooting my gun or being deployed to combat."

I tried out for Marine Corps Special Operations called MARSOC. Honestly, this is another time where I failed. I should have prepared more. I was already a sniper, and I had been through some hard training, so I was basically just like, "Bring it." But the thing is, with MARSOC selection, it's again all the best guys, and I should have again prepared more, because in sniper school I was basically killing it. I was doing really good, and at the MARSOC selection I was not killing it. I was basically surviving; my times should have been faster, and I should have just been stronger and had more endurance, because then I would have been more aware, and it wouldn't have been as challenging for me.

If I was stronger and more prepared, if I had taken the time to be stronger and more prepared, I probably would've done a lot better, but I didn't. I was also freshly married, so I was a little bit distracted. I feel like I could give myself that excuse because I was a little bit distracted. I was like, "I'm either gonna be MARSOC or I'm getting out of the Marine Corps." I don't think I was as balls-to-the-wall with MARSOC as I should've been in order to get in there. I was mostly more disenfranchised with the Marine Corps at that time.

### MARSOC Tryout & Getting Out

32. What made you try out for MARSOC (Marine Corps Special Operations)?

Yeah, so MARSOC I tried out because I was frustrated with the regular Marine Corps. I was like, "I'm either going special operations or I'm getting out." That was my thinking.

33. What was the tryout like? How far did you get?

The tryout was extremely hard and extremely professional. It wasn't even a month; it was like a few weeks of prep where they were increasingly running you through physical fitness stuff. Then it was like a week, two weeks, three weeks of the actual selection. The selection was extremely professional. I think it's modeled after the Green Beret selection.

I made it through, but I just didn't get selected. I think there were a lot of people trying out, and it wasn't the kind of thing where, if you're left standing, you're in. It was even if you make it through the selection, you still have to get chosen to be with them to join MARSOC. I wasn't a top performer, so that sucked.

34. How did it feel to not get selected? Was that devastating, or did you process it differently?

Feeling: how did I feel not getting selected? I felt some regret because I was like, "I should have prepared more." I was also like, "This is fate," because I was like, "I'm a scout sniper. If they want me, they can choose me." They didn't want me, so I was like, "I took it as a sign from God that it's not meant to be."

35. Did not getting selected change your trajectory? Would you still have gotten out if you'd made it?

Yeah, not getting selected definitely changed my trajectory, because after not getting selected I had to figure out what I was going to do with my life. I decided to go to a tech coding boot camp and learn how to code after that, after I got out of the Marine Corps. No, the thing is, if I would have made it into MARSOC, I would have not gotten out of the Marine Corps. I might've gotten out like another four years or eight years or something afterwards, but I would definitely have stayed in and re-enlisted with MARSOC.

36. What was the decision to leave the Marines like? Was it clear, or did you wrestle with it?

Yeah, when I got out of the Marines, I basically hated the Marines. I was just like, "I'm done with this. I'm gonna try something else. I wanna do something else with my life." I was just frustrated because it was all bullshit. We're training for nothing. We're not gonna deploy. I hated the leadership. Okay, I had good leadership at some points, but I was also just frustrated because here's the thing: we had one of the best sniper platoons in the Marine Corps at that time, and yet we did nothing on deployment, and that pissed me off.

I was like, "What's the point of training so hard when you don't do what you're trained to do? If we're not valuable on the battlefield?" I was just like, "I'm not gonna train hard just to sit around in the desert for years."

37. What did you think you were going to do when you got out?

When I got out, I thought I was going to start a business, and I actually bought a domain and started creating a website on Squarespace about what I was going to do and stuff like that. I bought the domain Wayne Enterprises because I thought I was just going to start several businesses and run them all and stuff like that.

38. How did it feel to be a civilian again after 5 years in the Marines?

How did it feel to be a civilian again? It wasn't much of a change because I was still around the military community a little bit. There's a big thing about being veterans. It actually was dumb because so many veterans just live off their prior military status. I was just like, "No, I want to do something else. I don't want to keep looking back in the rearview mirror." So I was basically just trying to move on and start a new life, start a new career, start afresh.

I think just with the Marines, the main thing I got out of it was a tolerance for pain and ambiguity and hardship. Like, nothing is going to be as hard as what I did in the Marines, basically, at least physically. Mainly just mental resilience and toughness in a general sense.

There are a few things, but it's just a general rule that I'm a hard worker, I feel like, but hard work doesn't get you where you want to go all the time. You need to also manage relationships and work closely with others. You can't just work; you can't just do hard work in a silo.

41. How do you think about your military service now - pride, complicated feelings, both?

I'm proud of being in Sniper Platoon and being a Sniper, but I'm also just like, pissed off that I didn't go through with MARSOC and just pissed off at the Marine Corps charges for without learning lessons. It feels like there's fresh guys getting in the Marine Corps, going through dumb stuff all over again. So it's a mixed bag. And I also think about people joining the ring courts. They join because they basically have no other options. There are other reasons, but it's mostly because they have no other options.

42. Is there a specific moment from those 5 years that defines who you are today?

A specific moment? No, but the idea of failing and trying again and coming back is something that sticks with me and that I'm proud of. I'm not afraid of failure.

## Section 4: The Path to Tech

### Coding Bootcamp & Early Career

43. When you read that Tim Ferriss line about needing to be technical - what clicked for you? Why did that land so hard?

That landed so hard because, as a sniper, I felt like we were the most dangerous people on the battlefield. We could do the most damage, and in the same way it is like, "Okay, if you're starting a business, you want to have outsize impact. You want to be the one who can pull the strings and make an impact." You need to be technical, so that kind of correlated. It was really hard to learn sniper skills and get into the sniper platoon, but then I was a technical asset that could be employed. That was really special, and it made sense for learning how to code. It was like the same thing. It's like if you know how to code, you have a lot of power. And you can make a big impact.

44. What made you choose Sabio specifically? How did you find it?

I went to Savio because there was a Marine that went through Savio, and he was recruiting people to come through Savio, and I just really liked this guy. He basically went through Savio, and then he was being an entrepreneur, and he was going back through Savio to have this. They do it in cohorts, and he was having one of the cohorts build out an app idea for him. He was a big inspiration to me, and I was just like, yeah, dude, I want to do what you do. He was making moves, and he's very successful now, I think, but no one else is making moves like that guy was making moves. I was just like, let's do Savio. I love the idea of a boot camp, like an expedited learning environment. I was just like, I was ready to just do whatever it takes to learn how to code. And I wanted to learn fast.

45. You failed a programming class before but succeeded at bootcamp - what was different?

They just taught it at the boot camp in a very practical way. It was literally like:

- You learned basic math.
- You learn multiplication.
- You learn geometry.
  They built you up in such an intuitive way with practical, hands-on learning that it was just great. In college, junior college, it was very abstract, concepts, lecture-based, versus the boot camp was very hands-on, output, hands-on, like "We'll show you how to do everything." That was a much better learning environment.

46. What was it like learning to code after being a scout sniper? Did the skills transfer, or was it a total reset?

Uhh, what was it like learning to code after being a scout sniper? It was just like being able to concentrate. I was like, "Hell yeah." I also loved the idea of being paid to think and use my brain, because I felt like I put a lot of thought and consideration into being a sniper. There was so much back planning and stuff going into it, but it didn't matter. You were at the mercy of being deployed or not, and you weren't getting paid to think as much. I love the idea of being paid to think and make good decisions when it came to coding and programming.

47. Was there a moment in bootcamp when you knew "I can actually do this"?

So I started. I was getting it, but we were all sort of at the same level. I loved demoing. That was maybe one of the big things that I did, because in Snipers you had to mission plan, and then you had to brief people on your missions. I was pretty good at that part. I put a lot of time and effort into the mission planning piece and being able to brief people. When it came to the Coding Boot Camp, when we would build stuff, I loved demoing what we built to everyone else. That was a good thing for me.

48. What was it like being hired by Sabio after graduating? How did that feel?

I felt on top of the world after getting hired by Savio; it was such an honor, but quickly I realized it was just like they mostly wanted me to just recruit people to do their program. That kinda sucked.

49. Why did you leave LA and come back to the East Coast?

I left LA to come back to the East Coast because I mostly got fired from Sabia because I couldn't recruit people. The problem was I needed to get on military bases in order to do presentations, and I couldn't do that. I basically had no network, and I was trying to figure out how to talk to people and tell them about coding. I had no network, and I was struggling to do sales. That was the reason.

### Career Before BuildOS

50. Census Bureau, Marine Corps lawyer platform, Air Force document system, Curri - that's a journey. What's the thread that connects them for you?

There wasn't really a connection between those. Those were all Marine Corps East Coast-based companies doing those things. Curry was the first one that was remote, and it was like the nut I was trying to crack because there's a Y Combinator-backed startup. I knew those were top-tier companies, and it was in a top-tier company, a major step ahead, step above all my other experiences.

51. The Air Force document ingest system you built - that was before LLMs. What was that like? What did you learn?

Yeah, the Air Force Document Ingest System. That was before LLMs. It was just a graph database underneath where you'd link different pieces together. It was too complicated. They were trying to make it work, but I knew it was interesting to play with. The thing is, it was so technical, and it was only one or two dudes that sort of understood what was going on, and they didn't teach anyone. That's a problem. If you're going to have a system, it needs to be understood by everyone so everyone can work on it and add to it. It was more like one or two dudes that were smart that were like, "Oh, we're going to make all this piece work, and no one else is going to know about it." Looking back, it's just like people just trying to monetize being smart and not actually building anything useful.

52. At Curri, you built the exception system and the integrations (Uber, Lyft, DoorDash). What's the project you're most proud of? Why?

So Curri was like an amazing experience. I learned so much, and I got to build so much, and I was with a lot of other great engineers. Yeah, I built this one thing. It was basically a data map system showing all of our customers and stats on our customers. It is important to see geolocations of different customers and where they're booking, so you can have a geographical understanding of what's happening within an area. It's going to be used as a sales tool. That was an amazing idea that they didn't understand. The sales people saw that, and they were like, "This is amazing." The technical people saw that, and they were confused. That pisses me off still to this day. But other than that, I built a bunch of other things and it was great.

53. You mentioned frustration with Linear at Curri - "just look at my pull requests." What was the underlying frustration there?

It was mostly me interacting with my manager. It was just like, "Get work done." It's like trying to measure your impact. Basically, boil it down to how many tickets are you moving in Linear? As opposed to trying to understand the impact of what I'm doing. That just pissed me off.

He wasn't interested in how what we're building makes the company better or makes us get more money or whatever. He was more concerned with, "Are the tickets getting checked off in Linear?" That pissed me off. He didn't have a startup vibe; he just had a traditional software engineering vibe where it's just like, "Move tickets!"

54. What made you finally leave Curri? Was there a breaking point, or a gradual realization?

Yeah, so they basically let me go because I was working on this map thing. They were like, "What I had built before was this a third-party integration engine for us being able to use Uber Lift Door Dash for deliveries, and that was being scaled up. There were other people doing what I had built and working on it, and I was just like, 'Okay, so we already got that done. I'm gonna work on something else.' They just didn't really understand what I was working on, and they didn't like how I was working at that time. I thought I was basically invaluable to the company, like I couldn't be fired, but they were just like, 'No, you're distracted,' and they let me go. That just kinda sucked.

Again, what I realized is that all the people that were there at Curry who were on site were just thought of more highly and more highly valued as opposed to me, who was remote. I really, if I was there in person, I feel like I would've been able to do much better, but I feel like they got the impression that I just didn't care and I was remote and not distracted, so they just kinda let me go. That sucked, but yeah, my learning was that you really got to work with people. You can't just trust that your work speaks for itself.
