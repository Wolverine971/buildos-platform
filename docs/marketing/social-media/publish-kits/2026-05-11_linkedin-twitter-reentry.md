## Pair 1 — Thinking environment / anti-AI

**Rationale:** Cheapest re-entry. Pure first-person observation, no product, no agents, no jargon. Reactivates the feed without forcing DJ to "announce." Lets him sound like himself before he sounds like a founder.

**Hook posture:** Confession + reframe, not realization. Punchier and harder than the v3 frame because the goal here is to land a strong stake on day one back. Lead lands the accusation on yourself before the reader can deflect it.

### LinkedIn

```
I've spent ten years buying productivity apps to avoid the actual work.

Not the work on my to-do list. The work of thinking clearly about what's on it.

Notion didn't fix it. Linear didn't fix it. A new app every six months didn't fix it. What I kept calling "I need to get organized" was always "I have not done the thinking yet."

What I actually wanted was a place where the messy half-formed version of an idea was allowed. Where I could come back two days later and not have to reconstruct what I meant.

Most of what gets called productivity software isn't about getting things done. It's about looking like you are.
```

### Twitter

```
i've spent ten years buying productivity apps to avoid the actual work.

not the work on my to-do list. the work of thinking clearly about what's on it.
```

**Alternate harder LinkedIn hook (swap top two lines if the confession framing feels too inward):**

```
Productivity software sells you the feeling of being organized. Not actually being organized.

I've fallen for the trade for ten years.
```

**Pairing note:** LinkedIn carries the confession + reframe; Twitter compresses to the hook + the second-beat punch. Same accusation, tweet just lands faster.

**Risk note:** Could read as "productivity guru takes a swing at productivity" — keep it first-person, no "you" until the closing line. The "looking like you are" landing is the snapback; don't soften it.

## Pair 2 — Go-between across agents

**Rationale:** Plants the agent-era flag once voice is warm. Uses the locked Connect Your Agents language ("reading off the same sheet of paper," "context surface") without naming the feature.

### LinkedIn

```
I have five AI tools open right now. ChatGPT, Codex, Claude, Claude Code, OpenClaw.

Each one is having a slightly different conversation with me the same related projects. None of them know what the other ones said. I am the integration layer. I am the one carrying context between them in my head and in copy-pastes.

The thing nobody talks about with agents is that you become the thing they share. Your attention is the shared memory. That's not a workflow, that's a tax.

What I actually want is for all of them to read off the same sheet of paper. The same project notes, the same context, the same view of what I'm working on. So when I open a new chat I'm not starting from zero, and the agents aren't contradicting each other because they're each guessing from scratch.

The agent era doesn't need smarter agents. It needs a place they can all read from.

It needs BuildOS.
```

### Twitter — 4-tweet thread

```
1/ i have five ai tools open right now. chatgpt, codex, claude, claude code, openclaw.

each one is having a slightly different conversation with me about the same project.

2/ none of them know what the other ones said. i am the integration layer. i am the one carrying context between them in copy-pastes.

3/ the thing nobody talks about with agents is that you become the thing they share. your attention is the shared memory.

that's not a workflow. that's a tax.

4/ the agent era doesn't need smarter agents. it needs a place they can all read from.
```

**Pairing note:** LinkedIn carries the lived workflow; Twitter compresses to the closing line as the headline thesis. Both end on the same image: a shared sheet of paper.

**Risk note:** Most likely failure is drifting into "and that's why we built X" — keep it observation only, do not name BuildOS, do not name the integration. The locked-language phrases ("reading off the same sheet of paper," "context surface") do the work without the pitch.

**Locked language source:** `docs/specs/connect-your-agents-promotion-readiness-2026-05-08.md` §3.

**Note on OpenClaw inclusion:** Project memory (`project_connect_your_agents_promo.md`) says don't mention OpenClaw in launch posts because it dilutes the "works with the tools you already use" pitch. That rule was scoped to the Connect Your Agents _launch_ posts. DJ explicitly listed OpenClaw here as part of his personal daily stack, which is a different framing — "what I actually use," not "what BuildOS works with." Keeping it in per his call.

## Pair 3 — Build-in-public: the creator testimonial hunt

**Rationale (longer, since this is the agent's pick):**

Angle #3 is **the local creator testimonial hunt as a build-in-public moment** — DJ announcing, in his own voice, that he's flying around to 10 creators in DC, Baltimore, and NYC to sit with them and watch them use BuildOS. Reasons:

- It launched **today** (2026-05-11). It's the freshest real thing in DJ's world.
- It maps directly to the **creator wedge** strategy and the Superhuman-style "the onboarding call IS the marketing" play that's already in the growth playbook.
- It's a **recruiting post** as well as a brand post — every comment is a potential creator who wants in. That's a loop, not a one-shot.
- It re-establishes the build-in-public lane on LinkedIn after 18 days dark, which is _the_ lane that compounds for solo founders.
- It is not an AI post, not a product post, not a positioning post — it's a "founder doing the work" post, which is the highest-trust kind of post DJ can make in week one back.

The two other angles are good but conceptual. This one is a thing happening this week.

### LinkedIn

```
I'm spending the next 60 days flying to creators.

The plan: 10 authors, YouTubers, and podcasters in DC, Baltimore, and New York. Sit down with each of them. Watch them use BuildOS for the first time. Not a demo, not a pitch — me shutting up while they try to do their actual work in it.

Every founder doc I've read says you should do this earlier than feels comfortable. The closer you get to the user, the less you can hide behind the deck. The deck is where bad assumptions go to feel rigorous.

So I'm doing it in person. One creator at a time. If the product is wrong, I'll see it on their face before they say a word. If it's right, I'll watch what changes.

If you're a creator in DC, Baltimore, or NYC making something complex — a book, a channel, a show, a course — and you'd be down to spend 90 minutes with me, I'd love to hear from you.
```

### Twitter — 3-tweet thread

```
1/ spending the next 60 days flying to creators.

10 authors, youtubers, and podcasters in dc, baltimore, nyc. sitting down with each of them and watching them use buildos for the first time.

2/ not a demo. not a pitch. me shutting up while they try to do their actual work in it.

the closer you get to the user the less you can hide behind the deck. the deck is where bad assumptions go to feel rigorous.

3/ if you're a creator in dc/baltimore/nyc making something complex — book, channel, show, course — and you'd be down to spend 90 minutes with me, dm me.
```

**Pairing note:** Both versions are identical in intent — LinkedIn carries the "why" (the deck line), Twitter carries the recruit (the DM ask). On LinkedIn the recruit is softer; on Twitter it's the explicit close.

**Risk note:** Only failure mode is the recruiting ask reading as "I need users" instead of "I'm doing the work" — the "watching them, shutting up" framing keeps it on the right side, don't soften that line.
