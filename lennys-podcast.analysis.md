# Amol Avasare / Anthropic transcript analysis

Companion to `lennys-podcast.md`.

Prepared on April 9, 2026.

## Quick status check

As of April 2026, public sources still describe Amol Avasare as Anthropic's Head of Growth, so for your note he appears to be current, not former.

## The big picture

This interview is not really about "growth hacks." It is about four deeper ideas:

1. In AI products, activation is the highest-leverage growth problem.
2. For AI-native products, bigger bets may matter more than endless micro-optimization.
3. Good growth often comes from quality, focus, and trust, not just funnel squeezing.
4. Cold email works when it is sharp, targeted, timely, and persistent.

For BuildOS specifically, the parts that matter most are:

- how he thinks about onboarding and activation in AI products
- his view that the right friction can improve conversion
- his "focus on one painful wedge" philosophy
- his use of AI to automate growth analysis and internal coordination
- his cold email approach for creating opportunities that do not exist yet

## What he explicitly said in the transcript

### 1. His Anthropic cold email was simple, direct, and timed well

Relevant section: around `03:15`

What he said, paraphrased:

- He was already a heavy Claude user.
- He noticed Anthropic had a great product but "obviously" did not yet have a growth team.
- He cold emailed Mike Krieger directly even though there was no listed role.
- His email was basically: I love the product, you need a growth team, I think I could help, want to talk?
- He had a tested subject line with high open rate, but he kept the exact wording secret.
- He deliberately avoided crowded channels like LinkedIn and work email when possible.
- He tried to reach people through personal email instead.
- He kept the message very short.
- His structure was: who I am, why I would be a good fit, we should chat.
- He believes in following up multiple times if he really cares, until told to stop.

Takeaway:

This was not magic copywriting. It was strong targeting plus a credible diagnosis plus a low-friction ask.

### 2. Activation is the main growth problem in AI

Relevant sections: around `13:55`, `18:05`, `20:57`

What he said, paraphrased:

- Day 0 / day 1 activation has always mattered, but in AI it matters even more.
- Models improve faster than product teams can translate new capabilities into clear user value.
- Many users still show up with low-value prompts, so the product has to guide them toward higher-value use cases.
- The highest leverage is matching the right user to the right feature or product.

Takeaway:

If your product just drops people into a blank prompt box, you are making activation harder than it needs to be.

### 3. Quality improvements can outperform conversion tricks

Relevant section: around `18:05`

What he said, paraphrased:

- At Mercury, they spent a quarter ignoring short-term metric obsession and just fixing onboarding quality.
- That quarter became one of the highest-impact growth efforts of his career.
- His meta-lesson is that quality drives growth.

Takeaway:

A smoother, clearer, more trustworthy onboarding flow can create more growth than "growth tactics."

### 4. The right friction helps conversion

Relevant sections: around `20:57`

What he said, paraphrased:

- He has repeatedly seen well-designed friction improve funnel completion.
- Bad friction is annoying and adds no value.
- Good friction helps users understand why the product is for them.
- Breaking a heavy form into smaller steps often helps by lowering cognitive load.
- Asking users a few questions up front can improve activation and also improve lifecycle marketing later.

Takeaway:

The common advice "remove all friction" is too simplistic. If friction increases relevance, clarity, or confidence, it can lift conversion.

### 5. Anthropic biases toward large swings, not just small wins

Relevant section: around `27:06`

What he said, paraphrased:

- Traditional growth teams often spend most time on small and medium experiments.
- At Anthropic, the mix is flipped much more toward larger bets.
- His reasoning: if the underlying AI capability is compounding exponentially, future product value will dwarf today's value.
- In AI-native products, tiny local optimizations can miss the larger opportunity.

Takeaway:

If the product's core value curve is steeply improving because of AI, the growth team should spend more time finding the next big unlock, not only shaving friction off old flows.

### 6. They are already automating parts of growth

Relevant sections: around `33:34`, `38:20`

What he said, paraphrased:

- Anthropic is experimenting with using Claude to identify opportunities, implement changes, test them, and analyze results.
- Right now it is mostly copy changes and small UI tweaks.
- He describes current performance as roughly "junior PM" level.
- He expects the scope of autonomous growth work to expand quickly.

Takeaway:

The near-term AI leverage point is not full autonomous strategy. It is low-risk, high-volume optimization work around copy, UX details, analysis, and monitoring.

### 7. PM bottlenecks are shifting

Relevant sections: around `41:07`, `47:19`, `51:13`

What he said, paraphrased:

- Engineers are getting more leverage from AI than PMs and designers today.
- That means PM and design can become the bottleneck.
- For small projects, engineers may need to act like mini PMs.
- He dislikes heavy documentation by default and prefers prototyping, Slack discussion, and quick kickoffs.

Takeaway:

In AI-native teams, speed increasingly comes from clear direction and cross-functional alignment, not from writing long planning docs.

### 8. Focus beats breadth

Relevant section: around `1:06:27`

What he said, paraphrased:

- Anthropic's focus on coding and B2B was partly strategic and partly forced by constraints.
- Constraints created clarity.
- Deep focus helped them find escape velocity.

Takeaway:

If you are early, broad "AI for everyone" positioning is usually weaker than a narrow wedge with obvious pain and a strong feedback loop.

### 9. Leave money on the table

Relevant sections: around `1:12:10`

What he said, paraphrased:

- Growth teams often make the mistake of squeezing every last dollar.
- He thinks good companies should be comfortable leaving money on the table.
- Anthropic is willing to trade off metrics for safety, quality, brand, and user trust.

Takeaway:

Short-term growth that damages trust is usually expensive later.

### 10. Internal transparency is a force multiplier

Relevant section: around `1:22:53`

What he said, paraphrased:

- Anthropic uses open Slack heavily, including "notebook channels" where people post ongoing thinking.
- He uses Claude/Cowork to scan Slack and identify potential misalignment.
- He also uses scheduled AI reviews of charts, goals, and team activity.

Takeaway:

If your internal knowledge is legible, both humans and agents can work faster.

## What applies most directly to you / BuildOS

BuildOS is an AI-powered productivity platform. That means the most transferable lessons are not Anthropic's scale, but the product shape.

### 1. Your onboarding should probably do more routing

If BuildOS currently relies on "sign up and figure it out," you should probably add more guided routing:

- role or job title
- team type
- primary use case
- most urgent workflow to solve first
- preferred output format

This is exactly the kind of "good friction" Amol is talking about.

### 2. Your product should feel outcome-first, not prompt-first

One of the strongest supporting signals from external research is Anthropic's own Cowork positioning: they explicitly say most AI tools are built around the prompt, while Cowork is built around the outcome. BuildOS should steal that frame where possible.

Practical translation:

- default users into outcomes, not blank canvases
- provide fast starts for 3-5 concrete jobs
- ask for the deliverable they want, then backfill the workflow

### 3. You should likely sharpen one wedge even more

The repo reads like a broad AI productivity platform. That may be directionally right, but Amol's view suggests the practical GTM wedge should be narrower.

Questions worth forcing:

- What is the single user segment where BuildOS is obviously better than general-purpose chat?
- Which workflow is painful, repetitive, and expensive enough that people would switch behavior?
- What wedge creates a data or distribution flywheel?

### 4. Your growth tooling should become more agentic internally

You do not need Anthropic scale to copy this.

You should likely have agents or scheduled jobs that:

- summarize signups and activation funnel changes each morning
- surface onboarding drop-off anomalies
- watch support conversations for repeated confusion
- run competitor onboarding teardowns
- flag internal product misalignment from Slack / docs / tickets

### 5. Your growth philosophy should skew toward trust

For an AI productivity tool, trust is part of the product. Anything that feels too growth-y, manipulative, or over-promised will hurt you.

That means:

- do not overstate capability
- do not hide limitations
- do not optimize activation in ways that produce bad-fit users
- do not force broad usage before one workflow is genuinely great

## Reverse-engineering his cold email strategy

This part is partly transcript-grounded and partly inference.

### What we know for sure

- He targets people very specifically.
- He reaches out when he has a real thesis, not just a desire.
- He optimizes heavily for the open.
- He avoids crowded channels.
- He keeps the body short.
- He explains who he is, why he fits, and asks for a conversation.
- He follows up if the target matters.

### What he is probably doing underneath

These are my inferences, not his direct words.

#### 1. He only sends emails when he has a sharp diagnosis

His Anthropic example was not "I'd love to work here."

It was:

- I use the product.
- I see a missing function.
- I think this gap matters.
- I am unusually qualified to fill it.

That is a much stronger cold email than enthusiasm alone.

#### 2. He likely writes subject lines that look human, relevant, and low-drama

Because he says he tested subject lines for open rate, I would assume the subject line style is:

- short
- easy to scan on mobile
- context-specific
- not clever for the sake of cleverness
- not spammy

I would guess it is closer to:

- `Growth at Anthropic`
- `Idea for Claude growth`
- `You need a growth team`

than to anything cute, clickbaity, or long.

Those examples are my approximations, not his exact line.

#### 3. He probably optimizes for asymmetry

A good cold email creates upside that is disproportionate to the effort.

His message likely tried to trigger one thought in Mike Krieger's head:

"This person sees something real, and replying costs me almost nothing."

#### 4. He uses persistence as a seriousness signal

His follow-up rule of thumb is effectively:

- if I care, I keep going
- if they are not interested, they can tell me

That is useful, but only if the message is respectful and personalized.

### The likely structure of his cold emails

#### Version A: Job / opportunity creation

1. Subject line
2. One line proving relevance
3. One line naming the gap or opportunity
4. One line establishing credibility
5. Very light CTA

Example structure:

```text
Subject: Growth at Anthropic

Mike,

I've been using Claude heavily and love the product.
It seems clear Anthropic needs a real growth function as adoption scales.
I've led growth at X / built Y / learned Z as a founder, and I think I could help.
Open to a quick chat?

- Name
```

Again, that is not his actual email. It is the most plausible reconstruction from what he described.

#### Version B: Customer / partner outreach

This is the same logic translated to your future outreach.

```text
Subject: BuildOS for [team / workflow]

[Name],

I took a look at how your team handles [specific workflow].
I think there is a clear opportunity to remove a lot of manual work around [pain point].
We're building BuildOS for exactly that type of outcome, and I think we'd be a fit because [specific reason].
Worth a 15-minute look?

- Name
```

#### Version C: User research / discovery outreach

```text
Subject: Quick question about [workflow]

[Name],

I saw that you work on [relevant function].
I'm researching how teams currently handle [specific task], especially where AI tools still fall short.
You seem exactly like the kind of person with a strong opinion here.
Open to a quick conversation this or next week?

- Name
```

### Rules to steal from him

- Target fewer people, better.
- Do not send a cold email unless you have an actual point of view.
- Keep the body short enough to read on a phone.
- Make the first reply easy.
- Use the least crowded channel that still feels appropriate.
- Follow up because busy does not mean no.
- Stop once the recipient opts out or clearly ignores multiple respectful attempts.

### Where his approach is strongest

- high-value targets
- founder-led outreach
- recruiting or partnerships
- customer discovery with senior operators
- situations where there is no formal process yet

### Where his approach is weaker

- mass outbound
- generic sales spam
- audiences where personal email feels invasive
- messages without a sharp insight

## A practical cold email playbook for you

If you want to use his style later, I would do it like this.

### Step 1: build a tiny, sharp target list

Not 500 people. Start with 10-25.

For each target, capture:

- their role
- why they are relevant now
- the pain or opportunity you think they have
- why you believe BuildOS is specifically relevant
- the lowest-friction ask you can make

### Step 2: write the thesis before the email

Before drafting, complete this sentence:

"This person should respond because they are likely dealing with **_, and I have a credible point of view on _**."

If you cannot finish that sentence cleanly, the email is not ready.

### Step 3: write a short email with one idea only

Bad cold emails try to do four things at once.

Good cold emails usually do one:

- point out a problem
- point out an opportunity
- ask for insight
- offer a relevant solution

### Step 4: use a low-friction CTA

Good:

- "Worth a quick chat?"
- "Open to a 15-minute call?"
- "If useful, I can send a one-page teardown."

Bad:

- "Can I book 45 minutes next Tuesday?"
- "I'd love to show you the full platform and roadmap."

### Step 5: follow up with a new angle, not just a bump

Suggested sequence:

- Email 1: core thesis
- Email 2: short follow-up with one new concrete point
- Email 3: close-the-loop message with easy out

Example:

```text
Subject: Re: BuildOS for finance ops

Wanted to follow up with one more concrete thought:
I think your team is a strong fit because the highest-friction part here seems to be turning scattered inputs into a finished output, not generating first-draft text.

If that is directionally right, happy to show how we're approaching it.
```

## Concrete experiments I would run for BuildOS

### Product / onboarding

1. Add a short onboarding router that asks role, use case, and desired outcome.
2. Break any dense setup screen into smaller steps and test completion rate.
3. Replace at least one blank-state experience with an outcome-first starter flow.
4. Instrument day-0 activation around first meaningful completed workflow, not just signup.

### Internal growth operations

1. Create a daily AI-generated growth brief covering signups, activation, and anomalies.
2. Run weekly competitor onboarding teardowns with an agent.
3. Set up a recurring "misalignment scan" across Slack, docs, and tickets.

### Outbound / cold email

1. Build a list of 20 ideal people to contact.
2. For each, write a one-sentence diagnosis before drafting the email.
3. Test 3-5 subject line styles, but keep them plain and specific.
4. Keep the body under roughly 100 words for first touch.
5. Prepare two follow-ups in advance so you actually send them.

## My bottom-line read

The most valuable thing to copy from Amol is not a trick. It is a way of operating:

- find the highest-leverage bottleneck
- add enough structure to route people correctly
- bias toward quality and trust
- focus hard
- use AI aggressively for internal leverage
- do direct outreach when you see an opening

His cold emailing works because it is the expression of that broader style: sharp observation, strong relevance, minimal fluff, and real conviction.
