# Usability Quick Research

Use this child when a UI decision needs user evidence instead of another internal opinion.

## When to Use

- Planning quick research, interview questions, usability tests, prototype tests, assumption checks, or lightweight synthesis.
- The user asks how to validate a screen, reduce UX risk, learn from users, or run a practical usability pass without a large research program.
- A design review depends on unknown user goals, language, confidence, or task success.

## Workflow

1. Write the decision, bet size, current belief, unknowns, and what evidence would change the team's mind.
2. Separate discovery questions from evaluative usability questions.
3. Choose the smallest credible method: desk research, stakeholder interview, user interview, prototype test, 3-user usability test, survey, analytics review, support-log review, or old-research audit.
4. Prepare neutral prompts, representative participants or proxies, proxy limitations, and observable success criteria.
5. Synthesize into decisions, design changes, risks, confidence level, and questions that remain open.

## Guardrails

- Do not use research as validation theater. Start with what you need to know, not what you hope to prove.
- Do not overbuild a research plan when three good sessions would answer the current design risk.
- Do not recruit poor-fit participants and treat their feedback as truth.
- Do not accept old research as automatically useful. Check whether it answers today's question with credible participants, current context, and clear methods.
- Do not fight for "research" as a label when stakeholders care about risk, confidence, and better decisions.
